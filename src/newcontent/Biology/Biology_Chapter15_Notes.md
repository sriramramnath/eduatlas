## 15.1 The Human Population

### Population Growth

The human population has grown dramatically over the past few centuries. This exponential growth is due to several factors that have improved human survival and reproduction rates.

**Factors contributing to population growth:**
- Improved food production and distribution
- Better nutrition leading to better health
- Improved sanitation and clean water supplies
- Development of vaccines and antibiotics
- Better medical care and hospitals
- Reduced infant mortality
- Increased life expectancy
- Improved living conditions

### Environmental Impact

As the human population grows, the demand for resources increases. This leads to greater environmental impact:

- Increased demand for food, water, and energy
- More land needed for housing, agriculture, and industry
- Greater production of waste and pollution
- Habitat destruction for development
- Overexploitation of natural resources

## 15.2 Deforestation

### Definition

Deforestation is the permanent removal of trees and other vegetation from an area, converting forest land to non-forest use. This is one of the most significant human impacts on the environment.

### Causes of Deforestation

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Agriculture** | Land cleared for crops (palm oil, soybeans) and cattle ranching |
| **Logging** | Trees cut for timber, paper, and wood products |
| **Urban development** | Land cleared for housing, roads, and infrastructure |
| **Mining** | Forests removed to access minerals |
| **Fuel wood** | Trees cut for cooking and heating in developing countries |
| **Dams** | Forests flooded for hydroelectric projects |

### Consequences of Deforestation

**Biodiversity Loss**
- Habitat destruction leads to species extinction
- Fragmentation isolates populations
- Loss of food sources and nesting sites
- Reduced genetic diversity
- Approximately 80% of land animals live in forests

**Climate Change**
- Fewer trees means less CO₂ absorbed from atmosphere
- Burning forests releases stored carbon
- Contributes to global warming
- Changes local and regional climate patterns
- Reduced rainfall in deforested areas

**Soil Erosion**
- Tree roots hold soil together
- Canopy protects soil from heavy rain
- Without trees, soil washes away
- Leads to landslides and flooding
- Soil becomes less fertile

**Water Cycle Disruption**
- Reduced transpiration
- Less water vapour in atmosphere
- Reduced rainfall
- Rivers may dry up
- Increased flooding during heavy rain

**Indigenous People**
- Loss of traditional homelands
- Loss of cultural heritage
- Displacement and conflict
- Loss of traditional knowledge

### Sustainable Forest Management

- Selective logging (only mature trees)
- Reforestation and afforestation
- Protected areas and national parks
- Sustainable logging practices
- Agroforestry (trees with crops)
- International agreements and certification (FSC)

## 15.3 Pollution

### Types of Pollution

**Air Pollution**
- Caused by burning fossil fuels, industry, vehicles
- Includes: CO₂, CO, SO₂, NOx, particulates
- Causes respiratory problems
- Contributes to acid rain and global warming

**Water Pollution**
- Caused by sewage, industrial waste, agricultural runoff
- Includes: fertilisers, pesticides, heavy metals, plastics
- Harms aquatic ecosystems
- Contaminates drinking water

**Land Pollution**
- Caused by waste disposal, mining, industry
- Includes: landfill, toxic chemicals, nuclear waste
- Contaminates soil
- Harms wildlife and humans

**Noise Pollution**
- Caused by traffic, industry, construction
- Affects wildlife behaviour
- Causes stress and hearing problems in humans

**Light Pollution**
- Caused by street lights, buildings
- Affects wildlife behaviour
- Disrupts migration patterns
- Affects human sleep

## 15.4 Eutrophication

### Definition

Eutrophication is the process by which water bodies become overly enriched with minerals and nutrients, leading to excessive growth of algae and other aquatic plants. This ultimately results in oxygen depletion and death of aquatic organisms.

### Causes

**Main cause: Excess nutrients (nitrates and phosphates) entering water bodies**

Sources of excess nutrients:
- **Agricultural fertilisers**: Washed from fields into rivers and lakes
- **Sewage**: Contains nitrates and phosphates from human waste and detergents
- **Animal waste**: Manure washed into waterways
- **Industrial effluent**: Nutrient-rich waste from factories

### The Process of Eutrophication

1. **Excess nutrients enter water**
   - Nitrates and phosphates from fertilisers, sewage
   - These are limiting factors for algal growth

2. **Algal bloom occurs**
   - Rapid growth of algae on water surface
   - Forms a green layer (scum) on water
   - Blocks sunlight from reaching deeper water

3. **Submerged plants die**
   - Cannot photosynthesise due to lack of light
   - Die and begin to decompose

4. **Decomposition by bacteria**
   - Bacteria multiply to break down dead plants and algae
   - Use oxygen for respiration

5. **Oxygen depletion**
   - Bacteria use up dissolved oxygen in water
   - Oxygen levels drop severely
   - Water becomes anoxic (low oxygen)

6. **Death of aquatic animals**
   - Fish and other organisms suffocate
   - Mass die-off of aquatic life
   - Only anaerobic organisms survive

7. **Ecosystem collapse**
   - Most life in the water body dies
   - Water becomes stagnant and smelly

### Prevention of Eutrophication

- Use fertilisers more carefully (correct amounts, right time)
- Plant buffer zones of vegetation near waterways
- Better sewage treatment
- Regulations on industrial discharge
- Use phosphate-free detergents
- Regular monitoring of water quality

## 15.5 Global Warming and Climate Change

### The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that keeps Earth warm enough for life. However, human activities have enhanced this effect, causing global warming.

**Natural Greenhouse Effect:**
1. Sun's short-wave radiation passes through atmosphere
2. Earth absorbs radiation and warms up
3. Earth emits long-wave infrared radiation
4. Greenhouse gases absorb some infrared radiation
5. Radiation re-emitted in all directions, warming Earth
6. This maintains Earth's average temperature at about 15°C

**Enhanced Greenhouse Effect:**
- Human activities increase greenhouse gases
- More infrared radiation absorbed
- Earth's temperature rises
- Causes global warming and climate change

### Greenhouse Gases

| Gas | Source | Contribution |
|-----|--------|--------------|
| **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** | Burning fossil fuels, deforestation, cement production | ~60% |
| **Methane (CH₄)** | Livestock, rice paddies, landfill, fossil fuel extraction | ~20% |
| **Nitrous oxide (N₂O)** | Fertilisers, fossil fuel combustion | ~6% |
| **CFCs** | Old refrigerants, aerosols (now banned) | Variable |

### Evidence for Climate Change

- Rising global average temperatures
- Melting glaciers and polar ice caps
- Rising sea levels
- More extreme weather events
- Changes in precipitation patterns
- Ocean acidification
- Changes in plant and animal distributions

### Consequences of Global Warming

**Rising Sea Levels**
- Thermal expansion of seawater
- Melting ice caps and glaciers
- Threat to low-lying countries and islands
- Flooding of coastal areas
- Saltwater intrusion into freshwater supplies

**Weather Changes**
- More frequent and severe storms
- Heat waves and droughts
- Changes in rainfall patterns
- Flooding in some areas, drought in others

**Ecosystem Impacts**
- Shifts in species distributions
- Coral bleaching
- Loss of Arctic habitat for polar bears
- Changes in migration patterns
- Increased extinction rates

**Human Impacts**
- Food security threats
- Water shortages
- Spread of tropical diseases
- Economic costs
- Climate refugees

### Reducing Climate Change

**Reducing CO₂ emissions:**
- Use renewable energy (solar, wind, hydroelectric)
- Improve energy efficiency
- Use public transport, cycle, walk
- Electric vehicles
- Carbon capture and storage
- Reduce deforestation
- Plant more trees (afforestation)

**International agreements:**
- Kyoto Protocol (1997)
- Paris Agreement (2015)
- Net zero targets

## 15.6 Invasive Species

### Definition

An invasive species is a non-native species that has been introduced to an area and causes harm to the environment, economy, or human health.

### How Species are Introduced

- Intentional: For agriculture, horticulture, pets
- Accidental: In cargo, ballast water, on vehicles
- Natural range expansion (assisted by climate change)

### Problems Caused by Invasive Species

- Outcompete native species for resources
- Predate on native species
- Introduce new diseases
- Alter habitats
- Cause economic damage
- Reduce biodiversity

**Examples:**
- Japanese knotweed: Damages buildings, outcompetes native plants
- Grey squirrel: Outcompetes red squirrel, carries disease
- Cane toad (Australia): Toxic to native predators
- Zebra mussel: Clogs water pipes, outcompetes native mussels
- Asian hornet: Predates on honeybees

### Control of Invasive Species

- Prevention: Border controls, restrictions on imports
- Early detection and rapid response
- Physical removal
- Chemical control (pesticides)
- Biological control
- Public education

## 15.7 Conservation

### Why Conservation is Important

**Ethical reasons:**
- Other species have a right to exist
- Future generations deserve to experience biodiversity

**Ecological reasons:**
- Maintains ecosystem stability
- Protects food webs
- Maintains nutrient cycles
- Provides ecosystem services (pollination, water purification)

**Economic reasons:**
- Tourism revenue
- Medicinal resources
- Food sources
- Genetic resources for crops

### Conservation Methods

**In-situ conservation** (in the natural habitat):
- National parks and nature reserves
- Protected areas
- Wildlife corridors
- Habitat restoration
- Legal protection of species
- Sustainable management

**Ex-situ conservation** (outside natural habitat):
- Zoos and captive breeding programmes
- Botanic gardens
- Seed banks (e.g., Svalbard Global Seed Vault)
- Frozen zoos (genetic material)

**International agreements:**
- Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES)
- Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)
- IUCN Red List of Threatened Species

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing eutrophication and pollution**: Eutrophication is a specific process involving nutrients and algal blooms.

2. **Not explaining the sequence of eutrophication**: Must include all steps from nutrients to oxygen depletion.

3. **Forgetting that the greenhouse effect is NATURAL**: It's the ENHANCED effect that causes problems.

4. **Not mentioning specific greenhouse gases**: Name CO₂ and methane and their sources.

5. **Confusing in-situ and ex-situ conservation**: In-situ = in natural habitat; ex-situ = outside natural habitat.
