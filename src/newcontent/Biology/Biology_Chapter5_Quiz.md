## Part A: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

### Question 1
Which blood vessel carries deoxygenated blood away from the heart?

A) Aorta  
B) Pulmonary vein  
C) Pulmonary artery  
D) Vena cava  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs. This is an exception to the general rule that arteries carry oxygenated blood. The aorta carries oxygenated blood, the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood (another exception), and the vena cava carries deoxygenated blood towards the heart.

---

### Question 2
Why does the left ventricle have a thicker wall than the right ventricle?

A) It receives blood from the lungs  
B) It pumps blood to the whole body at high pressure  
C) It contains more blood  
D) It has more valves  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: The left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body through the aorta, requiring high pressure. The right ventricle only pumps blood to the nearby lungs at lower pressure. The thicker muscular wall of the left ventricle generates the necessary force for systemic circulation.

---

### Question 3
What is the function of haemoglobin in red blood cells?

A) To fight infection  
B) To carry oxygen  
C) To clot blood  
D) To produce antibodies  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Haemoglobin is the red pigment in red blood cells that binds with oxygen in the lungs to form oxyhaemoglobin. In body tissues, oxygen is released from oxyhaemoglobin. White blood cells fight infection and produce antibodies, and platelets are involved in clotting.

---

### Question 4
Which valve prevents backflow of blood from the aorta into the left ventricle?

A) Tricuspid valve  
B) Bicuspid valve  
C) Semilunar valve  
D) Mitral valve  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: The semilunar valve (aortic valve) is located at the entrance to the aorta and prevents backflow of blood into the left ventricle. The tricuspid and bicuspid (mitral) valves are atrioventricular valves between the atria and ventricles.

---

### Question 5
The term "myogenic" refers to:

A) The ability of the heart to beat without external nerve stimulation  
B) The ability of muscles to contract  
C) The production of myelin sheath  
D) The formation of red blood cells  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation**: Myogenic means that the heart muscle can contract and relax rhythmically without receiving signals from nerves. The heartbeat is initiated by the sinoatrial node (pacemaker) within the heart itself, although the rate can be modified by nerves and hormones.

---

### Question 6
During ventricular systole:

A) The atria contract  
B) Blood is pushed into the atria  
C) The semilunar valves close  
D) The atrioventricular valves close  

**Answer: D**

**Explanation**: During ventricular systole, the ventricles contract, causing pressure to rise. This forces the atrioventricular valves (tricuspid and bicuspid) to close, creating the "lub" sound. The semilunar valves open to allow blood to be ejected into the arteries.

---

### Question 7
Which of the following is a characteristic of capillaries?

A) Thick walls with elastic tissue  
B) Valves to prevent backflow  
C) Walls one cell thick for exchange  
D) High blood pressure  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Capillaries have walls that are only one cell thick (endothelium), providing a short distance for diffusion of materials between blood and tissues. They do not have valves, thick walls, or high blood pressure. Their thin walls and slow blood flow make them ideal for exchange.

---

### Question 8
The pacemaker of the heart is located in:

A) The left ventricle  
B) The wall of the right atrium  
C) The septum  
D) The aorta  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: The sinoatrial node (SAN), or pacemaker, is located in the wall of the right atrium near where the vena cava enters. It generates electrical impulses that spread across the atria, causing them to contract, and then travel to the AVN and down to the ventricles.

---

### Question 9
What type of white blood cell engulfs and digests pathogens?

A) Lymphocyte  
B) Phagocyte  
C) Erythrocyte  
D) Platelet  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Phagocytes (such as neutrophils and monocytes) carry out phagocytosis - they engulf and digest pathogens. Lymphocytes produce antibodies. Erythrocytes are red blood cells, and platelets are involved in blood clotting, not pathogen destruction.

---

### Question 10
Blood is taken from the small intestine to the liver by the:

A) Hepatic artery  
B) Hepatic vein  
C) Hepatic portal vein  
D) Renal vein  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: The hepatic portal vein carries blood rich in absorbed nutrients from the intestines directly to the liver for processing. This is a portal vein because it connects two capillary beds. The hepatic artery supplies oxygenated blood to the liver, and the hepatic vein carries blood away from the liver.

---

### Question 11
In the formation of a blood clot, fibrinogen is converted to fibrin by:

A) Platelets  
B) Prothrombin  
C) Thrombin  
D) Vitamin K  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Thrombin is an enzyme that catalyses the conversion of soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin. The fibrin forms a mesh of fibres that traps blood cells to form a clot. Thrombin itself is formed from prothrombin in the clotting cascade.

---

### Question 12
Which heart sound is produced when the semilunar valves close?

A) Lub  
B) Dub  
C) Murmur  
D) Thump  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: The "dub" sound is produced when the semilunar valves (aortic and pulmonary valves) close at the beginning of diastole. The "lub" sound is produced when the atrioventricular valves close at the beginning of ventricular systole.

## Part B: True/False Questions

### Statement 1
"Arteries always carry oxygenated blood."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: This is a common misconception. While most arteries carry oxygenated blood, the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs. Arteries are defined by the direction of blood flow (away from the heart), not by oxygen content.

---

### Statement 2
"The right ventricle pumps blood to the lungs."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: The right ventricle receives deoxygenated blood from the right atrium and pumps it through the pulmonary artery to the lungs for oxygenation. This is part of the pulmonary circulation.

---

### Statement 3
"Veins have valves because blood pressure is low."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Blood pressure in veins is much lower than in arteries because the pressure generated by the heart has dissipated by the time blood reaches the veins. Valves prevent backflow of blood, ensuring it continues moving towards the heart despite the low pressure.

---

### Statement 4
"Red blood cells have a nucleus to control their activities."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Mature red blood cells have no nucleus. The absence of a nucleus creates more space inside the cell for haemoglobin, allowing each red blood cell to carry more oxygen. This adaptation maximises oxygen transport capacity.

---

### Statement 5
"The coronary arteries supply blood to the heart muscle."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: The coronary arteries branch from the aorta just above the heart and supply the heart muscle (myocardium) with oxygenated blood. The heart cannot obtain oxygen directly from the blood inside its chambers because the walls are too thick.

---

### Statement 6
"Plasma transports carbon dioxide as oxyhaemoglobin."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Plasma transports carbon dioxide mainly as hydrogen carbonate ions (HCO₃⁻), not as oxyhaemoglobin. Oxyhaemoglobin is the combination of haemoglobin with oxygen. Carbon dioxide is carried dissolved in plasma, bound to haemoglobin (as carbaminohaemoglobin), or as hydrogen carbonate ions.

---

### Statement 7
"During diastole, the heart muscle relaxes and fills with blood."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Diastole is the relaxation phase of the cardiac cycle. During diastole, the atria and ventricles are relaxed, the atrioventricular valves are open, and blood flows passively from the veins into the atria and then into the ventricles.

---

### Statement 8
"White blood cells have a nucleus and red blood cells do not."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: White blood cells (leucocytes) have a nucleus, which is important for their immune functions including producing antibodies. Red blood cells (erythrocytes) lose their nucleus during development to make more room for haemoglobin.

---

### Statement 9
"Statins are drugs that reduce blood cholesterol levels."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Statins are medications that lower blood cholesterol levels by inhibiting an enzyme involved in cholesterol synthesis in the liver. They are used to treat and prevent coronary heart disease by slowing the build-up of fatty deposits in arteries.

---

### Statement 10
"Capillaries have walls made of smooth muscle."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Capillary walls are made of a single layer of endothelial cells (squamous epithelium), not smooth muscle. This thin wall (one cell thick) provides a short distance for diffusion of materials between blood and tissues. Arteries and veins have smooth muscle in their walls.

---

### Statement 11
"The vena cava carries blood from the lungs to the heart."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: The vena cava carries deoxygenated blood from the body (not the lungs) to the right atrium. Blood from the lungs is carried to the heart by the pulmonary veins.

---

### Statement 12
"Atherosclerosis is the build-up of fatty deposits in artery walls."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Atherosclerosis is a condition where fatty deposits (atheroma) build up in the inner lining of arteries. These deposits contain cholesterol, calcium, and other substances. The build-up narrows the artery lumen, reducing blood flow and increasing the risk of blood clots.

## Part D: Extended Response Questions (Higher Tier)

### Question 13 (6 marks)
Compare the structure and function of arteries, veins, and capillaries.

**Mark Scheme Answer:**

**Arteries:**
- Thick muscular and elastic walls [1]
- Carry blood away from heart at high pressure [1]
- No valves needed due to high pressure [1]

**Veins:**
- Thin walls with little muscle [1]
- Carry blood towards heart at low pressure [1]
- Valves present to prevent backflow [1]

**Capillaries:**
- Walls one cell thick [1]
- Site of exchange between blood and tissues [1]
- Very small lumen, one red blood cell wide [1]

*Maximum 6 marks for comprehensive comparison*

---

### Question 14 (5 marks)
Describe how the heartbeat is controlled.

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
- Heart is myogenic (beats without nerve signals) [1]
- Sinoatrial node (SAN) acts as pacemaker [1]
- SAN generates electrical impulses that spread across atria [1]
- Impulses reach AVN, then travel down Bundle of His [1]
- Through Purkyne fibres to ventricle walls [1]
- Ventricles contract from bottom up [1]
- Heart rate can be modified by nerves and adrenaline [1]

*Maximum 5 marks*

---

### Question 15 (4 marks)
Explain how atherosclerosis develops and how it can lead to a heart attack.

**Mark Scheme Answer:**

**Development of atherosclerosis:**
- Damage to endothelium of artery [1]
- Cholesterol and lipids build up under the lining [1]
- Forms plaque that narrows the artery lumen [1]

**Leading to heart attack:**
- Reduced blood flow to heart muscle [1]
- Blood clot may form on plaque and block artery completely [1]
- Heart muscle receives insufficient oxygen [1]
- Muscle tissue dies (myocardial infarction) [1]

*Maximum 4 marks*
