## 18.1 Inheritance

### Basic Concepts

Inheritance is the process by which genetic information is passed from parents to offspring. Understanding inheritance requires knowledge of several key concepts:

**Gene**: A section of DNA that codes for a specific protein

**Allele**: Different versions of the same gene

**Genotype**: The genetic makeup of an organism (the combination of alleles)

**Phenotype**: The observable characteristics of an organism

### Dominant and Recessive Alleles

**Dominant allele**: Always expressed in the phenotype when present. Represented by a capital letter (e.g., B)

**Recessive allele**: Only expressed when two copies are present (homozygous). Represented by a lowercase letter (e.g., b)

### Homozygous and Heterozygous

**Homozygous**: Having two identical alleles (e.g., BB or bb)

**Heterozygous**: Having two different alleles (e.g., Bb)

## 18.2 Genetic Crosses and Punnett Squares

### Using Punnett Squares

A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict the genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross.

**Steps to complete a Punnett square:**
1. Write the genotypes of both parents
2. Determine the gametes each parent can produce
3. Draw a grid and write the gametes along the top and side
4. Combine the gametes to show possible offspring genotypes
5. Write the ratio of genotypes and phenotypes

### Example: Monohybrid Cross

**Cross: Heterozygous × Heterozygous (Bb × Bb)**

Where:
- B = dominant allele (brown eyes)
- b = recessive allele (blue eyes)

**Gametes**: B and b from each parent

| | B | b |
|---|---|---|
| **B** | BB | Bb |
| **b** | Bb | bb |

**Results:**
- Genotypes: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb
- Phenotypes: 3 brown eyes : 1 blue eyes
- Ratio: 3:1

### Different Types of Crosses

**1. Homozygous dominant × Homozygous recessive (BB × bb)**
- All offspring are Bb (heterozygous)
- All show dominant phenotype

**2. Heterozygous × Homozygous recessive (Bb × bb)**
- Genotypes: 1 Bb : 1 bb
- Phenotypes: 1 dominant : 1 recessive
- Ratio: 1:1

**3. Heterozygous × Homozygous dominant (Bb × BB)**
- Genotypes: 1 BB : 1 Bb
- All show dominant phenotype

## 18.3 Codominance

### Definition

Codominance occurs when both alleles are equally dominant and both are expressed in the phenotype of heterozygous individuals.

### Example: Blood Groups

The ABO blood group system shows both codominance and dominance:

| Allele | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Iᴬ | Produces antigen A (codominant with Iᴮ) |
| Iᴮ | Produces antigen B (codominant with Iᴬ) |
| i | Produces no antigen (recessive) |

**Possible genotypes and phenotypes:**

| Genotype | Phenotype (Blood Group) |
|----------|------------------------|
| IᴬIᴬ | Group A |
| Iᴬi | Group A |
| IᴮIᴮ | Group B |
| Iᴮi | Group B |
| IᴬIᴮ | Group AB (codominant - both expressed) |
| ii | Group O |

**Example cross: Iᴬi × Iᴮi**

| | Iᴬ | i |
|---|---|---|
| **Iᴮ** | IᴬIᴮ | Iᴮi |
| **i** | Iᴬi | ii |

**Results:**
- 1 Group AB : 1 Group A : 1 Group B : 1 Group O

## 18.4 Sex Determination

### Sex Chromosomes

In humans, sex is determined by the sex chromosomes:
- **Females**: XX (two X chromosomes)
- **Males**: XY (one X and one Y chromosome)

### Sex Determination Cross

All eggs contain an X chromosome. Sperm contain either X or Y chromosome.

**Cross: XX (female) × XY (male)**

| | X | Y |
|---|---|---|
| **X** | XX | XY |
| **X** | XX | XY |

**Results:**
- 50% XX (female)
- 50% XY (male)
- Ratio of males to females: 1:1

## 18.5 Sex-Linked Inheritance

### Definition

Sex-linked traits are determined by genes located on the sex chromosomes (usually the X chromosome). Since males have only one X chromosome, they express sex-linked traits differently from females.

### X-linked Recessive Inheritance

**Key features:**
- Males only need one copy of the allele to show the trait (XY = affected)
- Females need two copies to show the trait (XX = affected, XX = carrier)
- Males cannot be carriers
- Affected males pass the allele to all daughters (who become carriers)
- Affected males do not pass the allele to sons

### Example: Haemophilia

Haemophilia is a blood clotting disorder caused by a recessive allele on the X chromosome.

| Genotype | Phenotype |
|----------|-----------|
| XᴴXᴴ | Normal female |
| XᴴXʰ | Carrier female (normal) |
| XʰXʰ | Affected female (rare) |
| XᴴY | Normal male |
| XʰY | Affected male |

**Cross: Carrier female × Normal male (XᴴXʰ × XᴴY)**

| | Xᴴ | Y |
|---|---|---|
| **Xᴴ** | XᴴXᴴ | XᴴY |
| **Xʰ** | XᴴXʰ | XʰY |

**Results:**
- 25% normal female (XᴴXᴴ)
- 25% carrier female (XᴴXʰ)
- 25% normal male (XᴴY)
- 25% affected male (XʰY)

### Example: Red-Green Colour Blindness

Another X-linked recessive condition. Males are affected if they inherit one copy of the allele.

## 18.6 Family Pedigrees

### Interpreting Pedigree Charts

Pedigree charts show the inheritance of traits through generations of a family.

**Symbols:**
- □ Male
- ○ Female
- ■ ● Affected individual (shaded)
- □ ○ Unaffected individual (unshaded)
- Horizontal line between = mating/couple
- Vertical line down = offspring

### Determining Inheritance Patterns

**Autosomal dominant:**
- Every affected individual has at least one affected parent
- Males and females equally affected
- Does not skip generations

**Autosomal recessive:**
- Can skip generations
- Two unaffected parents can have affected children
- Males and females equally affected

**X-linked recessive:**
- More males affected than females
- Affected males cannot pass to sons
- Affected females must have affected father and carrier/affected mother

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |

| **Gene** | Section of DNA coding for a protein |
| **Allele** | Different version of the same gene |
| **Dominant** | Always expressed when present |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Wrong letter case**: Capital for dominant, lowercase for recessive

2. **Not showing gametes**: Always show the gametes in genetic crosses

3. **Forgetting ratios**: Express results as ratios of genotypes AND phenotypes

4. **Not explaining sex-linked inheritance**: Males only have one X chromosome

5. **Incorrect pedigree interpretation**: Learn the patterns for different inheritance types
