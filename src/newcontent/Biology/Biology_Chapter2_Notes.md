## 2.1 Classification of Living Organisms

### The Need for Classification

Classification is the organisation of living organisms into groups based on their similarities and differences. Scientists classify organisms for several important reasons that facilitate the study and understanding of the enormous diversity of life on Earth. Classification systems help biologists identify organisms, understand evolutionary relationships, communicate effectively about species across language barriers, and predict characteristics of newly discovered organisms based on their classification group.

The scientific study of classification is called **taxonomy**. Taxonomists use a hierarchical system to organise organisms into increasingly specific groups, from broad kingdoms down to individual species.

### The Five Kingdoms

Living organisms are classified into five major kingdoms, each with distinct characteristics that separate them from other groups. Understanding the features of each kingdom is fundamental to IGCSE Biology and provides the foundation for more detailed study of biodiversity.

#### Kingdom 1: Animals (Animalia)

Animals are multicellular eukaryotic organisms that form the most diverse kingdom in terms of species numbers. All animals share several key characteristics that distinguish them from other kingdoms. They are **heterotrophs**, meaning they cannot make their own food and must obtain nutrients by consuming other organisms. Animal cells lack cell walls, which gives them flexibility and allows the development of complex body structures including nervous systems and muscles for movement. Most animals reproduce sexually, although some simpler animals can reproduce asexually.

Animals can be further divided into two main groups:

**Vertebrates** are animals with a backbone (vertebral column). The backbone provides structural support and protects the spinal cord. Vertebrates are classified into five classes based on their characteristics:

| Class | Key Features | Examples |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| **Mammals** | Hair/fur; give birth to live young; produce milk; warm-blooded | Humans, dogs, whales, bats |
| **Birds** | Feathers; lay eggs with shells; beak; wings; warm-blooded | Eagles, penguins, sparrows |
| **Reptiles** | Dry scaly skin; lay eggs with soft shells; cold-blooded | Snakes, lizards, crocodiles |
| **Amphibians** | Moist skin; lay eggs in water; metamorphosis; cold-blooded | Frogs, toads, newts |
| **Fish** | Scales; gills for breathing; lay eggs in water; cold-blooded | Salmon, sharks, goldfish |

**Invertebrates** are animals without a backbone. They make up approximately 97% of all animal species. Major invertebrate groups include:

| Group | Key Features | Examples |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| **Arthropods** | Jointed legs; hard exoskeleton; segmented body | Insects, spiders, crustaceans |
| **Molluscs** | Soft body; often have a shell | Snails, octopuses, squids |
| **Annelids** | Segmented worms; body divided into rings | Earthworms, leeches |
| **Nematodes** | Roundworms; unsegmented | Threadworms, hookworms |
| **Flatworms** | Flattened bodies | Planarians, tapeworms |
| **Cnidarians** | Radial symmetry; tentacles with stinging cells | Jellyfish, sea anemones, corals |

#### Kingdom 2: Plants (Plantae)

Plants are multicellular eukaryotic organisms that are fundamental to life on Earth as they form the base of most food chains. Plants are **autotrophs**, meaning they can make their own food through photosynthesis using sunlight energy, carbon dioxide, and water. Plant cells have cell walls made of cellulose, which provides structural support and maintains cell shape. They also contain chloroplasts with chlorophyll for photosynthesis, and most have a large permanent vacuole containing cell sap.

Plants are classified into several major groups based on their characteristics:

**Flowering plants (Angiosperms)** produce seeds enclosed within fruits. They are the most diverse and widespread plant group, with over 300,000 known species. Angiosperms are further divided into:

- **Monocotyledons (Monocots)**: Seeds contain one cotyledon (seed leaf); leaves have parallel veins; vascular bundles scattered in stem; flower parts in multiples of three. Examples: grasses, wheat, rice, maize, lilies, orchids

- **Dicotyledons (Dicots)**: Seeds contain two cotyledons; leaves have net-like veins; vascular bundles arranged in a ring; flower parts in multiples of four or five. Examples: beans, peas, roses, sunflowers, oak trees

**Non-flowering plants** reproduce using spores or naked seeds:

| Group | Reproduction | Examples |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| **Ferns** | Spores produced in sporangia on leaf undersides | Bracken, maidenhair fern |
| **Conifers (Gymnosperms)** | Seeds in cones (naked seeds, not enclosed in fruit) | Pine, spruce, fir, cedar |
| **Mosses** | Spores produced in capsules | Sphagnum moss, haircap moss |

#### Kingdom 3: Fungi

Fungi are eukaryotic organisms that were once classified as plants but are now recognised as a separate kingdom due to their unique characteristics. Fungi are **saprotrophic heterotrophs**, meaning they obtain nutrients by absorbing soluble organic compounds from dead or decaying matter. They secrete enzymes onto their food source to digest it externally, then absorb the resulting nutrients.

The body of most fungi consists of thread-like structures called **hyphae** (singular: hypha), which together form a network called a **mycelium**. Fungal cell walls are made of **chitin**, not cellulose as in plants. Most fungi reproduce by releasing spores, which can be sexual or asexual depending on conditions.

Common examples of fungi include:

- **Mushrooms and toadstools**: Form visible fruiting bodies above ground
- **Moulds**: Grow as fuzzy growths on food and organic matter (e.g., Penicillium, Mucor)
- **Yeasts**: Single-celled fungi used in baking and brewing (e.g., Saccharomyces cerevisiae)
- **Bracket fungi**: Grow on trees and decompose wood

Fungi play crucial ecological roles as decomposers, recycling nutrients in ecosystems. Some fungi form beneficial relationships with plant roots (mycorrhizae), while others cause diseases in plants and animals.

#### Kingdom 4: Protoctists (Protists)

Protoctists are a diverse kingdom of eukaryotic organisms that do not fit into the animal, plant, or fungi kingdoms. They are mostly microscopic and can be unicellular or multicellular. Some protoctists have characteristics similar to animals (they are heterotrophic and can move), while others resemble plants (they have chloroplasts and can photosynthesise).

Protoctists can be classified into several groups:

- **Protozoa**: Animal-like protoctists that move and feed like animals
  - Amoeba: moves using pseudopodia (false feet)
  - Paramecium: moves using cilia (hair-like structures)
  - Plasmodium: causes malaria, transmitted by mosquitoes

- **Algae**: Plant-like protoctists that photosynthesise
  - Chlorella: unicellular green alga
  - Spirogyra: filamentous alga with spiral chloroplasts
  - Seaweeds: multicellular algae (though sometimes classified separately)

Some protoctists are pathogenic and cause diseases in humans, such as Plasmodium (malaria) and Trypanosoma (sleeping sickness).

#### Kingdom 5: Prokaryotes (Bacteria)

Prokaryotes are unicellular organisms that lack a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Their genetic material is a single circular chromosome of DNA located in a region called the nucleoid. Bacteria also often contain smaller circular DNA molecules called plasmids, which can carry genes for antibiotic resistance or other advantageous traits.

Bacterial cells are typically much smaller than eukaryotic cells (1-5 micrometres in length). They have a cell wall, but unlike plant cell walls, bacterial cell walls are made of **peptidoglycan** (not cellulose). Some bacteria have additional structures:

- **Capsule**: Slimy outer layer for protection
- **Flagella**: Tail-like structures for movement
- **Pili**: Hair-like structures for attachment and DNA transfer

Bacteria can be classified by their shape:
- **Cocci** (singular: coccus): Spherical bacteria
- **Bacilli** (singular: bacillus): Rod-shaped bacteria
- **Spirilla** (singular: spirillum): Spiral-shaped bacteria
- **Vibrios**: Comma-shaped bacteria

Bacteria reproduce asexually by **binary fission**, where one cell divides into two identical daughter cells. Under favourable conditions, bacteria can divide every 20 minutes, leading to rapid population growth.

Many bacteria are beneficial and essential for:
- Decomposition and nutrient cycling
- Digestion in animal intestines (gut flora)
- Nitrogen fixation (converting atmospheric nitrogen into compounds plants can use)
- Food production (yogurt, cheese, vinegar)

However, some bacteria are pathogenic and cause diseases:
- Salmonella: food poisoning
- Tuberculosis: lung disease
- Cholera: severe diarrhoea

## 2.2 Viruses

### Are Viruses Living?

Viruses are unique biological entities that challenge our definition of life. They are **not considered truly living** because they do not exhibit all seven characteristics of life. Viruses cannot carry out any life processes independently - they have no cellular structure, cannot respire, cannot grow, and cannot reproduce without a host cell. They are often described as "on the edge of life" or as non-living infectious agents.

### Structure of Viruses

Viruses are extremely small, much smaller than bacteria, typically measuring 20-300 nanometres. They can only be seen using electron microscopes. Despite their diversity, all viruses have a relatively simple structure consisting of:

- **Genetic material**: Either DNA or RNA (never both), which contains the instructions for making new viruses
- **Protein coat (capsid)**: Protects the genetic material
- Some viruses have an outer **envelope** derived from the host cell membrane

Viruses come in various shapes:
- Icosahedral (football-shaped): e.g., adenovirus
- Helical (rod-shaped): e.g., tobacco mosaic virus
- Complex: e.g., bacteriophages

### Viral Reproduction

Viruses can only reproduce inside living host cells. The lytic cycle of viral reproduction involves several key stages that students must understand for examinations:

1. **Attachment**: The virus attaches to specific receptor sites on the host cell surface
2. **Entry**: The virus injects its genetic material into the host cell (or the whole virus enters)
3. **Replication**: The viral genetic material takes over the host cell's machinery to produce viral components
4. **Assembly**: New viruses are assembled from the newly made components
5. **Release**: The host cell bursts (lysis) releasing new viruses to infect other cells

Some viruses can also enter a lysogenic cycle where their genetic material integrates into the host genome and remains dormant before becoming active.

### Pathogenic Viruses

Viruses cause a wide range of diseases in plants and animals:

**Human diseases caused by viruses**:
- HIV/AIDS (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)
- Influenza (flu)
- Common cold (rhinoviruses and others)
- COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2)
- Chickenpox and shingles (varicella-zoster virus)
- Measles, mumps, rubella
- Herpes
- Ebola

**Plant diseases caused by viruses**:
- Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV): Causes mosaic patterns on leaves
- Potato virus Y: Affects potato crops
- Cucumber mosaic virus: Affects cucumbers and related plants

## 2.3 Classification Systems

### Binomial Nomenclature

The binomial naming system was developed by Swedish botanist Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century and remains the standard method for naming species today. Every species has a two-part Latin name:

- **Genus name**: Written with a capital letter (e.g., Homo)
- **Species name**: Written with a lowercase letter (e.g., sapiens)
- The complete scientific name is written in italics or underlined: *Homo sapiens*

This system provides several advantages:
- Each species has a unique, universally recognised name
- The names are the same in all languages
- Related species share the same genus name, showing their relationship
- It avoids confusion from multiple common names for the same organism

Examples of binomial names:
- *Panthera leo* (lion)
- *Panthera tigris* (tiger)
- *Escherichia coli* (E. coli bacterium)
- *Drosophila melanogaster* (fruit fly)

### The Hierarchy of Classification

Living organisms are classified in a hierarchical system with seven main levels (taxa). Each level becomes more specific, with organisms in the same species being most closely related:

```
Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

KP C OF GS (King Phillip Came Over For Good Soup)
```

For example, human classification:
- Kingdom: Animalia
- Phylum: Chordata
- Class: Mammalia
- Order: Primates
- Family: Hominidae
- Genus: Homo
- Species: sapiens

As we move down the hierarchy from kingdom to species, organisms in each group become more similar to each other and share more characteristics. Organisms in the same genus are very closely related.

### Dichotomous Keys

A dichotomous key is a tool used to identify organisms based on their observable characteristics. "Dichotomous" means "divided into two parts" - each step in the key presents two contrasting choices. By following the appropriate path, users can identify an unknown organism.

Key features of dichotomous keys:
- Each step offers two mutually exclusive choices (1a or 1b)
- Choices are based on observable physical characteristics
- Following the correct path leads to the identification
- Keys can be presented as numbered questions or as a branching diagram

Example of a simple dichotomous key for vertebrates:

```
1a. Animal has hair or fur ........................ Go to 2
1b. Animal has no hair or fur ...................... Go to 3

2a. Animal lays eggs .............................. Monotreme (platypus, echidna)
2b. Animal gives birth to live young ............... Mammal

3a. Animal has feathers ........................... Bird
3b. Animal has no feathers ........................ Go to 4

4a. Animal has dry, scaly skin .................... Reptile
4b. Animal has moist skin or scales with gills ..... Go to 5

5a. Animal has gills throughout life ............... Fish
5b. Animal has gills only in young stage ........... Amphibian
```

## 2.4 Characteristics Used in Classification

### Structural Characteristics

Traditional classification systems were based primarily on observable structural (morphological) characteristics. These include:
- Body shape and size
- Presence or absence of specific structures (backbone, feathers, scales)
- Number of legs or other appendages
- Type of body covering (skin, scales, fur, feathers)
- Internal anatomy (skeleton type, organ systems)

While still useful, structural characteristics can sometimes be misleading due to convergent evolution, where unrelated organisms evolve similar structures independently.

### Physiological Characteristics

Physiological characteristics relate to how organisms function internally:
- Type of respiration (aerobic, anaerobic, or both)
- Methods of reproduction (sexual, asexual, or both)
- Nutrition type (autotrophic, heterotrophic, or both)
- Enzyme systems and metabolic pathways
- Body temperature regulation (endothermic or ectothermic)

### Behavioural Characteristics

Behaviour can also help distinguish between species:
- Courtship and mating behaviours
- Feeding behaviours
- Social structures
- Migration patterns
- Communication methods

### Modern Classification Methods

Modern classification increasingly relies on biochemical and genetic evidence:

**DNA sequencing** compares the sequence of bases in DNA between organisms. Organisms with more similar DNA sequences are more closely related. This has led to revisions in some classification groups based solely on structural characteristics.

**Protein analysis** compares the amino acid sequences of proteins such as cytochrome c. More closely related organisms have more similar protein sequences.

**Immunological comparisons** involve testing how antibodies react to proteins from different species. Stronger reactions indicate closer relationships.

These molecular methods have provided powerful evidence for evolutionary relationships and have sometimes revealed unexpected connections between organisms that look very different.

## 2.5 The Three Domain System

### Beyond the Five Kingdoms

Modern classification systems, based largely on molecular evidence, have proposed a level above kingdoms called domains. The three domain system, proposed by Carl Woese in 1990, divides life into three domains:

| Domain | Description | Kingdom(s) |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Archaea** | Ancient bacteria-like organisms with unique biochemistry; often live in extreme environments | Archaebacteria |
| **Bacteria** | True bacteria with peptidoglycan cell walls | Eubacteria |
| **Eukarya** | Organisms with eukaryotic cells (nucleus and membrane-bound organelles) | Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia |

The three domain system recognises that Archaea and Bacteria, though both prokaryotic, are as different from each other as they are from eukaryotes. Archaea include many extremophiles that live in harsh conditions:
- Thermophiles: live in hot environments (hot springs, hydrothermal vents)
- Halophiles: live in extremely salty environments
- Methanogens: produce methane in anaerobic conditions

## 2.6 Pathogens

### Definition

A pathogen is any organism or agent that causes disease. Pathogens can belong to any of the five kingdoms or be viruses. They cause disease by damaging host tissues, producing toxins, or triggering damaging immune responses.

### Types of Pathogens

| Type | Characteristics | Example Diseases |
|------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Bacteria** | Prokaryotic; reproduce rapidly; some produce toxins | Cholera, TB, Salmonella food poisoning |
| **Viruses** | Non-living; need host cells to reproduce | HIV/AIDS, influenza, COVID-19, measles |
| **Fungi** | Eukaryotic; hyphal growth | Athlete's foot, ringworm, crop diseases |
| **Protoctists** | Eukaryotic; often parasitic | Malaria (Plasmodium), amoebic dysentery |

### Transmission of Pathogens

Pathogens can be transmitted between hosts through various routes:

- **Direct contact**: Touching infected person (e.g., STDs, skin infections)
- **Indirect contact**: Touching contaminated surfaces (fomites)
- **Airborne**: Inhalation of droplets (e.g., flu, COVID-19)
- **Waterborne**: Contaminated water (e.g., cholera)
- **Foodborne**: Contaminated food (e.g., Salmonella)
- **Vector-borne**: Carried by another organism (e.g., mosquitoes transmit malaria)

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing "prokaryote" and "protist"**: Prokaryotes are bacteria (no nucleus); protists are eukaryotes (have a nucleus).

2. **Writing scientific names incorrectly**: Genus starts with capital letter, species with lowercase, both in italics (*Homo sapiens*, not Homo Sapiens or homo sapiens).

3. **Saying viruses are "living"**: Viruses are NOT considered living organisms - they cannot carry out life processes independently.

4. **Confusing "saprotroph" and "parasite"**: Saprotrophs feed on dead organic matter; parasites feed on living hosts.

5. **Forgetting that fungi are not plants**: Fungi have chitin cell walls (not cellulose), cannot photosynthesise, and are saprotrophs (not autotrophs).

6. **Mixing up arthropod groups**: Insects have 6 legs; arachnids (spiders) have 8 legs; crustaceans have more than 8 legs and two pairs of antennae.

7. **Not distinguishing between the three types of heterotrophs**: Herbivores (eat plants), carnivores (eat animals), omnivores (eat both), saprotrophs (eat dead matter).

## Higher Tier Content

### Evolutionary Relationships and Classification

Modern classification systems aim to reflect evolutionary relationships (phylogeny). Organisms in the same group share a common ancestor and are more closely related. Molecular evidence (DNA and protein sequences) has revolutionised our understanding of evolutionary relationships.

Evidence for evolutionary relationships includes:
- **DNA hybridisation**: Related species have more similar DNA that binds more strongly together
- **DNA sequencing**: Comparing base sequences to calculate genetic similarity
- **Protein sequences**: Comparing amino acid sequences in proteins like cytochrome c
- **Fossil evidence**: Shows the sequence of evolutionary changes over time

### The Importance of Classification

Understanding evolutionary relationships helps scientists:
- Predict characteristics of newly discovered species
- Trace the evolution of specific traits
- Understand how diseases spread between related species
- Make decisions about conservation priorities
- Develop new medicines based on compounds found in related species
