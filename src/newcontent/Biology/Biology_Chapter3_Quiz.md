## Part A: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

### Question 1
Which structure prevents food from entering the trachea during swallowing?

A) Larynx  
B) Epiglottis  
C) Bronchus  
D) Diaphragm  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: The epiglottis is a flap of cartilage located at the entrance to the larynx. During swallowing, it closes over the glottis (opening to the trachea) to prevent food and liquid from entering the respiratory tract. The larynx contains the vocal cords, bronchi carry air to the lungs, and the diaphragm is involved in breathing movements.

---

### Question 2
What happens to the diaphragm during inspiration?

A) It relaxes and becomes dome-shaped  
B) It contracts and flattens  
C) It contracts and becomes dome-shaped  
D) It relaxes and flattens  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: During inspiration, the diaphragm contracts and moves downwards, flattening from its normal dome shape. This increases the volume of the thoracic cavity, decreasing the pressure inside the lungs and causing air to rush in. During expiration, the diaphragm relaxes and returns to its dome shape.

---

### Question 3
The walls of the alveoli are one cell thick. How does this help gas exchange?

A) It prevents blood from leaking out  
B) It provides a short diffusion distance  
C) It allows more carbon dioxide to be stored  
D) It prevents alveoli from collapsing  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Having walls that are only one cell thick provides a very short distance for gases to diffuse across. This increases the rate of diffusion according to Fick's Law - the shorter the diffusion distance, the faster the rate of diffusion. This is one of the key adaptations of alveoli for efficient gas exchange.

---

### Question 4
Which component of cigarette smoke binds to haemoglobin and reduces oxygen transport?

A) Tar  
B) Nicotine  
C) Carbon monoxide  
D) Nitrogen  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Carbon monoxide has an affinity for haemoglobin approximately 200-300 times greater than oxygen. When it binds to haemoglobin, it forms carboxyhaemoglobin, which cannot carry oxygen. This reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, leading to tissues receiving less oxygen.

---

### Question 5
In fish gills, counter-current flow means:

A) Blood flows in the same direction as water  
B) Blood flows in the opposite direction to water  
C) Blood flows faster than water  
D) Water flows backwards over the gills  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: In counter-current flow, blood flows through the gill lamellae in the opposite direction to the water flowing over them. This maintains a concentration gradient for oxygen along the entire length of the lamellae, allowing more efficient extraction of oxygen from the water (about 80% compared to 50% with parallel flow).

---

### Question 6
Which of the following correctly describes the movement of oxygen during gas exchange?

A) Oxygen moves from blood to alveoli by diffusion  
B) Oxygen moves from alveoli to blood by active transport  
C) Oxygen moves from alveoli to blood by diffusion  
D) Oxygen moves from blood to alveoli by active transport  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Oxygen moves from the alveoli (where its concentration is higher) into the blood capillaries (where its concentration is lower) by diffusion down a concentration gradient. Active transport is not involved in gas exchange at the alveoli.

---

### Question 7
What is the function of cilia in the trachea?

A) To produce mucus  
B) To sweep mucus upwards towards the throat  
C) To warm the air  
D) To exchange gases  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Cilia are tiny hair-like projections on the epithelial cells lining the trachea. They beat in a coordinated wave-like motion to sweep mucus (which traps dust and bacteria) upwards towards the throat, where it can be swallowed or coughed up. Goblet cells produce mucus, blood vessels warm the air, and gas exchange occurs in alveoli, not the trachea.

---

### Question 8
Why does exhaled air contain more carbon dioxide than inhaled air?

A) Carbon dioxide is produced in the alveoli  
B) Carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the alveoli  
C) Carbon dioxide is produced by bacteria in the mouth  
D) Carbon dioxide is added to air as it passes through the trachea  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Carbon dioxide is a waste product of cellular respiration. It is carried in the blood to the lungs, where it diffuses from the blood (high concentration) into the alveoli (low concentration). This carbon dioxide is then exhaled, making the exhaled air contain more CO₂ than inhaled air.

---

### Question 9
During forced expiration, which muscles contract to pull the ribs downwards?

A) External intercostal muscles  
B) Internal intercostal muscles  
C) Deltoid muscles  
D) Abdominal muscles only  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: During forced expiration (such as during exercise or coughing), the internal intercostal muscles contract to pull the ribs downwards and inwards, reducing the thoracic volume more forcefully than quiet expiration. The external intercostals are used for inspiration.

---

### Question 10
Which respiratory disease is caused by the breakdown of alveoli walls, reducing the surface area for gas exchange?

A) Asthma  
B) Bronchitis  
C) Emphysema  
D) Pneumonia  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Emphysema is a condition where the walls between alveoli break down, creating larger air spaces but with a greatly reduced surface area for gas exchange. This leads to shortness of breath because less oxygen can diffuse into the blood. It is commonly caused by long-term smoking.

---

### Question 11
Insects exchange gases through:

A) Lungs and tracheae  
B) Gills and spiracles  
C) Spiracles and tracheae  
D) Skin and lungs  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Insects have a tracheal system for gas exchange. Air enters through openings called spiracles on the sides of the body, then travels through tracheae and smaller tracheoles directly to body cells. Insects do not use blood to transport gases - oxygen is delivered directly to tissues.

---

### Question 12
The cartilage rings in the trachea are C-shaped rather than complete rings. What is the advantage of this?

A) It allows the trachea to expand during breathing  
B) It allows food to pass easily through the oesophagus behind the trachea  
C) It makes the trachea lighter  
D) It allows air to escape if the trachea is damaged  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: The C-shaped cartilage rings have the open part facing the oesophagus, which runs behind the trachea. This allows the oesophagus to expand when food is swallowed, preventing the trachea from obstructing the passage of food. The closed part of the C still provides support to keep the trachea open.

## Part B: True/False Questions

### Statement 1
"Breathing and respiration are the same process."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Breathing (ventilation) and respiration are different processes. Breathing is the mechanical movement of air into and out of the lungs. Respiration is the chemical process occurring in all cells where glucose is broken down to release energy. Breathing is just one part of the respiratory system's function.

---

### Statement 2
"During inspiration, the pressure in the lungs is lower than atmospheric pressure."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: During inspiration, contraction of the diaphragm and external intercostal muscles increases the volume of the thoracic cavity. This causes the lungs to expand, decreasing the pressure inside them. When lung pressure falls below atmospheric pressure, air rushes in to equalise the pressure.

---

### Statement 3
"Tar in cigarette smoke increases the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Tar does not increase oxygen-carrying capacity. In fact, tar damages the cilia in the airways, leading to mucus accumulation and increased risk of infection. It is carbon monoxide (another component of cigarette smoke) that affects oxygen transport by binding to haemoglobin and reducing its oxygen-carrying capacity.

---

### Statement 4
"The walls of the alveoli are lined with ciliated epithelium."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Alveoli walls are lined with squamous (flattened) epithelium, not ciliated epithelium. Ciliated epithelium is found in the trachea and bronchi where it helps move mucus. The alveoli have thin, flat cells to minimise the diffusion distance for gas exchange. Cilia would interfere with gas exchange.

---

### Statement 5
"Fish extract oxygen from water using gills with counter-current flow."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Fish use gills for gas exchange. Water containing dissolved oxygen flows over the gill lamellae in the opposite direction to blood flow (counter-current flow). This maintains a concentration gradient along the entire length of the lamellae, maximising oxygen extraction from water.

---

### Statement 6
"Nicotine causes lung cancer."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Nicotine is the addictive component of cigarette smoke, but it is not a carcinogen. Tar contains the carcinogenic chemicals that cause lung cancer. Nicotine causes addiction, increases heart rate, constricts blood vessels, and raises blood pressure, but it is tar that causes the mutations leading to cancer.

---

### Statement 7
"Insects use their blood to transport oxygen around their bodies."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Insects do not use blood to transport oxygen. They have a tracheal system where air enters through spiracles and travels through tracheae and tracheoles directly to body cells. Oxygen diffuses directly from the tracheoles into the cells, and carbon dioxide diffuses out. Insect blood (haemolymph) does not carry oxygen.

---

### Statement 8
"Exhaled air contains less oxygen than inhaled air because oxygen diffuses into the blood in the lungs."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: In the alveoli, oxygen diffuses from the air (high concentration) into the blood (low concentration). This means that the air we breathe out has less oxygen than the air we breathe in. Inhaled air contains about 21% oxygen, while exhaled air contains about 16-17% oxygen.

---

### Statement 9
"Bronchioles have cartilage rings to keep them open."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Bronchioles do NOT have cartilage rings. They have smooth muscle in their walls instead. The smooth muscle can contract or relax to control the diameter of the airways. Cartilage rings are found in the trachea and bronchi (though the cartilage becomes less complete in the bronchi).

---

### Statement 10
"Gas exchange in the lungs occurs by diffusion."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Gas exchange in the lungs is a passive process that occurs by diffusion. Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli (high concentration) into the blood (low concentration), and carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood (high concentration) into the alveoli (low concentration). No energy is required for this process.

---

### Statement 11
"Amphibians can exchange gases through their skin."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Amphibians like frogs can carry out cutaneous respiration - gas exchange through their moist skin. Their skin is thin and highly vascularised (contains many blood vessels), allowing oxygen and carbon dioxide to diffuse through it. This is important because their lungs are relatively simple and not very efficient.

---

### Statement 12
"During quiet breathing, expiration requires energy from muscle contraction."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Quiet expiration is a passive process that does not require muscle contraction. The external intercostal muscles and diaphragm simply relax, and the elastic recoil of the lungs and gravity cause the thorax volume to decrease, pushing air out. Only forced expiration (during exercise) requires energy and active muscle contraction.

## Part D: Extended Response Questions (Higher Tier)

### Question 13 (6 marks)
Compare and contrast the respiratory systems of humans and fish, explaining how each is adapted to its environment.

**Mark Scheme Answer:**

**Similarities:**
- Both have large surface area for gas exchange (alveoli / gill lamellae) [1]
- Both have thin walls for short diffusion distance [1]
- Both have rich blood supply to maintain concentration gradient [1]
- Both have moist surfaces for gas diffusion [1]

**Differences:**
- Humans breathe air; fish extract oxygen from water [1]
- Humans use lungs with negative pressure breathing; fish use gills with mouth/operculum pumping [1]
- Fish use counter-current flow; humans do not [1]
- Humans transport gases in blood; fish also use blood but with different circulation [1]

**Environmental adaptations:**
- Air has more oxygen than water, so humans need less specialised extraction method [1]
- Water is denser and more viscous, so fish need efficient counter-current system [1]
- Fish gills collapse in air; human lungs work poorly in water [1]

*Maximum 6 marks for comprehensive answer*

### Question 15 (4 marks)
Describe and explain the role of counter-current flow in fish gills.

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
- Blood flows through gill lamellae in opposite direction to water flow [1]
- This maintains a concentration gradient along entire length of lamella [1]
- As blood gains oxygen, it meets water with even higher oxygen concentration [1]
- This allows continued diffusion along entire surface [1]
- More efficient than parallel flow - extracts about 80% of oxygen from water [1]
