## 4.1 Nutrients and Their Functions

### The Importance of Nutrition

All living organisms require nutrients to survive, grow, and maintain their bodies. Nutrients provide the raw materials for building new cells and tissues, the energy for all metabolic processes, and the components needed for regulating body functions. Humans, as heterotrophs, must obtain these nutrients from food since they cannot manufacture most of them internally. Understanding the different types of nutrients, their functions, and the consequences of deficiencies is fundamental to human biology and appears frequently in IGCSE examinations.

### Types of Nutrients

There are seven major classes of nutrients that the human body requires: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids (fats and oils), vitamins, minerals, fibre, and water. Each class has specific functions and is found in different food sources.

#### Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's primary source of energy. They are organic compounds containing carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically with the hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio of 2:1 (the same as in water, hence the name "carbo-hydrate"). Carbohydrates are classified into three main types based on the number of sugar units they contain:

**Monosaccharides** are simple sugars consisting of a single sugar unit. They are the building blocks of larger carbohydrates and are small enough to be absorbed directly into the bloodstream. Examples include:
- **Glucose**: Found in fruits, honey, and produced by digestion of starch; the main sugar used in cellular respiration
- **Fructose**: Found in fruits and honey; the sweetest natural sugar
- **Galactose**: Found in milk as part of lactose

**Disaccharides** are double sugars made of two monosaccharide units joined together. They must be digested into monosaccharides before absorption. Examples include:
- **Sucrose** (glucose + fructose): Table sugar, found in sugar cane and sugar beet
- **Maltose** (glucose + glucose): Found in germinating seeds and produced during starch digestion
- **Lactose** (glucose + galactose): Found in milk

**Polysaccharides** are complex carbohydrates made of many monosaccharide units joined together. They are not sweet and are insoluble in water. Examples include:
- **Starch**: Storage carbohydrate in plants; found in potatoes, rice, bread, pasta
- **Glycogen**: Storage carbohydrate in animals (stored in liver and muscles)
- **Cellulose**: Structural carbohydrate in plant cell walls; humans cannot digest cellulose, but it provides dietary fibre

**Functions of carbohydrates:**
- Primary source of energy for respiration (1g provides approximately 17 kJ)
- Glucose is essential for brain function
- Excess glucose is converted to glycogen or fat for storage

**Sources**: Rice, bread, pasta, potatoes, cereals, fruits, sugar

#### Proteins

Proteins are complex organic molecules containing carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen. Some proteins also contain sulphur. Proteins are made up of smaller units called amino acids joined together by peptide bonds. There are approximately 20 different amino acids that can combine in countless ways to form different proteins.

**Essential and non-essential amino acids:**
- **Essential amino acids** cannot be made by the body and must be obtained from food (e.g., leucine, lysine, valine)
- **Non-essential amino acids** can be synthesised by the body from other amino acids

**Functions of proteins:**
- Growth and repair of body tissues (muscles, skin, hair, nails)
- Making enzymes, hormones, and antibodies
- Haemoglobin for oxygen transport
- Can provide energy (1g provides approximately 18 kJ) when carbohydrates and fats are insufficient
- Structural proteins (collagen, keratin)

**Sources**: Meat, fish, eggs, dairy products, beans, pulses, nuts, soya

**First-class and second-class proteins:**
- **First-class proteins** (animal sources) contain all essential amino acids in the right proportions
- **Second-class proteins** (plant sources) lack one or more essential amino acids; vegetarians need to eat a variety of plant proteins to obtain all essential amino acids

#### Lipids (Fats and Oils)

Lipids are organic compounds containing carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, but with a much higher proportion of hydrogen than carbohydrates. They are insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents. Lipids exist as:
- **Fats**: Solid at room temperature (animal sources)
- **Oils**: Liquid at room temperature (plant sources)

**Structure of lipids:**
Lipids are made of glycerol and fatty acids. One molecule of glycerol combines with three fatty acid molecules to form a triglyceride.

**Functions of lipids:**
- High-energy source (1g provides approximately 38 kJ - more than double that of carbohydrates)
- Energy storage (adipose tissue)
- Insulation (subcutaneous fat layer)
- Protection of vital organs (cushioning)
- Component of cell membranes (phospholipids)
- Source of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
- Production of steroid hormones

**Sources**: Butter, margarine, cooking oils, fatty meats, dairy products, nuts

#### Vitamins

Vitamins are organic compounds required in small amounts for various metabolic processes. The body cannot synthesise most vitamins, so they must be obtained from the diet. Vitamins are classified into two groups based on solubility:

**Fat-soluble vitamins** (A, D, E, K):
- Stored in the body (liver and fatty tissue)
- Not needed every day in the diet
- Can be toxic if consumed in excess

**Water-soluble vitamins** (B complex, C):
- Not stored in the body (excess excreted in urine)
- Needed regularly in the diet
- Easily destroyed by heat and cooking

| Vitamin | Sources | Functions | Deficiency Disease |
|---------|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Vitamin A** | Liver, carrots, dark green vegetables, dairy products | Making rhodopsin (visual pigment); healthy skin and epithelial tissues | Night blindness; corneal damage |
| **Vitamin C** | Citrus fruits, peppers, strawberries, green vegetables | Collagen synthesis; wound healing; antioxidant; iron absorption | Scurvy (bleeding gums, poor wound healing) |
| **Vitamin D** | Sunlight on skin, oily fish, eggs, fortified foods | Calcium absorption; bone mineralisation | Rickets in children (soft bones); osteomalacia in adults |
| **Vitamin K** | Green vegetables, made by gut bacteria | Blood clotting | Excessive bleeding |

#### Minerals

Minerals are inorganic elements required in small amounts for various body functions. Unlike vitamins, minerals are not destroyed by cooking.

| Mineral | Sources | Functions | Deficiency |
|---------|---------|-----------|------------|
| **Calcium** | Dairy products, green vegetables | Strong bones and teeth; blood clotting; muscle contraction | Rickets; osteoporosis; poor blood clotting |
| **Iron** | Red meat, liver, eggs, green vegetables, pulses | Component of haemoglobin for oxygen transport | Anaemia (tiredness, pale skin, shortness of breath) |
| **Iodine** | Seafood, iodised salt | Component of thyroid hormones | Goitre (swollen thyroid gland) |
| **Sodium** | Table salt, processed foods | Nerve impulse transmission; water balance | Muscle cramps (rare) |
| **Potassium** | Bananas, fruits, vegetables | Nerve impulses; muscle contraction | Muscle weakness (rare) |

#### Fibre (Dietary Fibre)

Fibre is the indigestible part of plant food, mainly cellulose from plant cell walls. Although humans cannot digest fibre, it plays important roles in maintaining digestive health.

**Functions of fibre:**
- Adds bulk to food, giving a feeling of fullness
- Absorbs water, softening faeces and preventing constipation
- Stimulates peristalsis (muscular contractions of the gut)
- May reduce the risk of bowel cancer and heart disease

**Sources**: Whole grain foods, vegetables, fruits, beans, pulses

**Recommended daily intake**: 25-30 grams per day

#### Water

Water is essential for life and makes up approximately 60% of body weight. Although not strictly a nutrient, water is vital for survival.

**Functions of water:**
- Solvent for metabolic reactions
- Transport medium (blood, lymph)
- Component of cells and body fluids
- Temperature regulation (sweating)
- Removal of waste products (urine)
- Lubricant (joints, eyes)
- Reactant in hydrolysis reactions

**Sources**: Drinks, food (fruits and vegetables have high water content), metabolic water from respiration

## 4.2 Food Tests

Food tests are biochemical tests used to identify the presence of specific nutrients in food samples. Understanding these tests and their results is essential for IGCSE examinations.

### Test for Starch (Iodine Test)

**Procedure:**
1. Place a small sample of food on a white tile or in a test tube
2. Add a few drops of iodine solution

**Results:**
- If starch is present: Brown/orange iodine turns blue-black
- If starch is absent: Solution remains brown/orange

### Test for Reducing Sugars (Benedict's Test)

**Procedure:**
1. Place a sample of food in a test tube
2. Add Benedict's solution (blue)
3. Heat in a water bath at 80°C for 2-3 minutes

**Results:**
- If reducing sugar is present: Blue → Green → Yellow → Orange → Brick red precipitate
- The more sugar present, the more orange/red the colour
- If no reducing sugar: Solution remains blue

**Note**: This tests for monosaccharides (glucose, fructose) and some disaccharides (maltose, lactose). Sucrose is a non-reducing sugar and will not give a positive result unless first hydrolysed with dilute acid.

### Test for Protein (Biuret Test)

**Procedure:**
1. Place a sample of food solution in a test tube
2. Add Biuret reagent (sodium hydroxide and copper sulphate)

**Results:**
- If protein is present: Solution turns purple/violet
- If protein is absent: Solution remains blue

### Test for Lipids (Emulsion Test)

**Procedure:**
1. Place a sample of food in a test tube
2. Add ethanol and shake vigorously
3. Add water and shake

**Results:**
- If lipids are present: A cloudy white emulsion forms
- If lipids are absent: Solution remains clear

### Test for Vitamin C (DCPIP Test)

**Procedure:**
1. Add DCPIP solution (blue) to a test tube
2. Add food extract drop by drop

**Results:**
- If vitamin C is present: Blue DCPIP is decolourised
- The less vitamin C needed to decolourise, the higher the concentration

## 4.3 Balanced Diet

### Definition

A balanced diet contains all the essential nutrients in the correct proportions to maintain good health. The exact requirements vary depending on age, gender, activity level, and health status.

### Energy Requirements

Energy requirements vary between individuals based on several factors:

| Factor | Effect on Energy Requirements |
|--------|------------------------------|
| **Age** | Children and teenagers need more energy per kg for growth; elderly people need less |
| **Gender** | Males generally need more energy than females due to larger muscle mass |
| **Activity level** | Active people need more energy than sedentary people |
| **Pregnancy** | Pregnant women need extra energy for fetal development |
| **Lactation** | Breastfeeding women need extra energy for milk production |
| **Body size** | Larger people need more energy |

**Average daily energy requirements:**
- Adult male: approximately 10,500 kJ (2,500 kcal)
- Adult female: approximately 8,400 kJ (2,000 kcal)

### Malnutrition

Malnutrition refers to an imbalance in nutrient intake - either too little or too much of one or more nutrients.

**Undernutrition** includes:
- Protein-energy malnutrition (kwashiorkor, marasmus)
- Vitamin deficiencies (scurvy, rickets)
- Mineral deficiencies (anaemia, goitre)

**Overnutrition** includes:
- Obesity
- Diet-related diseases (type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease)

**Kwashiorkor** is a form of protein deficiency malnutrition:
- Swollen abdomen (due to fluid accumulation and enlarged liver)
- Thin arms and legs
- Poor growth
- Changes in hair and skin colour
- Common in developing countries where diet lacks protein

**Obesity** results from excessive energy intake:
- Body mass index (BMI) over 30
- Increased risk of type 2 diabetes, heart disease, stroke, certain cancers
- Treatment involves dietary changes and exercise

## 4.4 The Digestive System

### Overview

The digestive system is responsible for breaking down food into molecules small enough to be absorbed into the bloodstream. This process involves both mechanical digestion (physical breakdown) and chemical digestion (breakdown by enzymes).

### Structure of the Digestive System

**The Alimentary Canal**

The alimentary canal is a long tube running from mouth to anus, approximately 9 metres in length. It includes:

**Mouth (Buccal Cavity)**
- Teeth mechanically break food into smaller pieces (mastication)
- Salivary glands produce saliva containing:
  - Salivary amylase: Begins starch digestion
  - Mucus: Lubricates food
  - Water: Dissolves substances
- Tongue mixes food with saliva and forms bolus
- Taste buds detect flavours

**Oesophagus (Gullet)**
- Muscular tube connecting mouth to stomach
- Approximately 25 cm long
- Food moved by peristalsis (wave of muscular contraction)

**Stomach**
- Muscular J-shaped sac
- Produces gastric juice containing:
  - Hydrochloric acid: Kills bacteria; provides acidic pH (pH 2) for pepsin
  - Pepsin (protease): Begins protein digestion
  - Mucus: Protects stomach lining
- Churns food mechanically (mechanical digestion)
- Food becomes semi-liquid chyme
- Sphincter muscles control entry and exit

**Small Intestine (Duodenum and Ileum)**
- Approximately 6 metres long
- **Duodenum** (first 25 cm):
  - Receives bile from liver (via bile duct)
  - Receives pancreatic juice from pancreas
  - Bile emulsifies fats; pancreatic enzymes digest all food types
- **Ileum** (remainder):
  - Main site of digestion and absorption
  - Villi increase surface area for absorption

**Liver**
- Produces bile (stored in gall bladder)
- Bile emulsifies fats into small droplets
- Bile neutralises stomach acid
- Processes absorbed nutrients

**Pancreas**
- Produces pancreatic juice containing:
  - Pancreatic amylase: Digests starch to maltose
  - Trypsin (protease): Digests proteins to amino acids
  - Pancreatic lipase: Digests fats to fatty acids and glycerol
- Secretes into duodenum via pancreatic duct

**Large Intestine (Colon and Rectum)**
- Colon absorbs water and minerals
- Rectum stores faeces
- Faeces egested through anus

### Peristalsis

Peristalsis is the wave-like muscular contraction that moves food along the alimentary canal. It involves:

1. Circular muscles behind the food bolus contract
2. Longitudinal muscles ahead of the bolus relax
3. This creates a wave of constriction that pushes food forward
4. The process is involuntary (controlled by the autonomic nervous system)

## 4.5 Digestion

### Types of Digestion

**Mechanical Digestion**
- Physical breakdown of food into smaller pieces
- Increases surface area for enzyme action
- Examples: Chewing (mouth), churning (stomach)

**Chemical Digestion**
- Breakdown of large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules
- Catalysed by digestive enzymes
- Involves hydrolysis reactions (adding water to break bonds)

### Enzymes in Digestion

| Enzyme | Produced by | Site of action | Substrate | Product | Optimum pH |
|--------|-------------|----------------|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Salivary amylase** | Salivary glands | Mouth | Starch | Maltose | Neutral (pH 7) |
| **Pepsin** | Stomach wall | Stomach | Proteins | Peptides | Acidic (pH 2) |
| **Pancreatic amylase** | Pancreas | Duodenum | Starch | Maltose | Alkaline (pH 8) |
| **Trypsin** | Pancreas | Duodenum | Proteins | Peptides/amino acids | Alkaline (pH 8) |
| **Pancreatic lipase** | Pancreas | Duodenum | Fats | Fatty acids + glycerol | Alkaline (pH 8) |
| **Maltase** | Small intestine wall | Ileum | Maltose | Glucose | Alkaline |
| **Peptidases** | Small intestine wall | Ileum | Peptides | Amino acids | Alkaline |

### Role of Bile

Bile is produced in the liver, stored in the gall bladder, and released into the duodenum. It has two main functions:

**1. Emulsification of fats:**
- Bile salts break large fat droplets into smaller droplets
- This increases the surface area for lipase to act on
- Emulsification is NOT digestion (no chemical bonds are broken)

**2. Neutralisation:**
- Bile is alkaline (pH 8-8.6)
- It neutralises acidic chyme from the stomach
- Creates suitable pH for pancreatic enzymes

## 4.6 Absorption

### Site of Absorption

Most absorption occurs in the small intestine (ileum), which is adapted for efficient absorption of the products of digestion.

### Adaptations of the Small Intestine

**Villi**

The inner wall of the small intestine is folded into finger-like projections called villi (singular: villus). Each villus is approximately 1mm long and contains:

- **Single layer of epithelial cells**: Very thin walls for short diffusion distance
- **Microvilli**: Tiny projections on epithelial cells, further increasing surface area (brush border)
- **Network of blood capillaries**: For rapid absorption of glucose and amino acids
- **Lacteal** (lymph capillary): For absorption of fatty acids and glycerol

**Large surface area**:
- Folds in intestinal wall
- Villi (approximately 5 million)
- Microvilli on epithelial cells
- Total surface area: approximately 300 m²

### How Absorption Occurs

**Glucose and Amino Acids:**
- Absorbed into blood capillaries by diffusion and active transport
- Active transport allows absorption even when concentration is low in gut
- Transported to liver via hepatic portal vein

**Fatty Acids and Glycerol:**
- Absorbed into epithelial cells
- Recombined to form fats
- Packaged into chylomicrons
- Enter lacteals (lymph vessels)
- Eventually enter blood circulation

**Water:**
- Absorbed by osmosis throughout the digestive system
- Most absorption occurs in small intestine
- Remaining water absorbed in large intestine

## 4.7 Utilisation of Absorbed Nutrients

### Glucose

- Used for respiration in all cells
- Excess converted to glycogen in liver (glycogenesis)
- Further excess converted to fat and stored in adipose tissue

### Amino Acids

- Used for protein synthesis (growth and repair)
- Cannot be stored
- Excess amino acids deaminated in liver:
  - Nitrogen-containing part converted to urea
  - Carbon skeleton converted to carbohydrate or fat

### Lipids

- Used for energy or stored in adipose tissue
- Component of cell membranes
- Used to make steroid hormones

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Digestion** | The breakdown of large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules |
| **Absorption** | The movement of digested food molecules through the gut wall into the blood |
| **Assimilation** | The use of absorbed nutrients by cells |
| **Egestion** | The removal of undigested food from the body as faeces |
| **Peristalsis** | Wave-like muscular contractions that move food along the gut |
| **Villus** | Finger-like projection in the small intestine for absorption |
| **Lacteal** | Lymph vessel in the villus for fat absorption |
| **Emulsification** | Breaking large fat droplets into smaller droplets |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing emulsification with digestion**: Emulsification is physical, not chemical; no bonds are broken.

2. **Saying bile digests fats**: Bile emulsifies fats but does NOT digest them - lipase does the digestion.

3. **Forgetting that absorption mainly occurs in the small intestine**: Many students incorrectly state absorption occurs in the stomach.

4. **Confusing deamination with transamination**: Deamination is the removal of the nitrogen-containing part of amino acids.

5. **Not mentioning "insoluble to soluble" in digestion definitions**: The change in solubility is essential.
