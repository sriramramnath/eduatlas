# Chapter 20: Selective Breeding

## Detailed Notes for Pearson Edexcel IGCSE Biology

## 20.1 What is Selective Breeding?

### Definition

Selective breeding (also called artificial selection) is the process by which humans choose plants or animals with desirable characteristics to breed together, producing offspring with those desirable traits.

### Difference from Natural Selection

| Feature | Natural Selection | Selective Breeding |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| Who selects? | Nature/environment | Humans |
| Timescale | Thousands/millions of years | Decades/centuries |
| Purpose | Survival and reproduction | Human wants and needs |
| Variation source | Mutations, sexual reproduction | Existing variation |

---

## 20.2 The Process of Selective Breeding

### Steps in Selective Breeding

1. **Identify desired characteristics**: Decide which traits you want (e.g., high milk yield, disease resistance)

2. **Select parents**: Choose individuals that show the desired traits strongly

3. **Breed selected individuals**: Cross the selected parents

4. **Select offspring**: From the offspring, select those showing the desired traits most strongly

5. **Repeat over generations**: Continue selecting and breeding the best individuals for many generations

6. **Develop improved varieties**: Over time, the population shows the desired trait more strongly

## 20.3 Examples of Selective Breeding

### Animals

**Dairy Cattle**
- Selected for high milk yield
- Selected for quality of milk (protein/fat content)
- Selected for disease resistance
- Selected for temperament
- Result: Modern dairy cows produce much more milk than wild ancestors

**Meat Cattle**
- Selected for rapid growth
- Selected for high muscle mass
- Selected for efficient feed conversion
- Result: Fast-growing, muscular cattle for meat production

**Sheep**
- Selected for wool quality and quantity
- Selected for meat quality
- Selected for disease resistance
- Result: Breeds like Merino for fine wool

**Dogs**
- Selected for various traits over thousands of years
- Working dogs: selected for intelligence, speed, strength
- Companion dogs: selected for appearance, temperament
- Result: Over 400 breeds from wolf ancestors

**Horses**
- Racehorses: selected for speed and stamina
- Draft horses: selected for strength and size
- Result: Specialised breeds for different purposes

### Plants

**Wheat**
- Selected for high grain yield
- Selected for disease resistance
- Selected for drought resistance
- Selected for short stems (less likely to fall over)
- Result: Modern wheat varieties produce much more grain

**Rice**
- Selected for high yield
- Selected for disease resistance
- Selected for nutritional content
- Result: High-yielding varieties for food security

**Tomatoes**
- Selected for size
- Selected for taste
- Selected for disease resistance
- Selected for transport durability
- Result: Various varieties for different purposes

**Flowers**
- Selected for colour, size, fragrance
- Selected for flowering period
- Result: Many ornamental varieties

## 20.4 Advantages of Selective Breeding

### Economic Benefits
- Higher crop yields increase food production
- Better quality products (milk, meat, eggs, wool)
- Disease resistance reduces losses
- Faster growth reduces production time

### Food Security
- Contributes to feeding growing population
- Crops can be adapted to different climates
- Disease-resistant varieties reduce crop failures

### Specific Adaptations
- Animals bred for specific environments
- Crops bred for local conditions
- Working animals bred for specific tasks

---

## 20.5 Disadvantages and Problems

### Reduced Genetic Variation

- Only individuals with desired traits are bred
- Other genetic variations are lost
- Population becomes genetically uniform
- Less able to adapt to new challenges

### Inbreeding

**Definition**: Breeding closely related individuals

**Problems with inbreeding:**
- Recessive harmful alleles become expressed
- Reduced fertility
- Reduced disease resistance
- Smaller litter sizes
- Higher infant mortality

### Health Problems

**Dogs:**
- Pugs and bulldogs: breathing problems due to flat faces
- German Shepherds: hip problems
- Cavalier King Charles: heart problems
- Dachshunds: back problems

**Crops:**
- Monocultures vulnerable to disease
- All plants susceptible to same pests
- Irish Potato Famine example: all potatoes same variety

### Loss of Original Traits

- Wild-type characteristics lost
- Traditional varieties disappear
- Reduced genetic diversity globally

## 20.6 Selective Breeding vs Genetic Engineering

| Feature | Selective Breeding | Genetic Engineering |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Method | Traditional breeding | Direct DNA manipulation |
| Time | Many generations | Relatively quick |
| Species barrier | Same species only | Can cross species barrier |
| Precision | Less precise | Very precise |
| History | Thousands of years | Last 50 years |

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Selective breeding** | Humans select organisms with desired traits to breed |
| **Artificial selection** | Another term for selective breeding |
| **Inbreeding** | Breeding closely related individuals |
| **Monoculture** | Growing one variety of crop over large area |
| **Variety/Breed** | Type within a species produced by selective breeding |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing natural and artificial selection**: Natural = nature selects; Artificial = humans select

2. **Forgetting to mention "over many generations"**: Selective breeding takes time

3. **Not explaining both advantages AND disadvantages**: Always give balanced view

4. **Confusing selective breeding with genetic engineering**: Different processes

5. **Not giving specific examples**: Always use named examples in answers
