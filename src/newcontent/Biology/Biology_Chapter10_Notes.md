## 10.1 Photosynthesis

### Definition

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants and some other organisms convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. It is arguably the most important biological process on Earth, as it produces the food and oxygen that sustain almost all life on our planet.

### The Word Equation

**Carbon dioxide + Water → (light energy + chlorophyll) → Glucose + Oxygen**

### The Chemical Equation

**6CO₂ + 6H₂O → (light + chlorophyll) → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂**

### Raw Materials and Products

| Input | Source |
|-------|--------|
| Carbon dioxide | From air, enters through stomata |
| Water | From soil, absorbed by roots |
| Light energy | From sun, absorbed by chlorophyll |
| Chlorophyll | Present in chloroplasts |

| Output | Fate |
|--------|------|
| Glucose | Used for respiration, converted to starch, cellulose, proteins |
| Oxygen | Released into atmosphere through stomata |

### Where Photosynthesis Occurs

Photosynthesis takes place in the chloroplasts of plant cells, primarily in the mesophyll cells of leaves. The chloroplasts contain:

- **Chlorophyll**: Green pigment that absorbs light energy (mainly red and blue wavelengths)
- **Grana**: Stacks of thylakoid membranes where light-dependent reactions occur
- **Stroma**: Fluid-filled space where light-independent reactions occur

### Leaf Structure for Photosynthesis

The leaf is adapted for photosynthesis in several ways:

**1. Large Surface Area**
- Broad, flat shape maximises light absorption
- Large surface area to volume ratio for gas exchange

**2. Thin Structure**
- Short distance for gases to diffuse
- Light penetrates to all cells

**3. Transparent Upper Epidermis**
- Allows light to pass through to mesophyll
- Covered by waxy cuticle to reduce water loss

**4. Palisade Mesophyll**
- Located near upper surface
- Cells are columnar and tightly packed
- Contain many chloroplasts
- Positioned to receive maximum light

**5. Spongy Mesophyll**
- Irregularly shaped cells with air spaces
- Allows gas exchange between cells
- Large surface area for diffusion

**6. Stomata**
- Small pores on lower epidermis
- Allow CO₂ in and O₂ out
- Guard cells control opening/closing

**7. Vascular Bundles (Veins)**
- Xylem brings water to leaf
- Phloem transports products of photosynthesis

## 10.2 Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

### Limiting Factors

The rate of photosynthesis is affected by several environmental factors. The concept of limiting factors is crucial: at any given time, the factor that is nearest to its minimum value will limit the rate of the overall process.

### Light Intensity

**Effect**: As light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases - up to a point.

**Graph interpretation**:
- Rate increases linearly at low light intensities (light is limiting)
- Rate plateaus at high light intensities (another factor becomes limiting)
- Saturation point: Maximum rate reached

**Explanation**: Light provides energy for the light-dependent reactions. More light means more energy for splitting water and producing ATP and NADPH. However, beyond saturation, the enzymes involved become the limiting factor.

### Carbon Dioxide Concentration

**Effect**: As CO₂ concentration increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases - up to a point.

**Graph interpretation**:
- Rate increases linearly at low CO₂ concentrations (CO₂ is limiting)
- Rate plateaus at high CO₂ concentrations (another factor becomes limiting)
- Atmospheric CO₂ is about 0.04%, but plants can use higher concentrations in controlled conditions

**Explanation**: CO₂ is the source of carbon for making glucose. More CO₂ means more raw material for the Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions). In a closed environment, increasing CO₂ can significantly boost photosynthesis.

### Temperature

**Effect**: As temperature increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases up to an optimum, then decreases.

**Graph interpretation**:
- Rate increases between 0-35°C (more kinetic energy, faster enzyme activity)
- Maximum rate at optimum temperature (usually 25-35°C for most plants)
- Rate decreases above optimum (enzymes denature)

**Explanation**: Temperature affects enzyme activity. The Calvin cycle is particularly temperature-sensitive because it involves many enzyme-catalysed reactions. High temperatures cause enzymes to denature, permanently stopping photosynthesis.

### Interaction of Limiting Factors

At any moment, only one factor is limiting:
- In low light → Light is limiting
- With adequate light but low CO₂ → CO₂ is limiting
- With adequate light and CO₂ but low temperature → Temperature is limiting
- When all factors are optimal → Rate reaches maximum (limited by plant's genetic potential)

## 10.3 Uses of Glucose

### Immediate Use

**Respiration**
- Glucose is broken down to release energy
- Provides ATP for all cellular activities
- Occurs continuously in all living cells

### Storage

**Starch (in plants)**
- Glucose molecules joined together
- Insoluble, so does not affect water potential
- Stored in chloroplasts and storage organs (potatoes, grains)
- Can be broken down to glucose when needed

**Glycogen (in animals)**
- Similar to starch but more branched
- Stored in liver and muscles

### Structural Use

**Cellulose**
- Glucose molecules joined in long chains
- Forms cell walls in plants
- Very strong, provides structural support
- Humans cannot digest cellulose (fibre)

### Conversion to Other Substances

**Lipids (Fats and Oils)**
- Glucose converted to fatty acids and glycerol
- Stored in seeds (e.g., sunflower, olive)
- High energy storage

**Proteins**
- Glucose provides carbon skeleton
- Combined with nitrogen (from nitrates) to form amino acids
- Used for growth and enzymes

**Sucrose**
- Transport form in phloem
- Glucose + fructose
- Soluble, moves easily through plant

## 10.4 Mineral Requirements

### Nitrate Ions (NO₃⁻)

**Why plants need nitrogen**:
- Essential component of amino acids (proteins)
- Component of nucleic acids (DNA, RNA)
- Part of ATP and chlorophyll

**Symptoms of nitrogen deficiency**:
- Stunted growth
- Yellowing of leaves (chlorosis) - especially older leaves
- Weak stems

**Source**: Absorbed from soil as nitrate ions (NO₃⁻) or ammonium ions (NH₄⁺)

### Magnesium Ions (Mg²⁺)

**Why plants need magnesium**:
- Central atom in chlorophyll molecule
- Essential for photosynthesis
- Activates many enzymes

**Symptoms of magnesium deficiency**:
- Yellowing between leaf veins (interveinal chlorosis)
- Starts with older leaves (mobile nutrient)
- Reduced photosynthesis

**Source**: Absorbed from soil as magnesium ions (Mg²⁺)

## 10.5 Plant Nutrition Experiments

### Testing for Starch in Leaves

**Iodine Test**:
1. Kill leaf by boiling in water (stops enzyme activity)
2. Remove chlorophyll by boiling in ethanol
3. Rinse leaf in water (softens it)
4. Add iodine solution
5. Blue-black colour indicates starch

### Investigating Light as a Factor

**Destarching**: Plant is kept in darkness for 24-48 hours to use up stored starch

**Variegated leaf experiment**:
1. Destarch plant
2. Expose to light for several hours
3. Test leaf for starch
4. Only green parts contain starch (where chlorophyll is present)

**Light exclusion experiment**:
1. Cover part of leaf with black paper/card
2. Expose plant to light
3. Test for starch
4. Covered area shows no starch (no photosynthesis)

### Investigating Carbon Dioxide

**Soda lime experiment**:
1. Place plant in sealed container with soda lime (absorbs CO₂)
2. Control plant in sealed container without soda lime
3. Expose both to light
4. Test leaves for starch
5. Plant with soda lime shows no starch (no CO₂ for photosynthesis)

### Investigating Chlorophyll

**Variegated leaf**:
1. Take variegated leaf (green and white patches)
2. Draw leaf showing green/white areas
3. Test for starch
4. Only green areas contain starch (chlorophyll required)

## 10.6 Effect of Environmental Factors on Transpiration

While not directly photosynthesis, transpiration affects water availability for photosynthesis and is often examined alongside this topic.

**Factors affecting transpiration rate:**
- **Temperature**: Higher temperature → faster transpiration
- **Humidity**: Lower humidity → faster transpiration
- **Wind**: More wind → faster transpiration
- **Light intensity**: More light → stomata open → faster transpiration

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting to mention chlorophyll**: Light alone is not enough - chlorophyll must absorb the light energy.

2. **Saying plants "breathe in CO₂"**: Plants do not breathe - they exchange gases through stomata.

3. **Confusing starch and glucose**: Glucose is the product of photosynthesis; starch is the storage form.

4. **Not explaining why factors are limiting**: Always explain WHY a factor limits the rate.

5. **Forgetting that respiration occurs all the time**: Plants respire 24/7, but only photosynthesise in light.
