## 16.1 DNA Structure

### Discovery of DNA

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) was identified as the genetic material in the mid-20th century. James Watson and Francis Crick, using X-ray crystallography data from Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins, proposed the double helix structure of DNA in 1953.

### Structure of DNA

DNA is a polymer made up of repeating units called nucleotides. Each nucleotide consists of three components:

**Components of a Nucleotide:**
- **Phosphate group** - Contains phosphorus and oxygen
- **Deoxyribose sugar** - A 5-carbon sugar
- **Nitrogenous base** - One of four types

**The Four Bases:**
- **Adenine (A)**
- **Thymine (T)**
- **Cytosine (C)**
- **Guanine (G)**

**Base Pairing Rules:**
- Adenine always pairs with Thymine (A-T)
- Cytosine always pairs with Guanine (C-G)
- These are called complementary base pairs
- Held together by hydrogen bonds

**Double Helix Structure:**
- Two polynucleotide strands run in opposite directions (antiparallel)
- The strands are twisted around each other in a double helix
- The sugar-phosphate backbone is on the outside
- The bases are on the inside, paired across the helix
- The structure is like a twisted ladder

### The Importance of Complementary Base Pairing

- Allows DNA to replicate accurately
- Ensures genetic information is copied precisely
- A-T pair has 2 hydrogen bonds
- C-G pair has 3 hydrogen bonds (stronger)
- The sequence of bases encodes genetic information

## 16.2 Chromosomes

### What are Chromosomes?

Chromosomes are structures made of DNA that carry genetic information. They are found in the nucleus of cells and become visible during cell division.

**Key facts about chromosomes:**
- Made of DNA tightly coiled around proteins (histones)
- Contain genes arranged along their length
- Found in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells
- Different species have different numbers of chromosomes
- Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs)

### Chromosome Number

| Organism | Chromosome Number |
|----------|-------------------|
| Human | 46 (23 pairs) |
| Chimpanzee | 48 (24 pairs) |
| Dog | 78 (39 pairs) |
| Cat | 38 (19 pairs) |
| Fruit fly | 8 (4 pairs) |
| Garden pea | 14 (7 pairs) |

### Homologous Chromosomes

In body (somatic) cells, chromosomes exist in pairs:
- **Homologous chromosomes**: A pair of chromosomes that carry the same genes in the same positions
- One chromosome from each pair comes from each parent
- The genes may have different versions (alleles)

### Sex Chromosomes

- In humans, one pair of chromosomes determines sex
- **Females**: XX (two X chromosomes)
- **Males**: XY (one X and one Y chromosome)
- The Y chromosome is smaller and carries fewer genes
- The X chromosome carries many genes unrelated to sex

## 16.3 Genes

### Definition of a Gene

A gene is a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein. Genes are the basic units of heredity and determine inherited characteristics.

**Key points about genes:**
- Each gene occupies a specific position (locus) on a chromosome
- Genes can exist in different forms called alleles
- Different alleles produce variations in inherited characteristics
- Most genes code for proteins (or parts of proteins)
- Some genes code for RNA molecules

### How Genes Code for Proteins

The sequence of bases in DNA determines the sequence of amino acids in a protein:

1. The sequence of three bases (triplet/codon) codes for one amino acid
2. This is the genetic code
3. The code is read during protein synthesis
4. Different sequences produce different proteins
5. Different proteins have different functions

### The Genetic Code

- **Triplet code**: Three bases code for one amino acid
- **Degenerate**: Some amino acids are coded for by more than one triplet
- **Universal**: The same code is used by almost all organisms
- **Non-overlapping**: Each base is part of only one triplet
- **Start and stop codons**: Signal where genes begin and end

## 16.4 Genes and Alleles

### Definitions

**Gene**: A section of DNA that codes for a specific protein

**Allele**: Different versions of the same gene

### Examples of Alleles

| Gene | Alleles | Effect |
|------|---------|--------|
| Eye colour gene | Allele for brown eyes, allele for blue eyes | Different eye colours |
| Seed shape (pea) | Allele for round seeds, allele for wrinkled seeds | Different seed shapes |
| Blood group | Allele for A, B, or O | Different blood types |

### How Alleles Arise

Alleles arise through **mutations**:
- Changes in the DNA sequence
- Can occur spontaneously during DNA replication
- Can be caused by mutagens (radiation, chemicals)
- Most mutations are harmful or neutral
- Some mutations may be beneficial

### Genotype and Phenotype

**Genotype**: The genetic makeup of an organism - the combination of alleles it possesses

**Phenotype**: The observable characteristics of an organism - the visible expression of the genotype

Example:
- Gene: Seed colour in peas
- Alleles: Y (yellow) and y (green)
- Genotypes: YY, Yy, or yy
- Phenotypes: Yellow seeds (YY or Yy) or green seeds (yy)

## 16.5 DNA and Protein Synthesis

### Overview of Protein Synthesis

Protein synthesis is the process by which the genetic code in DNA is used to make proteins. It occurs in two main stages:

1. **Transcription**: DNA → mRNA (in the nucleus)
2. **Translation**: mRNA → Protein (at ribosomes in cytoplasm)

### Transcription

Transcription is the process of copying the genetic code from DNA to mRNA:

1. DNA double helix unwinds and separates at the gene
2. RNA polymerase binds to the promoter region of the gene
3. Free RNA nucleotides pair with complementary bases on the template strand:
   - Adenine pairs with Uracil (not Thymine)
   - Cytosine pairs with Guanine
4. RNA polymerase joins the nucleotides together
5. Pre-mRNA is formed
6. In eukaryotes, introns (non-coding sections) are removed (splicing)
7. Mature mRNA leaves the nucleus through nuclear pores

### Translation

Translation is the process of decoding the mRNA to make a protein:

1. mRNA attaches to a ribosome in the cytoplasm
2. The ribosome reads the mRNA in groups of three bases (codons)
3. tRNA molecules bring the correct amino acids:
   - Each tRNA has an anticodon that pairs with the codon
   - Each tRNA carries a specific amino acid
4. Amino acids are joined together by peptide bonds
5. The polypeptide chain grows
6. Translation stops at a stop codon
7. The protein folds into its functional shape

### The Role of Ribosomes

- Made of rRNA and proteins
- Found in cytoplasm or attached to rough ER
- Have two subunits that sandwich the mRNA
- Have binding sites for tRNA molecules
- Catalyse the formation of peptide bonds

### The Role of tRNA

- Transfer RNA
- Clover-leaf shaped molecule
- Has an anticodon at one end
- Carries a specific amino acid at the other end
- Each type of tRNA carries one specific amino acid

## 16.6 Genes and Inheritance

### Basic Principles

- Genes are inherited from parents
- Each parent contributes one allele for each gene
- The combination of alleles determines the phenotype

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Dominant allele** | Always expressed in the phenotype when present |
| **Recessive allele** | Only expressed when two copies are present |
| **Homozygous** | Having two identical alleles (e.g., BB or bb) |
| **Heterozygous** | Having two different alleles (e.g., Bb) |
| **Carrier** | Heterozygous for a recessive allele; doesn't show the trait |

### Representing Genetic Crosses

- Capital letters represent dominant alleles (e.g., B)
- Lowercase letters represent recessive alleles (e.g., b)
- Example: B = brown eyes (dominant); b = blue eyes (recessive)

**Example Cross:**
- Parent genotypes: Bb × Bb
- Gametes: B, b × B, b
- Punnett square shows offspring genotypes

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing gene and allele**: Gene = the section of DNA; Allele = different versions of that gene

2. **Using wrong letters for alleles**: Capital letters for dominant, lowercase for recessive

3. **Forgetting that chromosomes are in pairs**: Body cells have pairs (diploid); gametes have single chromosomes (haploid)

4. **Not stating that DNA is a polymer**: Always describe DNA as a polymer of nucleotides

5. **Confusing transcription and translation**: Transcription = DNA to mRNA; Translation = mRNA to protein
