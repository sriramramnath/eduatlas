## 11.1 The Need for Transport in Plants

### Why Plants Need Transport Systems

Like animals, plants require a transport system to move substances throughout their bodies. While plants may appear stationary and simple, they are complex multicellular organisms with cells that need a constant supply of water, minerals, and organic nutrients. Without an efficient transport system, large plants could not survive because diffusion alone would be too slow to meet the needs of all cells.

Plants have two types of transport tissue:
- **Xylem**: Transports water and dissolved mineral ions from roots to leaves
- **Phloem**: Transports dissolved sugars and amino acids from leaves to growing regions and storage organs

---

## 11.2 Xylem

### Structure of Xylem

Xylem is a tissue specialised for transporting water and minerals. Its structure is highly adapted to its function:

**Xylem Vessels**
- Long, continuous tubes formed from dead cells
- Cells called vessel elements joined end-to-end
- End walls break down to form continuous tubes
- Cells have thick walls strengthened with lignin
- Lignin makes walls waterproof and provides structural support
- No end walls, nucleus, or cytoplasm in mature cells
- Pits in walls allow water to move sideways between vessels

**Types of Xylem Cells**
- **Vessel elements**: Short, wide cells forming vessels
- **Tracheids**: Longer, narrower cells with tapered ends; found in all plants
- **Xylem parenchyma**: Living cells that store food
- **Xylem fibres**: Provide structural support

### Adaptations of Xylem for Water Transport

| Feature | Adaptation |
|---------|------------|
| Hollow tubes | Allows continuous water flow |
| No end walls | No barrier to water movement |
| Thick lignified walls | Strong; withstands tension; waterproof |
| Dead cells | No nucleus/cytoplasm to obstruct flow |
| Narrow tubes | Increases capillary action |
| Pits | Allows water exchange between vessels |

### Functions of Xylem

1. **Transport of water**: From roots to leaves
2. **Transport of minerals**: Dissolved in water
3. **Structural support**: Lignified walls provide strength

## 11.3 Phloem

### Structure of Phloem

Phloem is the tissue that transports organic nutrients (mainly sucrose) from where they are made (sources) to where they are needed (sinks).

**Phloem Components**

**Sieve Tube Elements**
- Living cells that form tubes
- End walls have pores forming sieve plates
- Cytoplasm of adjacent cells connected through pores
- No nucleus, ribosomes, or large vacuole
- Very thin layer of cytoplasm

**Companion Cells**
- Located alongside each sieve tube element
- Have nucleus and all organelles
- Provide energy and proteins for sieve tube elements
- Connected to sieve tube by plasmodesmata
- Act as "life support" for sieve tubes

**Other Phloem Cells**
- **Phloem parenchyma**: Storage cells
- **Phloem fibres**: Structural support

### Adaptations of Phloem for Sugar Transport

| Feature | Adaptation |
|---------|------------|
| Sieve plates | Allow flow between cells |
| Thin cytoplasm | Reduces resistance to flow |
| Companion cells | Provide ATP and proteins |
| Plasmodesmata | Connection between cells |
| Living cells | Active transport possible |

### Functions of Phloem

1. **Translocation**: Transport of sucrose and amino acids
2. **From source to sink**: Source = leaves (making sugars); Sink = growing regions, storage organs

## 11.4 Comparing Xylem and Phloem

| Feature | Xylem | Phloem |
|---------|-------|--------|
| **Function** | Transports water and minerals | Transports sugars and amino acids |
| **Direction** | Upward only (roots to leaves) | Both directions (source to sink) |
| **Cell type** | Dead cells | Living cells |
| **End walls** | None (continuous tube) | Sieve plates with pores |
| **Cytoplasm** | None | Thin layer of cytoplasm |
| **Nucleus** | None | Companion cells have nuclei |
| **Wall structure** | Thick, lignified | Thin, cellulose |
| **Process** | Passive (transpiration stream) | Active (requires energy) |

## 11.5 Water Uptake by Roots

### Root Structure

**Root Hair Cells**
- Extensions of epidermal cells
- Microscopic hair-like projections
- Greatly increase surface area for absorption
- Thin walls for rapid water uptake
- Located behind root tip in zone of maturation

### How Water Enters the Root

1. **Soil water**: Contains dissolved minerals
2. **Root hair cell**: Has higher solute concentration than soil water
3. **Osmosis**: Water moves into root hair cell down water potential gradient
4. **Across cortex**: Water moves through root cortex by:
   - **Symplast pathway**: Through cytoplasm via plasmodesmata
   - **Apoplast pathway**: Through cell walls (main pathway)
5. **Endodermis**: Water reaches endodermis (innermost layer of cortex)
6. **Casparian strip**: Waterproof band in endodermal cell walls
   - Blocks apoplast pathway
   - Forces water through cytoplasm of endodermal cells
   - Allows selective uptake of minerals
7. **Xylem**: Water enters xylem vessels

### Mineral Ion Uptake

Mineral ions are absorbed by:
- **Diffusion**: When concentration is higher in soil than root
- **Active transport**: When concentration is lower in soil; requires ATP; carrier proteins in membrane

## 11.6 Movement of Water Up Xylem

### Transpiration Pull

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from plant leaves, mainly through stomata. This creates a "pull" that draws water up through the xylem:

1. Water evaporates from mesophyll cell walls into leaf air spaces
2. Water vapour diffuses out through stomata
3. This reduces water potential in mesophyll cells
4. Water is drawn from xylem vessels by osmosis
5. Creates tension (negative pressure) in xylem
6. This tension is transmitted down the xylem to the roots
7. Pulls water column up from roots to leaves

### Cohesion-Tension Theory

Water molecules stick together (cohesion) and to the xylem walls (adhesion):

- **Cohesion**: Hydrogen bonds between water molecules
- **Adhesion**: Attraction between water and xylem walls
- These forces create a continuous column of water
- When water is pulled from the top, the whole column moves
- This explains how water can rise to great heights in tall trees

### Root Pressure

- Active transport of minerals into xylem in roots
- This creates lower water potential in xylem
- Water enters by osmosis
- Creates positive pressure that pushes water upward
- Important in young plants and spring
- Can cause guttation (water droplets on leaf edges)

## 11.7 Transpiration

### Definition

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant, mainly through the stomata of leaves.

### The Process of Transpiration

1. Water enters leaf through xylem
2. Water moves by osmosis into mesophyll cells
3. Water evaporates from cell walls into air spaces
4. Air inside leaf becomes saturated with water vapour
5. Water vapour diffuses out through stomata
6. Diffusion is down a water vapour concentration gradient

### Factors Affecting Transpiration Rate

| Factor | Effect | Explanation |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Temperature** | Higher temp = faster rate | More kinetic energy; faster evaporation; lower relative humidity |
| **Humidity** | Lower humidity = faster rate | Greater concentration gradient for diffusion |
| **Wind** | More wind = faster rate | Removes water vapour from leaf surface; maintains gradient |
| **Light intensity** | More light = faster rate | Stomata open in light; wider stomata allow more diffusion |

### Potometer Experiment

A potometer measures the rate of water uptake by a plant, which is approximately equal to the rate of transpiration:

1. Cut shoot underwater (prevent air entering xylem)
2. Insert shoot into potometer filled with water
3. Ensure apparatus is watertight
4. Introduce air bubble into capillary tube
5. Record distance moved by bubble per unit time
6. Change one environmental factor and repeat

### The Importance of Transpiration

**Benefits:**
- Creates transpiration pull for water transport
- Supplies water to leaves for photosynthesis
- Delivers minerals to all parts of plant
- Cools leaves by evaporation

**Disadvantages:**
- Can lead to water stress in dry conditions
- Wilting if water loss exceeds uptake

## 11.8 Translocation

### Definition

Translocation is the movement of dissolved organic substances (mainly sucrose) through phloem tissue, from sources to sinks.

### Sources and Sinks

**Sources**: Places where sugars are produced or released from storage
- Photosynthesising leaves
- Storage organs (when breaking down stored starch)
- Germinating seeds

**Sinks**: Places where sugars are used or stored
- Growing root tips
- Growing shoot tips
- Developing fruits
- Storage organs (when storing starch)
- Any actively respiring tissue

### The Process of Translocation

1. Sucrose is produced in mesophyll cells (source)
2. Sucrose is loaded into phloem sieve tubes (active transport)
3. This increases solute concentration in sieve tubes
4. Water enters sieve tubes from xylem by osmosis
5. This creates high pressure at the source end
6. At the sink, sucrose is unloaded (by diffusion or active transport)
7. Sucrose concentration decreases in sieve tubes
8. Water leaves sieve tubes and returns to xylem
9. This creates low pressure at the sink end
10. Pressure difference causes mass flow from source to sink

### The Mass Flow Hypothesis

This is the accepted explanation for translocation:
- Sugars actively loaded at source
- Creates high solute concentration
- Water enters by osmosis, creating high pressure
- Sugars unloaded at sink
- Creates low solute concentration
- Water leaves, creating low pressure
- Pressure difference causes flow from high to low pressure

### Evidence for Translocation

- Ringing experiments: Removing a ring of bark (phloem) causes swelling above the ring
- Radioactive tracers: Radioactive carbon shows movement from leaves to sinks
- Aphid stylets: Aphid mouthparts penetrate phloem; sap is under pressure

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Xylem** | Tissue that transports water and minerals upward |
| **Phloem** | Tissue that transports sugars and amino acids |
| **Transpiration** | Loss of water vapour from leaves through stomata |
| **Translocation** | Transport of organic substances in phloem |
| **Root hair cell** | Specialised cell for water and mineral absorption |
| **Source** | Part of plant where sugars are made or released |
| **Sink** | Part of plant where sugars are used or stored |
| **Cohesion** | Attraction between water molecules |
| **Adhesion** | Attraction between water and other surfaces |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing xylem and phloem**: Xylem = dead cells, water upward; Phloem = living cells, sugars both directions.

2. **Saying transpiration is "water loss from leaves"**: Specify water VAPOUR through STOMATA.

3. **Forgetting that translocation requires energy**: It involves active loading of sugars.

4. **Not mentioning root hair cells**: Always include them when discussing water uptake.

5. **Confusing transpiration and translocation**: They are completely different processes!
