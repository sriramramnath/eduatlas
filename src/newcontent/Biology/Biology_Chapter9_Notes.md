## 9.1 Introduction to Human Reproduction

### Sexual Reproduction

Human reproduction is sexual, involving the fusion of specialised sex cells (gametes) from two parents. The male gametes are sperm cells, produced in the testes, and the female gametes are egg cells (ova), produced in the ovaries. When a sperm fertilises an egg, a zygote is formed, which develops into a new individual with a unique combination of genetic material from both parents.

### Advantages of Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction produces offspring that are genetically different from each other and from their parents. This genetic variation is advantageous because:
- It increases the ability of a species to adapt to changing environments
- It reduces the risk of genetic diseases being expressed
- It provides the raw material for natural selection and evolution
- It allows beneficial combinations of genes to arise

## 9.2 The Male Reproductive System

### Structure and Function

The male reproductive system is designed for the production, storage, and delivery of sperm. Understanding the structure and function of each component is essential for examinations.

**Testes (Singular: Testis)**
- Paired organs located in the scrotum, outside the body cavity
- Produce sperm by meiosis (spermatogenesis)
- Produce testosterone (male sex hormone)
- Located outside body because sperm production requires temperature about 2°C lower than body temperature
- Contains seminiferous tubules where sperm are produced

**Scrotum**
- Skin sac that holds the testes
- Positioned outside the body to maintain lower temperature
- Muscles can raise or lower testes to regulate temperature

**Epididymis**
- Coiled tube attached to each testis
- Stores and matures sperm
- Sperm can be stored for several weeks

**Sperm Duct (Vas Deferens)**
- Tube that carries sperm from epididymis to urethra
- Muscular walls contract during ejaculation
- About 45 cm long

**Prostate Gland**
- Located below the bladder
- Secretes alkaline fluid that neutralises acidic conditions in vagina
- Contains nutrients and enzymes for sperm
- Part of the seminal fluid (semen)

**Seminal Vesicles**
- Paired glands that secrete fluid rich in fructose
- Provides energy for sperm
- Makes up about 60% of semen volume
- Contains prostaglandins that stimulate sperm motility

**Urethra**
- Tube passing through the penis
- Carries both urine and semen (but not simultaneously)
- During ejaculation, sphincter closes to prevent urine mixing with semen

**Penis**
- External organ for delivering sperm into the female reproductive tract
- Contains erectile tissue that fills with blood during sexual arousal
- Becomes erect for insertion into vagina during intercourse

### Semen

Semen is the fluid ejaculated during orgasm:
- Contains sperm (about 200-500 million per ejaculation)
- Contains seminal fluid from prostate gland and seminal vesicles
- Provides medium for sperm transport
- Nourishes sperm
- Protects sperm from acidic vagina

## 9.3 The Female Reproductive System

### Structure and Function

**Ovaries**
- Paired organs located in the lower abdomen
- Produce egg cells (ova) by meiosis (oogenesis)
- Produce female sex hormones (oestrogen and progesterone)
- Contain thousands of follicles, each containing an immature egg
- Release one egg approximately every 28 days (ovulation)

**Oviducts (Fallopian Tubes)**
- Tubes that carry eggs from ovaries to uterus
- Site of fertilisation (usually in the ampulla region)
- Lined with cilia that move the egg toward uterus
- Fimbriae (finger-like projections) sweep the egg into the tube

**Uterus (Womb)**
- Pear-shaped muscular organ
- Where embryo implants and develops
- Lined with endometrium (uterine lining)
- Endometrium thickens each month in preparation for pregnancy
- If no pregnancy occurs, lining is shed (menstruation)

**Cervix**
- Ring of muscle at the lower end of the uterus
- Opening to the vagina
- Dilates during childbirth
- Produces mucus that changes consistency during menstrual cycle

**Vagina**
- Muscular tube leading from cervix to external genitalia
- Receives penis during intercourse
- Birth canal during childbirth
- Acidic environment (protective against infection)

## 9.4 Gametes

### Comparison of Sperm and Egg Cells

| Feature | Sperm Cell | Egg Cell (Ovum) |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| Size | Very small (about 60 μm long) | Much larger (about 0.1 mm) |
| Mobility | Motile (can swim using tail) | Non-motile (moved by cilia) |
| Quantity produced | Millions per day | One per month (in humans) |
| Cytoplasm | Very little | Large amount |
| Energy reserves | Minimal | Large amounts |
| Nucleus | Haploid (23 chromosomes) | Haploid (23 chromosomes) |
| Special features | Flagellum, acrosome, mitochondria | Vesicles, cortical granules |

### Adaptations of Sperm Cells

**Flagellum (Tail)**
- Long, whip-like structure
- Enables swimming towards the egg
- Powered by ATP from mitochondria

**Mitochondria**
- Located in middle section
- Produce ATP through respiration
- Provide energy for tail movement

**Acrosome**
- Cap-like structure at head
- Contains digestive enzymes (hyaluronidase)
- Enables sperm to penetrate egg's protective layers

**Streamlined Shape**
- Small head containing nucleus
- Minimises resistance while swimming

**Haploid Nucleus**
- Contains 23 chromosomes
- Will combine with egg's 23 chromosomes

### Adaptations of Egg Cells

**Large Cytoplasm**
- Contains nutrients for early development
- Contains organelles needed by the embryo
- Rich in mitochondria

**Cytoplasm Contains:**
- Nutrient reserves (yolk)
- mRNA for protein synthesis
- Organelles for early cell divisions

**Protective Layers:**
- Zona pellucida (jelly coat)
- Corona radiata (follicle cells)
- Protect the egg and are penetrated by sperm

**Haploid Nucleus**
- Contains 23 chromosomes
- Will combine with sperm's 23 chromosomes

## 9.5 Fertilisation

### Definition

Fertilisation is the fusion of a male gamete (sperm) with a female gamete (egg) to form a zygote. This restores the diploid number of chromosomes (46 in humans).

### The Process of Fertilisation

1. **Sperm reach the egg**: After ejaculation, sperm swim through the cervix, uterus, and into the oviducts

2. **Capacitation**: Sperm undergo changes in the female reproductive tract that enable them to fertilise

3. **Acrosome reaction**: When sperm reaches the egg, enzymes from the acrosome digest the protective layers around the egg

4. **Penetration**: One sperm penetrates the zona pellucida and enters the egg

5. **Cortical reaction**: Egg releases enzymes that harden the zona pellucida, preventing other sperm from entering (polyspermy block)

6. **Fusion of nuclei**: The sperm nucleus fuses with the egg nucleus

7. **Zygote formation**: A diploid zygote is formed with 46 chromosomes

### Where Fertilisation Occurs

Fertilisation typically occurs in the ampulla (widest part) of the oviduct (Fallopian tube), about 24 hours after ovulation.

## 9.6 The Menstrual Cycle

### Overview

The menstrual cycle is a monthly cycle lasting approximately 28 days, controlled by hormones. It prepares the female body for possible pregnancy. If pregnancy does not occur, the cycle repeats.

### Stages of the Menstrual Cycle

**Days 1-5: Menstruation**
- Uterine lining (endometrium) breaks down
- Blood and tissue shed through vagina
- Lasts about 5 days

**Days 1-13: Follicular Phase**
- FSH from pituitary stimulates follicle development in ovaries
- Growing follicle produces oestrogen
- Oestrogen causes uterine lining to thicken
- Rising oestrogen eventually triggers LH surge

**Day 14: Ovulation**
- LH surge causes follicle to rupture
- Egg released from ovary into oviduct
- Most fertile period

**Days 15-28: Luteal Phase**
- Ruptured follicle becomes corpus luteum
- Corpus luteum produces progesterone
- Progesterone maintains uterine lining
- If no fertilisation, corpus luteum degenerates
- Drop in hormones causes menstruation to begin

### Hormonal Control

| Hormone | Produced by | Function |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **FSH** (Follicle-stimulating hormone) | Pituitary gland | Stimulates follicle development in ovary |
| **Oestrogen** | Ovaries (follicle) | Thickens uterine lining; inhibits FSH |
| **LH** (Luteinising hormone) | Pituitary gland | Triggers ovulation; develops corpus luteum |
| **Progesterone** | Corpus luteum | Maintains uterine lining; inhibits FSH and LH |

## 9.7 Pregnancy and Development

### Implantation

After fertilisation, the zygote divides by mitosis as it travels toward the uterus:
- Day 1: Zygote (single cell)
- Day 2-3: 2-8 cells (cleavage)
- Day 4: Morula (solid ball of cells)
- Day 5-7: Blastocyst (hollow ball with inner cell mass)

The blastocyst implants in the uterus lining about 6-7 days after fertilisation.

### The Placenta

The placenta is an organ that develops during pregnancy, connecting the developing fetus to the mother's blood supply.

**Functions of the placenta:**
- Allows exchange of materials between mother and fetus
- Oxygen and nutrients pass from mother to fetus
- Carbon dioxide and waste pass from fetus to mother
- Produces hormones (oestrogen, progesterone, hCG)
- Acts as a barrier to some pathogens
- Allows antibodies to pass from mother to fetus

**Structure of the placenta:**
- Develops from embryonic tissue (chorion)
- Contains chorionic villi that increase surface area
- Fetal blood and maternal blood are close but do NOT mix
- Separated by a thin barrier for exchange

### Umbilical Cord

- Connects fetus to placenta
- Contains:
  - Umbilical artery: Carries deoxygenated blood from fetus to placenta
  - Umbilical vein: Carries oxygenated blood from placenta to fetus
- Surrounded by protective jelly (Wharton's jelly)

### Amniotic Fluid

- Surrounds the fetus in the amniotic sac
- Protects fetus from physical damage
- Maintains constant temperature
- Allows movement and development
- Contains cells that can be used for genetic testing

## 9.8 Birth Control Methods

### Natural Methods

**Rhythm Method (Calendar Method)**
- Avoiding intercourse during fertile period
- Less reliable

**Withdrawal Method**
- Removing penis before ejaculation
- Not reliable

**Breastfeeding**
- Can suppress ovulation temporarily
- Not reliable long-term

### Barrier Methods

**Condoms (Male)**
- Thin rubber sheath worn over penis
- Prevents sperm entering vagina
- Also protects against STIs
- 98% effective with perfect use

**Femidom (Female Condom)**
- Inserted into vagina before intercourse
- Prevents sperm entering
- Protects against STIs

**Diaphragm/Cap**
- Dome-shaped device inserted into vagina to cover cervix
- Used with spermicide
- Must be fitted by healthcare professional

### Hormonal Methods

**Combined Oral Contraceptive Pill**
- Contains oestrogen and progesterone
- Prevents ovulation (inhibits FSH release)
- Thickens cervical mucus
- Thins uterine lining
- Over 99% effective with correct use

**Progesterone-only Pill**
- Contains only progesterone
- Thickens cervical mucus
- May prevent ovulation
- Must be taken at same time daily

**Contraceptive Implant/Injection**
- Slow-release progesterone
- Prevents ovulation
- Lasts months to years

### Intrauterine Devices (IUDs)

**Copper IUD**
- Copper prevents fertilisation
- Prevents implantation
- Can remain in place for years

**Hormonal IUD**
- Releases progesterone
- Prevents implantation
- Thickens cervical mucus

### Sterilisation

**Male (Vasectomy)**
- Sperm ducts cut and tied
- Permanent
- Sperm still produced but cannot exit

**Female (Tubal Ligation)**
- Oviducts cut and tied
- Permanent
- Eggs cannot reach uterus

## 9.9 Infertility Treatments

### Causes of Infertility

**Male factors:**
- Low sperm count
- Poor sperm motility
- Blocked sperm ducts

**Female factors:**
- Blocked oviducts
- Failure to ovulate
- Endometriosis
- Problems with uterus

### Treatments

**Fertility Drugs**
- Contain FSH
- Stimulate multiple follicle development
- Used for women who don't ovulate
- May result in multiple pregnancies

**Artificial Insemination (AI)**
- Sperm inserted into uterus
- Used when sperm count is low
- Can use partner's or donor sperm

**In Vitro Fertilisation (IVF)**
1. Fertility drugs stimulate multiple egg production
2. Eggs collected from ovaries
3. Eggs mixed with sperm in laboratory
4. Fertilisation occurs outside body
5. Embryos develop for 2-5 days
6. One or more embryos transferred to uterus
7. Remaining embryos can be frozen

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing oviduct and uterus**: Fertilisation occurs in the oviduct, implantation in the uterus.

2. **Saying blood mixes in placenta**: Maternal and fetal blood do NOT mix - they exchange materials across a barrier.

3. **Confusing umbilical artery and vein**: Artery carries blood FROM fetus (deoxygenated); vein carries blood TO fetus (oxygenated).

4. **Forgetting to mention LH in ovulation**: LH is the hormone that triggers ovulation.

5. **Not explaining hormone interactions**: FSH stimulates follicle; follicle produces oestrogen; oestrogen then inhibits FSH.
