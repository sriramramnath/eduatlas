## 6.1 The Nervous System

### The Need for Coordination

The human body contains many different organs and systems that must work together in a coordinated way. Coordination ensures that different parts of the body respond appropriately to internal and external changes (stimuli) to maintain optimal conditions for survival. Without coordination, our bodies would function chaotically - we would not be able to respond to danger, maintain balance, digest food, or carry out any complex behaviours.

### Two Coordination Systems

The human body has two main coordination systems that work together:

**The Nervous System**
- Uses electrical impulses (action potentials)
- Fast transmission (milliseconds)
- Short-lived responses
- Localised effects (specific targets)
- Involves nerves and the brain
- Examples: Reflex actions, muscle contraction

**The Endocrine (Hormonal) System**
- Uses chemical messengers called hormones
- Slower transmission (through bloodstream)
- Longer-lasting responses
- Widespread effects (target organs)
- Involves glands and hormones
- Examples: Growth, metabolism, reproduction

### Comparison of Nervous and Hormonal Coordination

| Feature | Nervous System | Hormonal System |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Signal type | Electrical impulses | Chemical (hormones) |
| Transmission | Along nerves | Through bloodstream |
| Speed | Very fast | Slow |
| Duration of response | Short | Long |
| Area affected | Localised | Widespread |
| Example | Reflex action | Insulin regulating blood glucose |

## 6.2 The Central Nervous System (CNS)

### Structure of the CNS

The central nervous system consists of the brain and spinal cord. It acts as the control centre of the nervous system, receiving information from sensory receptors, processing this information, and sending instructions to effectors (muscles and glands).

**The Brain**

The brain is protected by:
- The skull (cranium)
- Meninges (protective membranes)
- Cerebrospinal fluid (cushions the brain)

Major parts of the brain:

**Cerebrum**
- Largest part of the brain
- Divided into two cerebral hemispheres
- Controls conscious activities: thinking, memory, reasoning, decision-making
- Different regions control different functions (motor area, sensory area, association areas)

**Cerebellum**
- Located at the back of the brain, below the cerebrum
- Controls balance, posture, and coordination of movement
- Ensures smooth, coordinated muscle movements

**Medulla Oblongata**
- Located at the base of the brain, connecting to the spinal cord
- Controls involuntary (automatic) activities
- Regulates heart rate, breathing rate, blood pressure
- Contains centres for swallowing, vomiting, coughing

**The Spinal Cord**

The spinal cord:
- Extends from the medulla down through the vertebral column
- Protected by vertebrae (backbone)
- Carries nerve impulses between the brain and the rest of the body
- Contains reflex centres for simple reflex actions
- Consists of grey matter (cell bodies) surrounded by white matter (axons)

## 6.3 Neurons (Nerve Cells)

### Structure of Neurons

Neurons are specialised cells adapted for transmitting electrical impulses. They have three main parts:

**Cell Body**
- Contains nucleus and cytoplasm
- Contains organelles for protein synthesis
- Where neurotransmitters are made

**Dendrites**
- Short, branched extensions from the cell body
- Receive impulses from other neurons or receptors
- Carry impulses towards the cell body

**Axon**
- Long, single extension from the cell body
- Carries impulses away from the cell body
- Can be very long (up to 1 metre in humans)
- Surrounded by myelin sheath in many neurons

**Myelin Sheath**
- Fatty insulating layer produced by Schwann cells
- Insulates the axon
- Speeds up impulse transmission
- Gaps in the sheath called nodes of Ranvier allow saltatory conduction

### Types of Neurons

**Sensory Neurons**
- Carry impulses from sensory receptors to the CNS
- Have one long dendron and one axon
- Cell body is located in a side branch
- Example: Neurons carrying impulses from skin to spinal cord

**Relay Neurons (Intermediate/Association Neurons)**
- Found within the CNS (brain and spinal cord)
- Connect sensory neurons to motor neurons
- Have many short dendrites and short axons
- Process information and make decisions

**Motor Neurons**
- Carry impulses from the CNS to effectors (muscles or glands)
- Have long axons and many short dendrites
- Cell body at one end with dendrites attached
- Example: Neurons carrying impulses from spinal cord to muscles

## 6.4 Synapses

### What is a Synapse?

A synapse is a junction between two neurons, or between a neuron and an effector (such as a neuromuscular junction). The two cells do not touch - there is a small gap called the synaptic cleft (about 20 nm wide).

### Structure of a Synapse

- **Presynaptic membrane**: Membrane of the neuron sending the impulse
- **Synaptic cleft**: The gap between the two neurons
- **Postsynaptic membrane**: Membrane of the neuron receiving the impulse
- **Synaptic vesicles**: Contain neurotransmitters in the presynaptic ending
- **Receptors**: On the postsynaptic membrane that bind neurotransmitters

### How Synaptic Transmission Works

1. An electrical impulse arrives at the presynaptic ending
2. This causes calcium channels to open and calcium ions enter the presynaptic cell
3. Synaptic vesicles move to and fuse with the presynaptic membrane
4. Neurotransmitter (e.g., acetylcholine) is released into the synaptic cleft
5. Neurotransmitter diffuses across the synaptic cleft
6. Neurotransmitter binds to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane
7. This causes ion channels to open, generating an electrical impulse in the next neuron
8. Enzymes (e.g., acetylcholinesterase) break down the neurotransmitter
9. Products are taken back up by the presynaptic cell (reuptake)

### Importance of Synapses

- Allow neurons to communicate without touching
- Ensure one-way transmission of impulses (neurotransmitters only released from presynaptic side)
- Allow integration of information (many inputs to one neuron)
- Can amplify or modify signals
- Points where drugs can act

## 6.5 Reflex Actions

### What is a Reflex?

A reflex action is a rapid, automatic response to a stimulus that does not involve conscious thought. Reflexes are important for survival because they allow very quick responses to potentially dangerous situations.

### Characteristics of Reflex Actions

- **Rapid**: Occur in milliseconds
- **Automatic**: Do not require conscious thought
- **Involuntary**: Cannot be controlled consciously
- **Innate**: Not learned (mostly)
- **Protective**: Help avoid harm

### The Reflex Arc

A reflex arc is the neural pathway that controls a reflex action. The basic components are:

1. **Stimulus**: A change in the environment detected by a receptor
2. **Receptor**: Detects the stimulus and generates electrical impulses (e.g., pain receptors in skin)
3. **Sensory neuron**: Carries impulses from receptor to spinal cord
4. **Relay neuron**: In spinal cord, passes impulse to motor neuron
5. **Motor neuron**: Carries impulses from spinal cord to effector
6. **Effector**: Muscle (contracts) or gland (secretes) that produces the response
7. **Response**: The action produced (e.g., muscle contraction)

### Example: The Withdrawal Reflex (Touching a Hot Object)

1. **Stimulus**: Heat from the hot object
2. **Receptor**: Thermoreceptors in skin detect heat; pain receptors detect damage
3. **Sensory neuron**: Carries impulse to spinal cord
4. **Relay neuron**: In spinal cord, processes information
5. **Motor neuron**: Carries impulse to arm muscles
6. **Effector**: Biceps muscle contracts
7. **Response**: Hand is withdrawn from the hot object

At the same time, impulses are sent to the brain so you become consciously aware of the pain, but the reflex action occurs before you are aware of it.

## 6.6 The Eye

### Structure of the Eye

The eye is a sense organ that detects light and converts it into electrical impulses sent to the brain.

**External Features:**

**Sclera**
- Tough, white, outer coat
- Protects the eye and maintains shape
- Visible as the "white of the eye"

**Cornea**
- Transparent front part of the sclera
- Refracts (bends) light entering the eye
- No blood supply (receives oxygen from air)

**Conjunctiva**
- Thin, transparent membrane covering the front of the eye
- Protects the cornea
- Keeps the eye moist

**Internal Structures:**

**Choroid**
- Middle layer of the eye
- Contains blood vessels
- Contains black pigment to absorb light and prevent internal reflection

**Retina**
- Innermost layer at the back of the eye
- Contains light-sensitive cells (photoreceptors):
  - **Rods**: Detect light intensity; work in dim light; produce black and white images
  - **Cones**: Detect colour; work in bright light; three types for red, green, and blue light
- Area of sharpest vision called the **fovea** (or macula) - contains only cones

**Lens**
- Transparent, biconvex structure
- Focuses light onto the retina
- Changes shape for focusing on near and distant objects (accommodation)

**Iris**
- Coloured muscular ring
- Controls the size of the pupil
- Regulates amount of light entering the eye

**Pupil**
- Opening in the centre of the iris
- Allows light to enter the eye
- Size controlled by iris muscles

**Ciliary body**
- Contains ciliary muscles
- Attached to lens by suspensory ligaments
- Controls lens shape

**Vitreous humour**
- Clear, jelly-like substance
- Fills the main cavity of the eye
- Maintains eye shape

**Aqueous humour**
- Watery fluid
- Fills the front chamber of the eye
- Maintains pressure and nourishes the cornea and lens

**Optic nerve**
- Carries impulses from retina to brain
- Region where optic nerve leaves the eye is the blind spot (no photoreceptors)

### The Pupil Reflex

The pupil reflex automatically adjusts the size of the pupil to control the amount of light entering the eye. This protects the retina from damage in bright light and allows sufficient light in dim conditions.

**In Bright Light:**
1. Light receptors in retina detect bright light
2. Impulses sent to brain (midbrain)
3. Impulses sent to circular muscles of iris
4. Circular muscles contract
5. Radial muscles relax
6. Pupil constricts (becomes smaller)
7. Less light enters the eye

**In Dim Light:**
1. Light receptors detect dim light
2. Impulses sent to brain
3. Impulses sent to radial muscles of iris
4. Radial muscles contract
5. Circular muscles relax
6. Pupil dilates (becomes larger)
7. More light enters the eye

### Accommodation (Focusing)

Accommodation is the ability of the eye to focus on objects at different distances by changing the shape of the lens.

**Focusing on Distant Objects:**
- Ciliary muscles relax
- Suspensory ligaments become taut (tight)
- Lens becomes thinner and less curved (flatter)
- Light is refracted less
- Image focused on retina

**Focusing on Near Objects:**
- Ciliary muscles contract
- Suspensory ligaments relax (become slack)
- Lens becomes thicker and more curved (rounder)
- Light is refracted more
- Image focused on retina

## 6.7 The Endocrine System

### Hormones

Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands. They are secreted directly into the bloodstream and transported to target organs where they produce specific effects.

### Characteristics of Hormones

- Produced by endocrine glands
- Secreted into the bloodstream
- Transported throughout the body
- Affect only target organs with specific receptors
- Relatively slow to act (carried in blood)
- Effects are long-lasting
- Often involved in regulating body processes

### Major Endocrine Glands and Their Hormones

| Gland | Location | Hormone(s) | Function |
|-------|----------|------------|----------|
| **Pituitary gland** | Base of brain | Growth hormone (GH); TSH; FSH; LH | Master gland; controls other glands; growth; reproduction |
| **Thyroid gland** | Neck | Thyroxine | Controls metabolic rate |
| **Adrenal glands** | Above kidneys | Adrenaline | Prepares body for fight or flight |
| **Pancreas** | Abdomen | Insulin; Glucagon | Regulates blood glucose |
| **Ovaries** | Female pelvis | Oestrogen; Progesterone | Female sexual development; menstrual cycle |
| **Testes** | Male scrotum | Testosterone | Male sexual development; sperm production |

| Term | Definition |

| **Stimulus** | A detectable change in the environment |
| **Receptor** | A cell or organ that detects stimuli |
| **Effector** | An organ that produces a response (muscle or gland) |
| **Neuron** | A nerve cell specialised for transmitting electrical impulses |
| **Synapse** | A junction between two neurons |
| **Reflex** | A rapid, automatic response to a stimulus |
| **Reflex arc** | The neural pathway of a reflex action |
| **Hormone** | A chemical messenger produced by an endocrine gland |
| **Accommodation** | The ability to focus on objects at different distances |
| **Neurotransmitter** | A chemical that transmits signals across a synapse |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing sensory and motor neurons**: Sensory carries TO CNS; motor carries FROM CNS.

2. **Forgetting that synapses use neurotransmitters**: The electrical impulse does not cross the synapse - it's converted to a chemical signal.

3. **Not mentioning that reflex actions are involuntary**: Always state that reflexes do not involve conscious thought.

4. **Confusing circular and radial muscles**: Circular muscles constrict pupil; radial muscles dilate pupil.

5. **Saying the brain is involved in reflex actions**: Simple reflexes go through the spinal cord only; the brain is informed but not involved in the reflex response.
