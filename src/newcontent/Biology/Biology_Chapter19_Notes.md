## 19.1 The Theory of Evolution

### What is Evolution?

Evolution is the change in the heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. It explains how the diversity of life on Earth arose from common ancestors.

### Charles Darwin and Natural Selection

Charles Darwin proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection in his book "On the Origin of Species" (1859). His theory was based on observations made during his voyage on the HMS Beagle, particularly in the Galápagos Islands.

### Darwin's Observations

1. **Variation exists within species**: Individuals in a population show variation in their characteristics

2. **Characteristics can be inherited**: Some variations are passed from parents to offspring

3. **Competition for resources**: More offspring are produced than can survive, leading to competition

4. **Survival of the fittest**: Individuals with advantageous characteristics are more likely to survive and reproduce

---

## 19.2 Natural Selection

### Definition

Natural selection is the process by which organisms with advantageous characteristics are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those characteristics to their offspring.

### The Process of Natural Selection

1. **Variation**: Genetic variation exists within a population due to mutations and sexual reproduction

2. **Selection pressure**: Environmental factors create pressure that affects survival (predators, food availability, climate, disease)

3. **Selective advantage**: Some variations give individuals an advantage in surviving and reproducing

4. **Survival and reproduction**: Individuals with advantageous variations are more likely to survive and reproduce

5. **Inheritance**: Advantageous alleles are passed to offspring

6. **Over generations**: Advantageous alleles become more common in the population

7. **Evolution**: The population changes over time (evolves)

### Example: Peppered Moths

**Before Industrial Revolution:**
- Light-coloured moths were common
- They were camouflaged against light tree bark
- Dark moths were rare and easily seen by birds

**During Industrial Revolution:**
- Trees became darkened by soot
- Dark moths were now camouflaged
- Light moths were easily seen and eaten by birds
- Dark moths survived and reproduced more

**Result:** Dark moths became more common

**After clean air acts:**
- Trees became lighter again
- Light moths became more common again

### Example: Antibiotic Resistance in Bacteria

1. A population of bacteria includes some with resistance genes (from random mutation)

2. Antibiotics are introduced (selection pressure)

3. Non-resistant bacteria die

4. Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce

5. The population becomes dominated by resistant bacteria

This is an example of evolution happening in a short time period.

## 19.3 Sources of Variation

### Genetic Variation

**Mutations**
- Changes in DNA sequence
- Random events
- Most are harmful or neutral
- Some may be beneficial
- Source of new alleles

**Sexual reproduction**
- Meiosis: crossing over and independent assortment
- Random fertilisation: any sperm can fertilise any egg
- Creates new combinations of alleles

### Environmental Variation

Environmental factors can affect phenotype:
- Nutrition affects growth
- Sunlight affects skin colour
- Temperature affects flower colour in some plants

### Combined Effects

Many characteristics show both genetic and environmental influences:
- Height (genes + nutrition)
- Intelligence (genes + environment)

## 19.4 Speciation

### What is a Species?

A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring.

### Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species from existing species.

**Process of speciation:**

1. **Isolation**: A population becomes separated (geographically, behaviourally, or reproductively)

2. **Different environments**: Each group experiences different selection pressures

3. **Different adaptations**: Each group evolves different advantageous characteristics

4. **Genetic differences**: Over time, genetic differences accumulate

5. **Reproductive isolation**: Eventually, the groups can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring

6. **New species**: The groups are now different species

### Types of Isolation

**Geographical isolation** (allopatric speciation):
- Physical barrier separates populations (mountains, rivers, oceans)
- Populations evolve separately
- Cannot interbreed

**Reproductive isolation** (sympatric speciation):
- Populations in same area cannot interbreed
- Different breeding seasons, mating behaviours, or chromosome numbers

### Example: Darwin's Finches

- Finches arrived on the Galápagos Islands
- Different islands had different environments
- Populations became isolated on different islands
- Different selection pressures on each island
- Different beak shapes evolved for different food sources
- Eventually, different species formed

## 19.5 Evidence for Evolution

### Fossil Record

**What are fossils?**
- Preserved remains or traces of organisms from the past
- Found in sedimentary rocks
- Show how organisms have changed over time

**Evidence from fossils:**
- Simple organisms in oldest rocks, more complex in younger rocks
- Show sequence of evolution
- Transitional forms show intermediate stages
- Example: Fossils show evolution of whales from land mammals

### Comparative Anatomy

**Homologous structures:**
- Same basic structure but different functions
- Suggest common ancestry
- Example: Pentadactyl limb (five-fingered) in human hand, bat wing, whale flipper, horse leg

**Analogous structures:**
- Similar function but different structure
- Evolved independently (convergent evolution)
- Example: Bird wing and insect wing

### Comparative Biochemistry

**DNA and protein comparisons:**
- Organisms with more similar DNA/proteins are more closely related
- Cytochrome c protein compared across species
- Humans and chimpanzees: very similar DNA
- Humans and bacteria: very different DNA

### Embryology

- Early embryos of many vertebrates look very similar
- Suggests common ancestry
- Gill slits and tails appear in early human embryos

## 19.6 Evolution in Action

### Examples of Rapid Evolution

**Antibiotic resistance**
- Bacteria evolve resistance quickly
- Happens in years, not millions of years

**Pesticide resistance**
- Insects evolve resistance to pesticides
- Same mechanism as antibiotic resistance

**Metal tolerance in plants**
- Plants near mines evolved tolerance to heavy metals
- Evolution in decades

## 19.7 Extinction

### Definition

Extinction is the permanent loss of a species.

### Causes of Extinction

**Natural causes:**
- Climate change
- Volcanic eruptions
- Asteroid impacts
- New competitors
- New predators
- New diseases

**Human causes:**
- Habitat destruction
- Hunting/poaching
- Pollution
- Climate change (human-caused)
- Introduction of invasive species

### Mass Extinctions

- Several mass extinctions in Earth's history
- Most famous: Cretaceous-Tertiary extinction (65 million years ago)
- Killed dinosaurs and many other species
- Currently experiencing another mass extinction due to human activity

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Saying individuals evolve**: POPULATIONS evolve, not individuals

2. **Forgetting "heritable"**: Natural selection only works on heritable characteristics

3. **Not explaining the sequence**: Always explain variation → selection → reproduction → inheritance

4. **Confusing natural and artificial selection**: Natural = nature selects; Artificial = humans select

5. **Forgetting mutation as source of variation**: Mutations create new alleles
