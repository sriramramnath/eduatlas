## 14.1 What is an Ecosystem?

### Definitions

**Ecosystem**: A biological community of interacting organisms and their physical environment. It includes all the living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components in a defined area.

**Community**: All the populations of different species that live and interact in a particular area at the same time.

**Population**: A group of organisms of the same species living and breeding in a particular area at the same time.

**Habitat**: The place where an organism lives. It provides the organism with food, shelter, and conditions for reproduction.

**Niche**: The role and position that an organism has in its environment, including all its interactions with biotic and abiotic factors.

### Levels of Organisation

The hierarchy of biological organisation from largest to smallest:

**Biosphere** → **Ecosystems** → **Communities** → **Populations** → **Organisms** → **Organ Systems** → **Organs** → **Tissues** → **Cells** → **Organelles**

## 14.2 Biotic and Abiotic Factors

### Biotic Factors (Living)

Biotic factors are all the living components of an ecosystem that affect organisms. These factors involve interactions between living things:

**Competition**
- Organisms compete for limited resources
- **Intraspecific competition**: Between members of the same species
- **Interspecific competition**: Between different species
- Resources competed for: food, water, mates, territory, light (plants)

**Predation**
- Predator hunts, kills, and eats prey
- Creates predator-prey relationships
- Affects population sizes of both species
- Prey populations controlled by predation

**Symbiosis**
Close relationships between different species:

| Type | Relationship | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Mutualism** | Both benefit | Bees and flowers; nitrogen-fixing bacteria and legumes |
| **Commensalism** | One benefits, other unaffected | Barnacles on whales |
| **Parasitism** | One benefits, other harmed | Tapeworms in humans; ticks on dogs |

**Disease**
- Pathogens affect organism health
- Can control population sizes
- Influences competition outcomes

**Food availability**
- Determines population size
- Affects growth and reproduction
- Influences migration patterns

### Abiotic Factors (Non-living)

Abiotic factors are the non-living components that affect living organisms:

**Light Intensity**
- Essential for photosynthesis in plants
- Affects plant distribution and growth
- Influences animal behaviour (diurnal/nocturnal)
- In aquatic ecosystems, decreases with depth

**Temperature**
- Affects enzyme activity in all organisms
- Influences metabolic rates
- Determines distribution of species
- Affects germination and growth in plants

**Water Availability**
- Essential for all life
- Determines type of ecosystem (desert, rainforest, etc.)
- Affects plant transpiration rates
- Influences animal behaviour and distribution

**Soil/Purchase Factors**
- pH affects nutrient availability
- Mineral content affects plant growth
- Soil structure affects water retention
- Humus content provides nutrients

**Oxygen Availability**
- Required for aerobic respiration
- In water: affected by temperature, flow, pollution
- Low oxygen limits aquatic organisms
- Waterlogging affects root respiration

**Carbon Dioxide Concentration**
- Required for photosynthesis
- Affects plant growth rates
- In water: affects aquatic plant growth
- Higher levels can increase photosynthesis rate

**Wind Speed and Direction**
- Affects transpiration rates in plants
- Influences pollination in wind-pollinated plants
- Affects seed dispersal
- Creates wave action in aquatic systems

## 14.3 Feeding Relationships

### Food Chains

A food chain shows the flow of energy from one organism to another. It shows what eats what in a simple linear sequence.

**Structure of a Food Chain:**

```
Producer → Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer → Tertiary Consumer
   ↓              ↓                   ↓                    ↓
 (plant)      (herbivore)         (carnivore)         (top carnivore)
```

**Example:**
```
Grass → Rabbit → Fox → Eagle
```

**Key terms:**

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Producer** | Organism that makes its own food by photosynthesis | Grass, algae |
| **Primary consumer** | Herbivore that eats producers | Rabbit, caterpillar |
| **Secondary consumer** | Carnivore that eats primary consumers | Fox, bird |
| **Tertiary consumer** | Carnivore that eats secondary consumers | Eagle, lion |
| **Apex predator** | Top carnivore with no natural predators | Lion, shark |
| **Herbivore** | Animal that eats only plants | Rabbit, deer |
| **Carnivore** | Animal that eats other animals | Fox, eagle |
| **Omnivore** | Animal that eats both plants and animals | Humans, bears |

### Food Webs

A food web shows the interconnected food chains in an ecosystem. Most organisms eat more than one type of food and are eaten by more than one type of predator.

**Advantages of food webs over food chains:**
- Show more complex feeding relationships
- Indicate alternative food sources
- Show interdependence between species
- More realistic representation of ecosystems

**Interpretation of food webs:**
- Arrows show direction of energy flow
- Organisms may appear at multiple trophic levels
- Removing one species affects many others
- More complex webs are more stable

### Trophic Levels

Trophic levels describe the position of an organism in a food chain:

| Trophic Level | Organisms |
|---------------|-----------|
| 1st | Producers |
| 2nd | Primary consumers |
| 3rd | Secondary consumers |
| 4th | Tertiary consumers |
| 5th+ | Quaternary consumers, apex predators |

**Energy transfer between trophic levels:**
- Only about 10% of energy is transferred between levels
- 90% is lost as heat, movement, undigested material
- This limits the number of trophic levels in a food chain
- Explains why there are few top predators

### Ecological Pyramids

**Pyramid of Numbers**
- Shows the number of organisms at each trophic level
- Can be inverted (e.g., one tree supports many insects)
- Not always pyramid-shaped

**Pyramid of Biomass**
- Shows the total mass of organisms at each trophic level
- Always pyramid-shaped (in healthy ecosystems)
- More accurate than pyramid of numbers
- Measured in grams per unit area

**Pyramid of Energy**
- Shows the energy available at each trophic level
- Always pyramid-shaped
- Most accurate representation
- Measured in Joules or kJ per unit area per time

## 14.4 Energy Flow

### Energy Transfer

Energy flows through ecosystems:

1. **Energy enters**: Light energy from sun captured by producers
2. **Photosynthesis**: Converts light energy to chemical energy (glucose)
3. **Transfer**: Energy passed through food chains
4. **Loss**: Energy lost at each trophic level
5. **Output**: Energy lost as heat through respiration

### Energy Loss

Energy is lost between trophic levels due to:

- **Respiration**: Energy released for metabolism, mostly lost as heat
- **Movement**: Energy used for locomotion
- **Body temperature**: Maintaining body heat (endotherms)
- **Undigested food**: Egested as faeces
- **Excretion**: Energy in urea and other waste products
- **Not all organisms are eaten**: Some die naturally

### Energy Efficiency

- Producers capture only about 1% of solar energy
- About 10% of energy transferred between trophic levels
- 90% lost at each level
- Explains why food chains rarely exceed 4-5 levels
- Explains why herbivores are more common than carnivores

## 14.5 Nutrient Cycles

### The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is essential for life as it forms the backbone of organic molecules. The carbon cycle describes how carbon moves through the environment.

**Carbon stores:**
- Atmosphere: CO₂ (about 0.04%)
- Oceans: Dissolved CO₂ and carbonates
- Fossil fuels: Coal, oil, natural gas
- Living organisms: Carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, DNA
- Dead organic matter: Humus, peat

**Processes in the carbon cycle:**

**1. Photosynthesis**
- Plants absorb CO₂ from atmosphere
- Convert to glucose (organic carbon)
- Releases oxygen
- Equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

**2. Respiration**
- All living organisms respire
- Breaks down glucose to release energy
- Releases CO₂ to atmosphere
- Equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy

**3. Decomposition**
- Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead organic matter
- Release CO₂ through respiration
- Return nutrients to soil

**4. Combustion (Burning)**
- Burning fossil fuels releases stored CO₂
- Burning wood releases CO₂
- Major human impact on carbon cycle
- Increasing atmospheric CO₂

**5. Fossilisation**
- Dead organisms buried without decomposition
- Under heat and pressure, form fossil fuels
- Carbon stored for millions of years

**6. Ocean absorption**
- Oceans absorb CO₂ from atmosphere
- Marine organisms use carbon for shells (calcium carbonate)
- Some carbon sinks to ocean floor as sediment

### The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is essential for making proteins and DNA. Nitrogen gas (N₂) makes up 78% of the atmosphere, but plants and animals cannot use it directly.

**Nitrogen stores:**
- Atmosphere: N₂ gas (78%)
- Soil: Ammonium ions (NH₄⁺), nitrate ions (NO₃⁻)
- Living organisms: Proteins, DNA, ATP
- Dead organic matter

**Processes in the nitrogen cycle:**

**1. Nitrogen Fixation**
- Converting N₂ gas to ammonium compounds
- By nitrogen-fixing bacteria:
  - Rhizobium in root nodules of legumes
  - Azotobacter in soil
- Also by lightning (natural)
- Haber process (industrial)

**2. Nitrification**
- Ammonium compounds converted to nitrates
- By nitrifying bacteria:
  - Nitrosomonas: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ (nitrites)
  - Nitrobacter: NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrates)
- Nitrates absorbed by plant roots

**3. Assimilation**
- Plants absorb nitrates from soil
- Use to make amino acids and proteins
- Animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants

**4. Ammonification**
- Decomposition of proteins from dead organisms
- Proteins → amino acids → ammonium compounds
- By decomposers (bacteria and fungi)

**5. Denitrification**
- Nitrates converted back to N₂ gas
- By denitrifying bacteria (Pseudomonas)
- Occurs in waterlogged, anaerobic soils
- Returns nitrogen to atmosphere

### The Water Cycle

Water is essential for all life and cycles continuously through the environment.

**Processes in the water cycle:**

**1. Evaporation**
- Water changes from liquid to gas (water vapour)
- Heat energy from sun drives evaporation
- Mainly from oceans, lakes, and rivers
- Also from soil and plant surfaces

**2. Transpiration**
- Water loss from plants through stomata
- Water absorbed by roots → transported up xylem → evaporates from leaves
- Significant contributor to atmospheric water vapour

**3. Condensation**
- Water vapour cools and forms liquid water droplets
- Forms clouds
- Occurs when warm air rises and cools

**4. Precipitation**
- Water falls from atmosphere as rain, snow, hail
- Occurs when water droplets become too heavy

**5. Infiltration**
- Water soaks into the ground
- Becomes groundwater
- May enter aquifers (underground water stores)

**6. Run-off**
- Water flows over land surface
- Returns to rivers, lakes, oceans
- Carries nutrients and pollutants

## 14.6 Population Size

### Factors Affecting Population Size

Population size is determined by four factors:

| Factor | Effect on Population |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Births** | Increases population |
| **Deaths** | Decreases population |
| **Immigration** | Organisms entering → increases |
| **Emigration** | Organisms leaving → decreases |

**Population change = (Births + Immigration) - (Deaths + Emigration)**

### Population Growth Curves

**Sigmoid (S-shaped) Growth Curve:**

1. **Lag phase**: Slow growth; few individuals; slow reproduction
2. **Log/exponential phase**: Rapid growth; abundant resources; maximum reproduction
3. **Stationary phase**: Growth slows; carrying capacity reached; birth rate = death rate
4. **Death phase**: May occur if resources depleted

**Carrying Capacity**: The maximum population size that an ecosystem can support sustainably.

### Limiting Factors

Factors that limit population growth:
- Food availability
- Water availability
- Space/territory
- Disease
- Predation
- Competition
- Accumulation of waste

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Drawing arrows wrong in food chains**: Arrows show energy flow TO the consumer, not what is being eaten.

2. **Confusing community and ecosystem**: Community = living only; Ecosystem = living + non-living.

3. **Saying "plants breathe in CO₂"**: Plants absorb CO₂ for photosynthesis; they don't "breathe."

4. **Forgetting bacteria in nutrient cycles**: Always mention the specific bacteria and what they do.

5. **Not explaining why energy is lost**: Describe the specific ways energy is lost between trophic levels.
