# Chapter 13: Reproduction in Plants

## Detailed Notes for Pearson Edexcel IGCSE Biology

---

## 13.1 Types of Reproduction in Plants

### Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction involves only one parent and produces genetically identical offspring (clones). This type of reproduction does not involve the fusion of gametes and results in offspring that are genetically identical to the parent plant.

**Advantages of asexual reproduction:**
- Faster than sexual reproduction
- No need for pollinators or gamete transfer
- Well-adapted plants produce equally well-adapted offspring
- Can colonise new areas quickly
- Requires less energy

**Disadvantages of asexual reproduction:**
- No genetic variation in offspring
- All plants are equally susceptible to diseases or environmental changes
- May lead to overcrowding and competition
- Cannot adapt to changing environments

**Examples of natural asexual reproduction in plants:**
- **Runners (stolons)**: Horizontal stems above ground that produce new plants at nodes (strawberries)
- **Rhizomes**: Horizontal underground stems that produce new shoots (iris, ginger)
- **Tubers**: Swollen underground stems that store food and can produce new plants (potatoes)
- **Bulbs**: Underground stems with fleshy leaves that store food (onions, tulips, daffodils)
- **Corms**: Solid underground stems (crocus, gladioli)
- **Offsets**: Small plants produced at base of parent (succulents)
- **Plantlets**: Miniature plants produced on leaf edges (Kalanchoe)

### Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote, which develops into a new individual. In flowering plants, this occurs through pollination and fertilisation.

**Advantages of sexual reproduction:**
- Produces genetic variation in offspring
- Offspring may be better adapted to changing environments
- Allows natural selection and evolution
- Seeds can disperse to new locations
- Some seeds can remain dormant until conditions are favourable

**Disadvantages of sexual reproduction:**
- Slower than asexual reproduction
- Requires two parents (usually)
- Needs successful pollination
- Energy-consuming (producing flowers, fruits, seeds)
- Offspring may not be as well-adapted as parent

## 13.2 Structure of a Flower

### Parts of a Flower

**Sepals (Calyx)**
- Small, green, leaf-like structures
- Protect the flower bud before it opens
- Collective term: calyx

**Petals (Corolla)**
- Often brightly coloured
- Attract pollinators (insects, birds)
- May produce scent
- May produce nectar guides
- Collective term: corolla

**Stamens (Androecium) - Male Parts**
- **Anther**: Produces and releases pollen grains (contains male gametes)
- **Filament**: Stalk that holds anther in position
- Collective term: androecium

**Carpels/Pistils (Gynoecium) - Female Parts**
- **Stigma**: Receives pollen grains; sticky surface
- **Style**: Connects stigma to ovary; pollen tube grows through this
- **Ovary**: Contains ovules (contains female gametes)
- Collective term: gynoecium

### Types of Flowers

**Complete flowers**: Have all four parts (sepals, petals, stamens, carpels)

**Incomplete flowers**: Missing one or more parts

**Bisexual (hermaphrodite) flowers**: Have both male and female parts

**Unisexual flowers**: Have either male OR female parts
- **Staminate (male) flowers**: Have stamens only
- **Pistillate (female) flowers**: Have carpels only
- Plants with separate male and female flowers on same plant: monoecious (e.g., maize)
- Plants with male and female flowers on different plants: dioecious (e.g., holly)

## 13.3 Pollination

### Definition

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther (male part) to the stigma (female part) of a flower.

### Types of Pollination

**Self-pollination**: Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma of the SAME flower or another flower on the SAME plant

**Cross-pollination**: Transfer of pollen from anther of one plant to stigma of another plant of the SAME SPECIES

### Comparison of Self and Cross Pollination

| Feature | Self-pollination | Cross-pollination |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| Genetic variation | Low | High |
| Genetic material | From one parent | From two parents |
| Pollinators needed | Not always required | Usually required |
| Reliability | More reliable | Less reliable |
| Plant vigour | May decrease over generations | Usually maintained or improved |
| Adaptation to environment | Limited | Better |

### Adaptations for Different Pollination Methods

**Insect-pollinated flowers:**

| Feature | Adaptation |
|---------|------------|
| Large, brightly coloured petals | Attract insects visually |
| Scent | Attracts insects |
| Nectar | Rewards pollinators |
| Nectar guides | Direct insects to nectar |
| Sticky/spiky pollen | Adheres to insect bodies |
| Anthers and stigma inside flower | Contact with insect body |
| Small quantities of pollen | Efficient use of resources |

**Wind-pollinated flowers:**

| Feature | Adaptation |
|---------|------------|
| Small, dull petals (or absent) | No need to attract insects |
| No scent or nectar | Energy not wasted |
| Large quantities of pollen | Higher chance of reaching stigma |
| Light, smooth pollen | Carried easily by wind |
| Large, feathery stigmas | Catch airborne pollen |
| Anthers hanging outside flower | Exposed to wind |
| Produced before leaves | Less obstruction to wind |

### Comparison of Insect and Wind Pollination

| Feature | Insect-pollinated | Wind-pollinated |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Petals | Large, bright, scented | Small, dull, or absent |
| Nectar | Present | Absent |
| Pollen quantity | Small amounts | Large amounts |
| Pollen type | Sticky, spiky | Light, smooth |
| Stigma | Small, inside flower | Large, feathery, exposed |
| Anthers | Inside flower | Exposed to wind |
| Examples | Rose, sunflower, orchid | Grasses, maize, hazel, oak |

## 13.4 Fertilisation

### Process of Fertilisation in Plants

1. **Pollination**: Pollen grain lands on sticky stigma
2. **Recognition**: Stigma recognises compatible pollen
3. **Germination**: Pollen grain absorbs sugars from stigma and germinates
4. **Pollen tube growth**: Pollen tube grows down through style toward ovule
5. **Guidance**: Chemical signals from ovule guide pollen tube
6. **Penetration**: Pollen tube enters ovule through micropyle
7. **Double fertilisation** (in flowering plants):
   - One male nucleus fuses with egg cell to form zygote
   - Second male nucleus fuses with polar nuclei to form endosperm
8. **Seed development**: Zygote develops into embryo; endosperm provides food

### Male Gamete Formation

- Pollen mother cells in anther undergo meiosis
- Produce four haploid microspores
- Each microspore develops into a pollen grain
- Pollen grain contains tube nucleus and generative nucleus
- Generative nucleus divides to form two male gametes

### Female Gamete Formation

- Megaspore mother cell in ovule undergoes meiosis
- Produces four haploid megaspores
- Three degenerate; one develops into embryo sac
- Embryo sac contains:
  - Egg cell (female gamete)
  - Two polar nuclei
  - Antipodal cells
  - Synergid cells

## 13.5 Seed and Fruit Formation

### Seed Development

After fertilisation:

1. **Zygote** develops into embryo
   - Radicle (future root)
   - Plumule (future shoot)
   - Cotyledons (seed leaves)

2. **Endosperm** develops as food reserve
   - In some seeds, absorbed by cotyledons before maturity

3. **Ovule wall** hardens to form **seed coat (testa)**

4. **Ovary wall** develops into **fruit**

5. **Other flower parts** wither and fall off

### Fruit Formation

**True fruits**: Develop from ovary wall
- Examples: Tomato, grape, pea pod

**False fruits**: Develop from other parts
- Apple: Fleshy receptacle
- Strawberry: Fleshy receptacle with achenes (true fruits) on surface

**Types of fruits:**
- **Fleshy fruits**: Attractive to animals for dispersal (berries, drupes)
- **Dry fruits**: May split open (dehiscent) or remain closed (indehiscent)

## 13.6 Seed Structure

### Structure of a Typical Dicot Seed

**Testa (Seed Coat)**
- Hard, protective outer covering
- Protects embryo from damage and desiccation
- May contain chemicals that inhibit germination until conditions are right

**Micropyle**
- Small pore in seed coat
- Where pollen tube entered during fertilisation
- Allows water entry during germination

**Hilum**
- Scar where seed was attached to ovary wall (funicle)
- Point of attachment to fruit

**Cotyledons**
- Seed leaves
- Store food for developing embryo (in non-endospermic seeds)
- May become photosynthetic after germination

**Plumule**
- Embryonic shoot
- Develops into stem and leaves

**Radicle**
- Embryonic root
- First structure to emerge during germination

### Structure of a Monocot Seed (e.g., Maize)

**Endosperm**
- Large food reserve
- Starch-rich tissue
- Surrounds embryo

**Aleurone layer**
- Protein-rich outer layer of endosperm
- Produces enzymes during germination

**Embryo**
- Single cotyledon (scutellum)
- Plumule and radicle
- Coleoptile (protective sheath around plumule)
- Coleorhiza (protective sheath around radicle)

## 13.7 Germination

### Definition

Germination is the process by which a seed develops into a seedling. It begins when the seed absorbs water and ends when the seedling can photosynthesise independently.

### Conditions Required for Germination

**Water**
- Rehydrates the seed
- Activates enzymes
- Swells seed (testa may crack)
- Allows transport of dissolved food

**Oxygen**
- Required for aerobic respiration
- Releases energy for growth
- Particularly important for the developing root

**Suitable Temperature**
- Affects enzyme activity
- Too cold: enzymes work slowly
- Too hot: enzymes denature
- Optimum varies between species

**Light (for some seeds)**
- Some seeds require light to germinate
- Others are inhibited by light
- Many seeds are indifferent to light

### The Process of Germination

1. **Imbibition**: Seed absorbs water through micropyle
2. **Activation**: Enzymes activated; metabolism resumes
3. **Digestion**: Stored food broken down (starch → glucose; proteins → amino acids)
4. **Respiration**: Glucose respired to release energy
5. **Cell division**: Embryo cells divide and grow
6. **Testa rupture**: Seed coat splits
7. **Radicle emergence**: Root grows downward (gravitropism)
8. **Root development**: Root system established
9. **Plumule emergence**: Shoot grows upward
10. **Photosynthesis**: First green leaves appear; plant becomes independent

### Food Utilisation During Germination

**In starchy seeds (e.g., wheat, barley):**
- Amylase breaks down starch to maltose
- Maltase breaks down maltose to glucose
- Glucose used for respiration and building new cells

**In protein-rich seeds:**
- Protease enzymes break down proteins to amino acids
- Amino acids used to build new proteins

**In oily seeds (e.g., sunflower):**
- Lipase breaks down lipids to fatty acids and glycerol
- These are converted to sugars for energy

## 13.8 Seed Dispersal

### Why Seeds Need to be Dispersed

- Reduces competition between parent and offspring
- Colonises new areas
- Avoids overcrowding
- Increases chances of survival
- Spreads species to new locations

### Methods of Seed Dispersal

**Wind Dispersal**

Adaptations:
- Lightweight seeds
- Wings (sycamore, maple, ash)
- Parachutes/hairs (dandelion, thistle, willowherb)
- Censer mechanism (poppy)

Advantages:
- Can travel long distances
- No reliance on animals

Disadvantages:
- Dependent on wind
- Seeds may land in unsuitable places

**Animal Dispersal**

**External (epizoochory):**
- Hooked fruits attach to fur/clothing (burdock, goosegrass)
- Carried until rubbed off

**Internal (endozoochory):**
- Fleshy, attractive fruits (berries, drupes)
- Eaten by animals
- Seeds pass through digestive system
- Deposited with fertiliser (faeces)
- Examples: Blackberry, cherry, tomato

Advantages:
- Seeds deposited with fertiliser
- Directed dispersal to suitable habitats

**Water Dispersal**

Adaptations:
- Buoyant, waterproof fruits
- Fibrous or air-filled coats
- Examples: Coconut, water lily, mangrove

Advantages:
- Can colonise islands and riverbanks
- Long-distance dispersal possible

**Self-Dispersal (Autochory)**

Mechanical methods:
- Explosive mechanisms (explosive dehiscence)
- Tension in drying fruit wall
- Seeds ejected when ripe
- Examples: Pea pods, gorse, violet, squirting cucumber

Advantages:
- No reliance on external agents
- Seeds dispersed away from parent

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Pollination** | Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma |
| **Self-pollination** | Transfer within the same flower or plant |
| **Cross-pollination** | Transfer between different plants of same species |
| **Fertilisation** | Fusion of male and female gametes |
| **Germination** | Development of seed into seedling |
| **Seed dispersal** | Movement of seeds away from parent plant |
| **Asexual reproduction** | Reproduction without gamete fusion |
| **Sexual reproduction** | Reproduction involving gamete fusion |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing pollination and fertilisation**: Pollination is pollen transfer; fertilisation is gamete fusion.

2. **Forgetting that wind-pollinated flowers have no petals or nectar**: They don't need to attract insects.

3. **Not explaining WHY adaptations help**: Always link structure to function.

4. **Saying seeds need "soil" to germinate**: They need water, oxygen, and suitable temperature - soil is not essential initially.

5. **Confusing testa and fruit**: Testa is seed coat; fruit develops from ovary wall.
