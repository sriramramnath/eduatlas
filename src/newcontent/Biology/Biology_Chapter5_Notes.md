## 5.1 The Need for a Circulatory System

### Why Large Organisms Need a Circulatory System

All living cells require a constant supply of oxygen and nutrients, and need to remove waste products such as carbon dioxide. Small organisms like single-celled organisms can meet these needs through simple diffusion because they have a large surface area to volume ratio. However, as organisms increase in size, their surface area to volume ratio decreases, making diffusion alone insufficient to supply all their cells.

Humans and other large animals have evolved a circulatory system to overcome the limitations of diffusion. The circulatory system functions as a transport network that delivers essential substances to all cells and removes waste products efficiently, regardless of how far those cells are from the external environment.

### Features of an Efficient Circulatory System

An efficient circulatory system requires several key components:
- A pump (the heart) to generate pressure and move blood
- Vessels to carry blood to and from all parts of the body
- A transport medium (blood) to carry dissolved substances
- A system that ensures one-way flow of blood

## 5.2 The Human Circulatory System

### Double Circulation

Humans have a **double circulatory system**, meaning blood passes through the heart twice during each complete circuit of the body. This is one of the most important concepts to understand in this chapter and is frequently examined.

**Pulmonary Circulation** (heart → lungs → heart):
- Deoxygenated blood is pumped from the right side of the heart to the lungs
- In the lungs, carbon dioxide is exchanged for oxygen
- Oxygenated blood returns to the left side of the heart

**Systemic Circulation** (heart → body → heart):
- Oxygenated blood is pumped from the left side of the heart to all body tissues
- In the tissues, oxygen is exchanged for carbon dioxide
- Deoxygenated blood returns to the right side of the heart

### Advantages of Double Circulation

The double circulatory system provides significant advantages over single circulation (found in fish):
- Blood pressure is higher in systemic circulation, enabling faster delivery of oxygen to tissues
- The heart can pump blood to the lungs at lower pressure (protecting delicate lung capillaries) and to the body at higher pressure
- Oxygenated blood returning from the lungs gets an additional "boost" from the left ventricle before being sent to the body
- This system supports the high metabolic rate needed by warm-blooded mammals

## 5.3 Structure of the Heart

### External Structure

The human heart is a muscular organ approximately the size of a fist, located in the thoracic cavity slightly to the left of centre. It is surrounded by a protective sac called the pericardium, which contains fluid that reduces friction during heartbeats.

### Internal Structure

The heart is divided into four chambers:

**Right Atrium**
- Receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the vena cava
- Thin-walled as it only pumps blood a short distance to the right ventricle
- Contains the sinoatrial node (pacemaker)

**Right Ventricle**
- Receives blood from the right atrium
- Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery
- Has thicker walls than the atrium but thinner than the left ventricle

**Left Atrium**
- Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via the pulmonary vein
- Thin-walled like the right atrium

**Left Ventricle**
- Receives blood from the left atrium
- Pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body via the aorta
- Has the thickest walls of all chambers (about 3x thicker than right ventricle) because it needs to generate high pressure

### Blood Vessels Connected to the Heart

| Vessel | Carries | From | To |
|--------|---------|------|-----|
| **Vena cava** (superior and inferior) | Deoxygenated blood | Body | Right atrium |
| **Pulmonary artery** | Deoxygenated blood | Right ventricle | Lungs |
| **Pulmonary vein** | Oxygenated blood | Lungs | Left atrium |
| **Aorta** | Oxygenated blood | Left ventricle | Body |

### Valves in the Heart

Valves ensure one-way flow of blood through the heart:

**Atrioventricular valves** (between atria and ventricles):
- **Tricuspid valve** (right side): Three flaps, between right atrium and right ventricle
- **Bicuspid (mitral) valve** (left side): Two flaps, between left atrium and left ventricle

**Semilunar valves** (in arteries leaving the heart):
- **Pulmonary valve**: At the entrance to the pulmonary artery
- **Aortic valve**: At the entrance to the aorta

### How Valves Work

Valves prevent backflow of blood:
1. When pressure in the atrium exceeds pressure in the ventricle, the atrioventricular valves open
2. When the ventricle contracts, pressure increases, forcing the valves closed
3. The tendinous cords (chordae tendineae) attached to the valve flaps prevent them from being pushed back into the atrium
4. Semilunar valves open when ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure and close when arterial pressure exceeds ventricular pressure

## 5.4 The Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle refers to the sequence of events that occurs during one complete heartbeat. It consists of three main phases:

### Phase 1: Atrial Systole (Atria Contract)

1. Both atria contract simultaneously
2. Blood pressure in the atria increases
3. Atrioventricular valves are forced open
4. Blood is pushed into the ventricles
5. The ventricles are relaxed and filling
6. Approximately 70% of ventricular filling occurs passively before atrial contraction

### Phase 2: Ventricular Systole (Ventricles Contract)

1. Both ventricles contract simultaneously
2. Pressure in the ventricles rises rapidly
3. Atrioventricular valves close (creating the first heart sound - "lub")
4. When ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure, semilunar valves open
5. Blood is ejected into the pulmonary artery and aorta
6. Atria are relaxed and filling with blood

### Phase 3: Diastole (Heart Relaxes)

1. Ventricles relax
2. Pressure in the ventricles falls below arterial pressure
3. Semilunar valves close (creating the second heart sound - "dub")
4. Pressure in the ventricles falls below atrial pressure
5. Atrioventricular valves open
6. Blood flows passively from atria into ventricles
7. The cycle then repeats

### Heart Sounds

- **"Lub"**: First heart sound, caused by closure of atrioventricular valves
- **"Dub"**: Second heart sound, caused by closure of semilunar valves

## 5.5 Control of the Heartbeat

### The Pacemaker

The heart is myogenic, meaning it can contract and relax without receiving signals from nerves. The rhythm is controlled by the **sinoatrial node (SAN)**, also known as the pacemaker, located in the wall of the right atrium.

### How the Heartbeat is Controlled

1. The SAN generates electrical impulses (action potentials)
2. These impulses spread across both atria, causing them to contract simultaneously
3. The impulses reach the **atrioventricular node (AVN)** at the top of the septum
4. There is a slight delay at the AVN (0.1 seconds), allowing the atria to fully contract before the ventricles contract
5. The impulse then travels down the **bundle of His** in the septum
6. The impulse spreads through the **Purkyne fibres** in the ventricle walls
7. The ventricles contract from the bottom up, efficiently ejecting blood

### Nervous and Hormonal Control

While the heart beats automatically, its rate can be modified:

**Nervous control**:
- The medulla oblongata in the brain controls heart rate
- Sympathetic nerves increase heart rate (fight or flight response)
- Parasympathetic (vagus) nerves decrease heart rate
- Chemoreceptors in the aorta and carotid arteries detect changes in blood CO₂, O₂, and pH

**Hormonal control**:
- Adrenaline from the adrenal glands increases heart rate and strength of contraction
- This prepares the body for physical activity or stress

## 5.6 Blood Vessels

### Types of Blood Vessels

**Arteries**
Arteries carry blood away from the heart. They have the following structural adaptations:

- **Thick muscular walls**: Allow arteries to withstand high pressure from the pumping heart
- **Elastic fibres**: Allow arteries to stretch and recoil, maintaining blood pressure between heartbeats
- **Smooth muscle**: Can contract or relax to control blood flow (vasoconstriction and vasodilation)
- **Narrow lumen**: Maintains high pressure
- **No valves**: Blood is under high pressure, so backflow is not a problem

**Arterioles**
- Small branches of arteries that lead to capillaries
- Have smooth muscle in their walls to control blood flow to specific areas
- Important in directing blood to where it is needed most

**Capillaries**
Capillaries are tiny blood vessels where exchange of materials between blood and tissues occurs:

- **One cell thick walls**: Very thin walls (endothelium only) for short diffusion distance
- **Very small lumen**: Only one red blood cell can pass at a time, slowing blood for more exchange time
- **Permeable walls**: Allow exchange of materials
- **Form extensive networks**: Provide large surface area for exchange
- **Thin walls**: Allow rapid diffusion of oxygen, carbon dioxide, nutrients, and waste

**Venules**
- Small vessels that collect blood from capillaries
- Join together to form veins

**Veins**
Veins carry blood back to the heart:

- **Thin walls**: Blood pressure is low in veins
- **Large lumen**: Reduces resistance to blood flow
- **Valves**: Prevent backflow of blood (essential because pressure is low)
- **Less elastic tissue and smooth muscle** than arteries

### Comparison of Arteries and Veins

| Feature | Artery | Vein |
|---------|--------|------|
| Direction of flow | Away from heart | Towards heart |
| Wall thickness | Thick | Thin |
| Muscle and elastic tissue | Abundant | Little |
| Lumen size | Small relative to wall | Large relative to wall |
| Valves | Absent | Present |
| Blood pressure | High | Low |
| Pulse | Present | Absent |
| Blood oxygen content | Usually high (except pulmonary artery) | Usually low (except pulmonary vein) |

### Major Blood Vessels

**Arteries:**
- **Aorta**: Carries oxygenated blood from left ventricle to body
- **Pulmonary artery**: Carries deoxygenated blood from right ventricle to lungs
- **Coronary arteries**: Supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood
- **Renal artery**: Carries blood to the kidneys
- **Hepatic artery**: Carries blood to the liver

**Veins:**
- **Vena cava**: Carries deoxygenated blood from body to right atrium
- **Pulmonary vein**: Carries oxygenated blood from lungs to left atrium
- **Renal vein**: Carries blood from the kidneys
- **Hepatic vein**: Carries blood from the liver
- **Hepatic portal vein**: Carries blood from intestines to liver

## 5.7 Composition of Blood

Blood is a tissue consisting of cells suspended in a liquid called plasma. The average adult has approximately 5 litres of blood.

### Plasma

Plasma is the liquid part of blood (about 55% of blood volume). It is mostly water with dissolved substances:

**Transported in plasma:**
- **Carbon dioxide**: As hydrogen carbonate ions (HCO₃⁻)
- **Digested food**: Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, glycerol
- **Urea**: Waste product from protein metabolism
- **Hormones**: Chemical messengers
- **Antibodies**: Defence against pathogens
- **Fibrinogen**: Clotting protein
- **Mineral ions**: Sodium, potassium, calcium, etc.

### Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)

Red blood cells are the most numerous blood cells (about 5 million per mm³ of blood). Their adaptations include:

- **Biconcave disc shape**: Increases surface area for oxygen diffusion
- **No nucleus**: More space for haemoglobin
- **Contains haemoglobin**: The red pigment that carries oxygen
- **Flexible membrane**: Allows cells to squeeze through narrow capillaries
- **Produced in bone marrow**: Have a lifespan of about 120 days

**Haemoglobin and oxygen transport:**
- In the lungs (high O₂): Haemoglobin + Oxygen → Oxyhaemoglobin
- In tissues (low O₂): Oxyhaemoglobin → Haemoglobin + Oxygen
- This reversible reaction allows efficient oxygen loading and unloading

### White Blood Cells (Leucocytes)

White blood cells are part of the immune system (about 4,000-11,000 per mm³ of blood):

**Phagocytes** (neutrophils, monocytes):
- Engulf and digest pathogens (phagocytosis)
- Have a lobed nucleus
- Contain many lysosomes for digesting bacteria

**Lymphocytes**:
- Produce antibodies that destroy pathogens
- Have a large round nucleus
- Responsible for specific immunity

### Platelets (Thrombocytes)

Platelets are cell fragments involved in blood clotting:
- Produced in bone marrow from large cells called megakaryocytes
- About 250,000 per mm³ of blood
- Release chemicals that start the clotting process
- Important for preventing blood loss and infection

## 5.8 Blood Clotting

Blood clotting (coagulation) is a complex process that prevents blood loss when blood vessels are damaged. The process involves:

1. **Vessel damage**: When a blood vessel is cut, the vessel constricts (vasoconstriction) to reduce blood flow
2. **Platelet activation**: Platelets stick to the damaged area and release clotting factors
3. **Clotting cascade**: A series of reactions involving clotting factors
4. **Thrombin formation**: Prothrombin (inactive) is converted to thrombin (active enzyme)
5. **Fibrin formation**: Thrombin catalyses the conversion of fibrinogen (soluble) to fibrin (insoluble)
6. **Clot formation**: Fibrin forms a mesh of fibres that traps blood cells, forming a clot
7. **Scab formation**: The clot dries to form a scab that protects the wound

The clotting process can be remembered as:
**Damaged cells + Platelets → Thrombin → Fibrinogen → Fibrin (mesh) → Clot**

## 5.9 Coronary Heart Disease

### What is Coronary Heart Disease?

Coronary heart disease (CHD) occurs when the coronary arteries that supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood become narrowed or blocked. This reduces oxygen supply to the heart muscle, causing pain and potentially leading to a heart attack.

### Causes and Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Diet high in saturated fats** | Increases blood cholesterol levels |
| **High blood pressure** | Damages artery walls |
| **Smoking** | Carbon monoxide reduces oxygen supply; nicotine damages blood vessels |
| **Lack of exercise** | Reduces cardiovascular fitness |
| **Obesity** | Increases heart workload |
| **Genetic factors** | Family history of heart disease |
| **Age** | Arteries become less flexible with age |
| **Diabetes** | Damages blood vessels |

### Atherosclerosis

Atherosclerosis is the build-up of fatty deposits (atheroma) in artery walls:
1. The endothelium (inner lining) of the artery is damaged
2. Cholesterol and other lipids build up under the endothelium
3. White blood cells accumulate at the site
4. Calcium deposits cause the area to harden (forms plaque)
5. The artery lumen narrows, reducing blood flow
6. Blood clots may form on the plaque surface

### Treatment of Coronary Heart Disease

**Lifestyle changes:**
- Healthy diet (low in saturated fats, high in fibre)
- Regular exercise
- Quitting smoking
- Maintaining healthy weight

**Medications:**
- **Statins**: Lower blood cholesterol levels
- **Aspirin**: Reduces blood clotting
- **Beta-blockers**: Reduce heart rate and blood pressure
- **Nitrates**: Dilate blood vessels

**Surgical procedures:**
- **Coronary angioplasty**: A balloon is inflated to widen the narrowed artery, often with a stent inserted to keep the artery open
- **Coronary bypass surgery**: A blood vessel from elsewhere in the body is used to bypass the blocked section of coronary artery

## 5.10 Capillary Exchange

### Exchange at the Capillaries

Exchange of materials between blood and tissues occurs at the capillaries through several processes:

**Diffusion:**
- Oxygen diffuses from blood to tissues (high to low concentration)
- Carbon dioxide diffuses from tissues to blood
- Glucose and amino acids diffuse from blood to tissues

**Ultrafiltration:**
At the arterial end of capillaries:
- Blood pressure (hydrostatic pressure) is high
- This forces water and small molecules out of the capillary
- Large molecules (proteins, red blood cells) are too big to leave

**Osmosis:**
- Plasma proteins create an osmotic pressure that draws water back into the capillary
- At the venous end, osmotic pressure exceeds blood pressure
- Most of the filtered fluid returns to the capillary
- Excess fluid is collected by lymph vessels

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing pulmonary artery and vein**: Pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood; pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood (opposite to other arteries and veins).

2. **Forgetting to mention "away from" or "towards" the heart**: When defining arteries and veins, always mention the direction relative to the heart.

3. **Saying the left ventricle pumps blood to the left side of the body**: The left ventricle pumps to the ENTIRE body, not just the left side.

4. **Confusing the order of valves**: Tricuspid is on the right; bicuspid is on the left.

5. **Forgetting that coronary arteries supply the heart muscle**: The heart does not get oxygen from the blood inside its chambers.
