## 12.1 Plant Hormones

### Introduction to Plant Hormones

Unlike animals, plants do not have a nervous system. Instead, they coordinate their growth and responses to the environment using chemicals called plant hormones or plant growth substances. These hormones are produced in small quantities in one part of the plant and are transported to other parts where they cause specific responses.

### Characteristics of Plant Hormones

- Produced in small quantities in specific regions
- Transported to target tissues
- Have specific effects at specific concentrations
- Different hormones may work together or antagonistically
- Control growth and responses to environmental stimuli

### Types of Plant Hormones

| Hormone | Main Site of Production | Main Functions |
|---------|------------------------|----------------|
| **Auxins** (e.g., IAA) | Shoot tips, young leaves | Cell elongation; apical dominance; root growth; tropisms |
| **Gibberellins** | Young tissues, developing seeds | Stem elongation; seed germination; flowering |
| **Cytokinins** | Root tips | Cell division; delay leaf ageing |
| **Abscisic acid (ABA)** | Leaves, roots, stems | Closes stomata; seed dormancy; stress response |
| **Ethylene** | Ripening fruits, ageing tissues | Fruit ripening; leaf abscission |

## 12.2 Auxins

### Discovery of Auxins

Auxins were the first plant hormones to be discovered. The most important natural auxin is indole-3-acetic acid (IAA). The discovery came from experiments on phototropism by Darwin and later by Went.

### Functions of Auxins

**1. Cell Elongation**
- Auxins promote cell elongation in shoots
- Works by loosening cell walls (acid growth hypothesis)
- Proton pumps lower pH, activating enzymes that break bonds in cellulose
- Cell wall becomes more flexible
- Cell expands due to turgor pressure

**2. Apical Dominance**
- Auxin produced at shoot tip inhibits growth of side buds
- Ensures plant grows upward toward light
- Removing shoot tip (apex) removes source of auxin
- Side buds begin to grow (bushier plant)

**3. Root Growth**
- Low concentrations promote root growth
- High concentrations inhibit root growth
- Used in rooting powders for plant propagation

**4. Tropisms**
- Involved in phototropism (response to light)
- Involved in gravitropism (response to gravity)
- Causes differential growth by redistributing to one side

## 12.3 Tropisms

### Definition

A tropism is a growth movement of a plant in response to a directional stimulus. The direction of growth is related to the direction of the stimulus.

| Type | Stimulus | Response | Example |
|------|----------|----------|---------|
| **Phototropism** | Light | Growth towards (positive) or away from (negative) light | Shoots grow towards light |
| **Gravitropism** | Gravity | Growth towards (positive) or away from (negative) gravity | Roots grow downwards |
| **Hydrotropism** | Water | Growth towards water | Roots grow towards moisture |
| **Thigmotropism** | Touch | Growth response to contact | Tendrils coil around support |
| **Chemotropism** | Chemicals | Growth towards or away from chemicals | Pollen tubes grow towards ovules |

### Phototropism

**Observation:**
- Shoots grow towards light (positive phototropism)
- Roots grow away from light (negative phototropism, weaker response)

**Explanation:**
1. Light strikes one side of the shoot tip
2. Auxin is produced in the shoot tip
3. Auxin is transported to the shaded side
4. Higher concentration of auxin on shaded side
5. More cell elongation on shaded side
6. Shoot bends towards the light

**Evidence:**
- Darwin's experiments: Showed shoot tip perceives light
- Boysen-Jensen: Showed signal is chemical and passes through gelatine but not mica
- Went: Named the substance "auxin" and showed it causes bending

### Gravitropism (Geotropism)

**Observation:**
- Roots grow downwards (positive gravitropism)
- Shoots grow upwards (negative gravitropism)

**Explanation for Roots:**
1. Root tip detects gravity (statocytes in root cap contain statoliths)
2. Statoliths (starch grains) settle to bottom of cells
3. This triggers redistribution of auxin
4. Auxin moves to lower side of root
5. High auxin concentration INHIBITS root cell elongation
6. Upper side grows faster; root bends downward

**Explanation for Shoots:**
1. Shoot tip detects gravity
2. Auxin moves to lower side
3. High auxin concentration PROMOTES shoot cell elongation
4. Lower side grows faster; shoot bends upward

### Key Difference

The same concentration of auxin has different effects:
- In SHOOTS: High concentration → MORE elongation
- In ROOTS: High concentration → LESS elongation (inhibition)

## 12.4 Practical Investigations

### Investigating Phototropism

**Experiment 1: Unilateral Light**
1. Grow oat seedlings in a box
2. Shine light from one side only
3. Observe bending towards light
4. Control: Light from all directions → straight growth

**Experiment 2: Covering the Tip**
1. Cover shoot tip with aluminium foil cap
2. Expose to unilateral light
3. No bending occurs
4. Shows tip perceives light

**Experiment 3: Covering the Stem**
1. Cover stem below tip with foil
2. Expose to unilateral light
3. Bending still occurs
4. Shows stem doesn't perceive light

**Experiment 4: Cutting off the Tip**
1. Remove shoot tip
2. Expose to unilateral light
3. No bending occurs
4. Shows auxin produced in tip

### Investigating Gravitropism

**Using a Clinostat:**
1. Place seedlings on a slowly rotating clinostat
2. Gravity stimulus is equal on all sides
3. Roots and shoots grow straight
4. Control: Stationary seedlings show normal gravitropism

**Using Petri Dishes:**
1. Germinate seeds in Petri dishes with moist cotton wool
2. Turn dish 90°
3. Observe roots and shoots reorient

## 12.5 Commercial Uses of Plant Hormones

### Rooting Powders (Auxins)

**Use:** To stimulate root growth in stem cuttings
**How it works:** Auxins promote cell division and differentiation at cut end
**Application:**
1. Take cutting from parent plant
2. Dip cut end in rooting powder containing auxin (e.g., IBA)
3. Plant in compost
4. Roots develop more quickly
5. New plant grows

**Advantages:**
- Faster propagation
- Higher success rate
- Produces genetically identical plants (clones)

### Fruit Ripening (Ethylene)

**Use:** To control fruit ripening
**How it works:** Ethylene triggers biochemical changes in fruit
**Effects of ethylene:**
- Breaks down chlorophyll (colour change)
- Softens fruit (breaks down pectin)
- Converts starch to sugars
- Produces characteristic aromas

**Applications:**
- Bananas are shipped unripe (green)
- Ethylene added to ripen before sale
- Controlled ripening for market timing

**Storage:**
- Remove ethylene to delay ripening
- Low temperature and controlled atmosphere extend storage life

### Weedkillers (Synthetic Auxins)

**Use:** Selective weedkillers for broad-leaved weeds
**Examples:** 2,4-D, MCPA
**How they work:**
- Synthetic auxins are absorbed by leaves
- Cause uncontrolled growth in broad-leaved plants
- Narrow-leaved plants (grasses, cereals) are less affected
- Weeds die due to excessive growth

**Advantages:**
- Selective - kills weeds but not crops
- Systemic - moves throughout plant
- Effective at low concentrations

**Disadvantages:**
- May affect non-target plants if sprayed carelessly
- Resistance can develop
- Environmental concerns about chemical use

### Fruit Setting (Auxins and Gibberellins)

**Use:** To produce seedless fruit (parthenocarpy)
**How it works:** Hormones stimulate fruit development without fertilisation
**Application:** Spraying flowers with auxins/gibberellins
**Result:** Seedless tomatoes, cucumbers, grapes

### Delaying Fruit Drop (Auxins)

**Use:** To prevent premature fruit drop in orchards
**How it works:** Auxins prevent formation of abscission layer
**Benefit:** Extends harvest period

### Seed Dormancy Breaking (Gibberellins)

**Use:** To break dormancy in seeds and promote germination
**Application:** Treating seeds with gibberellins
**Useful for:** Seeds that normally need cold treatment or long dormancy

## 12.6 Micropropagation and Tissue Culture

### The Process

Modern horticulture uses plant hormones in micropropagation:

1. **Explant preparation**: Small piece of plant tissue taken (meristem, leaf, stem)
2. **Sterilisation**: Tissue sterilised to remove microorganisms
3. **Culture medium**: Contains nutrients, sucrose, and plant hormones
   - High auxin:cytokinin ratio → root formation
   - High cytokinin:auxin ratio → shoot formation
4. **Incubation**: Grown in sterile conditions with controlled light and temperature
5. **Multiplication**: Explants divided and subcultured
6. **Rooting**: Shoots transferred to rooting medium
7. **Hardening**: Plants gradually acclimatised to outdoor conditions

### Advantages of Micropropagation

- Large numbers of identical plants produced quickly
- Disease-free plants (if meristem used)
- Rare species can be propagated
- Year-round production
- Small space requirements
- Genetic uniformity maintained

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Saying plants have a nervous system**: They do NOT - they use hormones only.

2. **Confusing phototropism and gravitropism**: Photo = light; Gravi = gravity.

3. **Not explaining the role of auxin**: Always mention auxin redistribution causing differential growth.

4. **Forgetting that auxin INHIBITS root growth**: High auxin concentration inhibits root cell elongation.

5. **Not specifying direction of movement**: Always state which side auxin moves to and which side grows faster.
