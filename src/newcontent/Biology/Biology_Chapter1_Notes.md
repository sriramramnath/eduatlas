## 1.1 Characteristics of Living Organisms

### The Seven Life Processes (MRS GREN)

All living organisms carry out seven essential life processes. These processes distinguish living things from non-living matter and are fundamental to understanding biology at the IGCSE level. The acronym **MRS GREN** is commonly used to remember these processes:

| Life Process | Definition | Example |
|--------------|------------|---------|
| **M**ovement | Change in position or place of an organism or part of an organism | Animals moving towards food; plants turning towards light (phototropism) |
| **R**espiration | Chemical reactions in cells that release energy from glucose | Aerobic respiration: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy |
| **S**ensitivity | Ability to detect and respond to changes in the environment | Plant roots growing towards water; pupil dilation in bright light |
| **G**rowth | Permanent increase in size and dry mass | Seedling developing into mature plant; human development from infant to adult |
| **R**eproduction | Processes that make more of the same kind of organism | Asexual reproduction in bacteria; sexual reproduction in mammals |
| **E**xcretion | Removal of waste products of metabolism and substances in excess | Removal of carbon dioxide from lungs; excretion of urea by kidneys |
| **N**utrition | Taking in and utilising nutrients | Photosynthesis in plants; ingestion and digestion in animals |

### Detailed Explanation of Each Life Process

#### Movement

Movement in living organisms occurs at both cellular and organism levels. In animals, movement often involves the action of muscles and skeleton, enabling locomotion. Plants exhibit movement through growth responses called tropisms. For instance, shoots grow towards light (positive phototropism) while roots grow towards gravity (positive gravitropism). At the cellular level, cytoplasmic streaming enables organelles to move within cells, and unicellular organisms like amoeba move using pseudopodia.

#### Respiration

Respiration is NOT the same as breathing. This distinction is crucial for examinations and represents one of the most common misconceptions students hold. Respiration refers to the chemical process of releasing energy from glucose, which occurs in every living cell. There are two main types:

**Aerobic respiration** (with oxygen):
- Word equation: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy
- Chemical equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP
- Occurs in mitochondria
- Releases approximately 38 ATP molecules per glucose molecule

**Anaerobic respiration** (without oxygen):
- In animals: Glucose → Lactic acid + Energy (small amount)
- In yeast: Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Energy
- Releases only 2 ATP molecules per glucose molecule

The energy released during respiration is used for various cellular activities including muscle contraction, active transport, protein synthesis, cell division, and maintaining body temperature in warm-blooded animals.

#### Sensitivity

Living organisms must respond to changes in their environment (stimuli) to survive. This ability is called sensitivity or irritability. Responses can be rapid (like a reflex action) or slow (like plant tropisms). The nervous system and endocrine system work together in animals to coordinate responses to stimuli.

Examples of stimuli include:
- Light (phototropic responses in plants)
- Gravity (gravitropic responses in plant roots and shoots)
- Temperature (behavioural responses like seeking shade)
- Chemicals (chemotropism in pollen tubes; sense of smell in animals)
- Touch (thigmotropism in climbing plants; reflex actions in animals)

#### Growth

Growth involves a permanent increase in size and dry mass. The term "permanent" is important because processes like water absorption cause temporary increases in mass but do not constitute true growth. Growth occurs through cell division, cell enlargement, and cell differentiation. In plants, growth is concentrated at meristems (regions of actively dividing cells), while animal growth is more uniform and typically stops at maturity.

Growth patterns differ between organisms:
- **Indeterminate growth**: Plants continue growing throughout their lives
- **Determinate growth**: Animals grow to a certain size and then stop

#### Reproduction

Reproduction ensures the continuation of a species. There are two main types:

**Sexual reproduction** involves the fusion of gametes (sperm and egg) from two parents, resulting in offspring that are genetically different from both parents. This genetic variation is advantageous for evolution and adaptation.

**Asexual reproduction** involves only one parent and produces genetically identical offspring (clones). While faster and requiring less energy, asexual reproduction does not generate genetic variation.

#### Excretion

Excretion is the removal of waste products of metabolism. This is different from egestion, which is the removal of undigested food from the digestive system. Common excretory products include:

- **Carbon dioxide**: Waste product of aerobic respiration, excreted through lungs
- **Urea**: Waste product from protein metabolism, excreted by kidneys in urine
- **Excess water and salts**: Removed by kidneys and sweat glands

⚠️ **Common Exam Mistake**: Students often confuse excretion with egestion. Remember: Excretion removes metabolic waste (produced by cells), while egestion removes undigested food that never entered cells.

#### Nutrition

Nutrition involves obtaining and processing nutrients for energy and growth. The two main types are:

**Autotrophic nutrition**: Organisms make their own food using simple inorganic materials. Plants use photosynthesis to make glucose from carbon dioxide and water, using light energy. Some bacteria use chemosynthesis, obtaining energy from chemical reactions.

**Heterotrophic nutrition**: Organisms cannot make their own food and must consume other organisms. Animals, fungi, and most bacteria are heterotrophs.

## 1.2 Cell Structure

### Animal Cells

Animal cells are eukaryotic cells with the following structures:

| Structure | Description | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Cell membrane** | Thin, flexible, partially permeable barrier | Controls what enters and leaves the cell |
| **Cytoplasm** | Jelly-like substance containing organelles | Site of many chemical reactions |
| **Nucleus** | Large organelle containing genetic material | Controls cell activities; contains chromosomes |
| **Mitochondria** | Rod-shaped organelles with folded inner membrane | Site of aerobic respiration |
| **Ribosomes** | Tiny structures attached to rough ER or free in cytoplasm | Site of protein synthesis |

### Plant Cells

Plant cells contain all the structures found in animal cells, plus three additional features:

| Additional Structure | Description | Function |
|---------------------|-------------|----------|
| **Cell wall** | Rigid outer layer made of cellulose | Provides structural support; prevents cell from bursting |
| **Chloroplasts** | Green organelles containing chlorophyll | Site of photosynthesis |
| **Permanent vacuole** | Large, central, fluid-filled space | Contains cell sap; maintains cell turgidity |

### Comparing Animal and Plant Cells

```
Structure           | Animal Cell | Plant Cell
--------------------|-------------|------------
Cell membrane       | Present     | Present
Nucleus             | Present     | Present
Cytoplasm           | Present     | Present
Mitochondria        | Present     | Present
Ribosomes           | Present     | Present
Cell wall           | Absent      | Present (cellulose)
Chloroplasts        | Absent      | Present (in green parts)
Permanent vacuole   | Absent/Small| Present (large, central)
```

### Cell Specialisation

Cells become specialised for specific functions through differentiation. Their structure is adapted to maximise efficiency for their role.

#### Examples of Specialised Animal Cells

**Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)**
- Biconcave disc shape: Increases surface area for oxygen diffusion
- No nucleus: More space for haemoglobin
- Flexible membrane: Can squeeze through narrow capillaries
- Contains haemoglobin: Binds and transports oxygen

**Nerve Cells (Neurons)**
- Long axon: Transmits electrical impulses over long distances
- Dendrites: Increased surface area for receiving signals
- Myelin sheath: Insulates axon and speeds up transmission
- Cell body: Contains nucleus and controls cell activities

**Muscle Cells**
- Contain protein fibres that can contract: Enables movement
- Many mitochondria: Provides ATP for contraction
- Can be very long: Allows coordinated contraction

**Sperm Cells**
- Flagellum (tail): Enables swimming towards egg
- Many mitochondria: Provides energy for movement
- Acrosome: Contains enzymes to penetrate egg
- Haploid nucleus: Contains half the chromosomes

#### Examples of Specialised Plant Cells

**Palisade Mesophyll Cells**
- Found in upper leaf layer
- Many chloroplasts: Maximum photosynthesis
- Tall, columnar shape: Maximum light absorption
- Large vacuole: Keeps chloroplasts at edge of cell

**Root Hair Cells**
- Long, thin extension (root hair): Increases surface area for water and mineral absorption
- No chloroplasts: Underground, no light for photosynthesis
- Thin cell wall: Shorter diffusion distance
- Large vacuole: Stores absorbed water

**Guard Cells**
- Found in pairs around stomata
- Unevenly thickened cell walls: Allows controlled opening/closing of stomata
- Contain chloroplasts: Produce ATP for active transport of ions
- Change shape when turgid or flaccid: Controls gas exchange

## 1.3 Movement Across Cell Membranes

### The Cell Membrane Structure

The cell membrane is described as a **fluid mosaic model**. It consists of a phospholipid bilayer with proteins embedded within it. The membrane is partially permeable, meaning it allows some substances to pass through but not others.

Key features:
- Phospholipids form a bilayer with hydrophilic heads facing outwards
- Proteins form channels and carriers for transport
- Cholesterol maintains membrane fluidity
- Carbohydrate chains form the glycocalyx for cell recognition

### Types of Transport

#### Diffusion

**Definition**: The net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient.

Diffusion is a passive process requiring no energy input from the cell. It occurs because particles have kinetic energy and move randomly. Over time, this random movement results in a net movement from high to low concentration until equilibrium is reached.

Factors affecting the rate of diffusion:
- **Concentration gradient**: Steeper gradient = faster diffusion
- **Temperature**: Higher temperature = faster diffusion (more kinetic energy)
- **Surface area**: Larger surface area = faster diffusion
- **Distance**: Shorter distance = faster diffusion
- **Size of particles**: Smaller particles = faster diffusion

Examples in living organisms:
- Oxygen diffusing from alveoli into blood capillaries
- Carbon dioxide diffusing from blood into alveoli
- Oxygen diffusing from leaf air spaces into cells

#### Osmosis

**Definition**: The net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (dilute solution) to a region of lower water potential (concentrated solution) through a partially permeable membrane.

Osmosis is a special case of diffusion involving water molecules. Water potential is a measure of the tendency of water to move. Pure water has the highest water potential (0 kPa). Adding solutes lowers water potential.

**Important terminology**:
- **Hypotonic solution**: Lower solute concentration than cell (water enters cell)
- **Hypertonic solution**: Higher solute concentration than cell (water leaves cell)
- **Isotonic solution**: Same solute concentration as cell (no net water movement)

Effects on animal cells:
- In hypotonic solution: Cell swells and may burst (lysis) - no cell wall
- In hypertonic solution: Cell shrinks (crenation)
- In isotonic solution: Cell remains normal

Effects on plant cells:
- In hypotonic solution: Cell becomes turgid (vacuole fills, cell wall prevents bursting)
- In hypertonic solution: Cell becomes plasmolysed (cell membrane pulls away from cell wall)
- In isotonic solution: Cell becomes flaccid

⚠️ **Exam Tip**: Never say water moves from "weak solution to strong solution" - always use correct terminology (higher to lower water potential).

#### Active Transport

**Definition**: The movement of substances across a cell membrane from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration, against a concentration gradient, using energy from respiration.

Active transport requires:
- Carrier proteins in the cell membrane
- ATP (energy) from cellular respiration
- Specificity (each carrier transports particular molecules)

Examples of active transport:
- Mineral ion uptake by root hair cells (against concentration gradient)
- Glucose absorption in the small intestine
- Nerve cells maintaining ion gradients for impulse transmission
- Kidney tubules reabsorbing useful substances from urine

### Comparing Transport Methods

| Feature | Diffusion | Osmosis | Active Transport |
|---------|-----------|---------|------------------|
| Energy required | No | No | Yes (ATP) |
| Direction | High → Low concentration | High → Low water potential | Low → High concentration |
| Membrane needed | Not essential | Yes (partially permeable) | Yes |
| Carrier proteins | No | No | Yes |
| Examples | Gas exchange in lungs | Water uptake by roots | Mineral ion uptake |

## 1.4 Enzymes

### Definition and Nature of Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts - proteins that speed up chemical reactions in living organisms without being changed themselves. They are crucial for metabolism because they lower the activation energy needed for reactions to occur, allowing them to proceed at the relatively low temperatures found in living organisms.

Key characteristics:
- All enzymes are proteins (made of amino acid chains)
- They are specific (each enzyme catalyses only one type of reaction)
- They are not used up in reactions and can be reused
- They work best at an optimum temperature and pH
- They are affected by inhibitors and substrate concentration

### Enzyme Structure and the Lock and Key Model

Enzymes have a specific three-dimensional shape determined by their amino acid sequence. The **active site** is a region on the enzyme surface where the substrate binds. The **lock and key model** explains enzyme specificity:

- The substrate (key) fits precisely into the active site (lock)
- An enzyme-substrate complex forms
- The reaction occurs
- Products are released, leaving the enzyme unchanged

The more recent **induced fit model** suggests the active site changes shape slightly to fit the substrate more closely, improving the catalytic efficiency.

### Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

#### Temperature

- As temperature increases, enzyme activity increases (more kinetic energy, more collisions)
- At the optimum temperature (typically 37-40°C for human enzymes), activity is maximum
- Above the optimum, the enzyme denatures (active site changes shape)
- Denaturation is usually irreversible

**Graph description**: A bell-shaped curve with peak at optimum temperature. Activity increases gradually at low temperatures, peaks at optimum, then drops sharply above optimum.

#### pH

- Each enzyme has an optimum pH at which it works best
- Enzymes have different optimum pH values depending on their location:
  - Pepsin (stomach): optimum pH 2 (acidic)
  - Salivary amylase (mouth): optimum pH 7 (neutral)
  - Pancreatic enzymes (small intestine): optimum pH 8 (slightly alkaline)
- Deviation from optimum pH causes the enzyme to denature

**Graph description**: Bell-shaped curves, but the position of the peak varies with enzyme type.

#### Substrate Concentration

- As substrate concentration increases, enzyme activity increases
- At high substrate concentrations, all active sites are occupied (saturation)
- Further increases in substrate have no effect (Vmax reached)
- The limiting factor becomes enzyme concentration

#### Enzyme Concentration

- More enzyme molecules mean more active sites available
- If substrate is in excess, reaction rate increases proportionally with enzyme concentration
- If substrate is limited, reaction rate plateaus when all substrate is converted

### Denaturation

Denaturation is the permanent loss of enzyme function due to changes in the three-dimensional structure. When an enzyme denatures:

1. Hydrogen bonds and other weak bonds holding the protein's shape break
2. The active site changes shape
3. The substrate can no longer fit
4. The enzyme can no longer catalyse its reaction

Causes of denaturation:
- High temperature (typically above 40°C for most enzymes)
- Extreme pH values
- Heavy metal ions (e.g., lead, mercury)
- High salt concentrations

⚠️ **Exam Tip**: Never confuse denaturation with enzyme "death" - enzymes are not alive. Also, do not say the enzyme is "destroyed" - it still exists but has changed shape.

### Enzymes in Digestion

Digestive enzymes break down large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules that can be absorbed.

| Enzyme | Produced in | Site of action | Substrate | Products |
|--------|-------------|----------------|-----------|----------|
| **Amylase** | Salivary glands, Pancreas | Mouth, Small intestine | Starch | Maltose |
| **Maltase** | Small intestine | Small intestine | Maltose | Glucose |
| **Protease (pepsin)** | Stomach wall | Stomach | Proteins | Peptides |
| **Protease (trypsin)** | Pancreas | Small intestine | Proteins | Peptides and amino acids |
| **Lipase** | Pancreas | Small intestine | Fats | Fatty acids and glycerol |

### Commercial Uses of Enzymes

Enzymes have numerous applications in industry and everyday life:

**Biological detergents**:
- Contain proteases and lipases
- Break down protein and fat stains
- Work at lower temperatures than traditional detergents (energy-saving)

**Food industry**:
- Proteases in meat tenderisers
- Carbohydrases in making sugar syrups from starch
- Lactase in producing lactose-free milk

**Medical applications**:
- Enzymes in diagnostic tests (e.g., glucose oxidase in blood glucose meters)
- Enzymes to treat certain diseases

## 1.5 Practical Work

### Investigating Diffusion

**Classic experiment**: Diffusion of potassium permanganate crystals in water

1. Add a small crystal of potassium permanganate to a beaker of water
2. Observe the purple colour spreading over time
3. The colour spreads from high concentration (around crystal) to low concentration

This demonstrates diffusion without any energy input.

### Investigating Osmosis

**Using potato cylinders**:

1. Cut identical potato cylinders using a cork borer
2. Measure and record initial mass/length
3. Place cylinders in different concentrations of sugar solution
4. Leave for 30 minutes
5. Remove, blot dry, and measure final mass/length
6. Calculate percentage change in mass

**Expected results**:
- In dilute solutions: Potato gains mass (water enters by osmosis)
- In concentrated solutions: Potato loses mass (water leaves by osmosis)
- At a certain concentration: No change in mass (solution is isotonic)

### Investigating Enzyme Activity

**Effect of temperature on catalase**:

Catalase is an enzyme found in many cells that breaks down hydrogen peroxide into water and oxygen.

1. Cut equal-sized pieces of potato or liver (source of catalase)
2. Place in test tubes at different temperatures (use water baths)
3. Add equal volumes of hydrogen peroxide to each tube
4. Measure oxygen production (height of foam or gas collected in syringe)
5. Plot graph of temperature vs. oxygen produced

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing respiration with breathing**: Respiration is the chemical process in cells; breathing is the mechanical process of moving air in and out of lungs.

2. **Confusing excretion with egestion**: Excretion removes metabolic waste (made by cells); egestion removes undigested food (never entered cells).

3. **Describing osmosis incorrectly**: Water moves from high water potential to low water potential, NOT from "weak to strong solution."

4. **Stating enzymes "die" or are "killed"**: Enzymes are proteins, not living things. They denature (change shape) at high temperatures.

5. **Forgetting to mention the partially permeable membrane in osmosis**: This is essential for the definition.

6. **Saying active transport requires "energy from respiration" but not specifying ATP**: Be precise in your terminology.

7. **Confusing the terms hypertonic, hypotonic, and isotonic**: Remember that these terms compare the solution to the cell, not the cell to the solution.

8. **Drawing the wrong shape for diffusion graphs**: Temperature-enzyme activity graphs are bell-shaped, not straight lines.

## Higher Tier Content

### Carrier Proteins and Active Transport

At higher tier, you need to understand the mechanism of active transport in more detail:

1. Carrier proteins span the cell membrane
2. The substrate binds to the carrier protein on one side
3. ATP provides energy to change the shape of the carrier protein
4. The substrate is released on the other side of the membrane
5. The carrier protein returns to its original shape

This process allows cells to accumulate substances against a concentration gradient, which is essential for:
- Mineral ion uptake in roots
- Glucose absorption in the intestines
- Maintaining ion gradients in nerve cells

### Enzyme Kinetics (Higher Tier)

The Michaelis-Menten model describes enzyme kinetics:

- At low substrate concentrations, reaction rate is proportional to substrate concentration
- At high substrate concentrations, the reaction reaches Vmax (maximum velocity)
- The enzyme is said to be saturated when all active sites are occupied
