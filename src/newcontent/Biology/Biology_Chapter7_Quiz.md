## Part A: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

### Question 1
Which hormone lowers blood glucose concentration?

A) Glucagon  
B) Adrenaline  
C) Insulin  
D) Thyroxine  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Insulin is produced by β-cells in the pancreas and lowers blood glucose by stimulating cells to take up glucose and converting glucose to glycogen in the liver. Glucagon raises blood glucose, adrenaline raises blood glucose as part of the stress response, and thyroxine controls metabolic rate.

---

### Question 2
Type 1 diabetes is caused by:

A) Eating too much sugar  
B) The immune system destroying insulin-producing cells  
C) Cells becoming resistant to insulin  
D) Lack of exercise  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the immune system destroys the β-cells in the pancreas that produce insulin. This means the body cannot produce insulin. Type 2 diabetes involves insulin resistance. Lifestyle factors like diet and exercise are risk factors for Type 2, not causes of Type 1.

---

### Question 3
Which gland is known as the "master gland"?

A) Thyroid  
B) Adrenal  
C) Pituitary  
D) Pancreas  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: The pituitary gland is called the "master gland" because it produces hormones that control many other endocrine glands. It produces TSH (controls thyroid), FSH and LH (control reproductive organs), and other hormones involved in growth and water balance.

---

### Question 4
What effect does adrenaline have on the heart?

A) Decreases heart rate  
B) Has no effect on heart rate  
C) Increases heart rate  
D) Stops the heart temporarily  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Adrenaline increases heart rate and the force of heart contractions. This is part of the "fight or flight" response, ensuring that more blood (carrying oxygen and glucose) is delivered to muscles quickly. This prepares the body for rapid physical activity.

---

### Question 5
Glycogenolysis refers to:

A) The conversion of glucose to glycogen  
B) The breakdown of glycogen to glucose  
C) The production of glucose from amino acids  
D) The breakdown of glucose to release energy  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Glycogenolysis is the breakdown of glycogen to glucose. This occurs in the liver when blood glucose levels are low and is stimulated by glucagon. Glycogenesis is the conversion of glucose to glycogen. Gluconeogenesis is the production of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources.

---

### Question 6
Which cells in the pancreas produce insulin?

A) Alpha cells (α-cells)  
B) Beta cells (β-cells)  
C) Delta cells (δ-cells)  
D) Acinar cells  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Beta cells (β-cells) in the Islets of Langerhans produce insulin. Alpha cells produce glucagon, delta cells produce somatostatin, and acinar cells produce digestive enzymes (exocrine function). Understanding which cells produce which hormone is important for understanding diabetes.

---

### Question 7
What happens to blood glucose levels after a meal containing carbohydrates?

A) They decrease  
B) They stay the same  
C) They increase  
D) They fluctuate randomly  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: After eating a meal containing carbohydrates, glucose is absorbed from the small intestine into the bloodstream, causing blood glucose levels to increase. This rise triggers the release of insulin from the pancreas, which helps to lower blood glucose back to normal levels.

---

### Question 8
Which hormone is produced by the thyroid gland?

A) Adrenaline  
B) Insulin  
C) Thyroxine  
D) Glucagon  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: The thyroid gland produces thyroxine (T₄), which controls the body's metabolic rate. Adrenaline is produced by the adrenal glands, insulin and glucagon are produced by the pancreas. Iodine is essential for thyroxine production.

---

### Question 9
In Type 2 diabetes:

A) No insulin is produced  
B) Cells are resistant to insulin  
C) The pancreas is damaged  
D) Insulin is produced but broken down too quickly  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: In Type 2 diabetes, the pancreas still produces insulin, but the body's cells become resistant to its effects. This means insulin cannot work properly to lower blood glucose. Risk factors include obesity, poor diet, lack of exercise, and genetics.

---

### Question 10
The conversion of glucose to glycogen is called:

A) Glycogenolysis  
B) Glycogenesis  
C) Gluconeogenesis  
D) Glycolysis  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Glycogenesis is the formation of glycogen from glucose. This occurs in the liver and muscles when blood glucose is high and is stimulated by insulin. Glycogenolysis is the reverse process. Gluconeogenesis is making glucose from non-carbohydrate sources.

---

### Question 11
Which of the following is NOT an effect of adrenaline?

A) Increased heart rate  
B) Decreased breathing rate  
C) Dilation of pupils  
D) Release of glucose from the liver  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Adrenaline INCREASES breathing rate, not decreases it. This allows more oxygen to enter the lungs and be delivered to muscles. All the other options are effects of adrenaline as part of the fight or flight response.

---

### Question 12
Negative feedback in blood glucose regulation means:

A) High glucose causes more glucose release  
B) A change triggers responses that reverse the change  
C) Low glucose causes less glucose release  
D) The body ignores changes in glucose levels  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Negative feedback is a control mechanism where a change from the normal level triggers responses that bring the level back to normal. For example, high blood glucose triggers insulin release, which lowers blood glucose back toward normal.

## Part B: True/False Questions

### Statement 1
"Insulin is produced by the adrenal glands."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Insulin is produced by the pancreas, specifically by β-cells in the Islets of Langerhans. The adrenal glands produce hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol. The pancreas has both endocrine functions (producing insulin and glucagon) and exocrine functions (producing digestive enzymes).

---

### Statement 2
"Glucagon raises blood glucose levels."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Glucagon is produced by α-cells in the pancreas when blood glucose is low. It stimulates the liver to break down glycogen into glucose (glycogenolysis) and to produce glucose from amino acids and fats (gluconeogenesis), raising blood glucose levels.

---

### Statement 3
"Type 1 diabetes can be prevented by eating a healthy diet."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Type 1 diabetes cannot be prevented by diet or lifestyle changes. It is an autoimmune condition where the immune system destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. Type 2 diabetes, however, can often be prevented or delayed through healthy diet, regular exercise, and maintaining a healthy weight.

---

### Statement 4
"The liver stores glycogen."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: The liver is the main storage site for glycogen in the body (muscles also store glycogen but mainly for their own use). The liver can store approximately 100g of glycogen. When blood glucose rises, insulin stimulates the liver to convert glucose to glycogen for storage.

---

### Statement 5
"Adrenaline decreases heart rate."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Adrenaline INCREASES heart rate, along with the force of contraction. This is part of the "fight or flight" response, ensuring that muscles receive more oxygen and glucose quickly. Adrenaline prepares the body for rapid physical action.

---

### Statement 6
"Thyroxine controls the body's metabolic rate."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: Thyroxine, produced by the thyroid gland, is the main hormone controlling basal metabolic rate (BMR). It affects how quickly cells respire and use energy. Low thyroxine levels cause a slow metabolism (tiredness, weight gain), while high levels cause a fast metabolism (weight loss, rapid heart rate).

---

### Statement 7
"Insulin directly converts glucose to glycogen."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Insulin does NOT directly convert glucose to glycogen. Insulin is a hormone that STIMULATES cells (particularly liver cells) to take up glucose and convert it to glycogen. The actual conversion is carried out by enzymes within the cells, not by insulin itself.

---

### Statement 8
"The pituitary gland produces hormones that control other glands."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: The pituitary gland produces several hormones that control other endocrine glands. For example, TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) controls the thyroid, FSH and LH control the ovaries and testes, and ACTH controls the adrenal cortex. This is why it's called the "master gland."

---

### Statement 9
"Blood glucose levels remain constant throughout the day."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Blood glucose levels fluctuate throughout the day. They rise after meals (especially those containing carbohydrates) and fall during fasting and exercise. However, homeostatic mechanisms (insulin and glucagon) keep glucose within a narrow range (approximately 4-7 mmol/L before meals).

---

### Statement 10
"Glycogen is stored in the pancreas."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Glycogen is stored mainly in the LIVER and MUSCLES, not the pancreas. The pancreas produces insulin and glucagon to regulate blood glucose and glycogen storage, but it does not store significant amounts of glycogen itself.

---

### Statement 11
"High blood glucose stimulates insulin release."

**Answer: True**

**Explanation**: When blood glucose rises (such as after a meal), β-cells in the pancreas detect this and release insulin. Insulin then stimulates cells to take up glucose and the liver to store glucose as glycogen, bringing blood glucose back toward normal.

---

### Statement 12
"Type 2 diabetes always requires insulin injections."

**Answer: False**

**Correction**: Type 2 diabetes does NOT always require insulin injections. Many people with Type 2 diabetes can manage their condition through diet, exercise, weight loss, and oral medications (like metformin). Insulin injections may be needed in later stages if other treatments become insufficient.

Describe how blood glucose concentration is regulated in the human body.

**Mark Scheme Answer:**

**When blood glucose rises:**
- Detected by β-cells in pancreas [1]

- Detected by α-cells in pancreas [1]

- Liver breaks down glycogen to glucose [1]

- Change in one direction triggers response in opposite direction [1]
- Maintains glucose in narrow range [1]
