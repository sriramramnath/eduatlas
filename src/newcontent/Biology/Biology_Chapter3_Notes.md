## 3.1 The Respiratory System

### Structure of the Respiratory System

The human respiratory system is a complex organ system designed for efficient gas exchange - the process by which oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide is removed. The respiratory system works in close coordination with the circulatory system to deliver oxygen to all body cells and remove carbon dioxide, a waste product of cellular respiration.

The respiratory system consists of several key structures, each with specific adaptations for their function:

**The Nose and Nasal Cavity**

Air enters the respiratory system through the nostrils (external nares) and passes into the nasal cavity. The nasal cavity is lined with a mucous membrane containing goblet cells that secrete mucus and ciliated epithelium with tiny hair-like projections called cilia. The nasal cavity performs several important functions that protect the respiratory system and prepare inhaled air for the lungs:

- **Warming**: Blood vessels in the nasal lining warm the air to body temperature, preventing cold air from damaging delicate lung tissue
- **Moistening**: Mucus adds moisture to the air, preventing the lungs from drying out
- **Filtering**: Nose hairs trap large particles, while mucus traps smaller particles; cilia sweep the mucus towards the throat where it can be swallowed

**The Pharynx (Throat)**

The pharynx is a muscular tube that serves as a passageway for both air and food. It connects the nasal cavity to the larynx (for air) and the oesophagus (for food). The pharynx plays a role in speech as a resonating chamber for sound.

**The Larynx (Voice Box)**

The larynx is located at the top of the trachea and contains the vocal cords - two folds of tissue that vibrate when air passes over them to produce sound. The larynx also contains the epiglottis, a flap of cartilage that closes during swallowing to prevent food from entering the trachea.

**The Trachea (Windpipe)**

The trachea is a tube approximately 10-12 cm long that carries air from the larynx to the bronchi. Its structure is specially adapted for its function:

- **C-shaped cartilage rings**: Provide support to keep the trachea open at all times; the C-shape allows the oesophagus behind it to expand during swallowing
- **Ciliated epithelium**: Lined with cilia that beat in a coordinated wave-like motion
- **Goblet cells**: Secrete mucus to trap dust, bacteria, and other particles
- **Mucus escalator**: The combination of mucus and cilia moves trapped particles upwards towards the throat where they can be coughed up or swallowed

**The Bronchi**

The trachea divides into two bronchi (singular: bronchus), one leading to each lung. Like the trachea, bronchi have cartilage rings (though these are more complete and irregular), ciliated epithelium, and goblet cells. As the bronchi enter the lungs, they branch into smaller tubes called bronchioles.

**The Bronchioles**

Bronchioles are smaller branches of the bronchi that lack cartilage rings. Their walls contain smooth muscle that can contract or relax to control the diameter of the airways. During an asthma attack, these smooth muscles contract excessively, narrowing the airways and making breathing difficult. Bronchioles are lined with smooth muscle and lead to the alveoli.

**The Alveoli (Air Sacs)**

Alveoli are tiny air sacs at the end of the bronchioles where gas exchange occurs. The lungs contain approximately 300-500 million alveoli, providing an enormous surface area for gas exchange (approximately 70-80 m² in adults). The structure of alveoli is highly specialised for efficient gas exchange, and understanding these adaptations is crucial for examination success.

### The Lungs

The lungs are a pair of spongy organs located in the thoracic cavity, protected by the ribcage. They are surrounded by two membranes called pleural membranes, with a fluid-filled pleural cavity between them. This pleural fluid acts as a lubricant, reducing friction during breathing movements and helping the lungs stick to the chest wall.

The right lung has three lobes and is slightly larger than the left lung, which has two lobes to accommodate the heart. The lungs are the primary organs of respiration, containing the alveoli where gas exchange occurs.

## 3.2 Mechanism of Breathing

### The Thorax

The thorax is the upper part of the body, separated from the abdomen by the diaphragm. It contains:
- The lungs
- The heart
- Major blood vessels
- The trachea and bronchi
- The oesophagus

The thorax is bounded by:
- The ribcage (ribs and intercostal muscles)
- The diaphragm below
- The backbone (vertebral column) behind
- The sternum (breastbone) in front

### Breathing Movements

Breathing (ventilation) involves two processes: inspiration (breathing in) and expiration (breathing out). These movements are controlled by the action of two muscle groups: the intercostal muscles and the diaphragm.

**Inspiration (Inhalation)**

Inspiration is the active process of drawing air into the lungs. It requires energy and muscle contraction:

1. The external intercostal muscles contract, pulling the ribs upwards and outwards
2. The diaphragm contracts and flattens, moving downwards
3. These movements increase the volume of the thoracic cavity
4. The pleural membranes are pulled outwards, increasing the volume of the lungs
5. The pressure inside the lungs (intrapulmonary pressure) decreases below atmospheric pressure
6. Air rushes into the lungs down a pressure gradient

The process of inspiration is summarised in the equation:
**Muscle contraction → Increased thoracic volume → Decreased pressure → Air enters**

**Expiration (Exhalation)**

Quiet expiration is mainly a passive process that does not require muscle contraction:

1. The external intercostal muscles relax, allowing the ribs to fall downwards and inwards due to gravity
2. The diaphragm relaxes and returns to its dome shape
3. These movements decrease the volume of the thoracic cavity
4. The lungs are compressed, increasing the pressure inside them
5. Air is forced out of the lungs down a pressure gradient

For forced expiration (such as during exercise or coughing), the internal intercostal muscles contract to pull the ribs down more forcefully, and abdominal muscles contract to push the diaphragm upwards.

### Comparison of Inspiration and Expiration

| Feature | Inspiration | Expiration |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| External intercostal muscles | Contract | Relax |
| Internal intercostal muscles | Relax | Relax (contract for forced expiration) |
| Diaphragm | Contracts and flattens | Relaxes and becomes dome-shaped |
| Ribs | Move up and out | Move down and in |
| Thoracic volume | Increases | Decreases |
| Lung volume | Increases | Decreases |
| Pressure in lungs | Decreases (below atmospheric) | Increases (above atmospheric) |
| Air movement | Into lungs | Out of lungs |
| Energy required? | Yes (active) | No (passive, for quiet breathing) |

## 3.3 Gas Exchange

### The Site of Gas Exchange

Gas exchange occurs in the alveoli. The walls of the alveoli are extremely thin (one cell thick), which minimises the distance that gases must diffuse. Each alveolus is surrounded by a network of blood capillaries, ensuring a rich blood supply for efficient gas exchange.

### Adaptations of Alveoli for Gas Exchange

The alveoli have several structural adaptations that make them highly efficient at gas exchange. Students must be able to explain how each adaptation contributes to efficient diffusion:

**Large Surface Area**
- The lungs contain approximately 300-500 million alveoli
- The total surface area is about 70-80 m² (equivalent to half a tennis court)
- This large area provides more space for gas exchange to occur
- A larger surface area increases the rate of diffusion

**Thin Walls (One Cell Thick)**
- Alveolar walls consist of a single layer of flattened epithelial cells (squamous epithelium)
- Capillary walls are also one cell thick
- This creates a very short diffusion distance
- Gases can diffuse quickly between the air and blood

**Rich Blood Supply**
- Each alveolus is surrounded by a dense network of blood capillaries
- This maintains a steep concentration gradient
- Oxygen is quickly carried away by blood flow, keeping oxygen concentration low in the blood arriving at the alveoli
- Carbon dioxide is continually brought to the lungs, maintaining a high concentration gradient

**Moist Lining**
- The inner surface of alveoli is coated with a thin layer of moisture
- Gases dissolve in this moisture before diffusing
- This is essential because gases can only diffuse across cell membranes when dissolved in liquid

**Good Ventilation**
- Breathing movements constantly refresh the air in the alveoli
- This maintains a high oxygen concentration and low carbon dioxide concentration
- The concentration gradient is maintained for efficient diffusion

### The Process of Gas Exchange

Gas exchange occurs by diffusion - the movement of molecules from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration. The process can be described in detail:

**Oxygen Diffusion:**
1. Inhaled air in the alveoli has a high concentration of oxygen (approximately 21%)
2. Blood arriving at the alveoli (deoxygenated blood) has a low oxygen concentration
3. Oxygen dissolves in the moist lining of the alveoli
4. Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli into the blood capillaries down a concentration gradient
5. Oxygen combines with haemoglobin in red blood cells to form oxyhaemoglobin
6. Oxygenated blood is transported to body tissues

**Carbon Dioxide Diffusion:**
1. Blood arriving at the alveoli has a high concentration of carbon dioxide (waste from respiration)
2. Air in the alveoli has a lower carbon dioxide concentration
3. Carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the alveoli down a concentration gradient
4. Carbon dioxide is exhaled from the lungs

### Gas Exchange Summary

| Gas | Direction of Movement | From | To | Reason |
|-----|----------------------|------|-----|----|
| Oxygen | Into blood | Alveoli (high concentration) | Blood capillaries (low concentration) | Diffusion down concentration gradient |
| Carbon dioxide | Into alveoli | Blood capillaries (high concentration) | Alveoli (low concentration) | Diffusion down concentration gradient |

## 3.4 Composition of Inhaled and Exhaled Air

The air we breathe in (inhaled air) has a different composition from the air we breathe out (exhaled air). Understanding these differences demonstrates that gas exchange is occurring in the lungs.

| Component | Inhaled Air (%) | Exhaled Air (%) | Explanation |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Oxygen | 21 | 16-17 | Some oxygen diffuses into the blood |
| Carbon dioxide | 0.04 | 4 | Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli |
| Nitrogen | 78 | 78 | Nitrogen is not used or produced by the body |
| Water vapour | Variable | Saturated | Moisture is added in the respiratory tract |

**Key points to remember:**
- Exhaled air contains less oxygen than inhaled air because oxygen diffuses into the blood
- Exhaled air contains more carbon dioxide than inhaled air because carbon dioxide diffuses out of the blood
- Exhaled air is always saturated with water vapour due to the moist surfaces of the respiratory tract
- Nitrogen remains unchanged because it is inert and not involved in respiration

## 3.5 The Effects of Smoking

### Harmful Components of Cigarette Smoke

Cigarette smoke contains over 7,000 chemicals, many of which are harmful to the respiratory and circulatory systems. The three most significant components that students need to understand are:

**Tar**

Tar is a sticky brown substance that deposits in the lungs and airways. It causes several harmful effects:

- **Damages cilia**: Tar paralyses and destroys the cilia on the epithelial cells lining the airways. Without functioning cilia, the mucus escalator cannot work effectively.
- **Causes mucus accumulation**: With cilia damaged, mucus cannot be cleared from the airways. Smokers develop a persistent cough to try to clear this mucus ("smoker's cough").
- **Causes chronic bronchitis**: The accumulation of mucus leads to inflammation and infection of the bronchi.
- **Contains carcinogens**: Tar contains at least 70 known carcinogens (cancer-causing chemicals) that can lead to lung cancer.
- **Blocks gas exchange**: Tar deposits can damage alveoli and reduce the surface area available for gas exchange.

**Nicotine**

Nicotine is the addictive drug in tobacco. It affects the body in several ways:

- **Addiction**: Nicotine is highly addictive, making it difficult for smokers to quit.
- **Increases heart rate**: Nicotine stimulates the heart to beat faster, increasing cardiac workload.
- **Constricts blood vessels**: Nicotine causes vasoconstriction (narrowing of blood vessels), increasing blood pressure.
- **Increases risk of blood clots**: Makes blood platelets more sticky, increasing the risk of thrombosis.
- **Reduces oxygen delivery**: Carbon monoxide from smoke binds to haemoglobin, reducing the blood's oxygen-carrying capacity.

**Carbon Monoxide**

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colourless, odourless gas that is particularly dangerous:

- **Binds to haemoglobin**: Carbon monoxide binds to haemoglobin with an affinity approximately 200-300 times greater than oxygen.
- **Forms carboxyhaemoglobin**: When CO binds to haemoglobin, it forms carboxyhaemoglobin, which cannot carry oxygen.
- **Reduces oxygen transport**: This reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, leading to oxygen deprivation in tissues.
- **Causes shortness of breath**: During exercise, smokers may become breathless more quickly because their blood cannot carry sufficient oxygen.
- **Long-term damage**: Chronic exposure to CO can damage blood vessel walls, contributing to atherosclerosis.

### Smoking-Related Diseases

**Lung Cancer**

Lung cancer is one of the most serious consequences of smoking. Carcinogens in tar cause mutations in the DNA of lung cells, leading to uncontrolled cell division. Symptoms include persistent cough, coughing up blood, chest pain, and weight loss. Lung cancer has a poor prognosis because it is often diagnosed at a late stage.

**Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)**

COPD is a progressive disease that makes breathing increasingly difficult. It includes:

- **Chronic bronchitis**: Inflammation of the bronchi with excessive mucus production and persistent cough
- **Emphysema**: Destruction of the alveoli walls, reducing the surface area for gas exchange

In emphysema, the walls between alveoli break down, creating larger air spaces with less surface area. This severely impairs gas exchange, leading to shortness of breath even at rest. The damage is irreversible.

**Cardiovascular Disease**

Smoking is a major risk factor for cardiovascular diseases:

- **Atherosclerosis**: Damage to blood vessel walls leads to fatty deposits (atheroma) building up in arteries
- **Coronary heart disease**: Narrowing of coronary arteries can cause angina (chest pain) or heart attack
- **Stroke**: Blockage of blood vessels supplying the brain
- **Peripheral vascular disease**: Reduced blood flow to limbs, which can lead to gangrene

### Effects of Smoking on the Lungs - Summary Table

| Component | Effect on Respiratory System |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Tar | Damages cilia; causes mucus build-up; contains carcinogens |
| Nicotine | Addictive; increases heart rate and blood pressure |
| Carbon monoxide | Binds to haemoglobin; reduces oxygen transport |

## 3.6 Comparing Breathing in Humans and Other Organisms

### Gas Exchange in Fish

Fish extract oxygen from water using gills. Water contains dissolved oxygen, but at a much lower concentration than air (about 1% compared to 21% in air). Fish have evolved specialised structures to maximise gas exchange in this challenging environment:

**Structure of Fish Gills**
- Fish have four pairs of gills located in the opercular cavity, protected by a bony flap called the operculum
- Each gill consists of many thin filaments, which are covered in tiny folds called lamellae (singular: lamella)
- Lamellae greatly increase the surface area for gas exchange
- The gill filaments are supported by a bony gill arch
- Each lamella contains many blood capillaries for efficient gas exchange

**Adaptations of Gills for Gas Exchange**
- **Large surface area**: Numerous lamellae provide a huge surface area
- **Thin walls**: Lamellae walls are one cell thick for short diffusion distance
- **Rich blood supply**: Dense network of capillaries
- **Counter-current flow**: Blood flows in the opposite direction to water, maintaining a concentration gradient along the entire length of the lamella

**Counter-Current Exchange System**

The counter-current exchange system is a highly efficient adaptation found in fish gills. Blood flows through the lamellae in the opposite direction to the water flowing over them. This means:

- As blood flows through the lamella, it meets water that has a higher oxygen concentration
- This maintains a concentration gradient for diffusion along the entire length of the lamella
- More oxygen can be extracted from the water than if blood and water flowed in the same direction (parallel flow)
- Approximately 80% of oxygen can be extracted from water with counter-current flow, compared to only 50% with parallel flow

**Breathing Mechanism in Fish**
1. The mouth opens and the floor of the mouth is lowered
2. Water enters the mouth cavity
3. The mouth closes and the floor of the mouth is raised
4. This forces water over the gills
5. The operculum opens and water exits
6. Gas exchange occurs as water flows over the gill lamellae

### Gas Exchange in Amphibians

Amphibians like frogs use three methods of gas exchange:

- **Cutaneous respiration**: Oxygen and carbon dioxide diffuse through the moist skin. The skin is highly vascularised (contains many blood vessels) and must remain moist for gas exchange.
- **Buccal respiration**: The lining of the mouth (buccal cavity) is thin and moist, allowing some gas exchange.
- **Lung respiration**: Frogs have simple sac-like lungs. Air is pumped into the lungs by positive pressure breathing - the frog lowers the floor of its mouth to draw air in through the nostrils, then raises it to push air into the lungs.

### Gas Exchange in Insects

Insects have a tracheal system for gas exchange. They do not use blood to transport gases; instead, air is delivered directly to body cells:

**Structure of the Tracheal System**
- **Spiracles**: Small openings on the sides of the body that can open and close
- **Tracheae**: Large tubes that carry air throughout the body; supported by rings of chitin
- **Tracheoles**: Smaller branches that penetrate body tissues, ending close to individual cells

**Mechanism of Gas Exchange**
- Air enters through spiracles
- Oxygen diffuses from tracheoles directly into body cells
- Carbon dioxide diffuses from cells into tracheoles
- Some insects ventilate their tracheal system by rhythmic body movements

### Comparison of Gas Exchange Systems

| Feature | Human (Mammal) | Fish | Insect |
|---------|----------------|------|--------|
| Respiratory surface | Alveoli in lungs | Gill lamellae | Tracheoles |
| Medium | Air | Water | Air |
| Transport of gases | By blood | By blood | Direct to cells (no blood involved) |
| Breathing mechanism | Negative pressure (diaphragm and intercostals) | Mouth pumping and opercular movements | Spiracle opening/closing, body movements |
| Surface area increase | Many alveoli | Many lamellae | Branched tracheoles |
| Moist surface | Yes | Yes (water) | Yes (tracheal fluid) |

## Practical Investigations

### Investigating Breathing

**Measuring Lung Volume**

Lung volume can be estimated using a spirometer or simple methods:

- **Peak flow meter**: Measures the maximum speed of exhalation (peak expiratory flow rate)
- **Balloon method**: Taking a deep breath and exhaling into a balloon, then measuring its circumference

**Measuring Breathing Rate**

Breathing rate can be measured by counting the number of breaths per minute. A breath is one complete cycle of inspiration and expiration. Breathing rate increases during exercise to meet increased oxygen demand.

### Investigating the Effects of Exercise on Breathing

Exercise increases breathing rate and depth due to:
- Increased carbon dioxide concentration in the blood
- Detection by chemoreceptors in the aorta and carotid arteries
- Increased signals from the breathing centre in the medulla oblongata
- Increased contraction of intercostal muscles and diaphragm

**Recovery time**: The time taken for breathing rate to return to normal after exercise. Fitter individuals typically have shorter recovery times.

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing breathing with respiration**: Breathing is mechanical movement of air; respiration is the chemical process in cells releasing energy from glucose.

2. **Saying intercostal muscles "push" the ribs**: Muscles can only contract and pull, never push. External intercostals pull ribs up and out; internal intercostals pull ribs down and in.

3. **Forgetting to mention concentration gradient**: When describing gas exchange, always explain that diffusion occurs down a concentration gradient.

4. **Incorrectly stating that carbon dioxide is "absorbed" by the lungs**: Carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the alveoli, it is not actively absorbed.

5. **Not explaining how adaptations help gas exchange**: When asked about adaptations, you must explain HOW each feature increases the rate of gas exchange, not just list them.

6. **Confusing bronchi and bronchioles**: Bronchi have cartilage rings; bronchioles have smooth muscle but no cartilage.

## Higher Tier Content

### The Role of Surfactant

Alveoli produce a substance called surfactant (surface active agent), which reduces surface tension in the fluid lining the alveoli. Without surfactant, the high surface tension would cause alveoli to collapse during exhalation. Premature babies often have insufficient surfactant, leading to respiratory distress syndrome.

### Control of Breathing Rate

Breathing rate is controlled by the respiratory centre in the medulla oblongata of the brain. Chemoreceptors in the aorta and carotid arteries detect:

- Increased carbon dioxide concentration
- Decreased pH (increased hydrogen ion concentration)
- Decreased oxygen concentration (minor effect)

When CO₂ levels rise, signals are sent to increase breathing rate and depth, removing the excess CO₂ and restoring normal blood gas levels.
