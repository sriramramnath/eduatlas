## 7.1 The Endocrine System

### Introduction to Hormonal Control

The endocrine system provides chemical coordination of the body through hormones. Unlike the rapid, short-lived responses of the nervous system, hormonal responses are generally slower to develop but longer-lasting. This makes the endocrine system particularly suited for regulating processes that need to be maintained over extended periods, such as growth, metabolism, reproduction, and the balance of various substances in the blood.

### Endocrine Glands

Endocrine glands are specialised organs that produce and secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream. Unlike exocrine glands (such as sweat glands and salivary glands) that secrete through ducts, endocrine glands are ductless - they release their secretions directly into the surrounding blood capillaries.

The major endocrine glands in the human body include:

| Gland | Location | Hormones Produced |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| Pituitary gland | Base of brain | Growth hormone, TSH, FSH, LH, ADH, oxytocin |
| Thyroid gland | Neck (below larynx) | Thyroxine, calcitonin |
| Adrenal glands | Above kidneys | Adrenaline, cortisol, aldosterone |
| Pancreas | Behind stomach | Insulin, glucagon |
| Ovaries | Female pelvis | Oestrogen, progesterone |
| Testes | Male scrotum | Testosterone |

### Hormones as Chemical Messengers

Hormones are often described as "chemical messengers" because they carry information from one part of the body to another. Key characteristics of hormones include:

- They are produced by endocrine glands
- They are secreted into the bloodstream
- They travel throughout the body but only affect target organs
- Target organs have specific receptors that recognise and bind to the hormone
- They are effective in very small quantities
- They are often broken down by the liver and excreted by the kidneys
- Their effects can last from minutes to hours or even days

## 7.2 The Pituitary Gland - The Master Gland

### Location and Structure

The pituitary gland is a small, pea-sized structure located at the base of the brain, just below the hypothalamus. It is often called the "master gland" because it produces hormones that control many other endocrine glands.

### Hormones Produced by the Pituitary Gland

**Growth Hormone (GH)**
- Controls growth of bones and muscles
- Promotes protein synthesis
- Deficiency in childhood causes dwarfism
- Excess in childhood causes gigantism
- Excess in adults causes acromegaly

**Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH)**
- Stimulates the thyroid gland to produce thyroxine
- Controlled by negative feedback

**Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH) and Luteinising Hormone (LH)**
- Control reproductive functions
- In females: Control the menstrual cycle and ovulation
- In males: Stimulate sperm production and testosterone production

**Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH)**
- Controls water reabsorption in the kidneys
- Important in osmoregulation

**Oxytocin**
- Stimulates uterine contractions during childbirth
- Stimulates milk ejection during breastfeeding

## 7.3 The Thyroid Gland and Thyroxine

### Location and Structure

The thyroid gland is a butterfly-shaped gland located in the neck, just below the larynx (voice box). It consists of two lobes connected by a narrow band of tissue called the isthmus.

### Thyroxine Production and Function

The thyroid gland produces thyroxine (also called T₄ because it contains four iodine atoms). Iodine is essential for thyroxine synthesis, and dietary iodine deficiency can lead to thyroid problems.

**Functions of thyroxine:**
- Controls the basal metabolic rate (BMR) - the rate at which cells respire
- Regulates protein synthesis
- Important for normal growth and development
- Essential for brain development in children
- Increases sensitivity to adrenaline

### Control of Thyroxine Levels (Negative Feedback)

Thyroxine levels are controlled by a negative feedback mechanism involving the hypothalamus and pituitary gland:

1. When thyroxine levels are LOW:
   - Hypothalamus releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)
   - This stimulates the pituitary to release TSH
   - TSH stimulates the thyroid to produce more thyroxine
   - Thyroxine levels increase

2. When thyroxine levels are HIGH:
   - High thyroxine inhibits release of TRH from hypothalamus
   - High thyroxine inhibits release of TSH from pituitary
   - Less TSH means less stimulation of thyroid
   - Thyroxine levels decrease

This negative feedback maintains thyroxine at relatively constant levels.

### Thyroid Disorders

**Hypothyroidism (Underactive Thyroid)**
- Low thyroxine production
- Symptoms: Tiredness, weight gain, slow heart rate, feeling cold, constipation
- In children: Cretinism (stunted growth, intellectual disability)
- Treatment: Thyroxine replacement tablets

**Hyperthyroidism (Overactive Thyroid)**
- Excess thyroxine production
- Symptoms: Weight loss, rapid heart rate, anxiety, tremor, feeling hot
- Treatment: Medication, radioactive iodine, or surgery

**Goitre**
- Enlarged thyroid gland
- Can be caused by iodine deficiency or autoimmune conditions
- Visible swelling in the neck

## 7.4 The Adrenal Glands and Adrenaline

### Location and Structure

The adrenal glands are paired glands located on top of each kidney. Each adrenal gland has two distinct parts:
- **Adrenal medulla** (inner part): Produces adrenaline
- **Adrenal cortex** (outer part): Produces cortisol and aldosterone

### Adrenaline - The Fight or Flight Hormone

Adrenaline (epinephrine) is a hormone produced by the adrenal medulla in response to stress, fear, excitement, or exercise. It prepares the body for rapid action - the "fight or flight" response.

**Effects of Adrenaline:**

| Target Organ | Effect | Purpose |
|--------------|--------|---------|
| Heart | Increases heart rate and force of contraction | More blood pumped to muscles |
| Blood vessels to muscles | Dilates | More blood to muscles |
| Blood vessels to skin and gut | Constricts | Blood diverted to muscles |
| Bronchioles | Dilates | More oxygen into lungs |
| Liver | Stimulates glycogen breakdown to glucose | More glucose available for energy |
| Pupils | Dilate | Better vision |
| Hairs on skin | Erect ("goosebumps") | In animals, makes them appear larger |
| Mental alertness | Increases | Faster reactions |

These effects prepare the body for rapid physical activity, whether that means fighting a threat or running away from it.

### Adrenaline vs Noradrenaline

Noradrenaline (norepinephrine) is also produced by the adrenal medulla and has similar effects to adrenaline. Both are involved in the stress response, but adrenaline is more important for the acute fight or flight response.

## 7.5 The Pancreas and Blood Glucose Regulation

### The Pancreas as Both Endocrine and Exocrine Gland

The pancreas is unusual because it functions as both an endocrine gland (secreting hormones into blood) and an exocrine gland (secreting digestive enzymes into ducts).

**Exocrine function:**
- Produces pancreatic juice containing digestive enzymes
- Secretes into the duodenum via the pancreatic duct

**Endocrine function:**
- Islets of Langerhans produce hormones insulin and glucagon
- These hormones regulate blood glucose concentration

### Blood Glucose Regulation

Maintaining blood glucose within a narrow range (approximately 4-7 mmol/L before meals) is essential for health. Glucose is the primary fuel for cellular respiration, especially important for the brain.

**Why blood glucose must be controlled:**
- Too high (hyperglycaemia): Damages blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, eyes
- Too low (hypoglycaemia): Brain starved of energy, leading to confusion, unconsciousness, death

### Insulin and Decreasing Blood Glucose

When blood glucose rises (after a meal containing carbohydrates):

1. Detected by β-cells in the Islets of Langerhans
2. β-cells secrete insulin into the bloodstream
3. Insulin travels to target cells (liver, muscle, fat cells)
4. Effects of insulin:
   - Increases uptake of glucose by cells
   - Stimulates conversion of glucose to glycogen (glycogenesis) in liver and muscle
   - Stimulates conversion of glucose to fat
   - Increases use of glucose for respiration
5. Blood glucose levels decrease

### Glucagon and Increasing Blood Glucose

When blood glucose falls (fasting, exercise):

1. Detected by α-cells in the Islets of Langerhans
2. α-cells secrete glucagon into the bloodstream
3. Glucagon travels to target cells (mainly liver)
4. Effects of glucagon:
   - Stimulates breakdown of glycogen to glucose (glycogenolysis)
   - Stimulates production of glucose from amino acids and fats (gluconeogenesis)
5. Blood glucose levels increase

### Negative Feedback Control of Blood Glucose

Blood glucose is regulated by negative feedback:

**When blood glucose rises:**
- Insulin released
- Blood glucose decreases
- Less insulin released

**When blood glucose falls:**
- Glucagon released
- Blood glucose increases
- Less glucagon released

### Role of the Liver in Blood Glucose Regulation

The liver plays a central role in blood glucose regulation:
- Stores glycogen (can store about 100g)
- Converts glucose to glycogen (glycogenesis) when insulin is present
- Breaks down glycogen to glucose (glycogenolysis) when glucagon is present
- Produces new glucose from amino acids and fats (gluconeogenesis)

## 7.6 Diabetes Mellitus

### What is Diabetes?

Diabetes mellitus is a condition where the body cannot properly regulate blood glucose levels. This results in persistent high blood glucose (hyperglycaemia) that can cause serious health complications.

### Type 1 Diabetes

**Cause:**
- Autoimmune condition - the immune system destroys β-cells in the pancreas
- Usually develops in childhood or early adulthood
- No insulin is produced

**Symptoms:**
- Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Weight loss
- Tiredness
- Blurred vision

**Treatment:**
- Insulin injections (several times daily or continuous infusion via pump)
- Regular blood glucose monitoring
- Careful diet and exercise
- Cannot be prevented or cured

### Type 2 Diabetes

**Cause:**
- Cells become resistant to insulin (insulin resistance)
- Pancreas may not produce enough insulin
- Risk factors include obesity, poor diet, lack of exercise, age, genetics
- Increasingly common in younger people due to lifestyle factors

**Symptoms:**
- Similar to Type 1 but may develop more slowly
- Some people have no symptoms initially

**Treatment:**
- Lifestyle changes (diet, exercise, weight loss)
- Oral medications (e.g., metformin)
- May eventually need insulin injections
- Can often be prevented or delayed by healthy lifestyle

### Complications of Diabetes

Both types of diabetes can lead to serious complications if not well controlled:
- Cardiovascular disease
- Kidney damage (nephropathy)
- Nerve damage (neuropathy)
- Eye damage (retinopathy)
- Poor wound healing
- Increased risk of infections

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Hormone** | A chemical messenger produced by an endocrine gland |
| **Endocrine gland** | A ductless gland that secretes hormones into the bloodstream |
| **Target organ** | An organ with receptors for a specific hormone |
| **Negative feedback** | A mechanism where a change triggers responses that reverse the change |
| **Insulin** | Hormone that lowers blood glucose |
| **Glucagon** | Hormone that raises blood glucose |
| **Glycogenesis** | Conversion of glucose to glycogen |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing glycogen and glucose**: Glycogen is the storage form; glucose is the simple sugar.

2. **Saying insulin converts glucose to glycogen**: Insulin STIMULATES this conversion; it does not do it directly.

3. **Forgetting the liver's role**: Always mention the liver when discussing blood glucose regulation.

4. **Not mentioning negative feedback**: Always explain that negative feedback maintains constant conditions.

5. **Confusing Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes**: Type 1 = no insulin; Type 2 = insulin resistance.
