## 8.1 Homeostasis

### Definition and Importance

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a constant internal environment despite changes in external conditions. The internal environment refers to the tissue fluid that surrounds all body cells. For cells to function efficiently, conditions in this tissue fluid must be kept within narrow limits.

### Why Homeostasis is Essential

All enzymes and metabolic processes in cells work best within specific, narrow ranges of conditions. Deviations from these optimal conditions can lead to:
- Reduced enzyme activity
- Disrupted metabolic processes
- Cell damage or death
- Organ failure

### Factors Controlled by Homeostasis

The body maintains relatively constant levels of many important factors:

| Factor | Normal Range | Why Control is Important |
|--------|--------------|-------------------------|
| **Blood glucose** | 4-7 mmol/L | Energy supply; prevents cell damage |
| **Body temperature** | 36.5-37.5°C | Enzyme activity; metabolic rate |
| **Water content** | Balanced input/output | Cell turgor; blood volume |
| **Ion concentration** | Various specific levels | Nerve function; blood pressure |
| **Blood pH** | 7.35-7.45 | Enzyme activity; haemoglobin function |
| **Carbon dioxide** | Low levels | Prevents acidification; pH maintenance |

### Homeostatic Mechanisms

All homeostatic mechanisms involve three key components:

**1. Receptors**
- Detect changes (stimuli) in the internal environment
- Examples: Thermoreceptors detect temperature; osmoreceptors detect water concentration; chemoreceptors detect chemical changes

**2. Coordination centres**
- Process information from receptors
- Examples: Hypothalamus (temperature, water); pancreas (blood glucose)
- Send instructions to effectors

**3. Effectors**
- Produce responses that restore normal conditions
- Examples: Muscles (shivering); glands (sweating); organs (kidneys)

### Negative Feedback

Negative feedback is the main mechanism used in homeostasis:

1. A factor deviates from the normal level (set point)
2. Receptors detect this change
3. Coordination centres process the information
4. Effectors produce responses that reverse the change
5. The factor returns toward normal
6. When normal level is reached, corrective mechanisms are reduced

The term "negative" refers to the fact that the response is opposite to the stimulus - it reverses the change.

## 8.2 Thermoregulation

### Why Temperature Control is Important

Body temperature must be maintained around 37°C because:
- Enzymes work optimally at this temperature
- Lower temperatures slow enzyme activity and metabolic reactions
- Higher temperatures can denature enzymes
- All metabolic processes depend on proper enzyme function

### Sources of Body Heat

**Heat production:**
- Cellular respiration releases heat as a by-product
- Liver metabolism produces significant heat
- Muscle contraction generates heat
- Metabolic reactions in all cells

**Heat loss:**
- Radiation - heat radiates from body to cooler surroundings
- Conduction - direct contact with cooler objects
- Convection - air currents carry heat away
- Evaporation - sweating removes heat

### The Role of the Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus is the body's thermoregulatory centre:
- Located at the base of the brain
- Contains thermoreceptors that detect blood temperature
- Receives impulses from thermoreceptors in the skin
- Processes information and coordinates responses
- Controls both heat production and heat loss mechanisms

### Response to Cold (Hypothermia Prevention)

When body temperature falls below normal:

**Detection:**
- Thermoreceptors in skin detect cold
- Thermoreceptors in hypothalamus detect cooling blood

**Responses to increase heat production and reduce heat loss:**

| Response | Mechanism | Effect |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Vasoconstriction** | Arterioles near skin surface constrict | Less blood flows near skin surface; less heat lost by radiation |
| **Shivering** | Rapid involuntary muscle contractions | Heat generated from increased respiration in muscles |
| **Piloerection** | Hairs stand erect (goosebumps) | Traps insulating layer of air next to skin |
| **Increased metabolic rate** | Thyroid releases more thyroxine | More heat generated from respiration |
| **Behavioural responses** | Curl up, put on more clothes | Reduces exposed surface area; increases insulation |

### Response to Heat (Hyperthermia Prevention)

When body temperature rises above normal:

**Detection:**
- Thermoreceptors in skin detect heat
- Thermoreceptors in hypothalamus detect warming blood

**Responses to increase heat loss and reduce heat production:**

| Response | Mechanism | Effect |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Vasodilation** | Arterioles near skin surface dilate | More blood flows near skin surface; more heat lost by radiation |
| **Sweating** | Sweat glands secrete sweat | Evaporation of sweat removes heat |
| **Decreased metabolic rate** | Less thyroxine released | Less heat generated |
| **Behavioural responses** | Move to shade, remove clothes | Reduces heat absorption; increases heat loss |
| **Panting** (in dogs) | Rapid shallow breathing | Evaporation from tongue and mouth |

### Skin Structure and Thermoregulation

The skin plays a vital role in temperature regulation:

**Epidermis** (outer layer):
- Dead, keratinised cells provide waterproof barrier
- No blood vessels

**Dermis** (inner layer):
- Contains blood vessels (arterioles and capillaries)
- Sweat glands
- Hair follicles
- Sensory receptors
- Erector muscles (pili muscles) attached to hair follicles

### Vasoconstriction and Vasodilation

**Vasoconstriction (cold conditions):**
- Smooth muscle in arteriole walls contracts
- Arterioles narrow
- Blood diverted away from skin surface
- Blood flows through deeper vessels (capillaries bypass)
- Less heat lost from skin surface
- Skin appears pale

**Vasodilation (hot conditions):**
- Smooth muscle in arteriole walls relaxes
- Arterioles widen
- More blood flows near skin surface
- More heat lost by radiation
- Skin appears flushed/red

## 8.3 Osmoregulation

### Definition

Osmoregulation is the control of water and solute concentrations in body fluids. It ensures that cells are surrounded by tissue fluid with the correct water potential.

### Why Water Balance is Important

- Cells need constant water content for metabolic reactions
- Too much water: cells swell (may burst in animal cells)
- Too little water: cells shrink and cannot function
- Blood volume depends on water content
- Blood pressure depends on blood volume

### Water Gain and Loss

**Water gain:**
- Drinking fluids
- Water in food
- Metabolic water (produced during respiration)

**Water loss:**
- Urine (main route)
- Sweating
- Exhaled air (water vapour)
- Faeces

### The Kidneys and Osmoregulation

The kidneys are the main organs of osmoregulation. They:
- Filter blood to remove waste products
- Regulate water content of blood
- Regulate ion concentrations
- Maintain pH of blood

**Kidney structure:**
- Two bean-shaped organs
- Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons
- Nephrons are the functional units of the kidney

### The Nephron

Each nephron consists of:

**1. Bowman's capsule**
- Cup-shaped structure
- Contains the glomerulus (ball of capillaries)
- Site of ultrafiltration

**2. Proximal convoluted tubule**
- Coiled tube near Bowman's capsule
- Selective reabsorption of useful substances

**3. Loop of Henle**
- U-shaped loop descending into medulla
- Water and ion reabsorption

**4. Distal convoluted tubule**
- Coiled tube further from capsule
- Fine-tuning of water and ion balance

**5. Collecting duct**
- Receives fluid from several nephrons
- Final water reabsorption
- Leads to renal pelvis

### Formation of Urine

**Stage 1: Ultrafiltration**
- Blood enters glomerulus at high pressure
- Small molecules filtered into Bowman's capsule:
  - Water, glucose, urea, salts, amino acids, vitamins
- Large molecules remain in blood:
  - Proteins, red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets
- Filtrate has same concentration as blood plasma (without proteins)

**Stage 2: Selective Reabsorption**
- Proximal convoluted tubule:
  - All glucose reabsorbed (active transport)
  - Most amino acids reabsorbed
  - Some salts reabsorbed
  - Water follows by osmosis
- Loop of Henle:
  - Water reabsorbed from descending limb
  - Salts pumped out of ascending limb
- Distal convoluted tubule:
  - Fine-tuning of salt and water balance

**Stage 3: Tubular Secretion**
- Additional substances secreted from blood into tubule
- Including some drugs and hydrogen ions

**Stage 4: Excretion**
- Remaining fluid (urine) passes to collecting duct
- More water may be reabsorbed (controlled by ADH)
- Urine passes to renal pelvis, ureter, bladder

### Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH)

ADH controls water reabsorption in the kidneys:

**Produced by:** Hypothalamus (stored and released by pituitary gland)

**Target:** Collecting ducts of nephrons

**Mechanism:**
1. When blood water content is LOW:
   - Osmoreceptors in hypothalamus detect increased blood concentration
   - Pituitary gland releases more ADH
   - ADH makes collecting duct walls more permeable to water
   - More water reabsorbed into blood
   - Small volume of concentrated urine produced

2. When blood water content is HIGH:
   - Osmoreceptors detect decreased blood concentration
   - Less ADH released
   - Collecting duct walls less permeable to water
   - Less water reabsorbed
   - Large volume of dilute urine produced

## 8.4 Excretion

### Definition

Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products from the body. These are waste substances produced by cells during metabolism. This is different from egestion, which removes undigested food.

### Metabolic Waste Products

| Waste Product | Source | Toxicity | Excretory Route |
|---------------|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **Carbon dioxide** | Cellular respiration | Forms carbonic acid; lowers blood pH | Lungs (exhaled) |
| **Urea** | Breakdown of excess amino acids (deamination) | Toxic at high concentrations | Kidneys (urine) |
| **Excess water** | Metabolism; diet | Dilutes blood; affects cell function | Kidneys; sweat; lungs |
| **Excess salts** | Diet | Affects blood pressure and cell function | Kidneys; sweat |

### The Liver's Role in Excretion

**Deamination of Amino Acids:**
- Excess amino acids cannot be stored
- In the liver, amino acids are deaminated:
  - Amino group (NH₂) removed
  - Converted to ammonia (highly toxic)
  - Ammonia converted to urea (less toxic)
  - Urea transported to kidneys in blood
- The remaining carbon skeleton can be used for energy or converted to carbohydrate/fat

### Kidney Function

**Functions of the kidneys:**
1. Excretion of urea and other nitrogenous wastes
2. Osmoregulation (water balance)
3. Regulation of ion concentrations
4. Regulation of blood pH
5. Regulation of blood pressure (via renin production)

### Composition of Urine

Normal urine contains:
- Water (about 95%)
- Urea
- Salts (sodium, potassium, chloride)
- Uric acid
- Creatinine
- Small amounts of other substances

**Substances NOT normally found in urine:**
- Glucose (presence indicates diabetes)
- Protein (presence indicates kidney damage)
- Blood cells (presence indicates infection or injury)

## 8.5 Kidney Failure and Treatment

### Causes of Kidney Failure

- Diabetes (Type 1 and Type 2)
- High blood pressure
- Infections
- Autoimmune diseases
- Genetic conditions
- Toxic substances

### Consequences of Kidney Failure

- Build-up of urea and other toxins in blood
- Fluid retention (oedema)
- Electrolyte imbalances
- High blood pressure
- Anaemia (kidneys produce erythropoietin)
- Death if untreated

### Treatment Options

**1. Dialysis**
- Machine filters blood artificially
- Two types: Haemodialysis and Peritoneal dialysis
- Requires regular sessions (several hours, several times per week)
- Does not cure kidney failure

**Haemodialysis:**
- Blood removed from body and passed through dialysis machine
- Blood flows through dialysis tubing surrounded by dialysis fluid
- Small molecules diffuse between blood and dialysis fluid
- Dialysis fluid contains correct concentrations of glucose, salts, and other substances
- Waste products diffuse into dialysis fluid and are removed
- Clean blood returned to body

**2. Kidney Transplant**
- Healthy kidney from donor implanted into patient
- Can restore normal kidney function
- Patient must take immunosuppressant drugs for life
- Better quality of life than dialysis
- Limited availability of donor kidneys
- Risk of rejection

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Homeostasis** | Maintenance of a constant internal environment |
| **Negative feedback** | A mechanism where a change triggers responses that reverse the change |
| **Thermoregulation** | The control of body temperature |
| **Vasoconstriction** | Narrowing of blood vessels to reduce heat loss |
| **Vasodilation** | Widening of blood vessels to increase heat loss |
| **Osmoregulation** | Control of water and solute concentrations in body fluids |
| **Excretion** | Removal of metabolic waste products from the body |
| **Deamination** | Removal of the amino group from amino acids |
| **Ultrafiltration** | Filtration of small molecules from blood into the nephron |
| **Selective reabsorption** | Reabsorption of useful substances from filtrate back into blood |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing excretion with egestion**: Excretion removes metabolic waste; egestion removes undigested food.

2. **Saying ADH is produced by the pituitary gland**: ADH is produced by the hypothalamus and released by the pituitary.

3. **Forgetting to mention "negative feedback"**: Always explain that homeostatic mechanisms use negative feedback.

4. **Confusing vasoconstriction and vasodilation**: Constriction = vessels get narrower; dilation = vessels get wider.

5. **Not explaining how responses help**: Always link responses to their effect on temperature or water balance.
