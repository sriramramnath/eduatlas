## 17.1 The Cell Cycle

### Overview

The cell cycle is the series of events that takes place in a cell leading to its division and duplication. It is essential for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in organisms.

### Stages of the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of three main phases:

**1. Interphase**
This is the longest phase of the cell cycle, during which the cell prepares for division:
- **G1 (Gap 1)**: Cell grows, synthesises proteins and organelles
- **S (Synthesis)**: DNA replicates - each chromosome duplicates
- **G2 (Gap 2)**: Cell continues to grow and prepares for mitosis

**2. Mitosis (Nuclear Division)**
The nucleus divides, ensuring each daughter cell receives an identical copy of the genetic material. This phase is relatively short compared to interphase.

**3. Cytokinesis (Cell Division)**
The cytoplasm divides, forming two separate daughter cells. In animal cells, the cell membrane pinches inward; in plant cells, a cell plate forms.

## 17.2 Mitosis

### Definition

Mitosis is a type of cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell. Each daughter cell has the same number and type of chromosomes as the parent cell.

### Why Mitosis is Important

- **Growth**: Produces new cells for growth of multicellular organisms
- **Repair**: Replaces damaged or dead cells
- **Asexual reproduction**: Produces genetically identical offspring in some organisms
- **Maintenance**: Maintains chromosome number across generations of cells

### Stages of Mitosis

**Prophase**
- Chromosomes become visible (condense and shorten)
- Chromosomes are now made of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere
- Nuclear envelope breaks down and disappears
- Nucleolus disappears
- Centrioles move to opposite poles (in animal cells)
- Spindle fibres begin to form from centrioles

**Metaphase**
- Chromosomes line up at the cell equator (middle of cell)
- Each chromosome is attached to spindle fibres at its centromere
- This is called the metaphase plate
- Chromosomes are most easily visible at this stage

**Anaphase**
- Spindle fibres contract and shorten
- Sister chromatids separate at the centromere
- Each chromatid becomes an individual chromosome
- Chromatids are pulled to opposite poles of the cell
- V-shaped chromosomes as they are pulled by centromeres

**Telophase**
- Chromatids reach opposite poles
- Chromosomes begin to uncoil and lengthen
- Nuclear envelope reforms around each group of chromosomes
- Nucleolus reappears in each nucleus
- Spindle fibres break down and disappear

### Cytokinesis

After mitosis, the cytoplasm divides:

**In Animal Cells:**
- Cell membrane pinches inward from the outside
- Forms a cleavage furrow
- Cytoplasm divides, forming two separate cells

**In Plant Cells:**
- A cell plate forms at the equator
- New cell membrane and cell wall form
- Cytoplasm divides, forming two separate cells

### Remember: PMAT

**P**rophase - chromosomes appear  
**M**etaphase - middle  
**A**naphase - apart  
**T**elophase - two new nuclei

## 17.3 Chromosome Numbers

### Diploid and Haploid

**Diploid (2n)**: Cells containing two complete sets of chromosomes - one from each parent. Body cells (somatic cells) are diploid.

**Haploid (n)**: Cells containing one complete set of chromosomes. Gametes (sex cells) are haploid.

| Cell Type | Human Chromosome Number |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Body cells (somatic) | 46 (23 pairs) - Diploid |
| Gametes (sperm, egg) | 23 - Haploid |
| Zygote | 46 (23 pairs) - Diploid |

### Importance of Maintaining Chromosome Number

- Each species has a characteristic chromosome number
- Mitosis maintains the diploid number in body cells
- If chromosome number changed, cells would not function correctly
- Genetic stability is essential for normal development and function

## 17.4 Stem Cells

### Definition

Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that can divide repeatedly and differentiate into specialised cell types.

### Types of Stem Cells

**Embryonic Stem Cells**
- Found in early embryos
- Can differentiate into ANY type of cell (totipotent or pluripotent)
- Used in research and potential therapies

**Adult Stem Cells**
- Found in adult tissues (bone marrow, skin, brain)
- Can only differentiate into limited cell types (multipotent)
- Used for repair and replacement of cells

### Properties of Stem Cells

- **Self-renewal**: Can divide many times while remaining undifferentiated
- **Potency**: Can differentiate into specialised cell types
- **Unspecialised**: Do not have a specific function until differentiated

### Uses of Stem Cells

**Medical treatments:**
- Bone marrow transplants for leukaemia (adult stem cells)
- Treating blood disorders
- Potential treatment for: Parkinson's disease, spinal cord injuries, diabetes, heart disease

**Research:**
- Studying development and disease
- Drug testing
- Understanding cell differentiation

### Ethical Issues

**Arguments FOR stem cell research:**
- Potential to cure serious diseases
- Improve quality of life for patients
- Use embryos that would otherwise be discarded from IVF
- Adult stem cells can be used with fewer ethical concerns

**Arguments AGAINST embryonic stem cell research:**
- Embryos have the potential for human life
- Destroying embryos is morally wrong
- Adult stem cells could be used instead
- Religious objections

## 17.5 Cancer

### What is Cancer?

Cancer is a disease caused by uncontrolled cell division. Normal cells divide in a controlled manner, but cancer cells divide rapidly and uncontrollably, forming tumours.

### Types of Tumours

**Benign Tumours**
- Grow slowly
- Do not spread to other parts of body
- Have a covering (capsule)
- Usually not life-threatening
- Examples: Warts, uterine fibroids

**Malignant Tumours**
- Grow rapidly
- Spread to other parts of body (metastasis)
- Not enclosed - cells break away
- Life-threatening
- Example: Cancer

### How Cancer Develops

1. **Mutation**: Change in DNA affects genes controlling cell division
2. **Uncontrolled growth**: Cell divides repeatedly without control
3. **Tumour formation**: Mass of abnormal cells forms
4. **Metastasis** (malignant): Cells break away and spread

### Causes of Cancer (Carcinogens)

- **Chemicals**: Tobacco smoke, asbestos, certain chemicals
- **Radiation**: UV light, X-rays, radioactive materials
- **Viruses**: Some viruses can cause cancer (e.g., HPV, hepatitis B)
- **Genetic factors**: Some cancers have inherited risk
- **Age**: Risk increases with age due to accumulated mutations

### Cancer Treatments

- **Surgery**: Remove tumour
- **Chemotherapy**: Drugs that kill rapidly dividing cells
- **Radiotherapy**: Radiation to kill cancer cells
- **Immunotherapy**: Boost the body's immune system

## 17.6 Meiosis

### Definition

Meiosis is a type of cell division that produces four genetically different haploid daughter cells (gametes) from one diploid parent cell.

### Why Meiosis is Important

- Produces gametes (sperm and egg cells) for sexual reproduction
- Reduces chromosome number from diploid to haploid
- Creates genetic variation in offspring
- Ensures offspring have correct chromosome number

### Where Meiosis Occurs

- In animals: In reproductive organs (testes and ovaries)
- In plants: In flowers (in anthers and ovaries)
- In fungi: In sporangia

### Stages of Meiosis

Meiosis involves TWO divisions:

**Meiosis I (Reduction Division)**
- Separates homologous chromosome pairs
- Reduces chromosome number from diploid to haploid

**Prophase I**
- Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis)
- Crossing over occurs between non-sister chromatids
- Genetic material exchanged between chromosomes

**Metaphase I**
- Homologous chromosome pairs line up at equator
- Independent assortment - pairs arranged randomly

**Anaphase I**
- Homologous chromosomes pulled to opposite poles
- Chromatids stay together

**Telophase I**
- Two haploid nuclei form
- Cell divides into two cells

**Meiosis II**
- Similar to mitosis
- Separates sister chromatids
- Produces four haploid daughter cells

### Key Differences from Mitosis

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Divisions | One | Two |
| Daughter cells | Two | Four |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent (diploid) | Half (haploid) |
| Genetic variation | No | Yes (crossing over, independent assortment) |
| Function | Growth, repair | Gamete formation |

---

## 17.7 Sources of Genetic Variation in Meiosis

### Crossing Over

During Prophase I:
1. Homologous chromosomes pair up
2. Non-sister chromatids exchange segments
3. New combinations of alleles created
4. Genetic variation increases

### Independent Assortment

During Metaphase I:
1. Homologous chromosome pairs arrange randomly
2. Each pair aligns independently of others
3. Many possible combinations of chromosomes
4. Formula: 2ⁿ possible combinations (n = number of chromosome pairs)

### Random Fertilisation

- Any sperm can fertilise any egg
- Creates even more genetic variation
- In humans: 2²³ × 2²³ possible zygote combinations

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing mitosis and meiosis**: Mitosis = 2 identical cells; Meiosis = 4 different gametes

2. **Forgetting cytokinesis**: Cell division is not complete until the cytoplasm divides

3. **Wrong chromosome numbers**: Body cells = diploid (46); Gametes = haploid (23)

4. **Not explaining WHY mitosis is important**: Growth, repair, asexual reproduction

5. **Confusing chromatids and chromosomes**: Before replication = chromosome; After replication = two chromatids joined at centromere
