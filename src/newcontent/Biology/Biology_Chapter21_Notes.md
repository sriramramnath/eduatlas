## 21.1 What is Genetic Modification?

### Definition

Genetic modification (genetic engineering) is the process of altering an organism's DNA to introduce desirable characteristics. This involves taking a gene from one organism and inserting it into another organism.

### Comparison with Selective Breeding

| Feature | Selective Breeding | Genetic Engineering |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Method | Traditional breeding | Direct DNA manipulation |
| Time | Many generations | Relatively quick |
| Species barrier | Same species only | Can cross species barrier |
| Precision | Less precise | Very precise |
| Controversy | Less controversial | More controversial |

## 21.2 The Process of Genetic Engineering

### Steps in Genetic Engineering

**Step 1: Identify the desired gene**
- Find an organism that has the desired characteristic
- Identify the specific gene responsible for this characteristic

**Step 2: Isolate the gene**
- Cut the gene from the DNA using restriction enzymes
- Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific sequences
- Produces "sticky ends" that can be joined to other DNA

**Step 3: Insert gene into a vector**
- A vector is needed to carry the gene into the target cell
- Common vectors include plasmids (circular DNA from bacteria) and viruses
- The gene is inserted into the plasmid using DNA ligase enzyme

**Step 4: Insert the vector into the host organism**
- The vector carries the gene into the target cell
- For bacteria: heat shock or electroporation
- For plants: bacterial infection or gene gun
- For animals: viral vectors or microinjection

**Step 5: Replicate the modified organism**
- The transformed cell divides by mitosis
- All daughter cells contain the new gene
- A whole organism develops from the cell

## 21.3 Examples of Genetic Modification

### Bacteria and Insulin Production

**Problem**: People with Type 1 diabetes need insulin. Previously, insulin was extracted from pigs and cows.

**Solution**: Genetically modified bacteria produce human insulin.

**Process**:
1. Human gene for insulin production identified
2. Gene cut out using restriction enzymes
3. Gene inserted into plasmid using DNA ligase
5. Plasmid inserted into bacteria
6. Bacteria reproduce, producing human insulin
7. Insulin harvested and purified

**Advantages**:
- Unlimited supply
- Exactly matches human insulin
- No risk of disease transfer
- No ethical concerns about using animal products

### Genetically Modified Crops (GM Crops)

**Golden Rice**
- Modified to produce beta-carotene (vitamin A precursor)
- Vitamin A deficiency causes blindness in developing countries
- Golden rice could help prevent vitamin A deficiency

**Bt Crops** (Bt cotton, Bt maize)
- Gene from Bacillus thuringiensis bacteria inserted
- Produces protein toxic to insect pests
- Reduces need for pesticide spraying

**Herbicide-resistant crops**
- Gene for herbicide resistance inserted
- Farmers can spray herbicide to kill weeds without harming crop
- Examples: Roundup Ready soybeans

**Disease-resistant crops**
- Genes for disease resistance inserted
- Reduces crop losses
- Reduces pesticide use

### Other Examples

**Genetically modified bacteria**:
- Produce enzymes for food industry
- Clean up oil spills
- Produce medicines

**GM animals**:
- Goats that produce medicine in their milk
- Salmon that grow faster

## 21.4 Advantages of Genetic Engineering

### Agricultural Benefits
- Higher crop yields
- Reduced pesticide use
- Crops can grow in harsh conditions
- Enhanced nutritional content

### Medical Benefits
- Production of human insulin, growth hormone, antibodies
- Gene therapy for genetic disorders
- Production of vaccines
- Research into diseases

### Environmental Benefits
- Reduced pesticide use
- Less need for farmland due to higher yields
- Crops that can fix nitrogen (reducing fertiliser need)

### Economic Benefits
- Lower food costs
- New products and industries
- Reduced agricultural inputs

## 21.5 Concerns about Genetic Engineering

### Environmental Concerns

**Gene transfer to wild relatives**
- GM plants could cross-breed with wild plants
- Could create "super weeds"
- Could affect biodiversity

**Impact on ecosystems**
- Effect on non-target organisms
- Bt crops might harm beneficial insects
- Food chain disruption

**Loss of biodiversity**
- Monoculture reduces genetic diversity
- Traditional varieties may be lost

### Health Concerns

**Allergies**
- New proteins could cause allergic reactions
- Difficult to predict

**Antibiotic resistance**
- Marker genes used in process could transfer to bacteria
- Could contribute to antibiotic resistance

**Unknown long-term effects**
- GM foods relatively new
- Long-term effects not fully understood

### Ethical Concerns

**Playing God**
- Some believe humans shouldn't modify organisms
- Religious objections

**Animal welfare**
- Concerns about GM animals
- Animals as "bioreactors"

**Patenting life**
- Companies patenting GM organisms
- Farmers dependent on seed companies
- Traditional knowledge exploited

### Social Concerns

**Food security**
- Who controls GM technology?
- Dependence on large companies
- Impact on small farmers

**Labelling**
- Should GM foods be labelled?
- Consumer choice
- Public perception

## 21.6 Gene Therapy

### What is Gene Therapy?

Gene therapy involves inserting healthy genes into cells to treat genetic disorders.

### Types of Gene Therapy

**Somatic cell therapy**:
- Treats body (somatic) cells
- Only affects the individual treated
- Cannot be passed to offspring
- Example: Treating cystic fibrosis

**Germ line therapy**:
- Treats egg or sperm cells
- Changes are inherited
- More controversial

### Example: Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis is caused by a faulty gene affecting mucus production.

Potential treatment:
1. Healthy gene identified
2. Gene inserted into vector (virus or liposome)
3. Vector delivers gene to lung cells
4. Healthy gene produces correct protein
5. Mucus production normalised

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Genetic modification** | Altering an organism's DNA to introduce desirable traits |
| **Transgenic organism** | Organism with DNA from another species |
| **Vector** | Carrier that transfers a gene into a cell |
| **Plasmid** | Circular DNA in bacteria, used as a vector |
| **Restriction enzyme** | Enzyme that cuts DNA at specific sequences |
| **DNA ligase** | Enzyme that joins DNA pieces together |
| **Gene therapy** | Treating genetic disorders by inserting healthy genes |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing selective breeding with genetic engineering**: Different methods, different timeframes

2. **Forgetting to mention vectors**: Plasmids or viruses are needed to transfer genes

3. **Not mentioning specific enzymes**: Restriction enzymes cut DNA; DNA ligase joins DNA

4. **Only giving advantages**: Always discuss both benefits and concerns

5. **Not giving specific examples**: Name actual examples like GM insulin, Golden Rice
