## 7.1 Formation of Ions from Atoms

### What is an Ion?

An **ion** is an atom or group of atoms that has gained or lost electrons and therefore has an electrical charge.

- **Cation**: Positively charged ion (lost electrons)
- **Anion**: Negatively charged ion (gained electrons)

### How Do Atoms Form Ions?

Atoms form ions to achieve a **full outer shell** of electrons (stable electron configuration), usually the same as a **noble gas**.

### Formation of Cations (Positive Ions)

**Metals lose electrons** to form cations:

**Example: Sodium (Na)**
- Electron configuration: 2,8,1
- Loses 1 electron: Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
- Na⁺ has configuration: 2,8 (same as neon)

**Example: Magnesium (Mg)**
- Electron configuration: 2,8,2
- Loses 2 electrons: Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻
- Mg²⁺ has configuration: 2,8 (same as neon)

**Example: Aluminium (Al)**
- Electron configuration: 2,8,3
- Loses 3 electrons: Al → Al³⁺ + 3e⁻
- Al³⁺ has configuration: 2,8 (same as neon)

### Formation of Anions (Negative Ions)

**Non-metals gain electrons** to form anions:

**Example: Chlorine (Cl)**
- Electron configuration: 2,8,7
- Gains 1 electron: Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻
- Cl⁻ has configuration: 2,8,8 (same as argon)

**Example: Oxygen (O)**
- Electron configuration: 2,6
- Gains 2 electrons: O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻
- O²⁻ has configuration: 2,8 (same as neon)

| Group | Electrons in outer shell | Tendency | Typical ion charge |
|-------|-------------------------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | 1 | Lose 1 electron | +1 |
| 2 | 2 | Lose 2 electrons | +2 |
| 3 | 3 | Lose 3 electrons | +3 |

| 6 | 6 | Gain 2 electrons | -2 |
| 7 | 7 | Gain 1 electron | -1 |
| 8 (0) | 8 | Already stable | 0 (no ion) |

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Forgetting that transition metals can have multiple oxidation states**
   - Iron: Fe²⁺ AND Fe³⁺
   - Copper: Cu⁺ AND Cu²⁺

2. **Writing incorrect charges for polyatomic ions**
   - Carbonate is CO₃²⁻ (not CO₃⁻)
   - Sulfate is SO₄²⁻ (not SO₄⁻)

3. **Confusing ion charge with number of electrons**
   - Na⁺ has LOST 1 electron, not gained

## 7.2 Ionic Bonding and Ionic Compounds

### What is an Ionic Bond?

An **ionic bond** is the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.

### How Ionic Bonds Form

1. Metal atom **loses electrons** to form a positive ion (cation)
2. Non-metal atom **gains electrons** to form a negative ion (anion)
3. Opposite charges **attract** strongly, forming an ionic bond
4. Electrons are **transferred** from metal to non-metal

### Dot and Cross Diagrams

Dot and cross diagrams show the transfer of electrons during ionic bond formation:

**Example: Sodium Chloride (NaCl)**

```
Sodium atom (Na)        Chlorine atom (Cl)
    [•]                     [x]
 [•] [•] [•]             [x][x][x]
 [•] [•] [•]             [x][x][x]
 [•] [•]                   [x][x]
     [•]                     [x]

Sodium loses 1 e⁻ →     Chlorine gains 1 e⁻

Na⁺                      Cl⁻
 [x]                     [x][•]
[x] [x]                  [x][x][•]
[x] [x]                  [x][x][•]
                         [x][•]
```

The sodium ion has no dots (lost its outer electron), and the chloride ion has both dots and crosses (gained sodium's electron).

**Example: Magnesium Oxide (MgO)**

- Mg loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺
- O gains 2 electrons → O²⁻
- The ions attract: Mg²⁺ O²⁻

### Writing Formulae for Ionic Compounds

The formula of an ionic compound must have **zero overall charge**.

**Rules:**
1. Write the symbols with charges
2. Swap the charges (ignoring signs) to get the ratio
3. Simplify if necessary

**Examples:**

| Ions | Swap charges | Formula |
|------|--------------|---------|
| Na⁺ and Cl⁻ | 1:1 | NaCl |
| Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ | 1:2 | MgCl₂ |
| Al³⁺ and O²⁻ | 2:3 | Al₂O₃ |
| Ca²⁺ and NO₃⁻ | 1:2 | Ca(NO₃)₂ |

**Note:** When polyatomic ions appear more than once, use brackets:
- Ca(NO₃)₂ NOT CaNO₃₂

### Common Ionic Compounds

| Name | Formula | Ions present |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Sodium chloride | NaCl | Na⁺, Cl⁻ |
| Magnesium oxide | MgO | Mg²⁺, O²⁻ |
| Calcium carbonate | CaCO₃ | Ca²⁺, CO₃²⁻ |
| Sodium sulfate | Na₂SO₄ | Na⁺, SO₄²⁻ |
| Ammonium nitrate | NH₄NO₃ | NH₄⁺, NO₃⁻ |
| Copper(II) sulfate | CuSO₄ | Cu²⁺, SO₄²⁻ |

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Forgetting brackets around polyatomic ions**
   - Correct: Mg(NO₃)₂
   - Wrong: MgNO₃₂

2. **Not balancing charges correctly**
   - Al₂O₃: 2×(+3) + 3×(-2) = 0 ✓

3. **Showing electrons as dots and crosses incorrectly in diagrams**

## 7.3 Structure of Ionic Compounds

### The Ionic Lattice

Ionic compounds form a **giant ionic lattice structure**:
- Ions arranged in a **regular, repeating pattern**
- Each positive ion is surrounded by **negative ions**
- Each negative ion is surrounded by **positive ions**
- The pattern extends in **all three dimensions**

### Diagram of Sodium Chloride Lattice

```
Na⁺  Cl⁻  Na⁺  Cl⁻  Na⁺
Cl⁻  Na⁺  Cl⁻  Na⁺  Cl⁻
Na⁺  Cl⁻  Na⁺  Cl⁻  Na⁺
Cl⁻  Na⁺  Cl⁻  Na⁺  Cl⁻
```

In NaCl:
- Each Na⁺ is surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ ions
- Each Cl⁻ is surrounded by 6 Na⁺ ions
- The ratio of Na⁺:Cl⁻ is always 1:1

### Coordination Number

The **coordination number** is the number of ions of opposite charge that surround each ion.

| Compound | Coordination number |
|----------|---------------------|
| NaCl | 6 |
| CsCl | 8 |

### Key Features of Ionic Lattices

1. **Regular arrangement** - ions pack in the most efficient way
2. **Alternating charges** - opposite charges are close together
3. **Strong electrostatic forces** - act in all directions between ions
4. **No neutral molecules** - the lattice is one giant structure

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Drawing separate molecules** - ionic compounds do not exist as molecules

2. **Showing only one type of ion surrounded** - both cations AND anions are surrounded by opposite ions

## 7.4 Properties of Ionic Compounds

### 1. High Melting and Boiling Points

**Why?** Strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions require a lot of energy to overcome.

| Compound | Melting point |
|----------|---------------|
| NaCl | 801°C |
| MgO | 2852°C |
| CaO | 2572°C |

**Trends:**
- Compounds with **higher charges** have stronger bonds
- MgO (Mg²⁺, O²⁻) has higher melting point than NaCl (Na⁺, Cl⁻)

### 2. Solid ionic compounds do NOT conduct electricity

**Why?** Ions are **fixed in position** in the lattice and cannot move.

### 3. Molten or aqueous ionic compounds DO conduct electricity

**Why?** Ions are **free to move** and carry charge.

**Electrolytes** = substances that conduct electricity when molten or dissolved

### 4. Soluble in water (most)

**Why?** Water molecules attract ions and pull them away from the lattice.

**Note:** Not all ionic compounds are soluble (e.g., AgCl, BaSO₄ are insoluble)

### 5. Brittle

**Why?** If the lattice is hit, layers of ions can slide. This brings like charges together, causing **repulsion** and the crystal to shatter.

```
Normal lattice:          After hit:
+ - + - +               + - + - +
- + - + -               - + - + -
+ - + - +               + - + - +  ← layers slide
- + - + -               - + - + -  ← repulsion!

            Same charges align → repulsion → crystal breaks
```

### Summary Table of Properties

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| High melting/boiling points | Strong electrostatic forces need lots of energy to break |
| Do not conduct when solid | Ions cannot move - held in lattice |
| Conduct when molten or aqueous | Ions are free to move and carry charge |
| Often soluble in water | Water molecules surround and separate ions |
| Brittle | Layers slide, like charges meet, repulsion breaks crystal |

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Saying ionic compounds conduct when solid** - they DON'T

2. **Confusing the reason for conduction**
   - It's IONS that move, NOT electrons (unlike metals)

3. **Not explaining brittleness correctly**
   - Must mention repulsion between like charges

## 7.5 Limitations of the Ionic Model

### The Simple Model

The simple ionic model assumes:
- Ions are **perfect spheres**
- Ions have **discrete charges** (+1, +2, -1, -2, etc.)
- There is **no sharing** of electrons
- The bonding is **100% ionic**

### Limitations of the Model

#### 1. Ions are Not Perfect Spheres

- Ions can be **polarised** (distorted) by nearby ions
- Larger anions are more easily polarised
- This leads to some **covalent character** in ionic bonds

**Example:** In AgCl, the silver ion polarises the chloride ion, giving the bond some covalent character.

#### 2. No Bond is 100% Ionic

- Most "ionic" compounds have some **electron sharing**
- Electronegativity differences determine how ionic a bond is
- Only compounds with very large electronegativity differences are close to 100% ionic

#### 3. Some Compounds Show Intermediate Behaviour

**Example: Aluminium chloride (AlCl₃)**
- Expect ionic (Al³⁺ and Cl⁻)
- Actually sublimes at low temperature (178°C)
- Behaves more like a covalent compound
- Exists as Al₂Cl₆ molecules in gas phase

#### 4. Transition Metal Ions

The model doesn't explain well:
- Why transition metals can have multiple oxidation states
- The colours of transition metal compounds
- Variable properties within a group

#### 5. Lattice Defects

Real crystals have:
- Missing ions (vacancies)
- Extra ions in wrong places (interstitials)
- Impurities

### Why Do We Still Use the Model?

Despite limitations, the ionic model is useful because:
- It **explains most properties** correctly
- It allows us to **predict formulae** and behaviour
- It's **simple** and easy to understand
- More complex models are needed only for special cases

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Thinking the ionic model is perfect** - it has limitations

2. **Not knowing specific examples** of limitations (like AlCl₃)

3. **Forgetting that "ionic" bonds can have covalent character**

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Ion** | An atom or group of atoms with an electrical charge |
| **Cation** | A positively charged ion (formed by losing electrons) |
| **Anion** | A negatively charged ion (formed by gaining electrons) |
| **Ionic bond** | The strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions |
| **Ionic lattice** | A giant three-dimensional structure of alternating ions |
| **Electrolyte** | A substance that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved |
| **Coordination number** | The number of ions of opposite charge surrounding an ion |
| **Polarisation** | Distortion of an electron cloud by a nearby ion |

## Exam Tips ✓

1. **Use the correct terminology**: "electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions"

2. **Include charges** when writing about ions

3. **Explain properties** in terms of particles AND forces

4. **For conduction**: mention ions AND their ability to move

5. **For brittleness**: mention like charges coming together causing repulsion

6. **Know the limitations**: be able to give at least one example
