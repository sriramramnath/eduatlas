## 1.1 The Three States of Matter

### Introduction
Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. All substances can exist in three physical states: **solid**, **liquid**, and **gas**, depending on temperature and pressure conditions. Understanding the behaviour of particles in these states is fundamental to chemistry and explains many physical phenomena we observe daily.

### The Three States: An Overview

| State | Shape | Volume | Particle Arrangement | Particle Movement |
|-------|-------|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Solid | Fixed shape | Fixed volume | Packed tightly in regular pattern | Vibrate in fixed positions |
| Liquid | Takes shape of container | Fixed volume | Close together, irregular arrangement | Move freely past each other |
| Gas | Fills container | Fills container | Far apart, random arrangement | Move rapidly in all directions |

## 1.2 Particle Theory and Kinetic Theory

### The Kinetic Theory of Matter
The kinetic theory explains the behaviour of matter in terms of the movement of particles. This theory makes several fundamental assumptions about particles that form the basis of our understanding of states of matter and phase changes.

**Key Assumptions of Kinetic Theory:**

1. **All matter is made up of tiny particles** - These particles can be atoms, molecules, or ions depending on the substance. The arrangement and behaviour of these particles determine the properties of the substance in each state.

2. **Particles are in constant motion** - Even in solids, particles are not stationary; they vibrate about fixed positions. In liquids and gases, particles move more freely. The kinetic energy of particles increases with temperature, meaning they move faster at higher temperatures.

3. **Particles have kinetic energy** - The energy of movement is called kinetic energy. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. Higher temperatures mean greater average kinetic energy.

4. **There are forces of attraction between particles** - These intermolecular forces hold particles together. The strength of these forces varies between states and determines many physical properties including melting and boiling points.

5. **The spaces between particles differ** - In solids and liquids, particles are close together with little empty space. In gases, particles are far apart with large empty spaces between them.

### Limitations of Particle Theory
While particle theory provides an excellent model for understanding states of matter, it has some limitations that students should be aware of for higher-level understanding:

- **Particles are not solid spheres**: Real particles (atoms, molecules, ions) are not the solid, incompressible spheres often depicted in models. Atoms contain mostly empty space with electrons in orbitals.

- **Intermolecular forces are complex**: The simple model suggests uniform attraction between particles, but real intermolecular forces vary in type (hydrogen bonding, dipole-dipole, van der Waals) and strength.

- **Particles have internal structure**: Atoms contain protons, neutrons, and electrons, and molecules have specific shapes and bond angles that affect properties.

## 1.3 Solids: Structure and Properties

### Particle Arrangement in Solids
In solids, particles are arranged in a **regular, repeating three-dimensional pattern** called a lattice structure. This regular arrangement maximises the attractive forces between particles and minimises the potential energy of the system, making solids the most stable state at lower temperatures.

### Particle Movement in Solids
Particles in solids **vibrate about fixed positions** but cannot move from their places in the lattice. This vibration increases in amplitude as temperature rises, which explains why solids expand when heated. The particles possess kinetic energy due to this vibration, but it is the lowest among the three states.

### Properties of Solids Explained by Particle Theory

| Property | Explanation Using Particle Theory |
|----------|-----------------------------------|
| **Fixed shape** | Particles are held in fixed positions by strong forces of attraction. They cannot move past each other, so the solid maintains its shape. |
| **Fixed volume** | Particles are packed closely together with little empty space between them. The distances between particles cannot be compressed further under normal conditions. |
| **Cannot be compressed** | There is very little empty space between particles. Pushing particles closer together would require overcoming strong repulsive forces. |
| **High density** | Particles are packed closely together, meaning there is a large mass of particles in a given volume. |
| **Do not flow** | Particles cannot move past each other as they are held in fixed positions by strong forces. |

### Types of Solids
Students should understand that there are different types of solid structures:

- **Crystalline solids**: Have a regular, repeating pattern throughout (e.g., sodium chloride, diamond). They have sharp, definite melting points.

- **Amorphous solids**: Lack long-range order in their particle arrangement (e.g., glass, some plastics). They soften over a temperature range rather than melting at a specific point.

## 1.4 Liquids: Structure and Properties

### Particle Arrangement in Liquids
In liquids, particles are arranged **irregularly** and are still **close together**, similar to solids, but without the ordered lattice structure. This irregular arrangement allows particles to move past each other, which is why liquids can flow and take the shape of their container.

### Particle Movement in Liquids
Particles in liquids can **move freely past each other** in a random manner. They have more kinetic energy than particles in solids, which allows them to overcome some of the intermolecular forces holding them in fixed positions. However, they still remain close together due to attractive forces.

### Properties of Liquids Explained by Particle Theory

| Property | Explanation Using Particle Theory |
|----------|-----------------------------------|
| **Takes shape of container** | Particles can move past each other, allowing the liquid to flow and adapt to the shape of its container. |
| **Fixed volume** | Particles remain close together with little empty space. The volume doesn't change regardless of container shape. |
| **Cannot be compressed** | Like solids, particles are close together with minimal empty space, making compression difficult. |
| **Moderate density** | Particles are close together but not as densely packed as in solids, giving liquids slightly lower density. |
| **Can flow** | Particles can move past each other because they have enough kinetic energy to overcome some intermolecular forces. |

### Viscosity
Viscosity is a measure of a liquid's resistance to flow. It depends on the strength of intermolecular forces between particles:

- **High viscosity**: Strong intermolecular forces hold particles together more strongly, restricting movement (e.g., honey, syrup).

- **Low viscosity**: Weaker intermolecular forces allow particles to move past each other easily (e.g., water, petrol).

Viscosity **decreases with increasing temperature** because particles gain more kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular forces.

## 1.5 Gases: Structure and Properties

### Particle Arrangement in Gases
In gases, particles are arranged **randomly** and are **far apart** from each other. There is a large amount of empty space between particles - typically, the volume of a gas is about 1000 times greater than the equivalent volume of liquid. Particles are not held in any fixed positions.

### Particle Movement in Gases
Particles in gases move **rapidly and randomly in all directions**. They have high kinetic energy and travel in straight lines until they collide with other particles or the walls of the container. These collisions are perfectly elastic, meaning no kinetic energy is lost during collisions.

### Properties of Gases Explained by Particle Theory

| Property | Explanation Using Particle Theory |
|----------|-----------------------------------|
| **Fills container** | Particles move rapidly in all directions, spreading out to fill all available space. |
| **No fixed volume** | The volume of a gas depends on the container size, as particles spread to fill it. |
| **Easily compressed** | There are large spaces between particles, allowing them to be pushed closer together. |
| **Low density** | Particles are far apart with lots of empty space, resulting in low mass per unit volume. |
| **Exerts pressure** | Moving particles collide with container walls, exerting a force on them. |

### Gas Pressure
Gas pressure arises from the collisions of gas particles with the walls of their container. Each collision exerts a small force, and the sum of all these forces over the surface area creates the pressure. The pressure depends on:

- **Number of particles**: More particles mean more collisions per second.
- **Temperature**: Higher temperature means faster-moving particles that collide more frequently and with greater force.
- **Volume**: Smaller volume means particles collide with walls more frequently.

## 1.6 Changes of State

### Overview of State Changes
When substances are heated or cooled, they can change from one state to another. These changes are **physical changes** because no new substances are formed - the same particles remain, but their arrangement and movement change.

### Melting (Solid → Liquid)
**Melting** is the change from solid to liquid. When a solid is heated, particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously about their fixed positions. At the **melting point**, particles have enough energy to overcome the forces holding them in place in the lattice. They break free from their fixed positions and can move past each other.

**During melting:**
- Temperature remains constant despite continued heating
- Energy supplied is used to overcome intermolecular forces
- This energy is called **latent heat of fusion**
- The solid and liquid exist together at the melting point

### Freezing (Liquid → Solid)
**Freezing** is the reverse of melting - the change from liquid to solid. When a liquid is cooled, particles lose kinetic energy and move more slowly. At the **freezing point**, particles no longer have enough energy to move past each other. They settle into a regular arrangement, forming a solid.

- Freezing occurs at the **same temperature** as melting
- Energy is released as particles form the ordered structure

### Boiling (Liquid → Gas)
**Boiling** is the change from liquid to gas that occurs throughout the liquid at a specific temperature called the **boiling point**. At the boiling point, particles throughout the liquid have enough energy to completely overcome the intermolecular forces holding them together. They escape from the liquid surface to become gas particles.

**During boiling:**
- Temperature remains constant despite continued heating
- Energy supplied is used to overcome intermolecular forces completely
- This energy is called **latent heat of vaporisation**
- Bubbles of gas form throughout the liquid

### Evaporation (Liquid → Gas)
**Evaporation** is another process by which liquids change to gases, but it differs from boiling in important ways:

| Aspect | Boiling | Evaporation |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Where it occurs** | Throughout the liquid | Only at the surface |
| **Temperature** | At a specific temperature (boiling point) | At any temperature below boiling point |
| **Rate** | Rapid | Slow |
| **Bubbles** | Forms bubbles throughout | No bubbles |

**How evaporation works:** Not all particles in a liquid have the same kinetic energy - some have more, some less. Particles at the surface with higher kinetic energy can escape from the liquid into the air. This leaves behind particles with lower average kinetic energy, which is why evaporation causes cooling.

### Condensation (Gas → Liquid)
**Condensation** is the change from gas to liquid. When gas particles lose kinetic energy (usually by contacting a cold surface), they move more slowly. When they move slowly enough, intermolecular forces can pull them together to form a liquid. This is why water droplets form on cold surfaces on humid days.

### Sublimation (Solid → Gas)
**Sublimation** is a special state change where a solid changes directly to a gas without passing through the liquid state. This occurs when particles gain enough energy to break free from the solid lattice and escape as gas particles in one step.

**Examples of substances that sublime:**
- **Iodine**: Forms purple vapour when heated
- **Dry ice (solid CO₂)**: Used in stage effects and refrigeration
- **Ammonium chloride**: Used in chemical demonstrations

### Deposition (Gas → Solid)
**Deposition** is the reverse of sublimation - gas changes directly to solid. This occurs when gas particles lose energy rapidly and form a solid structure without becoming liquid first. Examples include frost formation on cold surfaces and the formation of snow in clouds.

## 1.7 Heating and Cooling Curves

### Understanding Heating Curves
A heating curve shows how the temperature of a substance changes as it is heated at a constant rate. The curve provides valuable information about state changes and the energy involved.

**Features of a heating curve:**

```
Temperature
    ↑
    |          ___________
    |         /           \
    |        /             \
    |   ____/               \____ Gas
    |  /    Boiling         
    | /     point
    |/___________________________
    |        Melting
    |         point
    |   _____
    |  /
    | /
    |/____________________________→ Time
```

**Flat sections (plateaus):** These occur during state changes (melting and boiling). During these sections, the temperature remains constant because the energy supplied is being used to overcome intermolecular forces rather than increase kinetic energy. The longer the plateau, the more energy is required for the state change.

**Sloped sections:** These occur when the substance is in a single state (solid, liquid, or gas). The temperature increases as particles gain kinetic energy.

### Understanding Cooling Curves
A cooling curve shows how temperature changes as a substance is cooled. It is essentially the reverse of a heating curve, with flat sections at the freezing and condensation points.

**Key differences from heating curves:**
- Flat sections occur at freezing point (same as melting point) and condensation point (same as boiling point)
- Sometimes **supercooling** occurs - the liquid cools below its freezing point before solidification begins
- This appears as a dip in the curve before the plateau

## 1.8 Factors Affecting Melting and Boiling Points

### Intermolecular Forces
The strength of intermolecular forces between particles is the primary factor determining melting and boiling points:

- **Strong intermolecular forces** → High melting and boiling points (e.g., ionic compounds, metals)
- **Weak intermolecular forces** → Low melting and boiling points (e.g., covalent molecules like oxygen, nitrogen)

### Pressure Effects
**Melting point:** For most substances, increased pressure raises the melting point slightly. However, water is unusual - increased pressure lowers its melting point (this is why ice skates work - pressure melts the ice slightly).

**Boiling point:** Increased pressure raises the boiling point. This is the principle behind pressure cookers, which cook food faster because water boils at a higher temperature under pressure. Conversely, at high altitudes where atmospheric pressure is lower, water boils at a lower temperature.

### Impurities
Adding impurities to a substance affects its melting and boiling points:

**Melting point depression:** Impurities lower the melting point of a solid. This is why salt is spread on icy roads - the salt lowers the melting point of ice, causing it to melt even at temperatures below 0°C.

**Boiling point elevation:** Impurities raise the boiling point of a liquid. This is why salt water boils at a higher temperature than pure water.

## 1.9 Gas Laws (Chemistry Only - Higher Tier)

### Boyle's Law: Pressure and Volume
Boyle's Law states that for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature:

**pV = constant** or **p₁V₁ = p₂V₂**

Where:
- p = pressure
- V = volume

This means pressure and volume are **inversely proportional** - if volume decreases, pressure increases, and vice versa. This is because compressing a gas (reducing volume) means particles collide with the walls more frequently.

**Practical applications:**
- Bicycle pumps
- Syringes
- Breathing mechanics

### Charles's Law: Volume and Temperature
Charles's Law states that for a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure:

**V/T = constant** or **V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂**

Where temperature is measured in Kelvin.

Volume is **directly proportional** to absolute temperature - as temperature increases, particles move faster and push the container walls outward, increasing volume.

### Pressure and Temperature
For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume:

**p/T = constant** or **p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂**

As temperature increases, particles move faster and collide with walls more frequently and with greater force, increasing pressure.

### The Kelvin Scale
The Kelvin scale is an absolute temperature scale where:
- 0 K = -273°C (absolute zero)
- At absolute zero, particles have minimum kinetic energy and stop moving
- Temperature in Kelvin = Temperature in °C + 273

## 1.10 Diffusion

### Definition of Diffusion
**Diffusion** is the random movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration, resulting in the uniform distribution of particles. It occurs in liquids and gases where particles can move freely.

### Evidence for Particle Movement
Diffusion provides excellent evidence for the movement of particles:

**Bromine gas experiment:** When a gas jar of air is placed on top of a gas jar of brown bromine vapour, the brown colour slowly spreads upward into the air-filled jar. This shows that:
- Bromine particles move randomly in all directions
- Air particles also move randomly
- Eventually, particles are evenly distributed throughout both jars

**Potassium manganate(VII) in water:** When a crystal of potassium manganate(VII) is placed in water, the purple colour spreads throughout the water as the solid dissolves and particles diffuse.

### Factors Affecting Diffusion Rate

| Factor | Effect on Diffusion Rate | Explanation |
|--------|-------------------------|-------------|
| **Temperature** | Higher temperature → faster diffusion | Particles have more kinetic energy and move faster |
| **Particle mass** | Lighter particles → faster diffusion | Lighter particles move faster at the same temperature |
| **State of matter** | Gas > Liquid >> Solid | Gases have more space between particles for movement |
| **Concentration gradient** | Steeper gradient → faster diffusion | Greater difference in concentration drives faster movement |

### Graham's Law of Diffusion
Lighter particles diffuse faster than heavier particles. This is because at the same temperature, lighter particles have higher average speeds, even though they have the same average kinetic energy as heavier particles.

**Evidence:** When ammonia solution and hydrochloric acid are placed at opposite ends of a glass tube, the white ring of ammonium chloride forms closer to the hydrochloric acid end. This is because ammonia molecules (M_r = 17) are lighter than hydrogen chloride molecules (M_r = 36.5) and diffuse faster.

## 1.11 Brownian Motion

### Definition
**Brownian motion** is the random, erratic movement of microscopic particles suspended in a fluid (liquid or gas). It is caused by the bombardment of the suspended particles by the fast-moving molecules of the fluid.

### Discovery and Significance
Robert Brown observed in 1827 that pollen grains suspended in water moved in a random, jerky manner. This observation was later explained by Einstein as being caused by collisions with water molecules. Brownian motion provides important evidence for:
- The existence of particles (molecules)
- The constant, random motion of particles
- The existence of intermolecular forces

## 1.12 Common Exam Mistakes and Important Points

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing melting and dissolving**: Melting is a state change (solid to liquid) caused by heating. Dissolving is when a solid breaks up into individual particles within a solvent - no heating is required.

2. **Stating particles "stop moving" in solids**: Particles in solids vibrate about fixed positions - they do NOT stop moving. Only at absolute zero (0 K) would motion theoretically cease.

3. **Confusing evaporation and boiling**: Evaporation occurs at the surface at any temperature below the boiling point. Boiling occurs throughout the liquid at a specific temperature.

4. **Saying intermolecular forces are "broken"**: Forces of attraction between particles are overcome or weakened during state changes - they are not actually broken like chemical bonds.

5. **Forgetting flat sections in heating curves**: During state changes, temperature remains constant because energy is used to overcome forces, not increase kinetic energy.

6. **Incorrect particle diagrams**: Make sure particle diagrams clearly show:
   - Solids: regular arrangement, touching
   - Liquids: irregular arrangement, touching
   - Gases: random arrangement, far apart

7. **Not mentioning energy during state changes**: Always explain that energy is used to overcome forces of attraction between particles during melting and boiling.

8. **Confusing sublimation and evaporation**: Sublimation is solid directly to gas. Evaporation is liquid to gas.

### Important Points for Exams

1. **Always use correct terminology**: "particles move faster" not "molecules get excited"; "overcome forces of attraction" not "break bonds".

2. **Link particle behaviour to properties**: Always explain properties (shape, volume, compressibility) in terms of particle arrangement and movement.

3. **Understand the difference between physical and chemical changes**: State changes are physical changes - no new substances are formed.

4. **Know the limitations of particle theory**: For higher marks, recognise that the simple particle model is a representation with limitations.

5. **Use diagrams effectively**: In exam questions, clear diagrams can help demonstrate understanding of particle arrangement.
