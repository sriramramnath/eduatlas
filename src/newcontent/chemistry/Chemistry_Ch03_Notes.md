## 3.1 Atoms, Elements, and Compounds

### 3.1.1 Elements

**Definition:** An element is a substance made up of only one type of atom. It cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.

**Key Points:**
- There are approximately 118 known elements
- Elements are listed in the Periodic Table
- Each element has a unique chemical symbol (e.g., Na for sodium, Fe for iron)
- Elements can exist as atoms (e.g., noble gases) or molecules (e.g., O₂, N₂)

**Examples of Elements:**
- **Metals:** Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Gold (Au), Sodium (Na)
- **Non-metals:** Oxygen (O), Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N), Sulfur (S)

### 3.1.2 Atoms

**Definition:** An atom is the smallest part of an element that can still be recognized as that element.

**Key Points:**
- Atoms are incredibly small (approximately 10⁻¹⁰ m in diameter)
- Atoms are the building blocks of all matter
- Atoms of the same element are identical in chemical properties

### 3.1.3 Compounds

**Definition:** A compound is a substance formed when two or more elements are chemically combined together.

**Key Points:**
- Compounds have properties different from the elements that make them
- The elements in a compound are held together by chemical bonds
- Compounds can only be separated into their elements by chemical reactions
- The composition of a compound is always fixed (Law of Constant Composition)

**Examples of Compounds:**
| Compound | Formula | Elements Present |
|----------|---------|------------------|
| Water | H₂O | Hydrogen, Oxygen |
| Carbon dioxide | CO₂ | Carbon, Oxygen |
| Sodium chloride | NaCl | Sodium, Chlorine |
| Calcium carbonate | CaCO₃ | Calcium, Carbon, Oxygen |

### 3.1.4 Molecules

**Definition:** A molecule is a group of two or more atoms chemically bonded together.

**Types of Molecules:**
- **Elemental molecules:** O₂ (oxygen), N₂ (nitrogen), O₃ (ozone)
- **Compound molecules:** H₂O (water), CO₂ (carbon dioxide), CH₄ (methane)

## 3.2 Atomic Structure

### 3.2.1 Subatomic Particles

Atoms are made up of three subatomic particles:

| Particle | Relative Mass | Relative Charge | Location |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| Proton | 1 | +1 | Nucleus |
| Neutron | 1 | 0 | Nucleus |
| Electron | 1/1836 (negligible) | -1 | Shells/orbitals |

**Key Points:**
- The nucleus contains protons and neutrons (collectively called nucleons)
- The nucleus is very small compared to the atom but contains most of the mass
- Electrons move around the nucleus in electron shells (energy levels)
- An atom is mostly empty space

### 3.2.2 Structure of the Atom

```
        Electron (negative)
           ↗    ↖
          /      \
    Electron  Electron
        ←  Nucleus  →
         (positive)
            \      /
             ↘    ↙
        Electron (negative)
```

**The Nucleus:**
- Contains protons and neutrons
- Has a positive charge (due to protons)
- Very dense and small (about 10⁻¹⁵ m in diameter)
- Contains most of the atom's mass

**The Electron Cloud:**
- Region surrounding the nucleus where electrons are found
- Electrons are arranged in energy levels (shells)
- The electron cloud makes up most of the atom's volume

## 3.3 Atomic Number, Mass Number, and Isotopes

### 3.3.1 Atomic Number (Z)

**Definition:** The atomic number is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.

**Key Points:**
- Each element has a unique atomic number
- In a neutral atom: Number of protons = Number of electrons
- The atomic number determines the element's identity
- Elements in the Periodic Table are arranged by atomic number

**Notation:** 
$$Z = \text{number of protons}$$

### 3.3.2 Mass Number (A)

**Definition:** The mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.

**Formula:**
$$A = Z + N$$
Where:
- A = Mass number
- Z = Atomic number (protons)
- N = Number of neutrons

**Calculating Neutrons:**
$$\text{Number of neutrons} = A - Z$$

### 3.3.3 Atomic Notation

Atoms are represented using the following notation:

$$\large _Z^A X$$

Where:
- X = Chemical symbol of the element
- A = Mass number (top left)
- Z = Atomic number (bottom left)

**Example:** $_{11}^{23}Na$ represents a sodium atom with:
- 11 protons (atomic number)
- 11 electrons (in a neutral atom)
- 12 neutrons (23 - 11 = 12)

### 3.3.4 Isotopes

**Definition:** Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

**Key Points:**
- Isotopes have the same atomic number but different mass numbers
- Isotopes have the same chemical properties (same electron arrangement)
- Isotopes have different physical properties (different masses)

**Example: Isotopes of Carbon**
| Isotope | Symbol | Protons | Neutrons | Mass Number | Use |
|---------|--------|---------|----------|-------------|-----|
| Carbon-12 | $^{12}_6C$ | 6 | 6 | 12 | Standard for atomic mass |
| Carbon-13 | $^{13}_6C$ | 6 | 7 | 13 | Research applications |
| Carbon-14 | $^{14}_6C$ | 6 | 8 | 14 | Radioactive dating |

**Example: Isotopes of Hydrogen**
| Isotope | Symbol | Protons | Neutrons | Mass Number |
|---------|--------|---------|----------|-------------|
| Hydrogen (Protium) | $^1_1H$ | 1 | 0 | 1 |
| Deuterium | $^2_1H$ | 1 | 1 | 2 |
| Tritium | $^3_1H$ | 1 | 2 | 3 |

## 3.4 Electron Configuration

### 3.4.1 Electron Shells (Energy Levels)

**Definition:** Electrons are arranged in shells (energy levels) around the nucleus.

**Rules for Electron Arrangement:**
1. Electrons fill shells starting from the closest to the nucleus
2. Each shell can hold a maximum number of electrons:
   - 1st shell: Maximum 2 electrons
   - 2nd shell: Maximum 8 electrons
   - 3rd shell: Maximum 8 electrons (for elements 1-20)
   - 4th shell: Begins filling after 3rd shell has 8 electrons

**The 2,8,8 Rule (for elements 1-20):**
This rule describes the maximum number of electrons in each shell for the first 20 elements.

|---------|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| Hydrogen | H | 1 | 1 |
| Helium | He | 2 | 2 |
| Lithium | Li | 3 | 2,1 |
| Beryllium | Be | 4 | 2,2 |
| Boron | B | 5 | 2,3 |
| Carbon | C | 6 | 2,4 |
| Nitrogen | N | 7 | 2,5 |
| Oxygen | O | 8 | 2,6 |
| Fluorine | F | 9 | 2,7 |
| Neon | Ne | 10 | 2,8 |
| Sodium | Na | 11 | 2,8,1 |
| Magnesium | Mg | 12 | 2,8,2 |
| Aluminium | Al | 13 | 2,8,3 |
| Silicon | Si | 14 | 2,8,4 |
| Phosphorus | P | 15 | 2,8,5 |
| Sulfur | S | 16 | 2,8,6 |
| Chlorine | Cl | 17 | 2,8,7 |
| Argon | Ar | 18 | 2,8,8 |
| Potassium | K | 19 | 2,8,8,1 |
| Calcium | Ca | 20 | 2,8,8,2 |

### 3.4.3 Drawing Electron Shell Diagrams

**Steps:**
1. Draw a circle to represent the nucleus
2. Write the number of protons and neutrons inside
3. Draw concentric circles for electron shells
4. Place electrons (dots or crosses) on each shell
5. Fill shells according to the 2,8,8 rule

**Example: Sodium Atom (Na)**
```
        •    •
      •   ⊕    •
     •   11p    •
    •    12n     •
     •          •
      •   •   •
        •   •
        
Electron configuration: 2,8,1
```

### 3.4.4 Relationship to the Periodic Table

- **Group number** = Number of electrons in outer shell (for main group elements)
- **Period number** = Number of electron shells

**Example:**
- Chlorine (Cl): Configuration 2,8,7 → Period 3, Group 7
- Potassium (K): Configuration 2,8,8,1 → Period 4, Group 1

## 3.5 Relative Atomic Mass

### 3.5.1 Definition

**Relative Atomic Mass (Aᵣ):** The weighted average mass of the atoms of an element relative to 1/12th of the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

**Key Points:**
- Carbon-12 is the standard reference (exactly 12.000 units)
- Relative atomic mass has no units (it's a ratio)
- The Aᵣ takes into account the relative abundances of isotopes

### 3.5.2 Calculating Relative Atomic Mass from Isotopes

**Formula:**
$$A_r = \frac{\sum(\text{mass of isotope} \times \text{abundance})}{100}$$

Or written as:
$$A_r = \frac{(m_1 \times a_1) + (m_2 \times a_2) + ...}{100}$$

Where:
- m₁, m₂ = masses of isotopes
- a₁, a₂ = percentage abundances

### 3.5.3 Worked Examples

**Example 1: Chlorine**

Chlorine has two isotopes:
- Chlorine-35: 75% abundance
- Chlorine-37: 25% abundance

Calculate the relative atomic mass of chlorine.

**Solution:**
$$A_r = \frac{(35 \times 75) + (37 \times 25)}{100}$$
$$A_r = \frac{2625 + 925}{100}$$
$$A_r = \frac{3550}{100} = 35.5$$

**Example 2: Copper**

Copper has two isotopes:
- Copper-63: 69.2% abundance
- Copper-65: 30.8% abundance

Calculate the relative atomic mass of copper.

**Solution:**
$$A_r = \frac{(63 \times 69.2) + (65 \times 30.8)}{100}$$
$$A_r = \frac{4359.6 + 2002}{100}$$
$$A_r = \frac{6361.6}{100} = 63.6$$

**Example 3: Magnesium**

Magnesium has three isotopes:
- Magnesium-24: 78.6% abundance
- Magnesium-25: 10.1% abundance
- Magnesium-26: 11.3% abundance

Calculate the relative atomic mass of magnesium.

**Solution:**
$$A_r = \frac{(24 \times 78.6) + (25 \times 10.1) + (26 \times 11.3)}{100}$$
$$A_r = \frac{1886.4 + 252.5 + 293.8}{100}$$
$$A_r = \frac{2432.7}{100} = 24.3$$

## 3.6 Common Exam Mistakes

### 3.6.1 Definitions Errors

❌ **Wrong:** "An atom is the smallest particle"
✅ **Correct:** "An atom is the smallest part of an element that can still be recognized as that element"

❌ **Wrong:** "Isotopes have different numbers of protons"
✅ **Correct:** "Isotopes have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons"

### 3.6.2 Calculation Errors

❌ **Wrong:** Mass number = protons + electrons
✅ **Correct:** Mass number = protons + neutrons

❌ **Wrong:** Number of neutrons = Atomic number - Mass number
✅ **Correct:** Number of neutrons = Mass number - Atomic number

### 3.6.3 Electron Configuration Errors

❌ **Wrong:** Potassium electron configuration = 2,8,9
✅ **Correct:** Potassium electron configuration = 2,8,8,1

❌ **Wrong:** Calcium electron configuration = 2,8,10
✅ **Correct:** Calcium electron configuration = 2,8,8,2

### 3.6.4 Common Confusions

| Don't confuse... | With... |
|------------------|---------|
| Atomic number | Mass number |
| Protons | Neutrons |
| Element | Compound |
| Molecule | Atom |
| Isotope | Ion |
