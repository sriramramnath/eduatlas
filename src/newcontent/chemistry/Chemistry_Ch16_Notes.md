## 16.1 Definitions of Acids and Bases

### Acids

**Definition:** An acid is a substance that **donates protons (H⁺ ions)** when dissolved in water.

**Key Points:**
- Acids contain hydrogen that can be released as H⁺ ions
- The H⁺ ion is actually a proton, so acids are **proton donors**
- Acids dissolve in water to produce aqueous H⁺ ions

**Examples of Common Acids:**
| Acid | Formula | Type |
|------|---------|------|
| Hydrochloric acid | HCl | Mineral acid |
| Sulfuric acid | H₂SO₄ | Mineral acid |
| Nitric acid | HNO₃ | Mineral acid |
| Ethanoic acid | CH₃COOH | Organic acid |
| Citric acid | C₆H₈O₇ | Organic acid |

---

### Bases and Alkalis

**Definition of Base:** A base is a substance that **accepts protons (H⁺ ions)**. Bases are **proton acceptors**.

**Definition of Alkali:** An alkali is a **soluble base** that dissolves in water to produce OH⁻ ions.

**Key Points:**
- All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis
- Only soluble bases (alkalis) produce OH⁻ ions in solution
- Metal oxides and hydroxides are common bases

**Examples of Common Alkalis:**
| Alkali | Formula | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Sodium hydroxide | NaOH | Strong alkali |
| Potassium hydroxide | KOH | Strong alkali |
| Calcium hydroxide | Ca(OH)₂ | Weak alkali (limewater) |
| Ammonia solution | NH₃(aq) | Weak alkali |

---

### Neutralisation Reactions

**General Equation:**
```
Acid + Base → Salt + Water
```

**Ionic Equation:**
```
H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)
```

This is the essential ionic equation for all neutralisation reactions between acids and alkalis.

## 16.2 The pH Scale and Indicators

### The pH Scale

**Definition:** The pH scale is a numerical scale from **0 to 14** that measures the acidity or alkalinity of a solution.

| pH Range | Classification |
|----------|----------------|
| pH < 7 | Acidic |
| pH = 7 | Neutral |
| pH > 7 | Alkaline |

**Key Points:**
- Lower pH = more acidic = higher H⁺ concentration
- Higher pH = more alkaline = lower H⁺ concentration
- Each pH unit represents a 10-fold change in H⁺ concentration
- pH is calculated using: pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]

---

### Indicators

**Definition:** An indicator is a substance that changes colour depending on the pH of the solution.

#### Common Indicators:

| Indicator | Colour in Acid | Colour in Neutral | Colour in Alkali |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Litmus | Red | Purple | Blue |
| Phenolphthalein | Colourless | Colourless | Pink |
| Methyl orange | Red | Orange | Yellow |
| Universal indicator | Red-orange-yellow | Green | Blue-purple |

**Universal Indicator:**
- A mixture of several indicators
- Gives a range of colours across the pH scale
- Can estimate pH to within 1 unit

---

### pH Meter

- Gives a **digital reading** of pH
- More **accurate** than indicators
- Can be used for continuous monitoring
- Requires calibration with buffer solutions

## 16.3 Strong vs Weak Acids

### Strong Acids

**Definition:** A strong acid is one that **completely dissociates** (ionises) in water, releasing all its H⁺ ions.

**Examples:**
- Hydrochloric acid: HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻ (100% dissociation)
- Sulfuric acid: H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻ (100% dissociation)
- Nitric acid: HNO₃ → H⁺ + NO₃⁻ (100% dissociation)

**Key Points:**
- All hydrogen ions are released
- Higher concentration of H⁺ ions for same concentration
- Lower pH than weak acids of same concentration
- Faster reaction rates with metals/carbonates

---

### Weak Acids

**Definition:** A weak acid is one that **partially dissociates** in water, releasing only some of its H⁺ ions.

**Examples:**
- Ethanoic acid: CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻ (partial dissociation)
- Citric acid (partial dissociation)
- Carbonic acid: H₂CO₃ ⇌ 2H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ (partial dissociation)

**Key Points:**
- The reversible arrow (⇌) indicates equilibrium
- Only a small fraction of molecules ionise
- Higher pH than strong acids of same concentration
- Slower reaction rates

---

### Comparing Strong and Weak Acids

| Property | Strong Acid | Weak Acid |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Dissociation | Complete | Partial |
| H⁺ concentration | High | Lower |
| pH (same concentration) | Lower | Higher |
| Conductivity | Higher | Lower |
| Reaction rate | Faster | Slower |
| Electrical conductivity | Higher | Lower |

## 16.4 Titrations

### What is a Titration?

**Definition:** A titration is a quantitative analytical technique used to determine the **exact concentration** of an unknown solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration.

---

### Titration Method

#### Equipment Required:
- Burette (accurately measures variable volumes)
- Pipette (measures fixed volumes accurately)
- Conical flask
- White tile
- Indicator
- Acid and alkali solutions

#### Step-by-Step Procedure:

1. **Prepare the burette:**
   - Rinse with water, then with the solution to be used
   - Fill with the acid (or alkali) of known concentration
   - Record initial reading

2. **Prepare the conical flask:**
   - Use a pipette to measure a known volume of the unknown solution
   - Transfer to a clean conical flask
   - Add a few drops of suitable indicator

3. **Perform the titration:**
   - Add solution from burette slowly
   - Swirl the flask continuously
   - Near the endpoint, add drop by drop
   - Stop when the indicator changes colour permanently

4. **Record results:**
   - Note the final burette reading
   - Calculate volume used (final – initial)
   - Repeat to obtain concordant results (within 0.1 cm³)

---

### Choosing the Right Indicator

| Titration Type | Best Indicator | Colour Change |
|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| Strong acid + Strong alkali | Phenolphthalein OR Methyl orange | Both work well |
| Strong acid + Weak alkali | Methyl orange | Red → Orange/Yellow |
| Weak acid + Strong alkali | Phenolphthalein | Colourless → Pink |
| Weak acid + Weak alkali | No suitable indicator | Use pH meter |

---

### Titration Calculations

#### The Formula:

The concentration can be calculated using:

**C₁V₁/n₁ = C₂V₂/n₂**

Where:
- C = concentration (mol/dm³)
- V = volume (cm³ or dm³)
- n = number of moles from balanced equation

**Or using:**
```
Number of moles = Concentration × Volume (in dm³)
```

---

#### Worked Example 1:

**Question:** 25.0 cm³ of sodium hydroxide solution of unknown concentration required 22.5 cm³ of 0.100 mol/dm³ hydrochloric acid for neutralisation. Calculate the concentration of the NaOH solution.

**Equation:** NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O

**Solution:**
1. Calculate moles of HCl:
   Moles of HCl = C × V = 0.100 × (22.5/1000) = 0.00225 mol

2. Use the ratio from equation (1:1):
   Moles of NaOH = Moles of HCl = 0.00225 mol

3. Calculate concentration of NaOH:
   Concentration = Moles ÷ Volume = 0.00225 ÷ (25.0/1000)
   = 0.0900 mol/dm³

---

#### Worked Example 2:

**Question:** 25.0 cm³ of sulfuric acid required 30.0 cm³ of 0.200 mol/dm³ sodium hydroxide for neutralisation. Calculate the concentration of the sulfuric acid.

**Equation:** H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

**Solution:**
1. Calculate moles of NaOH:
   Moles of NaOH = 0.200 × (30.0/1000) = 0.00600 mol

2. Use the ratio from equation (1:2):
   Moles of H₂SO₄ = 0.00600 ÷ 2 = 0.00300 mol

3. Calculate concentration of H₂SO₄:
   Concentration = 0.00300 ÷ (25.0/1000) = 0.120 mol/dm³

## 16.5 Required Practical: Titration Technique

### Aim
To determine the concentration of a solution of sodium hydroxide using a standard solution of hydrochloric acid.

### Materials
- Burette
- Pipette (25 cm³) and pipette filler
- Conical flask
- White tile
- 0.100 mol/dm³ HCl
- NaOH solution (unknown concentration)
- Phenolphthalein indicator

### Method

1. Rinse the burette with distilled water, then with HCl solution
2. Fill the burette with HCl solution and record initial reading
3. Use a pipette to transfer 25.0 cm³ of NaOH to a conical flask
4. Add 2-3 drops of phenolphthalein indicator (solution turns pink)
5. Place the flask on a white tile
6. Add HCl from burette slowly while swirling the flask
7. Near the endpoint, add drop by drop
8. Stop when the pink colour just disappears (colourless)
9. Record the final burette reading
10. Repeat until you have concordant results (within 0.1 cm³)

### Safety Considerations
- Wear eye protection
- Use a pipette filler (never mouth pipette)
- Handle acids and alkalis with care
- Clean up spills immediately

### Sources of Error

| Error | Effect | How to Minimise |
|-------|--------|-----------------|
| Not rinsing burette | Incorrect concentration | Rinse with solution to be used |
| Reading meniscus wrong | Incorrect volume | Read at eye level, bottom of meniscus |
| Not swirling flask | Incomplete mixing | Swirl continuously |
| Adding too fast | Overshooting endpoint | Add dropwise near endpoint |
| Parallax error | Incorrect reading | Read at eye level |

## Common Exam Mistakes

### ⚠️ Mistake 1: Confusing bases and alkalis
- **Remember:** All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis
- Alkalis are specifically **soluble** bases

### ⚠️ Mistake 2: Indicator colour changes
- Learn the colour changes for common indicators
- Phenolphthalein: colourless in acid, pink in alkali
- Methyl orange: red in acid, yellow in alkali

### ⚠️ Mistake 3: Units in calculations
- Always convert cm³ to dm³ by dividing by 1000
- Check that concentration is in mol/dm³

### ⚠️ Mistake 4: Ignoring molar ratios
- Always write the balanced equation
- Use the stoichiometric ratio in calculations
- For H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH, the ratio is 1:2

### ⚠️ Mistake 5: Strong vs concentrated
- Strong refers to degree of dissociation
- Concentrated refers to the amount dissolved
- A dilute strong acid is still strong!
- A concentrated weak acid is still weak!

### ⚠️ Mistake 6: Titration technique
- Always swirl the flask continuously
- Add dropwise near the endpoint
- Use a white tile to see colour change clearly
- Repeat for concordant results

## Quick Reference: Key Equations

**Neutralisation:**
```
Acid + Base → Salt + Water
H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)
```

**Strong acid dissociation (complete):**
```
HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻
```

**Weak acid dissociation (partial):**
```
CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻
```

**Moles calculation:**
```
Moles = Concentration × Volume (dm³)
Moles = Mass ÷ Mr
```

**Concentration:**
```
Concentration (mol/dm³) = Moles ÷ Volume (dm³)
Concentration (g/dm³) = Mass (g) ÷ Volume (dm³)
```
