## Part A: Multiple Choice Answers

| Question | Answer | Explanation |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Q1 | **B) Electrolysis** | Aluminium is very reactive (high in reactivity series) and forms very stable compounds. Carbon cannot reduce aluminium oxide, so electrolysis is required. |
| Q2 | **C) Removes acidic impurities** | Limestone (CaCO₃) decomposes to CaO, which reacts with silica (SiO₂) to form slag (CaSiO₃). |
| Q3 | **C) To lower the melting point of aluminium oxide** | Cryolite lowers the melting point from ~2050°C to ~950°C, saving energy and costs. |
| Q4 | **B) Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂** | Carbon monoxide is the reducing agent in the blast furnace, reducing iron(III) oxide to iron. |
| Q5 | **D) Cathode, which is negatively charged** | During electrolysis, Al³⁺ ions are attracted to the negatively charged cathode where they gain electrons. |
| Q6 | **B) Recycling uses only about 5% of the energy required for extraction** | Electrolysis requires huge amounts of energy; recycling avoids this energy-intensive process. |
| Q7 | **B) Copper and zinc** | Brass is an alloy of copper (Cu) and zinc (Zn). |
| Q8 | **C) Both oxygen and water** | Both must be present for iron to rust: Iron + Oxygen + Water → Rust. |
| Q9 | **C) Coating with a layer of zinc** | Galvanizing involves coating iron with zinc, which provides both barrier and sacrificial protection. |
| Q10 | **B) Plants** | Phytomining uses plants to absorb copper compounds from low-grade ores through their roots. |
| Q11 | **B) They react with oxygen produced to form carbon dioxide** | At the anode, oxygen is produced which reacts with carbon: C + O₂ → CO₂. |
| Q12 | **C) Chromium and nickel** | Stainless steel contains iron with chromium and nickel to provide corrosion resistance. |

## Part B: True/False Answers

| Question | Answer | Correction if False |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| Q1 | **TRUE** | Iron(III) oxide is reduced by carbon monoxide to produce iron. |
| Q2 | **FALSE** | Aluminium is extracted by **electrolysis** of molten aluminium oxide, not by reduction with carbon. |
| Q3 | **TRUE** | CaO from limestone reacts with SiO₂ impurities: CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (slag). |
| Q4 | **FALSE** | Copper can be extracted by smelting or from low-grade ores by phytomining/bioleaching. Electrolysis is used for purification. |
| Q5 | **TRUE** | Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular metallic structure, preventing layers from sliding. |
| Q6 | **FALSE** | Rust is hydrated **iron(III) oxide** (Fe₂O₃·xH₂O), not iron(II) oxide. |
| Q7 | **FALSE** | Sacrificial protection uses a **more reactive** metal (e.g., zinc) to protect iron. |
| Q8 | **TRUE** | Bacteria break down ores and produce a leachate containing copper ions. |
| Q9 | **FALSE** | The cathode is the **negative** electrode (cations are attracted to it). |

| Q11 | **TRUE** | Mild steel contains less than 1% carbon and is malleable, making it suitable for car bodies. |

## Part C: Short Answer Mark Schemes

### Q1. Aluminium Extraction

**(a) Ore name:** [1 mark]
- Bauxite (or aluminium oxide/Al₂O₃)

**(b) Why electrolysis:** [2 marks]
- Aluminium is very reactive (1 mark)
- Carbon is not reactive enough to reduce aluminium oxide (1 mark)
- OR: Aluminium forms very stable compounds that cannot be reduced by carbon (1 mark)

**(c) Role of cryolite:** [2 marks]
- Lowers the melting point (1 mark)
- From ~2050°C to ~950°C, saving energy/cost (1 mark)

---

### Q2. Blast Furnace

**(a) Raw materials (all four):** [2 marks]
- Haematite (iron ore) / Iron(III) oxide
- Coke (carbon)
- Limestone (calcium carbonate)
- Hot air (oxygen)

Award 1 mark for 2 correct, 2 marks for all 4 correct.

**(b) Reduction equation:** [2 marks]
```
Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂
```
1 mark for correct reactants and products, 1 mark for balancing.

**(c) Role of limestone:** [3 marks]
- Decomposes to form calcium oxide: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (1 mark)
- Calcium oxide reacts with silica (acidic impurity): CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (1 mark)
- Forms slag which removes impurities (1 mark)

---

### Q3. Alloys

**(a) Definition:** [2 marks]
An alloy is a mixture of a metal (1 mark) with one or more other elements (1 mark).

**(b) Why harder:** [2 marks]
- Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular structure (1 mark)
- Layers cannot slide over each other easily (1 mark)

**(c) Example and use:** [2 marks]
Any valid example, e.g.:
- Brass → musical instruments / door fittings
- Bronze → statues / medals
- Stainless steel → cutlery / surgical instruments

1 mark for correct alloy, 1 mark for appropriate use.

---

### Q4. Rusting

**(a) Word equation:** [2 marks]
Iron + Oxygen + Water → Hydrated iron(III) oxide

Award 1 mark for reactants, 1 mark for product.

**(b) Two conditions:** [2 marks]
- Oxygen (from air) (1 mark)
- Water (or moisture) (1 mark)

**(c) Two prevention methods:** [4 marks]
Two methods from:
1. **Painting** - barrier method, prevents oxygen and water reaching iron (2 marks)
2. **Oiling/greasing** - barrier method, used for moving parts (2 marks)
3. **Galvanizing** - coating with zinc, provides barrier and sacrificial protection (2 marks)
4. **Sacrificial anodes** - attaching more reactive metal (zinc/magnesium) that corrodes instead of iron (2 marks)

1 mark for naming method, 1 mark for explanation.

---

### Q5. Bioleaching [3 marks]

- Bacteria are used to break down low-grade copper ores (1 mark)
- Bacteria produce a solution (leachate) containing copper ions (1 mark)
- Copper is extracted from the solution by displacement with scrap iron or electrolysis (1 mark)

---

### Q6. Aluminium Electrolysis

**(a) Cathode material:** [1 mark]
Carbon (or carbon-lined steel)

**(b) Half-equation:** [2 marks]
```
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
```
1 mark for Al³⁺ and Al, 1 mark for 3 electrons.

**(c) Why molten:** [2 marks]
- Ions must be free to move (1 mark)
- In solid state, ions are fixed in position and cannot carry current (1 mark)

---

### Q7. Sacrificial Protection

**(a) Definition:** [2 marks]
A method of preventing rusting where a more reactive metal (1 mark) is attached to iron and corrodes instead of the iron (1 mark).

**(b) Why zinc works:** [2 marks]
- Zinc is more reactive than iron (1 mark)
- Zinc loses electrons more readily and corrodes preferentially (1 mark)

**(c) Example:** [1 mark]
Any valid example:
- Ship hulls
- Underground pipelines
- Oil rigs
- Galvanized roofs/buckets

---

### Q8. Aluminium Properties for Power Cables [2 marks]

Any two from:
- Good conductor of electricity (1 mark)
- Low density (lightweight) (1 mark)
- Resistant to corrosion (1 mark)
- Ductile (can be drawn into wires) (1 mark)

---

### Q9. Copper Extraction with Scrap Iron

**(a) Ionic equation:** [2 marks]
```
Cu²⁺(aq) + Fe(s) → Cu(s) + Fe²⁺(aq)
```
1 mark for correct species, 1 mark for balanced equation.

**(b) Why iron displaces copper:** [2 marks]
- Iron is more reactive than copper (1 mark)
- More reactive metals can displace less reactive metals from their compounds (1 mark)

---

### Q10. Comparison of Iron and Aluminium Extraction [4 marks]

| Aspect | Iron | Aluminium |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Ore | Haematite (Fe₂O₃) | Bauxite (Al₂O₃) |
| Method | Blast furnace (reduction with CO) | Electrolysis |

**Similarity:** [1 mark]
- Both involve extraction from oxide ores
- Both require high temperatures
- Both involve reduction of the metal

**Difference:** [1 mark]
- Iron uses chemical reduction (with CO), aluminium uses electrolysis
- Iron extraction is continuous, aluminium extraction is batch process
- Different energy requirements

Award marks for valid comparisons.

---

### Q11. Anode Replacement [3 marks]

- Oxygen is produced at the anode (1 mark)
- Oxygen reacts with the carbon anode (1 mark)
- Equation: C + O₂ → CO₂ (1 mark)

---

### Q12. Stainless Steel

**(a) Composition:** [2 marks]
- Iron (1 mark)
- Chromium and nickel (1 mark)

**(b) Why better than pure iron:** [2 marks]
- Harder and stronger than pure iron (1 mark)
- Resistant to corrosion/rusting (1 mark)

**(c) Why doesn't rust:** [2 marks]
- Chromium forms a protective oxide layer (1 mark)
- This layer prevents further oxidation/corrosion (1 mark)
