## 22.1 What is Organic Chemistry?

### Definition
**Organic chemistry** is the branch of chemistry that deals with the study of carbon-containing compounds (excluding carbonates, oxides of carbon, and carbides).

### Why Carbon is Special

Carbon is unique because:
1. **Can form four covalent bonds** - allowing it to bond with up to four other atoms
2. **Can form chains and rings** - carbon atoms can bond to each other in straight chains, branched chains, and rings
3. **Forms strong covalent bonds** - C-C bonds are strong and stable
4. **Can form double and triple bonds** - with other carbon atoms or other elements
5. **Forms bonds with many elements** - especially hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and halogens

### Number of Organic Compounds
- Over **10 million** known organic compounds
- This is more than all other elements' compounds combined

## 22.2 Crude Oil as a Source of Hydrocarbons

### What is Crude Oil?

**Crude oil** is a fossil fuel formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient marine organisms (plankton and algae) buried under layers of sediment and subjected to high pressure and temperature.

### Key Properties of Crude Oil
- **Appearance**: Dark, viscous liquid
- **Odour**: Strong, unpleasant smell
- **Composition**: Mixture of hydrocarbons (mostly alkanes)
- **Location**: Found in porous rocks underground

### Formation of Crude Oil
1. Marine organisms die and sink to the sea floor
2. Covered by layers of sediment
3. High pressure and temperature over millions of years
4. Organic matter transforms into crude oil and natural gas

### What are Hydrocarbons?
A **hydrocarbon** is a compound containing **only hydrogen and carbon atoms**.

### Types of Hydrocarbons
| Type | Contains | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Alkanes** | C-C single bonds only | Methane, ethane, propane |
| **Alkenes** | At least one C=C double bond | Ethene, propene |
| **Alkynes** | C≡C triple bonds | Ethyne (not in IGCSE) |

## 22.3 Fractional Distillation of Crude Oil

### Purpose
Crude oil is a **mixture** of many different hydrocarbons. Fractional distillation separates these into useful fractions based on their **boiling points**.

### Process of Fractional Distillation

1. **Heating**: Crude oil is heated to about **350-400°C** in a furnace
2. **Vaporization**: Most of the oil turns into vapour
3. **Fractionating column**: Hot vapour enters the column (hotter at bottom, cooler at top)
4. **Condensation**: Vapours rise and cool; each fraction condenses at its boiling point range
5. **Collection**: Fractions are collected at different levels

### The Fractionating Column
- **Temperature gradient**: Hot at bottom (~350°C), cool at top (~40°C)
- **Trays**: Bubble caps allow vapour to rise but collect liquid
- **Molecular size**: Smaller molecules rise higher (lower boiling points)

### Fractions and Their Uses

| Fraction | Boiling Point Range | Carbon Chain Length | Uses |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|------|
| **Refinery gases** | Below 40°C | C₁-C₄ | Bottled gas, camping gas |
| **Gasoline (petrol)** | 40-100°C | C₅-C₁₀ | Fuel for cars |
| **Naphtha** | 70-200°C | C₆-C₁₄ | Chemical feedstock, cracking |
| **Kerosene (paraffin)** | 150-250°C | C₁₀-C₁₆ | Jet fuel, paraffin heaters |
| **Diesel oil** | 250-350°C | C₁₄-C₂₀ | Fuel for lorries, buses, trains |
| **Fuel oil** | 350-450°C | C₂₀-C₄₀ | Ships, power stations |
| **Lubricating oil** | Above 350°C | C₂₀-C₅₀ | Engine oil, grease |
| **Bitumen** | Residue | C₅₀+ | Road surfacing, roofing |

### Key Points About Fractions
- **Smaller molecules**: Lower boiling point, more volatile, lighter colour, flow more easily
- **Larger molecules**: Higher boiling point, less volatile, darker colour, more viscous
- **Flammability**: Smaller molecules are more flammable

## 22.4 Homologous Series

### Definition
A **homologous series** is a family of organic compounds with:
1. The same **general formula**
2. Similar **chemical properties**
3. A gradual change in **physical properties** (e.g., boiling point)
4. Each member differs from the next by **-CH₂-**

### Characteristics of Homologous Series

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **General formula** | All members follow the same algebraic formula |
| **Functional group** | Same reactive group gives similar chemistry |
| **CH₂ difference** | Each successive member differs by CH₂ |
| **Gradation** | Physical properties change gradually with chain length |
| **Naming** | Systematic naming based on number of carbons |

### Main Homologous Series (IGCSE)

| Series | General Formula | Functional Group | Example |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|---------|
| **Alkanes** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ | None (C-C single bonds) | Methane, CH₄ |
| **Alkenes** | CₙH₂ₙ | C=C double bond | Ethene, C₂H₄ |
| **Alcohols** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH | -OH (hydroxyl) | Ethanol, C₂H₅OH |
| **Carboxylic acids** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH | -COOH (carboxyl) | Ethanoic acid, CH₃COOH |

## 22.5 Naming Organic Compounds

### The IUPAC System
Organic compounds are named systematically using the **IUPAC** (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) system.

### Naming Steps
1. **Count the carbon atoms** in the longest chain → determines the prefix
2. **Identify the functional group** → determines the suffix
3. **Number any branches** from the end nearest the functional group

### Prefixes for Carbon Chain Length

| Number of Carbons | Prefix | Example |
|-------------------|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Meth-** | Methane |
| 2 | **Eth-** | Ethane |
| 3 | **Prop-** | Propane |
| 4 | **But-** | Butane |
| 5 | **Pent-** | Pentane |
| 6 | **Hex-** | Hexane |
| 7 | **Hept-** | Heptane |
| 8 | **Oct-** | Octane |

### Naming Rules by Series

#### Alkanes
- Prefix + "-ane"
- Examples: Methane (1C), Ethane (2C), Propane (3C), Butane (4C)

#### Alkenes
- Prefix + "-ene"
- Double bond position indicated by number
- Examples: Ethene (C=C), Propene (C₃H₆), But-1-ene

#### Alcohols
- Prefix + "-anol" (replace final -e with -ol)
- Examples: Methanol, Ethanol, Propanol

#### Carboxylic Acids
- Prefix + "-anoic acid" (replace final -e with -oic acid)
- Examples: Methanoic acid, Ethanoic acid, Propanoic acid

## 22.6 General Formulas for Organic Compounds

### 22.6.1 Alkanes

**General Formula**: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

| Name | Molecular Formula | Structural Formula | Boiling Point |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Methane | CH₄ | CH₄ | -161°C |
| Ethane | C₂H₆ | CH₃CH₃ | -89°C |
| Propane | C₃H₈ | CH₃CH₂CH₃ | -42°C |
| Butane | C₄H₁₀ | CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃ | -0.5°C |
| Pentane | C₅H₁₂ | CH₃(CH₂)₃CH₃ | 36°C |
| Hexane | C₆H₁₄ | CH₃(CH₂)₄CH₃ | 69°C |

**Properties**:
- **Saturated** hydrocarbons (single C-C bonds only)
- Generally unreactive (called **paraffins** = "little affinity")
- Burn in air (combustion)
- Undergo substitution reactions with halogens (UV light)

**Key Reactions**:
```
Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
Substitution: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (UV light)
```

---

### 22.6.2 Alkenes

**General Formula**: CₙH₂ₙ

| Name | Molecular Formula | Structural Formula |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Ethene | C₂H₄ | CH₂=CH₂ |
| Propene | C₃H₆ | CH₂=CHCH₃ |
| Butene | C₄H₈ | CH₂=CHCH₂CH₃ (or but-1-ene) |
| Pentene | C₅H₁₀ | CH₂=CH(CH₂)₂CH₃ |

**Properties**:
- **Unsaturated** hydrocarbons (contain C=C double bond)
- More reactive than alkanes
- Undergo addition reactions
- Decolourise bromine water (test for unsaturation)

**Key Reactions**:
```
Addition with H₂: C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆ (ethane)
Addition with Br₂: C₂H₄ + Br₂ → C₂H₄Br₂ (1,2-dibromoethane)
Combustion: C₂H₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 2H₂O
```

**Test for Unsaturation**:
- Add bromine water (orange/brown)
- Alkenes decolourise bromine water (turns colourless)
- Alkanes do not decolourise bromine water

---

### 22.6.3 Alcohols

**General Formula**: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH or CₙH₂ₙ₊₂O

| Name | Molecular Formula | Structural Formula |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Methanol | CH₃OH | CH₃OH |
| Ethanol | C₂H₅OH | CH₃CH₂OH |
| Propanol | C₃H₇OH | CH₃CH₂CH₂OH |
| Butanol | C₄H₉OH | CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂OH |

**Properties**:
- Functional group: **-OH** (hydroxyl group)
- Soluble in water (small alcohols)
- Flammable
- Can be oxidised to carboxylic acids

**Key Reactions**:
```
Combustion: C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O
Oxidation: C₂H₅OH + 2[O] → CH₃COOH + H₂O
(ethanol → ethanoic acid)
```

**Uses of Ethanol**:
- Alcoholic beverages
- Fuel (bioethanol)
- Solvent
- Disinfectant

---

### 22.6.4 Carboxylic Acids

**General Formula**: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH

| Name | Molecular Formula | Structural Formula |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Methanoic acid | HCOOH | HCOOH |
| Ethanoic acid | CH₃COOH | CH₃COOH |
| Propanoic acid | C₂H₅COOH | CH₃CH₂COOH |
| Butanoic acid | C₃H₇COOH | CH₃CH₂CH₂COOH |

**Properties**:
- Functional group: **-COOH** (carboxyl group)
- Weak acids (partially dissociate in water)
- Characteristic sharp smell (vinegar = ethanoic acid)
- React with bases to form salts
- React with alcohols to form esters

**Key Reactions**:
```
With metals: 2CH₃COOH + Mg → (CH₃COO)₂Mg + H₂
With bases: CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
With carbonates: 2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂
Esterification: CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O
```

## 22.7 Structural Isomerism

### Definition
**Structural isomers** are compounds with the **same molecular formula** but **different structural formulas**.

### Example: Butane (C₄H₁₀)
Has two isomers:
1. **Butane**: CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃ (straight chain)
2. **2-methylpropane**: CH₃-CH(CH₃)-CH₃ (branched)

### Example: Pentane (C₅H₁₂)
Has three isomers:
1. **Pentane**: CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃
2. **2-methylbutane**: CH₃-CH(CH₃)-CH₂-CH₃
3. **2,2-dimethylpropane**: C(CH₃)₄

## 22.8 Bonding in Organic Molecules

### Types of Bonds

**Covalent Bonding in Hydrocarbons**:
- Each carbon forms **4 covalent bonds**
- Each hydrogen forms **1 covalent bond**
- Single bonds (σ bonds): strong overlap
- Double bonds: one σ bond + one π bond

### Representing Structures

| Representation | Example (Ethene) |
|----------------|------------------|
| **Molecular formula** | C₂H₄ |
| **Structural formula** | CH₂=CH₂ |
| **Displayed formula** | H H<br>││<br>C=C<br>││<br>H H |
| **Skeletal formula** | Triangle/diagram with lines |

## Common Exam Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Hydrocarbon Definition
❌ "Hydrocarbons contain carbon and hydrogen, and sometimes oxygen"
✅ "Hydrocarbons contain ONLY carbon and hydrogen"

### Mistake 2: Saturated vs Unsaturated
❌ "Saturated means contains many hydrogen atoms"
✅ "Saturated = contains only single C-C bonds; Unsaturated = contains C=C double bonds"

### Mistake 3: General Formulas
❌ Using wrong general formula (e.g., CₙH₂ₙ for alkanes)
✅ Memorise: Alkanes = CₙH₂ₙ₊₂; Alkenes = CₙH₂ₙ

### Mistake 4: Prefixes
❌ Confusing prop- (3C) with pent- (5C)
✅ Meth-1, Eth-2, Prop-3, But-4, Pent-5, Hex-6

### Mistake 5: Functional Groups
❌ "Alcohol functional group is OH"
✅ "Alcohol functional group is -OH (hydroxyl group), shown as a specific group attached to carbon"

### Mistake 6: Boiling Point Trends
❌ "Larger molecules have lower boiling points"
✅ "Larger molecules have HIGHER boiling points (stronger intermolecular forces)"

### Mistake 7: Fractional Distillation
❌ "Smallest molecules condense first at the bottom"
✅ "Smallest molecules have lowest boiling points, rise to the TOP and condense there"

### Mistake 8: Bromine Water Test
❌ "All hydrocarbons decolourise bromine water"
✅ "Only UNSATURATED hydrocarbons (alkenes) decolourise bromine water"

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Organic chemistry** | Study of carbon-containing compounds (excluding carbonates, oxides of carbon, and carbides) |
| **Hydrocarbon** | Compound containing only hydrogen and carbon |
| **Saturated** | Containing only single bonds between carbon atoms |
| **Unsaturated** | Containing at least one double bond between carbon atoms |
| **Homologous series** | Family of compounds with same general formula and similar properties, differing by CH₂ |
| **Functional group** | Atom or group of atoms responsible for characteristic reactions |
| **General formula** | Algebraic formula that applies to all members of a homologous series |
| **Fractional distillation** | Separation technique based on different boiling points |
| **Structural isomers** | Compounds with same molecular formula but different structural arrangements |
| **Crude oil** | Mixture of hydrocarbons formed from ancient marine organisms |
