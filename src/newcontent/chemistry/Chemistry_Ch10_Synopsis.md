# Chapter 10: Electrolysis - Concise Synopsis

## Quick Revision Guide for Pearson Edexcel IGCSE Chemistry

---

## Chapter Overview

This chapter covers electrolysis - the use of electricity to decompose ionic compounds. You need to understand how electrolysis works, predict products at electrodes, and apply this to industrial processes like aluminium extraction and electroplating.

---

## Key Points Summary

### 1. Basic Principles

> **Electrolysis** = Decomposition of an ionic compound using electricity

**Essential terms:**
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Electrolyte** | Ionic compound that conducts when molten/aqueous |
| **Cathode** | Negative electrode (attracts cations) |
| **Anode** | Positive electrode (attracts anions) |

### 2. What Happens at Each Electrode

```
CATHODE (−)          ANODE (+)
    ↓                    ↓
Cations (+)          Anions (−)
move here            move here
    ↓                    ↓
  GAIN                LOSE
electrons            electrons
    ↓                    ↓
REDUCTION           OXIDATION
```

**Remember:** OILRIG
- **O**xidation **I**s **L**oss of electrons
- **R**eduction **I**s **G**ain of electrons

### 3. Electrolysis of Molten Compounds (Simple!)

- **Cathode:** Metal forms (cations reduced)
- **Anode:** Non-metal forms (anions oxidised)

| Compound | Cathode product | Anode product |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Molten NaCl | Na (sodium) | Cl₂ (chlorine) |
| Molten PbBr₂ | Pb (lead) | Br₂ (bromine) |

### 4. Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions (More Complex!)

Water provides H⁺ and OH⁻ ions, so you need discharge rules:

**At the cathode:**
- Metal LESS reactive than H → **Metal** is produced
- Metal MORE reactive than H → **Hydrogen** is produced

**At the anode:**
- Halide ion present (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) → **Halogen** is produced
- No halide ion → **Oxygen** is produced

### Quick Prediction Table

| Electrolyte (aqueous) | Cathode product | Anode product |
|-----------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| NaCl | H₂ | Cl₂ |
| CuCl₂ | Cu | Cl₂ |
| CuSO₄ | Cu | O₂ |
| Na₂SO₄ | H₂ | O₂ |
| KNO₃ | H₂ | O₂ |

### 5. Extraction of Aluminium

**Key facts:**
- Ore: **Bauxite** (contains Al₂O₃)
- Electrolyte: Molten Al₂O₃ dissolved in **cryolite**
- **Cryolite** lowers melting point from 2050°C to 900°C
- Anodes are made of **carbon** and burn away

**Reactions:**
- Cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
- Anode: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
- Anode burns: C + O₂ → CO₂

### 6. Purification of Copper

| Electrode | Material | What happens |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| Anode (+) | Impure Cu | Dissolves: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ |
| Cathode (−) | Pure Cu | Deposits: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu |
| Electrolyte | CuSO₄ solution | Concentration stays constant |

### 7. Electroplating

**Purpose:** Coat an object with a thin layer of metal

**Setup:**
- **Cathode** = Object to be plated
- **Anode** = Plating metal
- **Electrolyte** = Solution of plating metal ions

**Example (silver plating):**
- Anode: Pure silver
- Cathode: Object (e.g., spoon)
- Electrolyte: Silver nitrate solution

## Essential Half-Equations

### Must Know These:

| Electrode | Half-equation | Process |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Cathode | 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ | Hydrogen from water |
| Cathode | Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu | Copper deposition |
| Cathode | Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al | Aluminium extraction |
| Anode | 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ | Chlorine from chloride |
| Anode | 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ | Oxygen from water |

## Key Definitions to Learn

1. **Electrolysis** - Decomposition of an ionic compound using electricity

2. **Electrolyte** - An ionic compound that conducts electricity when molten or in aqueous solution

3. **Cathode** - The negative electrode (where cations go)

4. **Anode** - The positive electrode (where anions go)

5. **Electroplating** - Coating an object with a metal using electrolysis

## Exam Tips

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ "Ions move through the wires"
✓ Ions move through the **electrolyte**; electrons flow through the **wires**

❌ "Hydrogen is always produced at the cathode"
✓ Only when the metal is **more reactive than hydrogen**

❌ "Oxygen is always produced at the anode"
✓ Only when **no halide ions** are present

❌ "Carbon anodes don't change"
✓ In aluminium extraction, carbon anodes **burn away**

### Memory Aids

**Cathode vs Anode:**
- **C**athode = **C**ations go there
- **Ca**thode = **Ca**tch electrons (negative)

**Discharge rules at cathode:**
- Use the reactivity series!
- K-Na-Ca-Mg-Al-Zn-Fe-Sn-Pb-H-Cu-Ag-Au
- Metals above H → hydrogen produced
- Metals below H → metal produced

## One-Minute Revision Summary

1. **Electrolysis needs mobile ions** - electrolyte must be molten or aqueous
2. **Cathode = negative** - cations gain electrons (reduction)
3. **Anode = positive** - anions lose electrons (oxidation)
4. **Aqueous solutions** - remember H⁺ and OH⁻ from water compete
5. **Cathode rules** - reactive metal → H₂; unreactive metal → metal
6. **Anode rules** - halide → halogen; no halide → O₂
7. **Aluminium** - cryolite lowers MP, carbon anodes burn away
8. **Copper purification** - transfers from impure anode to pure cathode
9. **Electroplating** - object is cathode, plating metal is anode
