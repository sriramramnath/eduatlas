## 11.1 Introduction to Group 1 Elements

### Definition
**Alkali metals** are the elements in Group 1 of the Periodic Table. They are called "alkali metals" because they react with water to form alkaline solutions (metal hydroxides).

### Elements in Group 1
| Element | Symbol | Atomic Number | Electronic Structure |
|---------|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| Lithium | Li | 3 | 2.1 |
| Sodium | Na | 11 | 2.8.1 |
| Potassium | K | 19 | 2.8.8.1 |
| Rubidium | Rb | 37 | 2.8.18.8.1 |
| Caesium | Cs | 55 | 2.8.18.18.8.1 |
| Francium | Fr | 87 | 2.8.18.32.18.8.1 |

## 11.2 Physical Properties of Alkali Metals

### General Physical Properties

| Property | Description | Trend Down the Group |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Appearance | Soft, silvery metals | Become softer down the group |
| Density | Low density (Li, Na, K float on water) | Increases down the group |
| Melting Point | Low for metals | Decreases down the group |
| Boiling Point | Low for metals | Decreases down the group |
| Conductivity | Good conductors of heat and electricity | Remains good throughout |

### Detailed Explanations

#### Softness
- Alkali metals are **soft** and can be cut with a knife
- Lithium is the hardest, becoming progressively softer down the group
- Caesium is the softest - like butter at room temperature
- **Reason**: Large atomic radii and only one delocalised electron per atom result in weak metallic bonding

#### Low Density
- Lithium, sodium, and potassium all **float on water**
- This is unusual for metals
- **Reason**: Atoms are large with low mass (low atomic mass to volume ratio)

#### Low Melting and Boiling Points
- Melting points range from 28°C (Cs) to 180°C (Li)
- Much lower than typical metals (e.g., iron melts at 1538°C)
- **Trend**: Melting and boiling points **decrease down the group**
- **Reason**: Metallic bonding becomes weaker as atomic radius increases (outer electron is further from nucleus, weaker attraction)

## 11.3 Electronic Configuration and Reactivity

### Electronic Structure
- All alkali metals have **one electron in their outer shell**
- Electronic configuration ends in ...s¹
- This single outer electron is easily lost to form a **1+ ion** (cation)

```
Li: 2.1        → Li⁺: 2
Na: 2.8.1      → Na⁺: 2.8
K: 2.8.8.1     → K⁺: 2.8.8
```

### Why Are Alkali Metals So Reactive?

1. **Single outer electron**: Only need to lose one electron to achieve stable noble gas configuration
2. **Low ionization energy**: The outer electron is easily removed
3. **Electropositive character**: Strong tendency to lose electrons

### Trend in Reactivity Down the Group

**Reactivity INCREASES down the group**: Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs

#### Explanation
| Factor | Effect Down Group | Impact on Reactivity |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Atomic radius | Increases | Outer electron further from nucleus |
| Shielding effect | Increases | More inner shells block nuclear attraction |
| Nuclear charge | Increases | But effect outweighed by shielding |
| Net attraction on outer electron | Decreases | Electron lost more easily |
| Ionization energy | Decreases | Easier to form M⁺ ion |

**Key Point**: Although nuclear charge increases down the group, the increased distance and shielding mean the outer electron is held less tightly and is more easily lost.

## 11.4 Chemical Reactions

### 11.4.1 Reaction with Water

#### General Equation
$$2M + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2MOH + H_2$$

Where M = Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs

#### Observations for Each Metal

| Metal | Observations | Equation |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Lithium | - Floats on water<br>- Moves slowly<br>- Fizzes gently<br>- Gradually disappears | 2Li + 2H₂O → 2LiOH + H₂ |
| Sodium | - Floats on water<br>- Melts into a ball (exothermic reaction)<br>- Moves rapidly on surface<br>- Fizzes vigorously | 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ |
| Potassium | - Floats on water<br>- Melts instantly<br>- Moves very rapidly<br>- Catches fire with lilac flame<br>- Fizzes very vigorously | 2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂ |
| Rubidium | - Explosive reaction<br>- Immediate ignition | 2Rb + 2H₂O → 2RbOH + H₂ |
| Caesium | - Violent explosion<br>- Extremely dangerous | 2Cs + 2H₂O → 2CsOH + H₂ |

#### Products
- **Metal hydroxide**: Alkaline solution (pH > 7)
- **Hydrogen gas**: Effervescence observed

#### Testing for Hydrogen
- Collect gas in a test tube
- Apply a lighted splint
- **Positive result**: "Squeaky pop" sound

### 11.4.2 Reaction with Oxygen

Alkali metals react with oxygen to form different types of oxides depending on the metal and conditions.

#### Types of Oxides Formed

| Oxide Type | Formula | Name | Formed By |
|------------|---------|------|-----------|
| Normal oxide | M₂O | Oxide | Li mainly |
| Peroxide | M₂O₂ | Peroxide | Na mainly |
| Superoxide | MO₂ | Superoxide | K, Rb, Cs |

#### Equations

**Lithium with oxygen:**
$$4Li + O_2 \rightarrow 2Li_2O \text{ (lithium oxide)}$$

**Sodium with oxygen:**
$$4Na + O_2 \rightarrow 2Na_2O \text{ (sodium oxide)}$$
$$2Na + O_2 \rightarrow Na_2O_2 \text{ (sodium peroxide)}$$

**Potassium with oxygen:**
$$K + O_2 \rightarrow KO_2 \text{ (potassium superoxide)}$$

#### Observations When Burning
- Lithium: Burns with a **crimson-red flame**
- Sodium: Burns with a **yellow-orange flame**
- Potassium: Burns with a **lilac (purple) flame**

---

### 11.4.3 Reaction with Chlorine

Alkali metals react vigorously with chlorine gas to form ionic chlorides (salts).

#### General Equation
$$2M + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2MCl$$

#### Specific Equations

**Lithium with chlorine:**
$$2Li + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2LiCl \text{ (lithium chloride)}$$

**Sodium with chlorine:**
$$2Na + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2NaCl \text{ (sodium chloride)}$$

**Potassium with chlorine:**
$$2K + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2KCl \text{ (potassium chloride)}$$

#### Observations
- Vigorous reaction
- Bright white flame
- White solid produced (metal chloride)
- Chlorine's greenish-yellow colour disappears

## 11.5 Compounds of Alkali Metals

### Common Compounds

| Compound | Formula | Uses |
|----------|---------|------|
| Sodium chloride | NaCl | Table salt, food preservation |
| Sodium hydroxide | NaOH | Soap making, drain cleaners |
| Sodium carbonate | Na₂CO₃ | Glass making, water softening |
| Sodium hydrogencarbonate | NaHCO₃ | Baking powder, antacid |
| Potassium hydroxide | KOH | Soap making (soft soap) |
| Potassium nitrate | KNO₃ | Fertilizers, gunpowder |
| Lithium carbonate | Li₂CO₃ | Treatment of bipolar disorder |

### Properties of Alkali Metal Compounds
- All common compounds are **ionic**
- Generally **white or colourless** solids
- **Soluble in water** (with few exceptions)
- Form **colourless solutions** unless anion is coloured
- Solutions are usually **alkaline** (hydroxides and carbonates)

## 11.6 Safety Precautions

### Hazards of Alkali Metals

| Hazard | Risk | Safety Measure |
|--------|------|----------------|
| Reactivity with water | Fire, explosion | Store under oil, handle in dry conditions |
| Reactivity with air | Oxidation, fire | Store in airtight containers |
| Skin contact | Burns | Wear gloves, use forceps |
| Eye contact | Serious damage | Wear safety goggles |
| Fire | Difficult to extinguish | Use dry sand or dry powder extinguisher |

### Storage
- Alkali metals are stored **under oil** (usually paraffin oil)
- This prevents reaction with:
  - Moisture in the air
  - Oxygen in the air
- Lithium floats on the oil, sodium and potassium sink

### Handling Guidelines
1. **Never touch with bare hands** - moisture on skin causes reaction
2. **Cut under oil** - fresh surfaces are highly reactive
3. **Use small pieces** - reduces risk of violent reactions
4. **Work in a fume cupboard** - when reacting with water
5. **Have sand ready** - to smother any fires
6. **Never use water** to extinguish alkali metal fires

### Disposal
- React leftover metal with ethanol (less vigorous than water)
- Allow complete reaction before disposal
- Never dispose of unreacted metal in water

## 11.7 Common Exam Mistakes

### ❌ Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Incorrect trend direction**
   - Mistake: Saying reactivity decreases down the group
   - Correct: Reactivity **increases** down the group

2. **Confusing melting point trend**
   - Mistake: Melting points increase down the group
   - Correct: Melting points **decrease** down the group

3. **Wrong explanation for reactivity**
   - Mistake: "Nuclear charge decreases down the group"
   - Correct: Nuclear charge increases, but the outer electron is further from nucleus and experiences more shielding, so it's lost more easily

4. **Incorrect formula for compounds**
   - Mistake: Writing LiO instead of Li₂O
   - Correct: Alkali metals form M⁺ ions, so oxide is M₂O

5. **Forgetting the hydrogen product**
   - Mistake: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH
   - Correct: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂

6. **Wrong explanation for storage**
   - Mistake: Stored under oil to stop them sinking
   - Correct: Stored under oil to prevent reaction with air and moisture

7. **Confusing Group 1 with Group 7 trends**
   - Group 1: Reactivity increases down the group
   - Group 7: Reactivity decreases down the group

8. **Incorrect flame colours**
   - Lithium: Crimson-red (NOT just "red")
   - Sodium: Yellow-orange (NOT "orange")
   - Potassium: Lilac (NOT "pink")

## 11.9 Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Alkali metals | Elements in Group 1 of the Periodic Table that react with water to produce alkaline solutions |
| Metallic bonding | The electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and delocalised electrons |
| Ionization energy | The energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms of an element |
| Shielding effect | The reduction in the effective nuclear charge on the outer electrons due to repulsion by inner shell electrons |
