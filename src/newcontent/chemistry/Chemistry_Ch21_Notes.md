## 21.1 Reversible Reactions

### Definition
A **reversible reaction** is a chemical reaction in which the products can react together to form the original reactants. The reaction can proceed in both forward and reverse directions.

### Symbol Notation
- Reversible reactions are represented using the **reversible arrow symbol**: ⇌
- The forward reaction (left to right) and reverse reaction (right to left) occur simultaneously

### Example Reactions

**1. Thermal Decomposition of Ammonium Chloride**
```
NH₄Cl(s) ⇌ NH₃(g) + HCl(g)
```
- On heating: forward reaction produces ammonia and hydrogen chloride gases
- On cooling: white crystals of ammonium chloride reform

**2. Hydration of Anhydrous Copper(II) Sulfate**
```
CuSO₄(s) + 5H₂O(l) ⇌ CuSO₄·5H₂O(s)
(white)                    (blue)
```
- Adding water: white anhydrous copper sulfate turns blue
- Heating: blue hydrated copper sulfate turns white

**3. Dehydration of Hydrated Cobalt(II) Chloride**
```
CoCl₂·6H₂O(s) ⇌ CoCl₂(s) + 6H₂O(l)
(pink)           (blue)
```
- Used as a test for water

**4. Reaction Between Nitrogen Dioxide and Dinitrogen Tetroxide**
```
2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g)
(brown)   (colourless)
```
- Temperature affects the equilibrium position

## 21.2 Chemical Equilibrium

### Definition
**Chemical equilibrium** is the state in a reversible reaction where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction, and the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant.

### Key Characteristics of Equilibrium

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Closed system** | Equilibrium can only be reached in a closed system where no substances can enter or leave |
| **Dynamic** | Both forward and reverse reactions continue to occur |
| **Constant concentrations** | The amounts of reactants and products stay the same (but are not necessarily equal) |
| **No macroscopic changes** | No observable changes in properties (colour, pressure, etc.) |

### The Equilibrium Position
- The **equilibrium position** describes the relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium
- If position is **to the right**: more products than reactants
- If position is **to the left**: more reactants than products

### Important Points to Remember
- Equilibrium does NOT mean the reaction has stopped
- Equilibrium does NOT mean equal amounts of reactants and products
- At equilibrium, both reactions are happening at the same rate

---

## 21.3 Le Chatelier's Principle

### Definition
**Le Chatelier's Principle** states that if a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change, the equilibrium position will shift to counteract the effect of that change.

### How to Apply the Principle
When a change is made to a system at equilibrium:
1. Identify what the change is
2. Work out how the system would "counteract" the change
3. Determine which direction the equilibrium shifts

## 21.4 Effects of Changing Conditions on Equilibrium

### 21.4.1 Effect of Concentration Changes

| Change | Equilibrium Shifts |
|--------|-------------------|
| Increase [reactant] | To the right (towards products) |
| Decrease [reactant] | To the left (towards reactants) |
| Increase [product] | To the left (towards reactants) |
| Decrease [product] | To the right (towards products) |

**Explanation**: The system shifts to reduce the effect of the concentration change.

**Example**: In the reaction:
```
Fe³⁺(aq) + SCN⁻(aq) ⇌ FeSCN²⁺(aq)
(yellow)              (blood red)
```
- Adding more Fe³⁺ ions shifts equilibrium right → deeper red colour
- Adding more SCN⁻ ions shifts equilibrium right → deeper red colour

---

### 21.4.2 Effect of Temperature Changes

The effect depends on whether the reaction is **exothermic** (releases heat) or **endothermic** (absorbs heat).

| Reaction Type | Increase Temperature | Decrease Temperature |
|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Exothermic** (ΔH negative) | Shifts left (towards reactants) | Shifts right (towards products) |
| **Endothermic** (ΔH positive) | Shifts right (towards products) | Shifts left (towards reactants) |

**Explanation**: 
- Treating heat as a reactant (endothermic forward) or product (exothermic forward)
- System shifts to counteract temperature change

**Example**: 
```
2NO₂(g) ⇌ N₂O₄(g)    ΔH = -58 kJ/mol (exothermic forward)
(brown)   (colourless)
```
- **Increase temperature**: equilibrium shifts left → more brown NO₂
- **Decrease temperature**: equilibrium shifts right → more colourless N₂O₄

---

### 21.4.3 Effect of Pressure Changes

Pressure only affects equilibria involving **gases**. It depends on the number of **moles of gas** on each side of the equation.

| Change | Equilibrium Shifts |
|--------|-------------------|
| Increase pressure | Towards side with fewer gas molecules |
| Decrease pressure | Towards side with more gas molecules |
| Same number of gas molecules on both sides | No effect |

**Example**:
```
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)
4 mol gas        2 mol gas
```
- **Increase pressure**: shifts right (towards fewer gas molecules)
- **Decrease pressure**: shifts left (towards more gas molecules)

**Important**: Pressure changes do NOT affect equilibria where equal moles of gas appear on both sides.

---

### 21.4.4 Effect of Catalysts

- A catalyst **does NOT affect the equilibrium position**
- It speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally
- Equilibrium is reached **more quickly** but the **position remains the same**
- The yield of products is unchanged

## 21.5 The Haber Process

### Overview
The **Haber process** is the industrial method for manufacturing ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen.

### The Reaction
```
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)    ΔH = -92 kJ/mol
```
- **Forward reaction is exothermic**
- 4 moles of gas become 2 moles of gas

### Sources of Raw Materials
| Raw Material | Source |
|--------------|--------|
| Nitrogen (N₂) | Air (fractional distillation) |
| Hydrogen (H₂) | Natural gas (methane) or cracking of hydrocarbons |

### Optimum Conditions

| Condition | Value Used | Reasoning |
|-----------|------------|-----------|
| **Temperature** | 450°C | Compromise: lower temp favours NH₃ but slows rate |
| **Pressure** | 200 atm | High pressure favours NH₃ (fewer moles of gas) |
| **Catalyst** | Iron (Fe) | Speeds up reaction, reduces energy costs |
| **Process** | Recycle unreacted gases | Improves overall yield to ~98% |

### Applying Le Chatelier's Principle to Haber Process

**Temperature (450°C)**:
- Lower temperature favours NH₃ formation (exothermic forward reaction)
- BUT at low temperatures, reaction rate is too slow
- 450°C is a **compromise** between yield and rate

**Pressure (200 atm)**:
- High pressure favours NH₃ (fewer gas moles on right)
- Higher pressure = higher yield
- BUT very high pressure is expensive and dangerous
- 200 atm is a **compromise** between yield and safety/cost

**Catalyst (Iron)**:
- Does not affect equilibrium position
- Allows reaction to proceed at a reasonable rate at lower temperatures
- Reduces energy costs

### Flow Diagram
```
N₂ + 3H₂ → Compressor → Converter (450°C, 200 atm, Fe catalyst)
                                           ↓
                                    Cooling chamber
                                           ↓
                              NH₃ liquefies and is removed
                                           ↓
                              Unreacted N₂ and H₂ recycled
```

### Uses of Ammonia
1. **Manufacture of fertilizers**: ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, urea
2. **Manufacture of nitric acid** (Ostwald process)
3. **Manufacture of nylon**
4. **Cleaning products**
5. **Refrigeration**

## 21.6 Industrial Applications

### Contact Process (Manufacture of Sulfuric Acid)

**Stage 1**: Sulfur burned in air
```
S(s) + O₂(g) → SO₂(g)
```

**Stage 2**: Sulfur dioxide oxidized to sulfur trioxide
```
2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g)    ΔH = -197 kJ/mol (exothermic)
```

**Optimum Conditions for Stage 2**:
| Condition | Value Used | Reasoning |
|-----------|------------|-----------|
| Temperature | 450°C | Compromise: yield vs rate |
| Pressure | 1-2 atm | Only slight pressure effect |
| Catalyst | Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) | Speeds up reaction |

**Stage 3**: Sulfur trioxide dissolved in concentrated sulfuric acid
```
SO₃(g) + H₂SO₄(l) → H₂S₂O₇(l) (oleum)
```

**Stage 4**: Oleum diluted with water
```
H₂S₂O₇(l) + H₂O(l) → 2H₂SO₄(l)
```

### Uses of Sulfuric Acid
1. Manufacture of fertilizers (ammonium sulfate, superphosphates)
2. Manufacture of detergents
3. Manufacture of paints and pigments
4. Car batteries
5. Dehydrating agent

## Common Exam Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Equilibrium Misconceptions
❌ "At equilibrium, the reaction has stopped"
✅ "At equilibrium, the forward and reverse reactions occur at equal rates (dynamic equilibrium)"

### Mistake 2: Equilibrium Position
❌ "At equilibrium, reactant and product concentrations are equal"
✅ "At equilibrium, reactant and product concentrations remain constant (not necessarily equal)"

### Mistake 3: Closed System
❌ Forgetting to mention that equilibrium requires a closed system
✅ Always state that equilibrium can only occur in a closed system

### Mistake 4: Temperature Effect Direction
❌ Getting the direction of shift wrong for exo/endothmic reactions
✅ Remember: "Increase temp shifts equilibrium in endothermic direction"

### Mistake 5: Pressure Effect
❌ Assuming pressure always affects equilibrium
✅ Only affects equilibria with different numbers of gas moles on each side

### Mistake 6: Catalyst Effect
❌ "A catalyst increases the yield of products"
✅ "A catalyst speeds up the reaction but does NOT change the equilibrium position or yield"

### Mistake 7: Haber Process Compromise
❌ Stating the "best" conditions without considering practical limitations
✅ Explain that conditions are a compromise between theoretical yield, rate, safety, and cost

### Mistake 8: Reversible Arrow
❌ Using single arrow (→) for reversible reactions
✅ Always use reversible arrow (⇌) for equilibrium reactions

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Reversible reaction** | A reaction where products can react to form the original reactants |

| **Closed system** | System where no substances can enter or leave |
| **Le Chatelier's Principle** | When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to counteract the change |
| **Equilibrium position** | Relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium |

| **Compromise conditions** | Conditions chosen to balance yield, rate, and cost |
