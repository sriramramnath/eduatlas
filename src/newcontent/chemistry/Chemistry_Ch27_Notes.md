## 1. Introduction to Carboxylic Acids

### Definition
**Carboxylic acids** are a homologous series of organic compounds that contain the **carboxyl functional group** (-COOH). They are weak acids found naturally in many foods and biological systems.

### Importance in the Specification
- Understanding functional groups in organic chemistry
- Recognizing the relationship between structure and properties
- Explaining weak acid behavior
- Understanding industrial and everyday applications

---

## 2. General Formula and Structure

### General Formula
The general formula for carboxylic acids is:

**CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH**

where n = 0, 1, 2, 3, etc.

Alternatively written as: **CₙH₂ₙO₂** (for the molecular formula)

### The Carboxyl Group
The **carboxyl group** (-COOH) consists of:
- A carbonyl group (C=O)
- A hydroxyl group (-OH) attached to the same carbon

```
     O
     ||
   R-C-OH
```

Where R = alkyl group or H

### Structural Features
| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Functional group | -COOH (carboxyl group) |
| Bonding | Contains C=O double bond and C-O single bond |
| Polarity | Highly polar due to -COOH group |
| Hydrogen bonding | Can form hydrogen bonds with water |

## 3. Naming Carboxylic Acids

### Naming Rules
1. Count the total number of carbon atoms in the chain (including the carboxyl carbon)
2. Use the appropriate prefix (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-)
3. Add the suffix "-oic acid"

### First Four Carboxylic Acids

| Name | Molecular Formula | Structural Formula | Number of C Atoms |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Methanoic acid** | HCOOH | HCOOH | 1 |
| **Ethanoic acid** | CH₃COOH | CH₃COOH | 2 |
| **Propanoic acid** | C₂H₅COOH | CH₃CH₂COOH | 3 |
| **Butanoic acid** | C₃H₇COOH | CH₃CH₂CH₂COOH | 4 |

### Detailed Structures

#### Methanoic Acid (Formic Acid)
- Simplest carboxylic acid
- Found in ant stings
- Formula: HCOOH
```
    O
    ||
  H-C-OH
```

#### Ethanoic Acid (Acetic Acid)
- Most well-known carboxylic acid
- Main component of vinegar
- Formula: CH₃COOH
```
        O
        ||
  CH₃-C-OH
```

#### Propanoic Acid
- Used as preservative
- Formula: C₂H₅COOH
```
         O
         ||
  CH₃-CH₂-C-OH
```

#### Butanoic Acid
- Found in rancid butter
- Formula: C₃H₇COOH
```
            O
            ||
  CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-C-OH
```

## 4. Physical Properties of Carboxylic Acids

### Solubility in Water

| Carboxylic Acid | Solubility in Water | Reason |
|-----------------|---------------------|--------|
| Methanoic acid | Very soluble | Small molecule, strong H-bonding |
| Ethanoic acid | Very soluble | Can form H-bonds with water |
| Propanoic acid | Soluble | Moderate H-bonding ability |
| Butanoic acid | Less soluble | Larger hydrophobic hydrocarbon chain |

**Key Point**: As the carbon chain length increases, solubility decreases because the non-polar hydrocarbon part becomes larger relative to the polar carboxyl group.

### Boiling Points

| Carboxylic Acid | Boiling Point (°C) | Reason for Trend |
|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Methanoic acid | 101 | Small molecule |
| Ethanoic acid | 118 | Moderate size |
| Propanoic acid | 141 | Increasing molecular mass |
| Butanoic acid | 164 | Largest of the four |

**Key Explanation**: Carboxylic acids have **higher boiling points** than alcohols of similar molecular mass because:
- They form **dimers** (pairs of molecules) through hydrogen bonding
- More energy is required to separate these associated molecules

### Volatility and Odor
- **Lower carboxylic acids** (C₁-C₃): Sharp, pungent odors
- **Ethanoic acid**: Vinegar smell
- **Butanoic acid**: Unpleasant "rancid butter" smell
- Volatility decreases as molecular mass increases

## 5. Chemical Properties of Carboxylic Acids

### 5.1 Weak Acid Behavior

#### What Makes Carboxylic Acids Weak Acids?

Carboxylic acids are **weak acids** because they **partially dissociate** in water:

```
CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ CH₃COO⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq)
```

Only a small fraction of the molecules dissociate to release H⁺ ions.

#### Comparison with Strong Acids

| Property | Carboxylic Acids (Weak) | Strong Acids (e.g., HCl) |
|----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Dissociation | Partial | Complete |
| H⁺ concentration | Lower | Higher |
| pH (same concentration) | Higher (3-5) | Lower (0-2) |
| Reaction rate | Slower | Faster |
| Conductivity | Lower | Higher |

#### Evidence for Weak Acid Nature

1. **pH testing**: Aqueous solutions have pH around 3-5 (not 0-2 like strong acids)
2. **Reaction rate**: Reacts more slowly with metals/carbonates than strong acids
3. **Conductivity**: Lower electrical conductivity than strong acids of same concentration

### 5.2 Reactions with Metals

Carboxylic acids react with **reactive metals** to produce a **salt** and **hydrogen gas**:

**General Equation:**
```
Carboxylic acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen
```

**Examples:**

1. **Ethanoic acid + Magnesium:**
```
2CH₃COOH(aq) + Mg(s) → (CH₃COO)₂Mg(aq) + H₂(g)
```
*Salt formed: Magnesium ethanoate*

2. **Ethanoic acid + Zinc:**
```
2CH₃COOH(aq) + Zn(s) → (CH₃COO)₂Zn(aq) + H₂(g)
```
*Salt formed: Zinc ethanoate*

3. **Ethanoic acid + Sodium:**
```
CH₃COOH(aq) + Na(s) → CH₃COONa(aq) + ½H₂(g)
```
*Or: 2CH₃COOH + 2Na → 2CH₃COONa + H₂*
*Salt formed: Sodium ethanoate*

**Observations:**
- Effervescence (bubbling)
- Slow reaction compared to strong acids
- Metal gradually dissolves
- Gas produced burns with a "pop" (hydrogen)

### 5.3 Reactions with Bases (Alkalis)

Carboxylic acids undergo **neutralization reactions** with bases:

**General Equation:**
```
Carboxylic acid + Base → Salt + Water
```

**Examples:**

1. **Ethanoic acid + Sodium hydroxide:**
```
CH₃COOH(aq) + NaOH(aq) → CH₃COONa(aq) + H₂O(l)
```
*Sodium ethanoate + water*

2. **Ethanoic acid + Potassium hydroxide:**
```
CH₃COOH(aq) + KOH(aq) → CH₃COOK(aq) + H₂O(l)
```
*Potassium ethanoate + water*

3. **Propanoic acid + Sodium hydroxide:**
```
C₂H₅COOH(aq) + NaOH(aq) → C₂H₅COONa(aq) + H₂O(l)
```
*Sodium propanoate + water*

### 5.4 Reactions with Carbonates

Carboxylic acids react with carbonates to produce a **salt**, **water**, and **carbon dioxide**:

**General Equation:**
```
Carboxylic acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + Carbon dioxide
```

**Examples:**

1. **Ethanoic acid + Sodium carbonate:**
```
2CH₃COOH(aq) + Na₂CO₃(s) → 2CH₃COONa(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
```

2. **Ethanoic acid + Sodium hydrogencarbonate:**
```
CH₃COOH(aq) + NaHCO₃(s) → CH₃COONa(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
```

3. **Ethanoic acid + Calcium carbonate:**
```
2CH₃COOH(aq) + CaCO₃(s) → (CH₃COO)₂Ca(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
```

**Observations:**
- Effervescence (bubbling)
- Solid carbonate dissolves
- Limewater turns milky (test for CO₂)

### 5.5 Reaction with Metal Oxides

**General Equation:**
```
Carboxylic acid + Metal oxide → Salt + Water
```

**Example:**
```
2CH₃COOH(aq) + CuO(s) → (CH₃COO)₂Cu(aq) + H₂O(l)
```
*Copper(II) ethanoate + water*

## 7. Comparison with Other Organic Compounds

### Comparison Table

| Property | Carboxylic Acids | Alcohols | Alkenes |
|----------|------------------|----------|---------|
| Functional group | -COOH | -OH | C=C |
| Acidity | Weak acid | Very weakly acidic | Not acidic |
| Reaction with Na | Salt + H₂ | Alkoxide + H₂ | No reaction |
| Reaction with NaOH | Salt + H₂O | No reaction | No reaction |
| Reaction with Na₂CO₃ | Salt + H₂O + CO₂ | No reaction | No reaction |
| pH of aqueous solution | 3-5 | ~7 | Insoluble |

### Why Carboxylic Acids are More Acidic than Alcohols

1. The carboxyl group stabilizes the negative charge on the conjugate base
2. The carboxylate ion (RCOO⁻) is resonance-stabilized
3. This makes dissociation more favorable

## 9. Laboratory Tests for Carboxylic Acids

### Test 1: Sodium Carbonate Test
- **Procedure**: Add solid sodium carbonate to the unknown solution
- **Observation for carboxylic acid**: Effervescence, gas turns limewater milky
- **Explanation**: CO₂ gas is produced

### Test 2: pH Test
- **Procedure**: Test with universal indicator paper or solution
- **Observation for carboxylic acid**: pH 3-5 (orange-yellow color)
- **Explanation**: Weak acid nature

### Test 3: Reaction with Reactive Metals
- **Procedure**: Add magnesium ribbon to the solution
- **Observation for carboxylic acid**: Slow effervescence, gas burns with "pop"
- **Explanation**: Hydrogen gas is produced

### Distinguishing Carboxylic Acids from Alcohols
- Both react with sodium to produce hydrogen
- **Only carboxylic acids** react with carbonates
- Use sodium carbonate to distinguish between them

## 10. Common Exam Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Incorrect Formula Writing
❌ **Wrong**: Writing CH₃COOH as C₂H₄O₂ without showing the functional group
✅ **Correct**: Always show the carboxyl group clearly: CH₃COOH

### Mistake 2: Confusing Salt Names
❌ **Wrong**: "Sodium ethanoic acid" or "sodium acetate" (though acetate is accepted in some contexts)
✅ **Correct**: Sodium ethanoate

### Mistake 3: State Symbols
❌ **Wrong**: Forgetting state symbols in equations
✅ **Correct**: Always include appropriate state symbols:
```
CH₃COOH(aq) + NaOH(aq) → CH₃COONa(aq) + H₂O(l)
```

### Mistake 4: Weak Acid Explanation
❌ **Wrong**: "Carboxylic acids are weak because they don't fully react"
✅ **Correct**: "Carboxylic acids are weak because they partially dissociate in water, releasing only some H⁺ ions"

### Mistake 5: Balancing Equations
❌ **Wrong**: 
```
CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂ (unbalanced)
```
✅ **Correct**:
```
2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂
```

### Mistake 6: Comparing Acid Strength
❌ **Wrong**: "Carboxylic acids are weaker acids than water"
✅ **Correct**: "Carboxylic acids are stronger acids than water but weaker than mineral acids like HCl"

### Mistake 7: Number of Carbon Atoms
❌ **Wrong**: Counting only the alkyl chain carbons
✅ **Correct**: The carboxyl carbon is always counted as part of the chain

### Mistake 8: Explaining High Boiling Points
❌ **Wrong**: "High boiling points because they are acids"
✅ **Correct**: "High boiling points due to extensive hydrogen bonding and dimer formation"

## 11. Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Carboxylic acid** | An organic compound containing the carboxyl functional group (-COOH) |
| **Carboxyl group** | The functional group -COOH, consisting of a carbonyl and hydroxyl group on the same carbon |
| **Weak acid** | An acid that partially dissociates in water, releasing only a fraction of its H⁺ ions |
| **Dimer** | Two identical molecules joined together, often by hydrogen bonds |
| **Vinegar** | A dilute solution of ethanoic acid (about 5%) used in food preservation |
| **Neutralization** | Reaction between an acid and a base to form a salt and water |
| **Carboxylate ion** | The negative ion formed when a carboxylic acid loses an H⁺ ion (RCOO⁻) |

## 13. Exam Tips

1. **Always show the carboxyl group** in structural formulas
2. **Balance equations carefully** - especially with carbonates (2:1 ratio often needed)
3. **Explain weak acid behavior** using partial dissociation
4. **Know the first four carboxylic acids** by name and formula
5. **Remember vinegar is dilute ethanoic acid** (5-8%)
6. **Use sodium carbonate** to distinguish carboxylic acids from alcohols
7. **Include state symbols** in all equations
8. **Name salts correctly** - change "-oic acid" to "-oate"
