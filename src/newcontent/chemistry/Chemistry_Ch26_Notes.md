## 26.1 Introduction to Alcohols

### Definition
**Alcohols** are a homologous series of organic compounds containing the **hydroxyl functional group (-OH)** attached to a carbon chain. They are widely used as solvents, fuels, and in the production of many chemicals.

### Key Characteristics
- **Functional Group**: Hydroxyl group (-OH)
- **General Formula**: CnH2n+1OH (or CnH2n+2O)
- **Homologous Series**: Each member differs by CH2 from the next
- **Naming Suffix**: "-ol"

---

## 26.2 General Formula and Structure of Alcohols

### General Formula
**CnH2n+1OH** or **CnH2n+2O**

### Molecular Formulas of the First Four Alcohols

| Name | n | Molecular Formula | Structural Formula |
|------|---|-------------------|-------------------|
| Methanol | 1 | CH3OH | CH3OH |
| Ethanol | 2 | C2H5OH | CH3CH2OH |
| Propanol | 3 | C3H7OH | CH3CH2CH2OH |
| Butanol | 4 | C4H9OH | CH3CH2CH2CH2OH |

### The Hydroxyl Group (-OH)

#### Structure
- The **hydroxyl group** (-OH) is the functional group that gives alcohols their characteristic properties
- The oxygen atom is bonded to:
  - One hydrogen atom
  - One carbon atom (from the hydrocarbon chain)

#### Displayed Formula

**Methanol (CH3OH)**:
```
      H
      |
  H - C - O - H
      |
      H
```

**Ethanol (C2H5OH)**:
```
    H H
    | |
H - C - C - O - H
    | |
    H H
```

**Propanol (C3H7OH)**:
```
    H H H
    | | |
H - C - C - C - O - H
    | | |
    H H H
```

**Butanol (C4H9OH)**:
```
    H H H H
    | | | |
H - C - C - C - C - O - H
    | | | |
    H H H H
```

## 26.3 Naming Alcohols

### Naming Rules (IUPAC System)

1. **Count the carbon atoms** in the longest chain containing the -OH group
2. **Use the appropriate prefix**:
   - 1 carbon: **meth-**
   - 2 carbons: **eth-**
   - 3 carbons: **prop-**
   - 4 carbons: **but-**
   - 5 carbons: **pent-**
   - 6 carbons: **hex-**

3. **Add the suffix "-ol"** to indicate an alcohol

4. **For alcohols with 3+ carbons**: Add a number to show the position of the -OH group
   - Number from the end that gives the **lowest number** for the -OH group

### The First Four Alcohols

| Carbon Atoms | Prefix | Name | Formula |
|--------------|--------|------|---------|
| 1 | meth- | Methanol | CH3OH |
| 2 | eth- | Ethanol | C2H5OH |
| 3 | prop- | Propan-1-ol | C3H7OH |
| 4 | but- | Butan-1-ol | C4H9OH |

### Isomerism in Alcohols

#### Propanol Isomers
- **Propan-1-ol**: CH3CH2CH2OH (OH on end carbon)
- **Propan-2-ol**: CH3CH(OH)CH3 (OH on middle carbon)

#### Butanol Isomers
- **Butan-1-ol**: CH3CH2CH2CH2OH
- **Butan-2-ol**: CH3CH2CH(OH)CH3

### Important Note
For IGCSE, you mainly need to know the "straight-chain" alcohols (propan-1-ol and butan-1-ol), but you should be aware that isomers exist.

## 26.4 Properties of Alcohols

### Physical Properties

#### 1. Appearance and State
- Colorless liquids at room temperature
- Characteristic smell (ethanol has a "spirit-like" odor)
- Volatile (evaporate easily)

#### 2. Boiling Points
| Alcohol | Boiling Point (°C) |
|---------|-------------------|
| Methanol | 65 |
| Ethanol | 78 |
| Propanol | 97 |
| Butanol | 118 |

**Trend**: Boiling point **increases** as the carbon chain length increases.
- This is due to stronger **intermolecular forces** between larger molecules
- More energy is needed to overcome these forces

#### 3. Solubility in Water
- **Small alcohols** (methanol, ethanol, propanol) are **completely miscible** with water
- They dissolve freely in water in all proportions
- **Larger alcohols** become less soluble as the carbon chain increases

**Why are alcohols soluble in water?**
- The -OH group can form **hydrogen bonds** with water molecules
- This allows alcohols to mix with water

#### 4. Flammability
- Alcohols are **flammable**
- They burn in air to produce carbon dioxide and water
- Methanol and ethanol are used as fuels

### Chemical Properties

#### Comparison with Water
Alcohols share some properties with water because they both contain -OH groups:

| Property | Alcohols | Water |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Contains -OH group | Yes | Yes (H-OH) |
| Can form hydrogen bonds | Yes | Yes |
| Neutral pH | Yes (pH 7) | Yes |
| Reacts with sodium | Yes (produces hydrogen) | Yes (produces hydrogen) |

#### Reaction with Sodium
Alcohols react with sodium metal to produce:
- **Hydrogen gas** (H2)
- **Sodium alkoxide** (e.g., sodium ethoxide from ethanol)

**Equation**:
```
2C2H5OH + 2Na → 2C2H5ONa + H2
Ethanol + Sodium → Sodium ethoxide + Hydrogen
```

**Observation**: Sodium sinks in ethanol, then bubbles of hydrogen gas are produced. The reaction is less vigorous than with water.

## 26.5 Reactions of Alcohols

### 1. Combustion of Alcohols

Alcohols burn completely in air (oxygen) to produce carbon dioxide and water.

#### General Equation
```
Alcohol + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water
```

#### Specific Example: Combustion of Ethanol
```
C2H5OH + 3O2 → 2CO2 + 3H2O
```

**Word Equation**:
Ethanol + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water

#### Observations
- Burns with a **clean, blue flame**
- No smoke produced (unlike alkenes)
- Releases heat energy

#### Use as Fuels
- **Ethanol** is used as a fuel:
  - In **spirit burners**
  - Added to **petrol** (gasohol)
  - In some countries, cars run on ethanol (bioethanol)
- Methanol is used as a fuel in **racing cars**

---

### 2. Oxidation of Alcohols

Alcohols can be **oxidized** to form carboxylic acids (for primary alcohols like methanol and ethanol).

#### Oxidizing Agents
- **Acidified potassium dichromate** (K2Cr2O7/H2SO4)
- **Potassium manganate(VII)** (KMnO4)

#### Reaction with Acidified Potassium Dichromate

**Observation**:
- The orange dichromate solution **turns green**
- This indicates oxidation has occurred

**Equation for Ethanol Oxidation**:
```
C2H5OH + 2[O] → CH3COOH + H2O
Ethanol + Oxidizing agent → Ethanoic acid + Water
```

**Word Equation**:
Ethanol → Ethanoic acid + Water (by oxidation)

#### Complete Oxidation Pathway

```
Alcohol → Aldehyde → Carboxylic Acid
(may go through aldehyde stage, but for IGCSE, focus on alcohol → carboxylic acid)
```

#### Specific Examples

| Alcohol | Oxidation Product |
|---------|-------------------|
| Methanol (CH3OH) | Methanoic acid (HCOOH) |
| Ethanol (C2H5OH) | Ethanoic acid (CH3COOH) |
| Propanol (C3H7OH) | Propanoic acid (C2H5COOH) |
| Butanol (C4H9OH) | Butanoic acid (C3H7COOH) |

---

### 3. Dehydration of Alcohols

**Dehydration** is the removal of water from a compound. Alcohols can be dehydrated to form **alkenes**.

#### General Equation
```
Alcohol → Alkene + Water
```

#### Conditions
- **Catalyst**: Concentrated sulfuric acid (H2SO4) or concentrated phosphoric acid (H3PO4)
- **Temperature**: Heated (typically 170°C with sulfuric acid)
- **Type of reaction**: Elimination reaction

#### Specific Example: Dehydration of Ethanol
```
C2H5OH → C2H4 + H2O
Ethanol → Ethene + Water
```

**Conditions**: Concentrated sulfuric acid catalyst, heat

**Word Equation**:
Ethanol → Ethene + Water (by dehydration)

#### Mechanism (Simplified)
The -OH group and a hydrogen atom from an adjacent carbon are removed as a water molecule, forming a C=C double bond.

## 26.6 Production of Ethanol

Ethanol can be produced by two main industrial methods:

### Method 1: Fermentation

#### Process
Fermentation uses **yeast** to convert sugars into ethanol and carbon dioxide.

**Equation**:
```
C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2
Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide
```

**Word Equation**:
Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide (by fermentation with yeast)

#### Conditions
- **Catalyst**: Yeast (contains enzymes)
- **Temperature**: 25-35°C (optimal for yeast enzymes)
- **Anaerobic conditions**: No oxygen present
- **Dilute solution**: Typically 10-15% ethanol

#### Why Temperature is Limited
- Below 25°C: Reaction is too slow
- Above 35°C: Yeast enzymes are **denatured** (stop working)
- Optimum temperature is around 30°C

#### Why the Process Stops
- When ethanol concentration reaches about **15%**, the yeast is killed
- This is why fermentation produces a dilute ethanol solution
- Further concentration requires **distillation**

#### Sources of Sugar
- **Sugar cane** or **sugar beet** (directly contain sugar)
- **Barley** (starch converted to sugar by malting)
- **Corn** (starch source)
- **Fruit juices** (contain natural sugars)

---

### Method 2: Hydration of Ethene

#### Process
Ethene (from cracking of crude oil) reacts with steam to produce ethanol.

**Equation**:
```
C2H4 + H2O ⇌ C2H5OH
Ethene + Steam ⇌ Ethanol
```

**Word Equation**:
Ethene + Steam ⇌ Ethanol (by hydration)

#### Conditions
- **Catalyst**: Phosphoric acid (H3PO4) on silica support
- **Temperature**: 300°C
- **Pressure**: 60-70 atmospheres

#### Reversible Reaction
- The reaction is **reversible**
- Not all ethene is converted to ethanol
- Unreacted ethene and steam are **recycled** to improve yield

---

## 26.7 Comparing Fermentation and Hydration Methods

| Feature | Fermentation | Hydration of Ethene |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Raw Materials** | Renewable (sugar from plants) | Non-renewable (crude oil) |
| **Energy Requirements** | Low (ambient temperature) | High (300°C, 60-70 atm) |
| **Rate of Reaction** | Slow (days to weeks) | Fast (continuous process) |
| **Product Purity** | Dilute (10-15% ethanol) | Pure ethanol produced |
| **Equipment Needed** | Simple equipment | Complex, expensive equipment |
| **Type of Process** | Batch process | Continuous process |
| **Sustainability** | Sustainable, carbon-neutral | Non-sustainable |
| **Cost** | Low setup cost | High setup cost |
| **Atom Economy** | Lower (CO2 by-product) | Higher (100% atom economy) |

### Advantages and Disadvantages

#### Fermentation

**Advantages:**
- Uses **renewable resources** (sugar crops)
- **Low energy** requirements
- **Low technology** - simple equipment
- **Carbon neutral** - CO2 released was recently absorbed by plants
- Can use **waste materials** (fruit pulp, etc.)

**Disadvantages:**
- **Slow** process (takes days)
- Produces **dilute** ethanol solution
- Requires **distillation** to concentrate
- **Batch process** - not continuous
- Competes with **food production** for land

#### Hydration of Ethene

**Advantages:**
- **Fast** reaction rate
- Produces **pure** ethanol
- **Continuous** process
- **High atom economy** (100% theoretical)
- **Large scale** production possible

**Disadvantages:**
- Uses **non-renewable** crude oil
- **High energy** requirements
- **Expensive** equipment needed
- **Reversible reaction** - not 100% conversion
- Contributes to **greenhouse gases**

## 26.10 Common Exam Mistakes

### 1. Formula Errors
❌ Writing CnH2nOH instead of CnH2n+1OH
✅ Remember: The alcohol has **one extra hydrogen** before the -OH

### 2. Fermentation Conditions
❌ Stating "high temperature" for fermentation
✅ Optimal temperature is **25-35°C** - higher temperatures denature yeast enzymes

### 3. Hydration Conditions
❌ Forgetting the catalyst or pressure for hydration
✅ **Phosphoric acid catalyst**, 300°C, **60-70 atm pressure**

### 4. Oxidation Products
❌ Writing "alcohol oxidizes to aldehyde"
✅ For IGCSE, state that alcohol oxidizes to **carboxylic acid**

### 5. Dehydration
❌ Confusing dehydration with hydration
✅ **Hydration** = addition of water (ethene → ethanol)
✅ **Dehydration** = removal of water (ethanol → ethene)

### 6. Reversible Reaction Symbol
❌ Using → instead of ⇌ for hydration of ethene
✅ The hydration reaction is **reversible**, use the **reversible arrow (⇌)**

### 7. Comparison Questions
❌ Just listing features without explaining
✅ Always **compare both methods** point by point with explanations

### 8. Distillation
❌ Not mentioning distillation for purifying fermentation products
✅ Fermentation produces **dilute ethanol** that needs **distillation**

## Key Takeaways

✓ Alcohols contain the **hydroxyl functional group (-OH)** with formula CnH2n+1OH

✓ Ethanol is the most important alcohol: used as fuel, solvent, and in drinks

✓ Alcohols undergo **combustion** (produce CO2 + H2O), **oxidation** (produce carboxylic acids), and **dehydration** (produce alkenes)

✓ Ethanol can be produced by **fermentation** (sugar + yeast, renewable) or **hydration** (ethene + steam, non-renewable)

✓ **Fermentation**: 25-35°C, anaerobic, yeast enzymes; **Hydration**: H3PO4 catalyst, 300°C, 60-70 atm

✓ Oxidation of ethanol produces **ethanoic acid** (orange dichromate turns green)

✓ Dehydration of ethanol produces **ethene** (using concentrated sulfuric acid)
