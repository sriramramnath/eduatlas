## 19.1 Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

### Key Definitions

**Exothermic Reaction**: A chemical reaction that releases thermal energy to the surroundings, resulting in a temperature increase in the surroundings.

**Endothermic Reaction**: A chemical reaction that absorbs thermal energy from the surroundings, resulting in a temperature decrease in the surroundings.

**Enthalpy Change (ΔH)**: The overall energy change during a chemical reaction, measured in kilojoules per mole (kJ/mol).

- **ΔH is negative (-)** for exothermic reactions (energy is released)
- **ΔH is positive (+)** for endothermic reactions (energy is absorbed)

### Understanding Energy Changes

During any chemical reaction:
1. **Energy must be supplied** to break bonds in the reactants
2. **Energy is released** when new bonds form in the products

The overall enthalpy change depends on the difference between:
- Energy absorbed to break bonds (endothermic process)
- Energy released when forming bonds (exothermic process)

### Examples of Exothermic Reactions

| Reaction | Equation | Application |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Combustion | CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O | Heating, cooking, engines |
| Neutralisation | HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O | Indigestion remedies |
| Oxidation reactions | 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO | Flares, fireworks |
| Respiration | C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O | Energy release in cells |
| Hand warmers | Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃ (rusting) | Disposable hand warmers |
| Self-heating cans | CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ | Self-heating food/drinks |

### Examples of Endothermic Reactions

| Reaction | Equation | Application |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Thermal decomposition | CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ | Making quicklime |
| Photosynthesis | 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ | Plant energy storage |
| Cracking of hydrocarbons | Long chain → short chain + alkene | Oil refinery |
| Sports injury packs | NH₄NO₃ + H₂O (dissolving) | Cold packs |
| Electrolysis | 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ | Hydrogen production |
| Citric acid + sodium hydrogencarbonate | C₆H₈O₇ + 3NaHCO₃ → Products | Baking, cooling |

## 19.2 Energy Level Diagrams

### Structure of Energy Level Diagrams

Energy level diagrams show the relative energy levels of reactants and products:

```
EXOTHERMIC REACTION:

Energy
   ↑
   |      Reactants
   |         ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |              \
   |               \  ΔH (negative)
   |                \
   |                 ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |                Products
   |____________________________→
            Reaction pathway
```

```
ENDOTHERMIC REACTION:

Energy
   ↑
   |                 ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |                Products
   |                /
   |               /  ΔH (positive)
   |              /
   |      Reactants
   |         ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |____________________________→
            Reaction pathway
```

### Key Features to Label

1. **Horizontal axis**: Reaction pathway (or progress of reaction)
2. **Vertical axis**: Energy (in kJ/mol)
3. **Reactants level**: Starting energy level
4. **Products level**: Final energy level
5. **ΔH arrow**: Shows enthalpy change with direction and value
6. **Activation energy (Ea)**: Energy hump between reactants and activated complex

### Important Points

- The **height difference** between reactants and products = ΔH
- For exothermic: products are **lower** than reactants
- For endothermic: products are **higher** than reactants
- The **activation energy barrier** is always present (even for exothermic reactions)

## 19.3 Bond Breaking and Bond Making

### Bond Energy

**Bond Energy**: The amount of energy required to break one mole of a particular covalent bond, measured in kJ/mol.

### Bond Breaking (Endothermic)

- Breaking bonds **always requires energy input**
- Energy is **absorbed** from the surroundings
- This is an **endothermic process**
- ΔH for bond breaking is **positive (+)**

### Bond Making (Exothermic)

- Making new bonds **always releases energy**
- Energy is **released** to the surroundings
- This is an **exothermic process**
- ΔH for bond making is **negative (-)**

### Common Bond Energies Table

| Bond | Bond Energy (kJ/mol) |
|------|---------------------|
| C–C | 347 |
| C=C | 612 |
| C≡C | 838 |
| C–H | 413 |
| C–O | 358 |

| H–H | 436 |
| N≡N | 946 |
| C–N | 286 |
| H–Cl | 432 |
| H–Br | 366 |
| Cl–Cl | 243 |
| Br–Br | 193 |

### Energy Summary

| Process | Energy Direction | ΔH Sign |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| Bond breaking | Absorbed (IN) | Positive (+) |
| Bond making | Released (OUT) | Negative (-) |

## 19.4 Calculating Energy Changes from Bond Energies

### The Formula

**ΔH = Σ(Bond energies of bonds broken) – Σ(Bond energies of bonds formed)**

Or alternatively:
**ΔH = Energy absorbed – Energy released**

### Step-by-Step Method

1. **Write the balanced equation** with displayed formulae showing all bonds
2. **Identify all bonds broken** in the reactants
3. **Identify all bonds formed** in the products
4. **Calculate total energy for bonds broken** (sum of bond energies)
5. **Calculate total energy for bonds formed** (sum of bond energies)
6. **Apply the formula**: ΔH = Energy in – Energy out

### Worked Example 1: Combustion of Methane

**Equation**: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

**Step 1**: Draw displayed formulae

Reactants:
- CH₄: 4 × C–H bonds
- 2O₂: 2 × O=O bonds

Products:
- CO₂: 2 × C=O bonds
- 2H₂O: 2 × (2 × O–H) = 4 × O–H bonds

**Step 2**: Calculate energy required to break bonds
- 4 × C–H = 4 × 413 = 1652 kJ/mol
- 2 × O=O = 2 × 498 = 996 kJ/mol
- **Total energy in** = 1652 + 996 = **2648 kJ/mol**

**Step 3**: Calculate energy released when bonds form
- 2 × C=O = 2 × 805 = 1610 kJ/mol
- 4 × O–H = 4 × 464 = 1856 kJ/mol
- **Total energy out** = 1610 + 1856 = **3466 kJ/mol**

**Step 4**: Calculate ΔH
- ΔH = 2648 – 3466 = **–818 kJ/mol** (exothermic)

### Worked Example 2: Hydrogenation of Ethene

**Equation**: C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆

**Bonds broken**:
- C=C: 612 kJ/mol
- 4 × C–H: 4 × 413 = 1652 kJ/mol
- H–H: 436 kJ/mol
- **Total in** = 612 + 1652 + 436 = **2700 kJ/mol**

**Bonds formed**:
- C–C: 347 kJ/mol
- 6 × C–H: 6 × 413 = 2478 kJ/mol
- **Total out** = 347 + 2478 = **2825 kJ/mol**

**ΔH** = 2700 – 2825 = **–125 kJ/mol** (exothermic)

## 19.5 Activation Energy

### Definition

**Activation Energy (Ea)**: The minimum amount of energy that particles must have to collide successfully and react.

### Key Points

1. Activation energy is **always required** for a reaction to start
2. It represents the **energy barrier** that must be overcome
3. Even exothermic reactions need activation energy to begin
4. Activation energy is **supplied** by:
   - Heating
   - Light (in photochemical reactions)
   - Spark or flame (combustion)
   - Sound waves (sonochemistry)

### Energy Level Diagrams with Activation Energy

```
EXOTHERMIC REACTION with Ea:

Energy
   ↑
   |      Reactants
   |         ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |              \
   |               \ Ea (activation energy)
   |                ×  (activated complex)
   |               /
   |              /
   |             /
   |            /
   |           ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |          Products
   |____________________________→
       Reaction pathway
```

```
ENDOTHERMIC REACTION with Ea:

Energy
   ↑
   |                ×  (activated complex)
   |               / \
   |              /   \ Ea (activation energy)
   |             /     \
   |            /       ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |           /        Products
   |          /
   |         /
   |   ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |   Reactants
   |____________________________→
       Reaction pathway
```

### The Activated Complex

- The **activated complex** (or transition state) is the temporary, unstable arrangement of atoms
- It exists at the **peak** of the activation energy barrier
- It has **higher energy** than both reactants and products
- It exists for only a **very short time**

## 19.6 Applications of Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

### Exothermic Applications

#### 1. Hand Warmers
- **Disposable hand warmers**: Iron powder oxidises (rusts)
  - Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃ (exothermic)
  - Lasts 1-10 hours
  - Air activates the reaction when packet is opened

- **Reusable hand warmers**: Sodium ethanoate crystallisation
  - Supersaturated solution crystallises when metal disc clicked
  - Can be reused by boiling to redissolve crystals

#### 2. Self-Heating Cans
- Reaction: CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂
- Water and calcium oxide kept separate until needed
- Button pressed to mix reactants
- Heats food/drink in 3-5 minutes

#### 3. Combustion Fuels
- Natural gas, petrol, diesel, coal
- Release large amounts of energy for:
  - Heating buildings
  - Cooking
  - Transport (engines)
  - Electricity generation

#### 4. Neutralisation
- Indigestion remedies: Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O
- Quick-relief from stomach acid

### Endothermic Applications

#### 1. Sports Injury Cold Packs
- Ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃) dissolving in water
- NH₄NO₃(s) → NH₄⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)
- Absorbs heat from surroundings
- Reduces swelling and pain

#### 2. Cooking
- Baking powder reactions are endothermic
- Thermal decomposition reactions in cooking
- Proteins denaturing (requires energy)

#### 3. Instant Cold Drinks
- Urea or ammonium salts dissolve in water
- Can lower temperature by 10-15°C

#### 4. Cooling Processes
- Endothermic reactions used in some cooling systems
- Melting of ice (physical endothermic process)

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Sign Conventions
❌ **Wrong**: "Exothermic reactions have positive ΔH"
✅ **Correct**: Exothermic reactions have **negative** ΔH (energy lost to surroundings)

### 2. Activation Energy
❌ **Wrong**: "Exothermic reactions don't need activation energy"
✅ **Correct**: **ALL** reactions need activation energy to start

### 3. Bond Breaking vs Making
❌ **Wrong**: "Breaking bonds releases energy"
✅ **Correct**: Breaking bonds **requires/absorbs** energy; making bonds releases energy

### 4. Energy Level Diagrams
❌ **Wrong**: Drawing products higher than reactants for exothermic reactions
✅ **Correct**: Products are **lower** than reactants for exothermic reactions

### 5. Bond Energy Calculations
❌ **Wrong**: Using the wrong number of bonds (not counting all bonds)
✅ **Correct**: Count **every single bond** in displayed formulae

### 6. Units
❌ **Wrong**: Forgetting units or using incorrect units (J instead of kJ)
✅ **Correct**: Always include units: **kJ/mol**

### 7. Temperature vs Heat
❌ **Wrong**: Confusing temperature change with energy change
✅ **Correct**: Temperature is measured in °C; energy is measured in kJ/mol

### 8. Reversible Reactions
❌ **Wrong**: Assuming all exothermic reactions go to completion
✅ **Correct**: Exothermic in one direction means endothermic in reverse

## Key Equations and Relationships

| Concept | Equation/Relationship |
|---------|----------------------|
| Enthalpy change | ΔH = Energy in (bonds broken) – Energy out (bonds formed) |
| Energy change sign | Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (negative); Endothermic: ΔH > 0 (positive) |
| Bond energy | Energy to break one mole of a specific covalent bond |
| Activation energy | Minimum energy for successful collision |

## Summary Table: Exothermic vs Endothermic

| Feature | Exothermic | Endothermic |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| ΔH sign | Negative (-) | Positive (+) |
| Energy transfer | Released TO surroundings | Absorbed FROM surroundings |
| Temperature change | Increases | Decreases |
| Products energy level | Lower than reactants | Higher than reactants |
| Bond energy relationship | Bonds formed release more than bonds broken absorb | Bonds broken absorb more than bonds formed release |
| Example | Combustion, neutralisation | Photosynthesis, thermal decomposition |

## Exam Tips

1. **Always state the sign of ΔH** in your answer
2. **Include units** (kJ/mol) for all energy values
3. **Draw clear, labelled** energy level diagrams
4. **Show all working** in bond energy calculations
5. **Use displayed formulae** to count bonds accurately
6. **Remember**: breaking = energy IN; making = energy OUT
7. **Activation energy** is always positive and applies to all reactions
