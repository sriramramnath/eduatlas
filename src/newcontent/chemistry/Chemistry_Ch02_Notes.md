## 2.1 Elements

### Definition of an Element
An **element** is a pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. Each element contains only one type of atom. Elements are the fundamental building blocks of all matter, and everything in the universe is made from one or more of the 118 known elements.

### Understanding "One Type of Atom"
When we say an element contains "one type of atom," this refers to atoms with the same number of protons in their nuclei (same atomic number). However, atoms of the same element may have different numbers of neutrons (isotopes), but they are still considered the same element because they have identical chemical properties.

### Classification of Elements
Elements can be broadly classified into two main categories based on their physical and chemical properties:

#### Metals
Metals are elements that typically exhibit the following characteristic properties:
- **Appearance**: Lustrous (shiny) when freshly cut or polished
- **State at room temperature**: Mostly solids (exception: mercury is liquid)
- **Electrical conductivity**: Good conductors of electricity
- **Thermal conductivity**: Good conductors of heat
- **Malleability**: Can be hammered into shape without breaking
- **Ductility**: Can be drawn into wires
- **Melting and boiling points**: Generally high
- **Density**: Generally high
- **Magnetic properties**: A few are magnetic (iron, cobalt, nickel)

**Examples of metals:** Iron, copper, gold, silver, aluminium, sodium, calcium

#### Non-metals
Non-metals display contrasting properties to metals:
- **Appearance**: Dull (non-lustrous) if solid
- **State at room temperature**: Can be solids, liquids (bromine), or gases
- **Electrical conductivity**: Poor conductors (insulators), except graphite
- **Thermal conductivity**: Poor conductors of heat
- **Malleability**: Brittle if solid (break when hammered)
- **Ductility**: Non-ductile
- **Melting and boiling points**: Generally low
- **Density**: Generally low

**Examples of non-metals:** Carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, chlorine, phosphorus

### Semi-metals (Metalloids)
Some elements display properties intermediate between metals and non-metals. These are called **metalloids** or **semi-metals**. They often have semiconductor properties, making them valuable in electronics.

**Examples:** Silicon, germanium, boron, arsenic

### Representation of Elements
Elements are represented by **chemical symbols** - one or two letters derived from the element's name (usually in English or Latin):

| Element | Symbol | Origin |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Hydrogen | H | English |
| Carbon | C | English |
| Oxygen | O | English |
| Sodium | Na | Latin: Natrium |
| Potassium | K | Latin: Kalium |
| Iron | Fe | Latin: Ferrum |
| Copper | Cu | Latin: Cuprum |
| Gold | Au | Latin: Aurum |
| Lead | Pb | Latin: Plumbum |

## 2.2 Compounds

### Definition of a Compound
A **compound** is a pure substance formed when two or more different elements are chemically combined in fixed proportions by mass. The properties of a compound are completely different from those of the constituent elements.

### Key Characteristics of Compounds

1. **Fixed composition**: A given compound always contains the same elements in the same proportions by mass. This is known as the **Law of Constant Composition**. For example, water (H₂O) always contains hydrogen and oxygen in a mass ratio of 1:8, regardless of its source.

2. **Chemically bonded**: The elements in a compound are held together by chemical bonds (ionic or covalent). These bonds involve the sharing or transfer of electrons between atoms.

3. **Different properties**: Compounds have properties that are distinctly different from their constituent elements. This transformation of properties is one of the most important characteristics of compounds.

4. **Cannot be separated by physical means**: The constituent elements of a compound cannot be separated by simple physical methods such as filtration, distillation, or magnetic separation. Chemical reactions are required to break down compounds into their elements.

5. **Energy changes during formation**: When elements combine to form compounds, energy is usually released (exothermic). Conversely, energy is required to break compounds apart.

### Examples Showing Property Changes

| Compound | Formula | Constituent Elements | Properties of Elements | Properties of Compound |
|----------|---------|---------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Water | H₂O | Hydrogen + Oxygen | H₂: colourless, flammable gas; O₂: colourless, supports combustion | Colourless liquid, extinguishes fire |
| Sodium chloride | NaCl | Sodium + Chlorine | Na: soft, reactive metal; Cl₂: toxic, greenish-yellow gas | White crystalline solid, essential for life |
| Carbon dioxide | CO₂ | Carbon + Oxygen | C: black solid; O₂: colourless gas | Colourless gas, used in fire extinguishers |
| Iron sulfide | FeS | Iron + Sulfur | Fe: magnetic, grey metal; S: yellow, non-magnetic powder | Black solid, non-magnetic |

### Chemical Formulae of Compounds
Compounds are represented by chemical formulae that show:
- The symbols of the elements present
- The relative numbers of atoms of each element (using subscript numbers)

**Examples:**
- H₂O: 2 hydrogen atoms + 1 oxygen atom
- CO₂: 1 carbon atom + 2 oxygen atoms
- NaCl: 1 sodium atom + 1 chlorine atom
- H₂SO₄: 2 hydrogen atoms + 1 sulfur atom + 4 oxygen atoms

## 2.3 Mixtures

### Definition of a Mixture
A **mixture** consists of two or more substances (elements and/or compounds) that are physically mixed together but not chemically combined. The substances in a mixture retain their individual properties and can be present in any proportion.

### Key Characteristics of Mixtures

1. **Variable composition**: The proportions of substances in a mixture can vary. For example, a mixture of salt and water can have any amount of salt dissolved in it (up to the solubility limit).

2. **No chemical bonding**: The components of a mixture are not chemically bonded to each other. They retain their individual chemical identities.

3. **Retain original properties**: Each component of a mixture retains its own characteristic properties. For example, iron filings in a mixture with sulfur remain magnetic and grey.

4. **Can be separated by physical means**: The components of a mixture can be separated using physical methods based on differences in their physical properties. No chemical reactions are required.

5. **No fixed melting/boiling point**: Mixtures often melt or boil over a range of temperatures rather than at a single, fixed temperature.

### Types of Mixtures

#### Homogeneous Mixtures
Homogeneous mixtures have a **uniform composition throughout** - the components are evenly distributed and cannot be distinguished visually.

**Examples:**
- **Solutions**: Salt water, sugar solution, air (mixture of gases)
- **Alloys**: Brass (copper + zinc), bronze (copper + tin), steel (iron + carbon)

Properties of homogeneous mixtures:
- Single phase visible
- Components cannot be seen separately
- Same composition throughout
- Also called "solutions" when one component is dissolved in another

#### Heterogeneous Mixtures
Heterogeneous mixtures have a **non-uniform composition** - the components are not evenly distributed and can often be distinguished visually.

**Examples:**
- **Suspensions**: Muddy water, sand in water
- **Colloids**: Milk, fog, smoke
- **Simple mixtures**: Iron filings and sulfur, oil and water, salad dressing

Properties of heterogeneous mixtures:
- Two or more phases visible
- Components may be visible separately
- Composition varies from one region to another
- May separate over time (e.g., suspension settles)

## 2.4 Comparing Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

### Summary Table

| Feature | Element | Compound | Mixture |
|---------|---------|----------|---------|
| **Composition** | One type of atom only | Two or more elements chemically combined in fixed proportions | Two or more substances physically mixed in any proportion |
| **Chemical bonding** | Atoms may be bonded (e.g., O₂) | Elements are chemically bonded | No chemical bonding between components |
| **Properties** | Unique to that element | Different from constituent elements | Components retain their individual properties |
| **Separation** | Cannot be broken down chemically | Can be separated by chemical reactions only | Can be separated by physical methods |
| **Melting/Boiling point** | Fixed | Fixed | Variable (often over a range) |
| **Energy change in formation** | N/A | Usually energy released (exothermic) | No energy change |

## 2.5 Separation Techniques

Understanding how to separate mixtures is essential in chemistry. The choice of separation method depends on the physical properties of the components in the mixture.

### Filtration

**Principle:** Separation based on differences in particle size and solubility.

**Process:** A mixture of an insoluble solid and a liquid is poured through filter paper in a funnel. The solid (residue) is trapped on the filter paper, while the liquid (filtrate) passes through.

**Applications:**
- Separating sand from water
- Removing precipitates from solutions
- Purifying drinking water (as part of water treatment)

**Key terms:**
- **Residue**: The solid left on the filter paper
- **Filtrate**: The liquid that passes through the filter paper

### Crystallisation

**Principle:** Separation based on differences in solubility at different temperatures.

**Process:** A solution is gently heated to evaporate some of the solvent, making the solution more concentrated. When the solution is cooled, crystals form because the solubility decreases at lower temperatures. The crystals can then be separated by filtration.

**Applications:**
- Obtaining pure copper(II) sulfate crystals from solution
- Producing salt from sea water
- Purifying soluble solids

**Key steps:**
1. Heat the solution to evaporate some solvent (do NOT dry completely)
2. Allow the concentrated solution to cool slowly
3. Crystals form as the solution cools
4. Filter to collect the crystals
5. Wash crystals with cold distilled water
6. Dry crystals between filter papers

### Simple Distillation

**Principle:** Separation based on differences in boiling points.

**Process:** A mixture of liquids (usually a solution) is heated. The component with the lower boiling point boils first, its vapour rises, passes through a condenser where it is cooled and condensed back to liquid, and is collected as the distillate.

**Applications:**
- Obtaining pure water from sea water (desalination)
- Separating a liquid from a dissolved solid
- Purifying liquids with significantly different boiling points

**Key terms:**
- **Distillate**: The purified liquid collected after condensation

### Fractional Distillation

**Principle:** Separation of liquids with similar boiling points using a fractionating column.

**Process:** Similar to simple distillation but with a fractionating column attached. The column provides a large surface area for repeated condensation and vaporisation. Liquids with lower boiling points are collected first, followed by those with higher boiling points.

**Applications:**
- Separating crude oil into fractions (petrol, diesel, kerosene, etc.)
- Separating liquid air into nitrogen, oxygen, and argon
- Separating ethanol from fermented mixtures

**How the fractionating column works:**
1. Vapour rises up the column
2. Vapour condenses on the glass beads or plates
3. The condensed liquid is re-heated by rising hot vapour
4. This repeated vaporisation-condensation enriches the vapour in the more volatile component
5. Eventually, the vapour reaching the top is mostly the component with the lowest boiling point

### Chromatography

**Principle:** Separation based on differences in solubility and adsorption (attraction to the stationary phase).

**Process:** In paper chromatography, a small spot of the mixture is placed on a baseline drawn in pencil on chromatography paper. The paper is placed in a solvent, with the solvent level below the baseline. As the solvent rises up the paper by capillary action, the components of the mixture move at different rates depending on their solubility in the solvent and their attraction to the paper. This separates the components into different spots.

**Applications:**
- Separating and identifying coloured substances (e.g., dyes in food colouring)
- Analyzing drugs in forensic science
- Identifying amino acids in protein analysis

**Key terms:**
- **Stationary phase**: The material that doesn't move (chromatography paper)
- **Mobile phase**: The solvent that moves up the paper
- **Rf value**: Ratio of the distance moved by the substance to the distance moved by the solvent

**Calculating Rf values:**
$$R_f = \frac{\text{distance moved by substance}}{\text{distance moved by solvent}}$$

Rf values are always between 0 and 1 and can be used to identify substances by comparing with known values.

### Evaporation

**Principle:** Separation of a dissolved solid from a solution by heating.

**Process:** A solution is heated strongly until all the solvent evaporates, leaving the solid behind. This is different from crystallisation where heating is stopped before the solution dries completely.

**Applications:**
- Obtaining salt from sea water
- Recovering dissolved solids from solutions

**Note:** Evaporation to dryness is NOT suitable for substances that decompose on heating.

### Magnetic Separation

**Principle:** Separation based on magnetic properties.

**Process:** A magnet is used to separate magnetic materials from non-magnetic materials in a mixture.

**Applications:**
- Separating iron from sulfur mixture
- Removing iron from recycled waste
- Separating iron ore from gangue in mining

### Using a Separating Funnel

**Principle:** Separation based on differences in density and immiscibility.

**Process:** A separating funnel is used to separate two immiscible liquids (liquids that don't mix). The denser liquid forms the lower layer and can be drained off through the tap, leaving the less dense liquid behind.

**Applications:**
- Separating oil and water
- Separating organic solvents from aqueous solutions

## 2.6 Atom Economy (Chemistry Only)

### Definition
**Atom economy** is a measure of the proportion of reactant atoms that become part of the desired product in a chemical reaction. It is an important concept in green chemistry as it indicates how efficiently raw materials are used.

### Calculation
$$\text{Atom economy} = \frac{\text{M_r of desired product}}{\text{Sum of M_r of all reactants}} \times 100\%$$

### Importance
- High atom economy reactions are more sustainable
- Reduce waste and environmental impact
- More economically efficient
- Important for industrial processes

### Example
For the reaction: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

If CaO is the desired product:
- M_r of CaO = 40 + 16 = 56
- M_r of CaCO₃ = 40 + 12 + (16 × 3) = 100
- Atom economy = (56/100) × 100% = 56%

## 2.7 Common Exam Mistakes and Important Points

### Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing compounds and mixtures**: A compound is chemically bonded and cannot be separated by physical means. A mixture is not chemically bonded and can be separated physically.

2. **Saying compounds "contain molecules"**: Some compounds are ionic (made of ions in a lattice), not molecules. Only covalent compounds consist of molecules.

3. **Confusing homogeneous and heterogeneous**: Homogeneous means uniform throughout; heterogeneous means non-uniform with visible different regions.

4. **Incorrect separation technique selection**: Match the technique to the physical property difference:
   - Different particle size/solubility → filtration
   - Different boiling points → distillation
   - Different solubilities → crystallisation or chromatography
   - Magnetic vs non-magnetic → magnetic separation

5. **Drawing incorrect diagrams**: Always include:
   - Labels for all apparatus
   - Arrows showing direction of vapour/liquid flow
   - Water in and out for condensers

6. **Forgetting the baseline must be in pencil**: Ink would dissolve and contaminate the chromatogram.

7. **Using evaporation when crystallisation is required**: Some substances decompose when heated to dryness.

### Important Exam Points

1. **Use precise terminology**: Elements are "chemically combined" in compounds, not "mixed."

2. **Understand property changes**: Be able to explain why compounds have different properties from their constituent elements (bonds involve electron transfer/sharing, creating new species with different electron arrangements).

3. **Know all separation techniques**: Be able to name, describe, and explain the principle behind each technique.

4. **Understand Rf values**: Know how to calculate and use them for identification.

5. **Recognise practical applications**: Link separation techniques to real-world uses.
