## 6.1 Writing Balanced Chemical Equations

### What is a Chemical Equation?

A **chemical equation** is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulae. It shows:
- The **reactants** (starting materials) on the left
- The **products** (substances formed) on the right
- An arrow (→) indicating the direction of reaction

### Rules for Balancing Chemical Equations

1. **Write the correct formulae** for all reactants and products
2. **Count the atoms** of each element on both sides
3. **Balance the equation** by adding coefficients (numbers in front of formulae)
4. **Never change subscripts** in a chemical formula to balance an equation

### The Law of Conservation of Mass

> **Law of Conservation of Mass**: In a chemical reaction, mass is neither created nor destroyed. The total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.

This is why equations must be balanced - atoms are conserved in a chemical reaction.

### Steps to Balance an Equation

**Example: Combustion of methane**

1. Write unbalanced equation:
   CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

2. Count atoms:
   - Left: C=1, H=4, O=2
   - Right: C=1, H=2, O=3

3. Balance H first (put 2 before H₂O):
   CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
   - Left: C=1, H=4, O=2
   - Right: C=1, H=4, O=4

4. Balance O (put 2 before O₂):
   CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
   - Left: C=1, H=4, O=4
   - Right: C=1, H=4, O=4 ✓

### Common Balanced Equations to Know

| Reaction | Balanced Equation |
|----------|-------------------|
| Combustion of hydrogen | 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O |
| Combustion of carbon | C + O₂ → CO₂ |
| Combustion of sulfur | S + O₂ → SO₂ |
| Thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate | CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ |
| Reaction of magnesium with hydrochloric acid | Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂ |
| Neutralisation | HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O |
| Photosynthesis | 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ |

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Changing subscripts instead of adding coefficients**
   - Wrong: H₂O → H₂O₂ (changes meaning entirely)
   - Correct: 2H₂O (just more water molecules)

2. **Forgetting to balance diatomic molecules** (H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂)

3. **Not simplifying coefficients when possible**
   - 2H₂ + 2O₂ → 2H₂O should be H₂ + O₂ → H₂O (though the first is technically balanced, always use simplest whole numbers)

## 6.2 State Symbols

### What are State Symbols?

**State symbols** are abbreviations used in chemical equations to show the physical state of each substance:

| Symbol | Meaning | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **(s)** | Solid | Solid substance |
| **(l)** | Liquid | Pure liquid (not solution) |
| **(g)** | Gas | Gaseous substance |
| **(aq)** | Aqueous | Dissolved in water |

### Examples with State Symbols

**Combustion of hydrogen:**
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

**Thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate:**
CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

**Reaction of sodium with water:**
2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g)

**Neutralisation reaction:**
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

**Precipitation reaction:**
AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)

### Important Points to Remember

- **(l)** is used for pure liquids like water, ethanol, or molten substances
- **(aq)** is used for solutions in water - never for the solvent water itself in a reaction
- **(g)** is used for gases like H₂, O₂, CO₂, NH₃
- **(s)** is used for solids including precipitates formed in reactions

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Confusing (l) and (aq)**
   - Water as a reactant or product is (l)
   - Substances dissolved in water are (aq)

2. **Forgetting state symbols when asked**

3. **Using wrong states**
   - Water at room temperature is (l), not (g)
   - Ionic compounds in solution are (aq), not (l)

## 6.3 Ionic Equations

### What is an Ionic Equation?

An **ionic equation** shows only the species that actually take part in a reaction. It removes **spectator ions** - ions that appear on both sides unchanged.

### How to Write Ionic Equations

1. **Write the balanced molecular equation** with state symbols
2. **Split all aqueous ionic compounds** into their ions
3. **Identify spectator ions** (same ions on both sides)
4. **Cancel spectator ions**
5. **Write the final ionic equation**

### Example: Silver Nitrate + Sodium Chloride

**Step 1: Molecular equation**
AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)

**Step 2: Split aqueous ionic compounds**
Ag⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) + Na⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)

**Step 3: Identify spectator ions**
Na⁺(aq) and NO₃⁻(aq) appear on both sides unchanged

**Step 4: Cancel spectator ions**
Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)

### Example: Neutralisation

**Molecular equation:**
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

**Split into ions:**
H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + H₂O(l)

**Ionic equation:**
H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

> **Note:** This is the general ionic equation for neutralisation between any strong acid and any strong alkali!

### Key Ionic Equations to Know

| Reaction Type | Ionic Equation |
|---------------|----------------|
| Neutralisation (strong acid + strong base) | H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l) |
| Acid + carbonate | 2H⁺(aq) + CO₃²⁻(aq) → H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) |
| Acid + metal (e.g., Mg) | Mg(s) + 2H⁺(aq) → Mg²⁺(aq) + H₂(g) |
| Precipitation of AgCl | Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) |
| Precipitation of BaSO₄ | Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s) |

### Rules for Ionic Equations

1. **Never split** solids (s), liquids (l), or gases (g) into ions
2. **Only split** aqueous (aq) ionic compounds
3. **Covalent molecules** (like H₂O, CO₂, NH₃) stay as molecules
4. **Elements** (like Mg, Zn, H₂) stay as they are

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Splitting solids, liquids, or gases into ions**
   - AgCl(s) must stay as AgCl(s), not Ag⁺ + Cl⁻

2. **Forgetting state symbols** in ionic equations

3. **Not balancing ionic equations properly**
   - Must balance both atoms AND charges

4. **Cancelling the wrong ions**
   - Always check ions appear identically on both sides

## 6.4 Calculating Reacting Masses

### The Mole Concept

The **mole** is the unit for amount of substance.

> **Avogadro's constant (Nₐ)** = 6.02 × 10²³ particles per mole

**One mole** of any substance contains 6.02 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, or formula units).

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| n = m/Mr | moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol) |
| m = n × Mr | mass = moles × molar mass |
| n = N/Nₐ | moles = number of particles ÷ Avogadro's constant |

### Using Balanced Equations for Calculations

The **coefficients** in a balanced equation show the **mole ratio** between substances.

**Example: What mass of CO₂ is produced when 12g of carbon burns?**

C + O₂ → CO₂

**Step 1:** Calculate moles of carbon
- n(C) = mass ÷ Mr = 12 ÷ 12 = 1 mol

**Step 2:** Use mole ratio from equation
- Ratio C:CO₂ = 1:1
- So n(CO₂) = 1 mol

**Step 3:** Calculate mass of CO₂
- m(CO₂) = n × Mr = 1 × 44 = 44 g

### Example: Reacting Mass Calculation

**What mass of magnesium oxide is formed when 6g of magnesium burns in air?**

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

**Step 1:** Calculate moles of Mg
- Ar(Mg) = 24
- n(Mg) = 6 ÷ 24 = 0.25 mol

**Step 2:** Use mole ratio
- Ratio Mg:MgO = 2:2 = 1:1
- n(MgO) = 0.25 mol

**Step 3:** Calculate mass of MgO
- Mr(MgO) = 24 + 16 = 40
- m(MgO) = 0.25 × 40 = 10 g

### Limiting Reactant

The **limiting reactant** is the reactant that is completely used up first in a reaction. It determines how much product can be formed.

**Example:**
If 2 mol of H₂ reacts with 2 mol of O₂:

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

- H₂ needed for 2 mol O₂ = 4 mol
- Only 2 mol H₂ available
- **H₂ is the limiting reactant**
- Product formed based on H₂: 2 mol H₂O

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Not using mole ratios** from balanced equations

2. **Forgetting to balance the equation first**

3. **Using wrong molar masses**
   - Always check: H₂O = 18, not 17 (O = 16, H = 1 × 2)

4. **Not identifying the limiting reactant** when both reactant amounts are given

5. **Units confusion**
   - Mass in grams, molar mass in g/mol

## 6.5 Percentage Yield

### What is Percentage Yield?

**Percentage yield** compares the actual amount of product obtained in an experiment with the theoretical maximum amount possible.

> **Percentage yield** = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100%

### Why is Yield Less Than 100%?

1. **Incomplete reactions** - not all reactants convert to products
2. **Side reactions** - reactants may form unwanted by-products
3. **Loss during transfer** - spillage, left on filter paper, in apparatus
4. **Reversible reactions** - products reform reactants
5. **Impurities in reactants**

### Example Calculation

**A student reacts 6g of magnesium with oxygen and obtains 8g of magnesium oxide. Calculate the percentage yield.**

**Step 1:** Calculate theoretical yield
- 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
- n(Mg) = 6 ÷ 24 = 0.25 mol
- n(MgO) = 0.25 mol (1:1 ratio)
- Theoretical yield = 0.25 × 40 = 10 g

**Step 2:** Calculate percentage yield
- % yield = (8 ÷ 10) × 100 = **80%**

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Confusing actual and theoretical yield** in the formula

2. **Not calculating theoretical yield from the balanced equation**

3. **Forgetting to multiply by 100** for percentage

## 6.6 Atom Economy

### What is Atom Economy?

**Atom economy** measures how efficiently the atoms in the reactants are converted into the desired product.

> **Atom economy** = (Mr of desired product ÷ sum of Mr of all products) × 100%

For reactions with only one product, atom economy = 100%

### Why is Atom Economy Important?

- High atom economy means **less waste**
- More sustainable and environmentally friendly
- Reduces costs of waste disposal
- Better use of raw materials

### Example Calculation

**Calculate the atom economy for producing hydrogen from the reaction of methane with steam.**

CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂

**Step 1:** Calculate Mr of desired product (H₂)
- Mr(H₂) = 2

**Step 2:** Calculate sum of Mr of all products
- Mr(CO) = 28, Mr(H₂) = 2
- Total = 28 + (3 × 2) = 34

**Step 3:** Calculate atom economy
- Atom economy = (3 × 2 ÷ 34) × 100 = **17.6%**

### Atom Economy vs Percentage Yield

| Feature | Percentage Yield | Atom Economy |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| Measures | Actual vs theoretical mass | Efficiency of atom usage |
| Depends on | Experimental conditions | Reaction pathway chosen |
| Can change | Yes (improve technique) | No (fixed for a reaction) |
| Target | As high as possible | As high as possible |

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Using reactants instead of products** in the calculation

2. **Forgetting to multiply by 100** for percentage

3. **Not multiplying by coefficient** if multiple moles of product formed

4. **Confusing atom economy with percentage yield**

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Balanced equation** | Equation with equal numbers of each atom on both sides |
| **State symbol** | Symbol showing physical state: (s), (l), (g), (aq) |
| **Ionic equation** | Equation showing only species that participate in the reaction |
| **Spectator ion** | Ion that does not change during a reaction |

| **Atom economy** | Measure of how efficiently atoms are used in a reaction |
