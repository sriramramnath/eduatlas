## 10.1 Principles of Electrolysis

### What is Electrolysis?

**Definition:** Electrolysis is the decomposition of an ionic compound into its elements using electricity.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Electrolyte** | An ionic compound that conducts electricity when molten or in aqueous solution |
| **Electrode** | A conductor (usually graphite or metal) that carries electricity into or out of the electrolyte |
| **Anode** | The positive electrode (attracts anions) |
| **Cathode** | The negative electrode (attracts cations) |
| **Anion** | A negatively charged ion (attracted to the anode) |
| **Cation** | A positively charged ion (attracted to the cathode) |

### How Electrolysis Works

```
         POWER SUPPLY
              |
    + ------------------- -
    |                     |
  ANODE                 CATHODE
  (+ ve)                (- ve)
    |                     |
    ↓                     ↓
Anions (−)            Cations (+)
move to                move to
anode                  cathode
    |                     |
  LOSE                  GAIN
electrons              electrons
  (oxidation)         (reduction)
```

### The Process

1. **Dissolve or melt** the ionic compound to free the ions
2. **Apply voltage** using a power supply
3. **Cations** (positive ions) move to the **cathode** (negative electrode)
4. **Anions** (negative ions) move to the **anode** (positive electrode)
5. At the electrodes, ions become **discharged** (gain or lose electrons to become neutral)

### Important Principle

> **At the cathode:** Reduction occurs (gain of electrons)
> **At the anode:** Oxidation occurs (loss of electrons)

### Why Electrolytes Must Be Molten or Aqueous

- In solid ionic compounds, ions are held in fixed positions and cannot move
- When **molten** or in **aqueous solution**, ions are free to move and carry charge
- Without mobile ions, electricity cannot flow through the electrolyte

## 10.2 Electrolysis of Molten Ionic Compounds

### General Principle

When a molten ionic compound is electrolysed:
- The **metal ions** (cations) are discharged at the cathode
- The **non-metal ions** (anions) are discharged at the anode

### Example 1: Molten Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

**Electrolyte:** NaCl(l)
**Ions present:** Na⁺ and Cl⁻

| Electrode | Process | Half-equation | Product |
|-----------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Cathode (−) | Na⁺ ions gain electrons | Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na | Sodium metal |
| Anode (+) | Cl⁻ ions lose electrons | 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ | Chlorine gas |

**Overall:** 2NaCl(l) → 2Na(l) + Cl₂(g)

**Observations:**
- Silvery liquid sodium forms at the cathode
- Green-yellow chlorine gas bubbles at the anode

### Example 2: Molten Lead(II) Bromide (PbBr₂)

**Electrolyte:** PbBr₂(l)
**Ions present:** Pb²⁺ and Br⁻

| Electrode | Process | Half-equation | Product |
|-----------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Cathode (−) | Pb²⁺ ions gain electrons | Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb | Lead metal |
| Anode (+) | Br⁻ ions lose electrons | 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻ | Bromine gas |

**Overall:** PbBr₂(l) → Pb(l) + Br₂(g)

**Observations:**
- Grey lead metal forms at the cathode
- Orange-brown bromine vapour at the anode

### Writing Half-Equations

**At the cathode (reduction):**
- Balance the atoms
- Add electrons to the left side
- Ensure charges balance

**At the anode (oxidation):**
- Balance the atoms
- Add electrons to the right side
- Ensure charges balance

## 10.3 Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions

### The Complexity of Aqueous Solutions

In aqueous solutions, **water** is also present and can be electrolysed:
- Water partially dissociates: H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻
- This means **H⁺ ions** and **OH⁻ ions** are also available to be discharged

### Discharge Rules at the Cathode

At the cathode, **reduction** occurs. The product depends on the metal:

| Metal in compound | Product at cathode |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Copper, silver, gold** (less reactive metals) | The metal itself |
| **Potassium, sodium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium, zinc** (reactive metals) | Hydrogen gas |

**Remember:** If the metal is more reactive than hydrogen, hydrogen gas is produced instead of the metal.

### Discharge Rules at the Anode

At the anode, **oxidation** occurs. The product depends on the anion:

| Anion present | Product at anode |
|---------------|------------------|
| **Halide ions** (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) | The halogen (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) |
| **Other ions** (SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻) | Oxygen gas |

**Why oxygen?** If no halide ions are present, OH⁻ ions from water are discharged:
- 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

### Summary: Predicting Products

**At the cathode:**
- Is the metal MORE reactive than hydrogen? → **Hydrogen gas**
- Is the metal LESS reactive than hydrogen? → **The metal**

**At the anode:**
- Are halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) present? → **The halogen**
- No halide ions? → **Oxygen gas**

### Example 1: Aqueous Sodium Chloride (NaCl(aq))

**Ions present:** Na⁺, Cl⁻, H⁺, OH⁻ (from water)

| Electrode | Process | Half-equation | Product |
|-----------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Cathode (−) | H⁺ discharged (Na more reactive than H) | 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ | Hydrogen gas |
| Anode (+) | Cl⁻ discharged (halide present) | 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ | Chlorine gas |

**Overall products:** H₂(g) and Cl₂(g)
**Left in solution:** NaOH(aq) (sodium hydroxide)

### Example 2: Aqueous Copper(II) Sulfate (CuSO₄(aq))

**Ions present:** Cu²⁺, SO₄²⁻, H⁺, OH⁻

| Electrode | Process | Half-equation | Product |
|-----------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Cathode (−) | Cu²⁺ discharged (Cu less reactive than H) | Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu | Copper metal |
| Anode (+) | OH⁻ discharged (no halide) | 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ | Oxygen gas |

**Overall products:** Cu(s) and O₂(g)

### Example 3: Aqueous Sodium Sulfate (Na₂SO₄(aq))

**Ions present:** Na⁺, SO₄²⁻, H⁺, OH⁻

| Electrode | Process | Half-equation | Product |
|-----------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Cathode (−) | H⁺ discharged (Na more reactive than H) | 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ | Hydrogen gas |
| Anode (+) | OH⁻ discharged (no halide) | 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ | Oxygen gas |

**Overall products:** H₂(g) and O₂(g) - essentially electrolysis of water!

## 10.4 Electrolysis of Copper(II) Sulfate Solution

### Using Carbon (Inert) Electrodes

**Electrolyte:** CuSO₄(aq)

| Electrode | Product | Observations |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| Cathode (−) | Copper metal | Brown/pink deposit on electrode |
| Anode (+) | Oxygen gas | Bubbles of colourless gas |

**Half-equations:**
- Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
- Anode: 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

**Changes to the solution:**
- Blue colour fades as Cu²⁺ ions are removed
- Solution becomes colourless when all Cu²⁺ is discharged
- pH decreases (becomes more acidic) as H⁺ ions remain in solution

### Using Copper Electrodes (Electrolytic Refining)

When **copper electrodes** are used instead of inert electrodes:

| Electrode | Process | Observations |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| Cathode (−) | Cu deposited | Copper electrode gets thicker |
| Anode (+) | Cu dissolves | Copper electrode gets thinner |

**Half-equations:**
- Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (copper deposited)
- Anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ (copper dissolves)

**Key points:**
- The concentration of Cu²⁺ in solution **remains constant**
- Copper is transferred from the anode to the cathode
- Impure copper anode becomes pure copper cathode

### Purification of Copper

**Purpose:** To produce pure copper from impure copper

**Process:**
1. **Anode:** Made of impure copper (contains silver, gold, and other metals)
2. **Cathode:** Made of pure copper (thin sheet)
3. **Electrolyte:** Copper(II) sulfate solution

**What happens:**
- Copper at the anode oxidises and dissolves: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻
- Cu²⁺ ions migrate to the cathode and are reduced: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
- **Impurities:** Silver and gold fall to the bottom as "anode sludge" (they don't dissolve)

**Why is pure copper needed?**
- Electrical wiring requires pure copper for maximum conductivity
- Impurities reduce conductivity

## 10.5 Extraction of Aluminium

### Why Electrolysis is Needed

- Aluminium is a very **reactive metal** (above carbon in the reactivity series)
- It cannot be extracted by heating its ore with carbon
- Electrolysis is required to extract aluminium from its ore

### The Ore: Bauxite

- Bauxite is the main ore of aluminium
- Contains aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) with impurities
- Must be purified to obtain pure aluminium oxide (alumina)

### The Process

**Electrolyte:** Molten aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃)
- Melting point of Al₂O₃ is approximately **2050°C** - very high!
- To reduce energy costs, Al₂O₃ is dissolved in **molten cryolite** (Na₃AlF₆)
- Cryolite lowers the melting point to about **900°C**

**Cell:** Hall-Héroult cell

```
         Carbon anodes (+)
              ↓ ↓ ↓
    ┌─────────────────────┐
    │   Molten cryolite   │
    │   + Al₂O₃           │
    │                     │
    │      O₂ + CO₂       │ ← Bubbles off
    │                     │
    └─────────────────────┘
              ↓
        Molten Al
        (tapped off)
    Carbon cathode lining (−)
```

### Electrodes and Reactions

| Electrode | Material | Process | Half-equation |
|-----------|----------|---------|---------------|
| Anode (+) | Carbon | O²⁻ ions lose electrons | 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ |
| Cathode (−) | Carbon | Al³⁺ ions gain electrons | Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al |

**Important:** The oxygen produced reacts with the carbon anode:
- C + O₂ → CO₂

### Why the Anodes Need Regular Replacement

- The carbon anodes react with oxygen produced at the anode
- They gradually **burn away** to form carbon dioxide
- Anodes must be replaced regularly - this is a significant cost

### Overall Equation

2Al₂O₃(l) → 4Al(l) + 3O₂(g)

### Key Points About the Process

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Location** | Near cheap electricity sources (hydroelectric power) |
| **Temperature** | About 900°C |
| **Why cryolite?** | Lowers melting point, reduces energy costs |
| **Products** | Aluminium at cathode, oxygen at anode |
| **Anode problem** | Carbon anodes burn away, need replacing |
| **Energy use** | Very high - large amounts of electricity needed |

## 10.6 Electroplating

### What is Electroplating?

**Definition:** Electroplating is the coating of an object with a thin layer of metal using electrolysis.

### Purpose of Electroplating

1. **Decoration** - Make objects look more attractive (e.g., silver-plated jewellery)
2. **Protection** - Prevent corrosion (e.g., tin-plated steel for food cans)
3. **Hardness** - Increase surface hardness (e.g., chromium plating on taps)

### How Electroplating Works

```
Power Supply
    |
+ ──────────────── -
|                 |
Anode (+)       Cathode (−)
(Metal to       (Object to
be plated)       be plated)
    |               |
    ↓               ↓
Metal ions      Metal ions
leave anode     deposit on
and move        object
through
electrolyte
```

### Electroplating with Silver

**To silver-plate a metal object:**

| Component | Material |
|-----------|----------|
| Anode (+) | Pure silver |
| Cathode (−) | Object to be plated (e.g., spoon) |
| Electrolyte | Silver nitrate solution (AgNO₃) |

**Half-equations:**
- Anode: Ag → Ag⁺ + e⁻ (silver dissolves)
- Cathode: Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag (silver deposits)

**Result:** A thin layer of silver coats the object

### Electroplating with Nickel

**To nickel-plate an object:**

| Component | Material |
|-----------|----------|
| Anode (+) | Nickel |
| Cathode (−) | Object to be plated |
| Electrolyte | Nickel(II) sulfate solution (NiSO₄) |

### Key Principles of Electroplating

1. The **object to be plated** is always the **cathode** (negative electrode)
2. The **plating metal** is the **anode** (positive electrode)
3. The **electrolyte** contains ions of the plating metal
4. The concentration of metal ions in solution remains **constant** (anode dissolves at same rate as metal deposits on cathode)

### Factors Affecting Electroplating Quality

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Current** | Higher current = faster plating but may be uneven |
| **Time** | Longer time = thicker coating |
| **Temperature** | Affects rate and quality of deposit |
| **Concentration** | Affects rate of deposition |
| **Surface preparation** | Clean surface = better adhesion |

## Common Exam Mistakes

1. **Confusing cathode and anode**
   - Remember: **Cathode** = **Cation** (positive ions go there)
   - Cathode is **negative**, Anode is **positive**

2. **Wrong products in aqueous solutions**
   - Remember the discharge rules:
   - Reactive metals → hydrogen at cathode
   - Halides → halogen at anode; others → oxygen

3. **Not balancing half-equations**
   - Always check atoms AND charges balance

4. **Forgetting about water in aqueous solutions**
   - Aqueous solutions contain H⁺ and OH⁻ from water dissociation

5. **Saying ions "flow" through the wire**
   - Ions move through the **electrolyte**, electrons flow through the **wires**

6. **Confusing electroplating with electrolytic refining**
   - Electroplating: coating an object
   - Refining: purifying a metal

7. **Not explaining why cryolite is used**
   - It **lowers the melting point** of Al₂O₃, reducing energy costs

8. **Forgetting that carbon anodes burn away in aluminium extraction**
   - The O₂ produced reacts with the carbon anode: C + O₂ → CO₂
