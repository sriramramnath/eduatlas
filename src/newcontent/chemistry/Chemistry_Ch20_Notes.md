## 20.1 Rate of Reaction

### Key Definition

**Rate of Reaction**: The rate of a chemical reaction is a measure of how fast the reactants are converted into products. It is usually measured as the change in concentration, mass, or volume per unit time.

### Measuring Rate

Rate can be measured by:
1. How quickly **reactants are used up**
2. How quickly **products are formed**

**Formula:**
```
Rate = Change in quantity (mass/volume/concentration)
       ─────────────────────────────────────────────
                    Time taken
```

**Units commonly used:**
- g/s (grams per second)
- cm³/s (cubic centimeters per second)
- mol/dm³/s (moles per cubic decimeter per second)

---

## 20.2 Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

There are **four main factors** that affect the rate of a chemical reaction:

### 1. Temperature
### 2. Concentration (and pressure for gases)
### 3. Surface Area (particle size)
### 4. Catalysts

---

## 20.3 Collision Theory

### The Fundamental Principle

Chemical reactions occur when particles of reactants **collide** with each other. However, not all collisions result in a reaction.

**Successful Collision**: A collision that leads to a chemical reaction.

For a collision to be successful, particles must:
1. **Collide** with each other
2. Have **sufficient energy** (at least the activation energy)
3. Have the **correct orientation**

### Key Concepts

**Collision Frequency**: The number of collisions per unit time between reactant particles.

**Activation Energy**: The minimum energy that particles must have to react upon collision.

### Summary: Collision Theory

| Factor | Effect on Collision Theory |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Temperature | Higher temp → particles have more kinetic energy → more frequent collisions AND more particles have energy ≥ Ea |
| Concentration | Higher conc → more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions |
| Surface area | Larger SA → more particles exposed → more frequent collisions |
| Catalyst | Lowers Ea → more particles have sufficient energy for successful collisions |

## 20.4 Effect of Temperature on Rate

### How Temperature Affects Rate

**Increasing temperature increases the rate of reaction because:**

1. **Particles move faster** - they have more kinetic energy
2. **More frequent collisions** - particles collide more often
3. **More energetic collisions** - a higher proportion of particles have energy ≥ activation energy
4. **More successful collisions** - more collisions result in a reaction

### The Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

This distribution shows the spread of energies among particles in a gas:

```
Number of
particles
    ↑
    |        Lower temp
    |       ∕
    |      ∕
    |     ∕
    |    ∕ ← Ea (activation energy)
    |   ∕    │
    |  ∕     │ More particles
    | ∕      │ have energy ≥ Ea
    |∕_______│______ at higher temp
    |       ∕│
    |      ∕ │
    |     ∕  │ Higher temp
    |    ∕   │
    |___∕____│_______________→ Energy
```

**Key points:**
- At higher temperature, the curve shifts right and flattens
- More particles have higher energies
- The area to the right of Ea is larger (more particles can react)
- **A small temperature increase can cause a large rate increase** (approximately doubling rate per 10°C rise)

## 20.5 Effect of Concentration on Rate

### How Concentration Affects Rate

**Increasing concentration increases the rate of reaction because:**

1. **More particles per unit volume**
2. **Shorter distances between particles**
3. **More frequent collisions** per unit time
4. **More successful collisions** per unit time

### For Gases: Pressure

Increasing pressure on a gas has the same effect as increasing concentration:
- Particles are forced closer together
- More collisions per unit time
- Faster reaction rate

### Example: Reaction of Magnesium with Hydrochloric Acid

```
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
```

| Concentration of HCl | Rate of H₂ production |
|----------------------|----------------------|
| 0.5 mol/dm³ | Slow |
| 1.0 mol/dm³ | Moderate |
| 2.0 mol/dm³ | Fast |

Higher concentration = faster rate

## 20.6 Effect of Surface Area (Particle Size) on Rate

### How Surface Area Affects Rate

**Increasing surface area increases the rate of reaction because:**

1. **More particles are exposed** and available to react
2. **More frequent collisions** between exposed particles and reactants
3. **More successful collisions** per unit time

### Particle Size Relationship

| Particle Form | Surface Area | Rate of Reaction |
|---------------|--------------|------------------|
| Single large lump | Small | Slow |
| Several smaller pieces | Medium | Medium |
| Powder/fine particles | Large | Fast |

### Example: Reaction of Calcium Carbonate with Acid

```
CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + CO₂ + H₂O
```

| Form of CaCO₃ | Surface Area | Time to collect 50 cm³ CO₂ |
|---------------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Large marble chips | Small | ~120 seconds |
| Small marble chips | Medium | ~60 seconds |
| Powdered CaCO₃ | Large | ~15 seconds |

**Important:** The **total mass** of reactant is the same, but surface area differs.

### Why Does Surface Area Matter?

Only particles on the **surface** of a solid can collide with other reactants:
- Inside a solid lump, particles are surrounded by other solid particles
- Breaking a solid into smaller pieces exposes more particles
- More exposed particles = more possible collisions = faster reaction

## 20.7 Catalysts

### Definition

**Catalyst**: A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being chemically changed itself.

### How Catalysts Work

Catalysts work by:
1. **Providing an alternative reaction pathway** with lower activation energy
2. **Lowering the activation energy** required for the reaction
3. **Allowing more particles** to have sufficient energy to react

### Energy Profile with Catalyst

```
Energy
   ↑
   |      Reactants
   |         ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |              \
   |               \ Ea (without catalyst)
   |                ×
   |               / \
   |  Ea (with   /   \
   |  catalyst) ×     \
   |           / \     \
   |          /   \     \
   |         /     ×     \ (original Ea path)
   |        /    /  \
   |       /   /     \
   |      /  /        \
   |     / /           \
   |    //             \
   |   ∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙∙
   |   Products
   |____________________________→
       Reaction pathway
```

### Key Points About Catalysts

| Feature | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Rate of reaction | Increases |
| Activation energy | Decreases |
| Enthalpy change (ΔH) | **Unchanged** |
| Catalyst mass | Remains the same (not used up) |
| Catalyst chemical composition | Remains the same |
| Position of equilibrium | **Unchanged** (in reversible reactions) |

### Types of Catalysts

| Type | Example | Reaction |
|------|---------|----------|
| Heterogeneous catalyst | Iron (Fe) | Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃) |
| Heterogeneous catalyst | Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) | Contact process (SO₂ + ½O₂ → SO₃) |
| Heterogeneous catalyst | Platinum (Pt) | Catalytic converters |
| Enzyme (biological catalyst) | Amylase | Starch → maltose |
| Enzyme (biological catalyst) | Yeast enzymes | Fermentation |

### Catalytic Converters

Catalytic converters in car exhausts contain platinum, palladium, and rhodium:
- Convert harmful gases to less harmful products
- **2CO + 2NO → N₂ + 2CO₂** (removes toxic gases)
- **C₇H₁₆ + 11O₂ → 7CO₂ + 8H₂O** (oxidises unburnt hydrocarbons)

### Enzymes

**Enzymes**: Biological catalysts made of proteins that speed up reactions in living organisms.

**Characteristics:**
- Work at moderate temperatures (usually 30-40°C)
- Have an optimum pH
- Are specific to one reaction or type of reaction
- Can be denatured by high temperatures or extreme pH

## 20.8 Measuring Rate of Reaction

### Methods for Measuring Rate

#### Method 1: Measuring Volume of Gas Produced

**Set-up:**
```
     Gas syringe
        ↓
    ┌───────┐
    │       │ ← Gas
    │  ┌──┐ │
    │  │  │ │ ← Reaction mixture
    │  └──┘ │
    └───────┘
    Conical flask
```

**Procedure:**
1. Add reactants to flask and quickly connect gas syringe
2. Record volume of gas at regular time intervals
3. Plot graph of volume vs time

**Suitable for reactions producing gas:**
- Metal + acid → salt + hydrogen
- Carbonate + acid → salt + CO₂ + H₂O
- Decomposition reactions

#### Method 2: Measuring Mass Loss

**Set-up:**
```
         Balance
            ↓
    ┌───────────────┐
    │   Cotton wool │ ← Allows gas to escape
    │  (loosely in) │
    │   ┌───────┐   │
    │   │React- │   │
    │   │  ion  │   │
    │   └───────┘   │
    └───────────────┘
```

**Procedure:**
1. Place reactants on balance
2. Record mass at regular intervals
3. Plot graph of mass vs time

**Suitable for reactions producing a gas that escapes:**
- Carbonate + acid (CO₂ escapes)
- Metal + acid (H₂ escapes)

#### Method 3: Measuring Time for a Precipitate to Form

**Set-up:**
```
    Eye level → ↓
         ┌─────────────┐
         │   Clear     │
         │  solution   │
         │   ┌─────┐   │
         │   │View-│   │ ← Mark underneath
         │   │ ing │   │
         │   │cross│   │
         └─────────────┘
```

**Procedure:**
1. Place flask over a marked cross
2. Add reactants and start timer
3. Stop timer when cross disappears (precipitate blocks view)
4. Rate ∝ 1/time

**Suitable for reactions forming a precipitate:**
- Sodium thiosulfate + hydrochloric acid:
  Na₂S₂O₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + S + SO₂ + H₂O
  (sulfur precipitate forms)

## 20.9 Interpreting Rate Graphs

### Types of Rate Graphs

#### Graph 1: Quantity vs Time

```
Quantity
(product)
    ↑
    |           ●●●●●●●●●● ← Plateau (reaction complete)
    |         ●
    |       ●
    |     ●
    |   ●
    | ●
    |●
    |____________________________→
           Time
```

**Key features:**
- **Steep initial gradient** = fast initial rate
- **Curve flattening** = rate decreasing as reactants are used up
- **Plateau** = reaction complete (no more change)

#### Graph 2: Rate vs Concentration

```
Rate    ↑
    |              ●●●●●
    |           ●●
    |         ●
    |       ●
    |     ●
    |   ●
    | ●
    |●
    |____________________________→
           Concentration
```

**Rate is directly proportional to concentration** (for many reactions)

### Calculating Rate from a Graph

**Rate = Gradient of the tangent**

For curved graphs:
1. Draw a tangent to the curve at the point of interest
2. Calculate the gradient of this tangent
3. Gradient = Δy/Δx = rate at that time

```
Quantity
    ↑
    |           ●●●●●●
    |         ●╱
    |       ●╱ Tangent
    |     ●╱
    |   ●╱
    | ●╱
    |●╱
    |__╱________________________→
    │  Time
    └──┘
      Δx

    Gradient = Δy/Δx
```

### Comparing Rates

When comparing different conditions:
- **Steeper curve** = faster reaction
- **Earlier plateau** (same height) = faster reaction
- **Lower plateau** = less product formed (e.g., lower concentration)

## 20.10 Required Practical: Investigating Rate of Reaction

### Core Practical: Effect of Concentration on Rate

**Reaction:** Sodium thiosulfate + Hydrochloric acid
```
Na₂S₂O₃(aq) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + S(s) + SO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
```

**Aim:** To investigate how concentration affects rate

**Equipment:**
- Sodium thiosulfate solution (different concentrations)
- Hydrochloric acid (fixed volume and concentration)
- Conical flask
- Stopclock/timer
- Measuring cylinder
- Paper with marked cross
- Safety goggles

**Variables:**
- **Independent variable:** Concentration of sodium thiosulfate
- **Dependent variable:** Time for cross to disappear
- **Control variables:** Volume of acid, volume of thiosulfate, temperature, same flask, same cross

**Method:**
1. Draw a cross on paper and place under flask
2. Add 50 cm³ of sodium thiosulfate solution to flask
3. Add 10 cm³ of hydrochloric acid and start timer
4. Look down through flask and stop timer when cross disappears
5. Record time
6. Repeat with different concentrations of thiosulfate
7. Calculate rate = 1/time for each concentration

**Safety:**
- Wear safety goggles
- SO₂ is produced (toxic gas) - use in well-ventilated area
- Do not inhale fumes directly

**Results Table:**

| [Na₂S₂O₃] (mol/dm³) | Time (s) | Rate (1/time, s⁻¹) |
|---------------------|----------|-------------------|
| 0.4 | | |
| 0.3 | | |
| 0.2 | | |
| 0.1 | | |

**Expected Outcome:**
- Higher concentration → shorter time → higher rate
- Graph of rate vs concentration should show positive correlation
- Rate ∝ concentration (direct proportionality)

### Alternative Practical: Effect of Temperature on Rate

**Reaction:** Magnesium + Hydrochloric acid
```
Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
```

**Aim:** To investigate how temperature affects rate

**Method:**
1. Set up water bath at required temperature
2. Add fixed volume of HCl to flask and allow to reach temperature
3. Add fixed mass of magnesium ribbon
4. Collect hydrogen gas in gas syringe
5. Record volume of gas at regular intervals
6. Repeat at different temperatures
7. Compare initial rates (initial gradients)

**Expected Outcome:**
- Higher temperature → faster rate
- Approximately doubling rate per 10°C rise

### Alternative Practical: Effect of Surface Area on Rate

**Reaction:** Calcium carbonate + Hydrochloric acid
```
CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
```

**Aim:** To investigate how particle size affects rate

**Method:**
1. Add fixed volume of HCl to flask
2. Add fixed mass of CaCO₃ (same mass, different particle size)
3. Collect CO₂ gas in gas syringe
4. Record volume at regular intervals
5. Repeat with different particle sizes (large chips, small chips, powder)

**Expected Outcome:**
- Smaller particles (larger surface area) → faster rate
- Same total volume of gas produced (same mass of reactant)

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Incomplete Explanations
❌ **Wrong:** "Increasing temperature increases the rate because particles move faster"
✅ **Correct:** "Increasing temperature increases the rate because particles have more kinetic energy, move faster, and collide more frequently. MORE IMPORTANTLY, more particles have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy, so more collisions are successful."

### 2. Catalyst Misconceptions
❌ **Wrong:** "Catalysts increase the rate by being used up"
✅ **Correct:** Catalysts are **NOT used up** - they provide an alternative pathway with lower activation energy

### 3. Surface Area Confusion
❌ **Wrong:** "Powder has more mass than lumps"
✅ **Correct:** Powder has the **same mass** but **larger surface area**

### 4. Collision Theory Errors
❌ **Wrong:** "More collisions = faster rate"
✅ **Correct:** More **successful** collisions = faster rate. Collisions must have sufficient energy AND correct orientation.

### 5. Graph Interpretation
❌ **Wrong:** "The curve goes up because rate increases"
✅ **Correct:** The curve shows amount of product. The **gradient** represents the rate. The curve flattens because rate decreases as reactants are used up.

### 6. Fair Testing
❌ **Wrong:** Not mentioning control variables in practical questions
✅ **Correct:** Always state control variables and explain why they must be kept constant

### 7. Units
❌ **Wrong:** Rate = "1/time" without units
✅ **Correct:** Rate = 1/time with units (s⁻¹)

### 8. Enzyme Temperature
❌ **Wrong:** "Enzymes work best at high temperatures"
✅ **Correct:** Enzymes have an **optimum temperature** (~37°C for human enzymes) and are **denatured** at high temperatures

## Exam Tips

1. **Always link back to collision theory** when explaining rate changes
2. **Mention both frequency AND energy** for temperature effects
3. **State control variables** in practical questions
4. **Draw graphs carefully** with labeled axes
5. **Use scientific terms:** "activation energy," "successful collisions," "alternative pathway"
6. **Remember:** Catalysts change rate, not position of equilibrium or ΔH
7. **Practical questions:** Always mention safety precautions
8. **Calculation questions:** Show working and include units
