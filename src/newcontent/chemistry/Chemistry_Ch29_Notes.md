## 29.1 Introduction to Polymers

### What is a Polymer?

A **polymer** is a large molecule made up of many small, repeating units called **monomers** joined together by covalent bonds.

- **Monomer**: Small molecule that can join together to form a polymer
- **Polymer**: Large molecule made from many monomers
- **Polymerisation**: The chemical reaction in which monomers join together to form polymers

### Types of Polymers

| Type | Source | Examples |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Natural polymers** | Found in nature | Proteins, starch, cellulose, DNA, natural rubber |
| **Synthetic polymers** | Made by humans | Poly(ethene), PVC, nylon, polyesters |

---

## 29.2 Addition Polymerisation

### Definition

**Addition polymerisation** is a reaction in which unsaturated monomers (containing C=C double bonds) join together to form a polymer without the loss of any small molecules.

### Key Features of Addition Polymerisation

1. **Requires unsaturated monomers** - must contain C=C double bond
2. **No by-products formed** - all atoms from monomers end up in polymer
3. **Double bond opens** - C=C becomes C-C single bond in polymer
4. **High pressure and catalyst** often needed

### The Mechanism of Addition Polymerisation

```
Step 1: Initiation
- The double bond in the monomer is activated (by heat, pressure, or catalyst)

Step 2: Propagation  
- The activated monomers join together, one after another
- Each C=C double bond opens to form two new single bonds

Step 3: Termination
- The polymer chain stops growing
```

### General Equation for Addition Polymerisation

For an alkene monomer with general structure CH₂=CHR:

**n CH₂=CHR → -[-CH₂-CHR-]-n-**

Where:
- n = number of monomers (very large number, typically thousands)
- The repeating unit is shown in square brackets with subscript n

## 29.3 Formation of Common Addition Polymers

### 29.3.1 Poly(ethene) / Polythene

**Monomer**: Ethene (CH₂=CH₂)

**Formation**:
```
n CH₂=CH₂  →  -[-CH₂-CH₂-]-n-
```

**Conditions**:
- **High pressure**: 1000-3000 atmospheres
- **Temperature**: 100-300°C
- **Initiator**: Small amount of oxygen or organic peroxide

**Types of Poly(ethene)**:

| Type | Conditions | Properties | Uses |
|------|------------|------------|------|
| **LDPE** (Low Density) | High pressure, high temperature | Branched chains, flexible, lower melting point | Plastic bags, cling film, squeeze bottles |
| **HDPE** (High Density) | Lower pressure, catalyst (Zeigler-Natta) | Straight chains, stronger, higher melting point | Milk bottles, buckets, pipes |

**Properties**:
- Chemically inert (unreactive)
- Electrical insulator
- Waterproof
- Flexible and tough
- Can be transparent or opaque

**Uses**:
- Plastic bags and carrier bags
- Food packaging and containers
- Cling film (LDPE)
- Milk bottles (HDPE)
- Pipes and buckets
- Dustbins

---

### 29.3.2 Poly(propene) / Polypropylene

**Monomer**: Propene (CH₂=CHCH₃)

**Formation**:
```
n CH₂=CHCH₃  →  -[-CH₂-CH(CH₃)-]-n-
```

**Properties**:
- Stronger and more rigid than poly(ethene)
- Higher melting point (~160°C)
- Resistant to fatigue (can be bent repeatedly)
- Chemically inert
- Can be made into fibres

**Uses**:
- Ropes and carpets (as fibres)
- Food containers (microwave-safe)
- Car bumpers
- Bottle crates
- Medical equipment
- Thermal underwear

**Why poly(propene) is suitable for ropes and carpets**:
- Strong and lightweight
- Resistant to wear and tear
- Does not absorb water
- Can be drawn into fibres

---

### 29.3.3 Poly(chloroethene) / PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

**Monomer**: Chloroethene (vinyl chloride, CH₂=CHCl)

**Formation**:
```
n CH₂=CHCl  →  -[-CH₂-CHCl-]-n-
```

**Properties**:
- Rigid and strong
- Flame resistant (due to chlorine)
- Good electrical insulator
- Resistant to chemicals
- Can be made flexible with plasticisers

**Types of PVC**:

| Type | Additive | Uses |
|------|----------|------|
| **Rigid PVC** | No plasticiser | Window frames, pipes, gutters |
| **Flexible PVC** | Plasticisers added | Cable insulation, flooring, hoses |

**Uses**:
- Window frames and doors
- Water and drainage pipes
- Electrical cable insulation
- Floor coverings
- Raincoats and inflatable products
- Medical tubing

**Why PVC is suitable for window frames**:
- Strong and rigid
- Does not rot or corrode
- Flame resistant
- Good insulator
- Low maintenance

---

### 29.3.4 Poly(tetrafluoroethene) / PTFE (Teflon)

**Monomer**: Tetrafluoroethene (CF₂=CF₂)

**Formation**:
```
n CF₂=CF₂  →  -[-CF₂-CF₂-]-n-
```

**Properties**:
- **Very low friction** - the most slippery solid known
- **Non-stick** surface
- **Chemically inert** - resistant to almost all chemicals
- **High melting point** (~327°C)
- **Heat resistant**
- **Electrically insulating**
- Hydrophobic (water-repellent)

**Uses**:
- Non-stick coating for frying pans and cookware
- Bearing surfaces in machinery
- Waterproof outdoor clothing (Gore-Tex)
- Electrical insulation
- Chemical-resistant pipes and gaskets
- Dental floss

**Why PTFE is suitable for non-stick coatings**:
- Extremely low friction coefficient
- Non-reactive with food
- Stable at high cooking temperatures
- Creates a smooth, water-repellent surface

## 29.4 Condensation Polymerisation (Chemistry Only)

### Definition

**Condensation polymerisation** is a reaction in which monomers join together to form a polymer with the loss of a small molecule (usually water or HCl).

### Key Features of Condensation Polymerisation

1. **Two different functional groups** needed on monomers
2. **Small molecule eliminated** - usually water (H₂O) or hydrogen chloride (HCl)
3. **No C=C double bond required**
4. **Often involves two different monomers**

### Comparison: Addition vs Condensation Polymerisation

| Feature | Addition Polymerisation | Condensation Polymerisation |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| Monomer structure | Unsaturated (C=C) | Contains reactive functional groups |
| Number of monomer types | Usually one | Often two different types |
| By-products | None | Small molecule (H₂O, HCl) |
| Atom economy | 100% | Less than 100% |

## 29.5 Formation of Polyesters

### What is a Polyester?

A **polyester** is a condensation polymer formed from:
- A **diol** (alcohol with two -OH groups)
- A **dicarboxylic acid** (acid with two -COOH groups)

### The Ester Linkage

Polyesters contain the **ester linkage** (-COO-) formed between:
- The -OH group from the alcohol
- The -COOH group from the carboxylic acid

**Ester linkage formation**:
```
-COOH + HO-  →  -COO- + H₂O
```

### Formation of Polyesters

**General equation**:
```
n HOOC-R-COOH + n HO-R'-OH  →  -[-OC-R-COO-R'-O-]-n- + 2n H₂O
```

Where R and R' represent the carbon chains.

### Example: Terylene (PET)

**Monomers**:
- Ethane-1,2-diol (ethylene glycol): HO-CH₂-CH₂-OH
- Benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid (terephthalic acid): HOOC-C₆H₄-COOH

**Formation**:
```
n HO-CH₂-CH₂-OH + n HOOC-C₆H₄-COOH  →  
-[-O-CH₂-CH₂-OOC-C₆H₄-CO-]-n- + 2n H₂O
```

**Properties of Terylene (PET)**:
- Strong and durable
- Can be drawn into fibres
- Resistant to stretching and shrinking
- Quick drying
- Resistant to most chemicals

**Uses of Terylene (PET)**:
- Clothing fabrics (often blended with cotton)
- Drink bottles (PET bottles)
- Magnetic recording tapes
- Sails and ropes
- Food packaging

## 29.6 Structure and Properties of Polymers

### Factors Affecting Polymer Properties

#### 1. Chain Length
- **Longer chains** → stronger polymer
- More entanglement between chains
- Stronger intermolecular forces

#### 2. Branching
- **Branched chains** → lower density, lower melting point
- Branched chains cannot pack as closely
- Example: LDPE (branched) vs HDPE (linear)

#### 3. Intermolecular Forces
- **Stronger forces** between chains → stronger, higher melting point
- Types: van der Waals forces, dipole-dipole forces, hydrogen bonding
- Polymers with polar groups have stronger forces

#### 4. Cross-linking
- **Cross-links** between chains → rigid, infusible polymer
- Cannot be melted and reshaped (thermosetting)
- Example: Vulcanised rubber

#### 5. Chain Flexibility
- **Flexible chains** → softer, more elastic
- Presence of double bonds or certain groups can increase flexibility

### Thermoplastic vs Thermosetting Polymers

| Property | Thermoplastic | Thermosetting |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Structure** | Linear or branched chains | Cross-linked network |
| **Effect of heating** | Softens and melts | Decomposes (does not melt) |
| **Reshaping** | Can be reshaped repeatedly | Cannot be reshaped |
| **Solubility** | Often soluble in some solvents | Insoluble |
| **Examples** | Poly(ethene), PVC, PET | Bakelite, melamine, epoxy resins |

### Relating Properties to Uses

| Polymer | Key Property | Application |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Poly(ethene) | Flexible, waterproof | Plastic bags, packaging |
| Poly(propene) | Strong, fatigue-resistant | Ropes, containers |
| PVC | Rigid, flame-resistant | Window frames, cables |
| PTFE | Non-stick, low friction | Non-stick pans |
| PET | Strong, can form fibres | Clothing, bottles |

## 29.7 Uses of Polymers

### Summary Table of Polymer Uses

| Polymer | Properties | Common Uses |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Poly(ethene)** | Flexible, waterproof, chemically inert | Bags, bottles, packaging, pipes |
| **Poly(propene)** | Strong, heat-resistant, fatigue-resistant | Ropes, carpets, car parts, containers |
| **PVC** | Rigid, flame-resistant, durable | Windows, pipes, cable insulation |
| **PTFE** | Non-stick, inert, low friction | Non-stick pans, bearings, waterproof clothing |
| **PET (polyester)** | Strong, lightweight, recyclable | Bottles, clothing, packaging |

### Choosing the Right Polymer

When selecting a polymer for a particular use, consider:
1. **Mechanical properties** - strength, flexibility, hardness
2. **Thermal properties** - melting point, heat resistance
3. **Chemical resistance** - will it react with contents?
4. **Electrical properties** - insulation required?
5. **Cost** - is it economical?
6. **Safety** - food contact, medical use?

## 29.8 Disposal and Environmental Issues

### The Problem with Polymer Waste

**Why polymer waste is a problem**:
1. **Non-biodegradable** - most synthetic polymers do not decompose naturally
2. **Large volume** - polymers make up a significant portion of household waste
3. **Landfill space** - taking up valuable land space
4. **Visual pollution** - litter is unsightly
5. **Harm to wildlife** - animals can be trapped or ingest plastic
6. **Marine pollution** - plastic waste in oceans harms marine life
7. **Resource waste** - polymers are made from crude oil (finite resource)

### Methods of Polymer Disposal

#### 1. Landfill

**Process**: Waste is buried in the ground

**Advantages**:
- Simple and convenient
- Low cost

**Disadvantages**:
- Takes up land space
- Non-biodegradable polymers persist for hundreds of years
- Can contaminate soil and groundwater
- Produces methane (greenhouse gas)

#### 2. Incineration

**Process**: Waste is burned at high temperatures

**Advantages**:
- Reduces volume of waste by ~90%
- Can generate energy (waste-to-energy)
- Destroys hazardous materials

**Disadvantages**:
- Releases CO₂ (greenhouse gas)
- May release toxic gases (need scrubbers)
- Produces ash that must be disposed of
- PVC releases harmful HCl when burned

#### 3. Recycling

**Process**: Polymers are collected, sorted, cleaned, and reprocessed

**Advantages**:
- Conserves crude oil resources
- Reduces landfill use
- Lower energy consumption than making new plastic
- Reduces pollution

**Disadvantages**:
- Must be sorted by type (different polymers cannot be mixed)
- Can be expensive
- Quality may decrease with each recycling
- Some polymers cannot be recycled easily

**Recycling Symbols**:

| Symbol | Polymer | Common Items |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| 1 PET | Poly(ethylene terephthalate) | Drinks bottles, food containers |
| 2 HDPE | High-density poly(ethene) | Milk bottles, detergent bottles |
| 3 PVC | Poly(chloroethene) | Pipes, window frames |
| 4 LDPE | Low-density poly(ethene) | Carrier bags, cling film |
| 5 PP | Poly(propene) | Yogurt pots, bottle caps |
| 6 PS | Polystyrene | Food containers, packaging |
| 7 Other | Other polymers | Various |

#### 4. Feedstock Recycling

**Process**: Polymers are broken down into monomers or other chemicals that can be used as raw materials

**Advantages**:
- Can handle mixed or contaminated plastics
- Produces useful chemicals
- Reduces reliance on crude oil

**Disadvantages**:
- High energy input required
- Not yet widely available
- Expensive technology

## 29.9 Biodegradable and Non-biodegradable Polymers

### Definitions

**Biodegradable polymers**: Polymers that can be broken down by microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) into natural substances such as water, carbon dioxide, and biomass.

**Non-biodegradable polymers**: Polymers that cannot be broken down by microorganisms and persist in the environment for very long periods.

### Non-biodegradable Polymers

Most synthetic polymers are non-biodegradable:
- Poly(ethene)
- Poly(propene)
- PVC
- PTFE

**Why they don't biodegrade**:
- Strong C-C bonds are not easily broken by enzymes
- No natural microorganisms can digest them
- They have only existed for ~100 years (not enough time for evolution)

**Environmental impact**:
- Persist in environment for hundreds of years
- Accumulate in oceans and on land
- Break into microplastics (tiny particles)
- Microplastics can enter food chain

### Biodegradable Polymers

#### Types of Biodegradable Polymers

**1. Natural biodegradable polymers**:
- Starch-based polymers
- Cellulose-based polymers
- Polylactic acid (PLA) from corn starch

**2. Synthetic biodegradable polymers**:
- Polyglycolic acid (PGA)
- Polycaprolactone (PCL)

**3. Photodegradable polymers**:
- Break down when exposed to UV light
- Contain light-sensitive groups

#### How Biodegradable Polymers Work

1. Microorganisms secrete enzymes
2. Enzymes break the polymer chains
3. Smaller molecules are absorbed by microorganisms
4. Final products: CO₂, H₂O, and biomass

#### Conditions for Biodegradation

- Presence of microorganisms
- Suitable temperature and moisture
- Oxygen (for aerobic) or absence of oxygen (for anaerobic)
- Often require industrial composting facilities

**Time to biodegrade**:
- Biodegradable plastic bag: 3-6 months (in ideal conditions)
- Paper: 2-5 months
- Orange peel: 6 months
- Plastic bag (non-biodegradable): 500+ years

### Advantages of Biodegradable Polymers

- Reduce dependence on crude oil (some types)
- Can compost into useful material
- Reduce landfill burden
- Less persistent in environment

### Disadvantages of Biodegradable Polymers

- Often more expensive than conventional plastics
- May require specific conditions to biodegrade
- Can produce methane in landfill (greenhouse gas)
- May contaminate recycling streams if mixed
- Some require industrial composting (not home compost)

## Common Exam Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Polymer Formation Equations
❌ Drawing the polymer chain with C=C double bonds
✅ The double bond OPENS during polymerisation; the polymer has only C-C single bonds

### Mistake 2: Writing Polymer Formulas
❌ Forgetting the subscript 'n' for the repeating unit
✅ Always write: -[-repeating unit-]-n-

### Mistake 3: Addition vs Condensation
❌ "Addition polymerisation produces water as a by-product"
✅ Addition polymerisation has NO by-products; condensation polymerisation produces small molecules like H₂O

### Mistake 4: Monomer Structure
❌ Drawing a polymer with C=C as the monomer for condensation polymerisation
✅ Condensation polymerisation requires monomers with reactive functional groups (e.g., -OH and -COOH), not C=C

### Mistake 5: Identifying Polymers
❌ Confusing poly(propene) with poly(ethene)
✅ Look for the methyl (-CH₃) group in poly(propene) and the chlorine in PVC

### Mistake 6: Biodegradable Definition
❌ "Biodegradable means the polymer disappears completely"
✅ Biodegradable means broken down by microorganisms into natural products (CO₂, H₂O, biomass)

### Mistake 7: Recycling Issues
❌ "All plastics can be recycled together"
✅ Different polymers must be separated before recycling; they cannot be mixed

### Mistake 8: Polymer Names
❌ Writing "polyethene" instead of "poly(ethene)"
✅ Always use brackets: poly(ethene), poly(propene), poly(chloroethene)

### Mistake 9: Environmental Impact
❌ "Burning plastic is always bad for the environment"
✅ Incineration can be controlled with scrubbers and can generate energy, but does release CO₂

### Mistake 10: PTFE Properties
❌ "PTFE is used because it is cheap"
✅ PTFE is expensive; it is used for its unique non-stick and low-friction properties
