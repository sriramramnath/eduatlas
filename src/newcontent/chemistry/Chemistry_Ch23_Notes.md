## 1. Formation and Composition of Crude Oil

### 1.1 What is Crude Oil?

**Definition:** Crude oil is a **finite, non-renewable fossil fuel** found in underground reservoirs. It is a complex **mixture of hydrocarbons** (compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen atoms).

### 1.2 Formation of Crude Oil

Crude oil was formed over **millions of years** through the following process:

1. **Ancient marine organisms** (plankton and algae) died and sank to the ocean floor
2. These organisms were buried under **layers of sand and mud**
3. Over millions of years, more sediment accumulated, creating **high pressure** and **high temperature** conditions
4. The absence of **oxygen** prevented complete decomposition
5. The organic matter was transformed into crude oil through a process called **diagenesis** and **catagenesis**

**Key Points to Remember:**
- Crude oil forms from **marine organisms**, NOT dinosaurs or land plants
- Formation requires **anaerobic conditions** (no oxygen)
- The process takes **millions of years**
- Crude oil is a **finite resource** – it cannot be replaced on a human timescale

### 1.3 Composition of Crude Oil

Crude oil consists of:
- **Hydrocarbons** – compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen
- Mostly **alkanes** (saturated hydrocarbons)
- Molecules with different chain lengths (from C₁ to over C₇₀)
- Also contains small amounts of sulfur, nitrogen, and oxygen compounds

## 2. Fractional Distillation of Crude Oil

### 2.1 The Need for Separation

Crude oil is a **mixture** of many different hydrocarbons with different boiling points. To make it useful, it must be **separated** into fractions with similar boiling points.

### 2.2 Principle of Fractional Distillation

**Key Concept:** Hydrocarbons have **different boiling points** depending on their molecular size:
- **Shorter chain** hydrocarbons have **lower boiling points** (fewer intermolecular forces)
- **Longer chain** hydrocarbons have **higher boiling points** (more intermolecular forces)

**Definition of a Fraction:** A fraction is a group of hydrocarbons with **similar boiling points** and **similar chain lengths**.

### 2.3 The Fractionating Column

The fractional distillation occurs in a **fractionating column**:

```
        ┌─────────────────────────┐ ← Gases out (cool)
        │                         │
        │     REFINERY GAS        │ (20°C) - LPG
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     GASOLINE/PETROL     │ (40-70°C)
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     NAPHTHA             │ (70-120°C) - Chemicals
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     KEROSENE            │ (150-200°C) - Jet fuel
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     DIESEL OIL          │ (200-300°C)
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     FUEL OIL            │ (300-400°C) - Ships
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     LUBRICATING OIL     │ (350-400°C)
        ├─────────────────────────┤
        │                         │
        │     BITUMEN             │ (>400°C) - Roads
        └─────────────────────────┘
```

### 2.4 Process of Fractional Distillation

1. Crude oil is **heated** to about 400°C in a furnace
2. The hot vapour enters the **bottom of the fractionating column**
3. The column is **hotter at the bottom** and **cooler at the top**
4. Vapours **rise** through the column and **cool down**
5. When a vapour reaches a temperature below its boiling point, it **condenses**
6. Fractions are **collected at different levels** based on their boiling points

### 2.5 Fractions and Their Uses

| Fraction | Boiling Point Range | Chain Length | Main Uses |
|----------|---------------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Refinery Gas** | Below 25°C | C₁-C₄ | LPG for heating and cooking |
| **Gasoline/Petrol** | 40-70°C | C₅-C₁₀ | Car fuel |
| **Naphtha** | 70-120°C | C₆-C₁₂ | Chemical feedstock, making plastics |
| **Kerosene** | 150-200°C | C₁₀-C₁₆ | Jet fuel, paraffin heaters |
| **Diesel Oil** | 200-300°C | C₁₅-C₂₀ | Lorries, buses, trains, cars |
| **Fuel Oil** | 300-400°C | C₂₀-C₃₀ | Ships, power stations |
| **Lubricating Oil** | 350-400°C | C₂₀-C₄₀ | Engine oil, waxes |
| **Bitumen** | Above 400°C | C₄₀+ | Road surfacing, roofing |

## 3. Properties of Hydrocarbons

### 3.1 Trends in Physical Properties

As hydrocarbon chain length **increases**:

| Property | Trend | Explanation |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Boiling Point** | **Increases** | Longer chains have more electrons, stronger intermolecular forces (van der Waals forces) require more energy to overcome |
| **Viscosity** | **Increases** | Longer chains tangle together more, making the liquid thicker and flow less easily |
| **Volatility** | **Decreases** | Longer chains have higher boiling points, so they evaporate less easily |
| **Flammability** | **Decreases** | Shorter chains vaporise more easily and mix better with oxygen |
| **Colour** | Darker | Longer chain fractions are darker (from colourless gases to black bitumen) |

### 3.2 Boiling Point Explained

**Key Principle:** Boiling point depends on the strength of **intermolecular forces** (forces between molecules), NOT the strength of covalent bonds within molecules.

- **Van der Waals forces** (also called London forces or temporary dipole-dipole forces) exist between all molecules
- Longer hydrocarbon chains have **more electrons** and **greater surface area**
- This results in **stronger van der Waals forces**
- More energy (higher temperature) is needed to overcome these forces

### 3.3 Viscosity Explained

**Definition:** Viscosity is a measure of a fluid's resistance to flow. High viscosity = thick, slow-flowing liquid.

- **Short chain** hydrocarbons: low viscosity (runny, like petrol)
- **Long chain** hydrocarbons: high viscosity (thick, like bitumen)

### 3.4 Flammability Explained

**Definition:** Flammability refers to how easily a substance catches fire and burns.

- **Short chain** hydrocarbons vaporise easily at room temperature
- Vapour mixes readily with oxygen in the air
- This makes them **highly flammable**
- **Long chain** hydrocarbons do not vaporise easily
- They require heating before they can burn
- This makes them **less flammable**

## 4. Cracking

### 4.1 What is Cracking?

**Definition:** Cracking is the **breaking down of long-chain hydrocarbons** into **shorter-chain hydrocarbons** by heating.

### 4.2 Why is Cracking Necessary?

**Supply and Demand Problem:**
- Crude oil contains more **long-chain** hydrocarbons than needed
- There is high demand for **short-chain** hydrocarbons (petrol, naphtha)
- Cracking converts less useful **long-chain fractions** into more useful **short-chain fractions**

### 4.3 Types of Cracking

#### A. Thermal Cracking
- **High temperature** (450-750°C)
- **High pressure** (up to 70 atmospheres)
- Produces mostly **alkenes** (unsaturated hydrocarbons)
- Used to make **alkenes** for polymer production

#### B. Catalytic Cracking
- **Moderate temperature** (450-550°C)
- **Slightly above atmospheric pressure**
- Uses a **zeolite catalyst** (aluminium silicate)
- Produces **branched alkanes** and **cycloalkanes** (better for petrol)
- More efficient and cheaper than thermal cracking

### 4.4 The Cracking Process

**General Equation:**
```
Long-chain alkane  →  Shorter-chain alkane  +  Alkene
   (saturated)           (saturated)        (unsaturated)
```

**Example:**
```
C₁₄H₃₀  →  C₈H₁₈  +  C₆H₁₂
(tetradecane)  (octane)   (hexene)
```

**Another Example:**
```
C₁₀H₂₂  →  C₈H₁₈  +  C₂H₄
(decane)    (octane)   (ethene)
```

### 4.5 Products of Cracking

| Product | Type | Uses |
|---------|------|------|
| **Shorter alkanes** | Saturated | Fuels (petrol, LPG) |
| **Alkenes** | Unsaturated | Making polymers (plastics), ethanol |

### 4.6 Balanced Equations for Cracking

When writing cracking equations:
- **Conserve atoms** – same number of C and H on both sides
- Products include one **alkane** and one **alkene**
- Check that the equation balances

**Worked Example:**
C₁₄H₃₀ can crack to form three molecules of ethene (C₂H₄) and one alkane.

1. Carbon atoms: 14 = (3 × 2) + Cₐₗₖₐₙₑ → Cₐₗₖₐₙₑ = 14 - 6 = 8
2. Hydrogen atoms: 30 = (3 × 4) + Hₐₗₖₐₙₑ → Hₐₗₖₐₙₑ = 30 - 12 = 18

**Equation:** C₁₄H₃₀ → 3C₂H₄ + C₈H₁₈

## 5. Complete and Incomplete Combustion

### 5.1 Combustion of Hydrocarbons

All hydrocarbons can be burned (combusted) to release energy. The type of combustion depends on the **amount of oxygen available**.

### 5.2 Complete Combustion

**Conditions:** **Plentiful/excess oxygen**

**Products:** Carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O)

**General Equation:**
```
Hydrocarbon + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy
```

**Example (Methane):**
```
CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
```

**Example (Propane):**
```
C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
```

**Characteristics:**
- **Blue flame**
- **Clean combustion**
- Maximum energy released
- No soot or smoke

### 5.3 Incomplete Combustion

**Conditions:** **Limited/insufficient oxygen**

**Products:** Carbon monoxide (CO) and/or carbon (C) and water

**Type 1 – Producing Carbon Monoxide:**
```
2CH₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO + 4H₂O
```

**Type 2 – Producing Carbon (Soot):**
```
CH₄ + O₂ → C + 2H₂O
```

**Characteristics:**
- **Yellow/orange flame**
- **Sooty flame**
- Less energy released
- Dangerous – carbon monoxide is toxic

### 5.4 Dangers of Carbon Monoxide

**Carbon monoxide (CO)** is:
- **Colourless** and **odourless**
- **Toxic** – binds to haemoglobin in blood, preventing oxygen transport
- Produced by **faulty gas appliances** and car engines
- Causes **carbon monoxide poisoning** – can be fatal

## 6. Environmental Considerations

### 6.1 Fossil Fuels and the Environment

Burning fossil fuels releases **carbon dioxide**, a **greenhouse gas** that contributes to:
- **Global warming**
- **Climate change**

### 6.2 Sulfur Dioxide

Some crude oil contains sulfur impurities. Burning produces **sulfur dioxide (SO₂)**:
- Contributes to **acid rain**
- Causes respiratory problems

### 6.3 Sustainable Alternatives

- **Biofuels** – renewable alternatives to fossil fuels
- **Electric vehicles** – reducing petrol/diesel consumption
- **Hydrogen fuel cells** – clean energy source

## Common Exam Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing Crude Oil Formation
❌ **Wrong:** "Crude oil formed from dinosaurs"
✅ **Correct:** "Crude oil formed from ancient marine plankton and algae"

### Mistake 2: Stating Fractions Have Fixed Boiling Points
❌ **Wrong:** "Petrol boils at 50°C"
✅ **Correct:** "Petrol has a boiling point range of 40-70°C"

### Mistake 3: Explaining Boiling Point Incorrectly
❌ **Wrong:** "Longer chains have higher boiling points because covalent bonds are stronger"
✅ **Correct:** "Longer chains have higher boiling points because there are stronger intermolecular forces between molecules"

### Mistake 4: Confusing Cracking Products
❌ **Wrong:** "Cracking produces only alkanes"
✅ **Correct:** "Cracking produces shorter alkanes AND alkenes"

### Mistake 5: Forgetting to Balance Cracking Equations
❌ **Wrong:** "C₁₄H₃₀ → C₈H₁₈ + C₆H₁₂" without checking balance
✅ **Correct:** Always count C and H atoms on both sides

### Mistake 6: Incomplete Combustion Products
❌ **Wrong:** "Incomplete combustion always produces carbon monoxide"
✅ **Correct:** "Incomplete combustion can produce carbon monoxide OR carbon (soot) OR both"

### Mistake 7: Temperature Gradient in Fractionating Column
❌ **Wrong:** "The column is hotter at the top"
✅ **Correct:** "The column is hotter at the bottom and cooler at the top"

### Mistake 8: Confusing Alkenes and Alkanes
❌ **Wrong:** "Crude oil contains alkenes"
✅ **Correct:** "Crude oil contains mostly alkanes; alkenes are produced by cracking"

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Hydrocarbon** | A compound containing only carbon and hydrogen atoms |
| **Alkane** | A saturated hydrocarbon with single bonds only |
| **Fraction** | A group of hydrocarbons with similar boiling points |
| **Fractional Distillation** | Separation of a mixture into fractions based on different boiling points |
| **Cracking** | Breaking down long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter-chain hydrocarbons |
| **Viscosity** | A measure of a fluid's resistance to flow |
| **Volatility** | The tendency of a substance to vaporise |
| **Flammability** | How easily a substance catches fire and burns |
| **Complete Combustion** | Combustion with plentiful oxygen, producing CO₂ and H₂O |
| **Incomplete Combustion** | Combustion with limited oxygen, producing CO and/or C |
| **Finite Resource** | A resource that cannot be replaced once used up |

## Exam Tips

1. **Always use correct terminology** – "intermolecular forces" not "bonds between molecules"
2. **Include state symbols** in equations when required
3. **Balance equations** – check C and H atoms on both sides
4. **Learn the order of fractions** – from lowest to highest boiling point
5. **Explain trends** in terms of intermolecular forces and chain length
6. **State conditions** for cracking (temperature, catalyst, pressure)
7. **Distinguish between types of combustion** – give products and conditions
