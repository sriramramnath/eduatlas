## Part A: Multiple Choice Answers

| Question | Answer | Explanation |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Q1** | **B** | Oxygen relights a glowing splint. A lighted splint is used for hydrogen. |
| **Q2** | **C** | Carbon dioxide turns limewater milky due to formation of calcium carbonate precipitate. |
| **Q3** | **C** | Potassium ions (K⁺) produce a lilac (pale purple) flame. Lithium is crimson, sodium is yellow, calcium is orange-red. |
| **Q4** | **B** | Cu²⁺ forms a blue precipitate of Cu(OH)₂ which is insoluble in excess NaOH. |
| **Q5** | **D** | Al³⁺ gives white Al(OH)₃ which dissolves in excess NaOH. Ca²⁺ gives white ppt that does NOT dissolve. Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ give green and brown precipitates. |
| **Q6** | **B** | Barium chloride (BaCl₂) is added to test for sulfates. Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄(s). |
| **Q7** | **A** | Chloride ions (Cl⁻) form white AgCl precipitate. Bromide gives cream, iodide gives yellow. |
| **Q8** | **B** | Ammonia is alkaline and turns damp red litmus blue. |
| **Q9** | **D** | Calcium ions give an orange-red (brick red) flame colour. |
| **Q10** | **B** | HNO₃ removes carbonate ions which would otherwise form white precipitate with Ag⁺ and give a false positive. |
| **Q11** | **C** | Flame emission spectroscopy is quantitative - it can measure concentration of metal ions. It can identify multiple elements simultaneously. |
| **Q12** | **C** | Both Al³⁺ and Zn²⁺ give white precipitates that dissolve in excess NaOH. Use NH₃: Al(OH)₃ does NOT dissolve in excess NH₃, but Zn(OH)₂ DOES dissolve. |

## Part C: Short Answer Mark Schemes

### Q1. Gas Tests (3 marks)

**(a) Oxygen gas (1 mark):**
- Place a **glowing splint** into the gas (½ mark)
- The splint **relights** / bursts into flame (½ mark)

**(b) Carbon dioxide gas (1 mark):**
- Bubble the gas through **limewater** / calcium hydroxide solution (½ mark)
- The limewater turns **milky/cloudy** / white precipitate forms (½ mark)

**(c) Hydrogen gas (1 mark):**
- Place a **lighted splint** at the mouth of the test tube (½ mark)
- A **"pop" sound** is heard (½ mark)

### Q3. Distinguishing Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ (3 marks)

**Method:**
1. Add sodium hydroxide solution to the unknown solution (1 mark for method)

**Observations:**
2. If **green precipitate** forms → **iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺)** present (1 mark)

3. If **brown/rust-coloured precipitate** forms → **iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺)** present (1 mark)

**Award full marks for:**
- Correct method (add NaOH)
- Correct identification of Fe²⁺ with green precipitate
- Correct identification of Fe³⁺ with brown precipitate

**Acceptable equations:**
```
Fe²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₂(s) [green]
Fe³⁺ + 3OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₃(s) [brown]
```

---

### Q4. Sulfate Test (3 marks)

**(a) Ionic equation (1 mark):**
```
Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s)
```
*Marks awarded for:*
- Correct ions on left (½ mark)
- Correct product with state symbol (½ mark)

**(b) Why add HCl (1 mark):**
- To **remove carbonate ions** / prevent formation of barium carbonate
- OR: Carbonate ions would give a white precipitate with BaCl₂ and give a **false positive**
- OR: To ensure any white precipitate is due to sulfate, not carbonate

*(Any valid explanation = 1 mark)*

**(c) Colour of precipitate (1 mark):**
- **White** precipitate
