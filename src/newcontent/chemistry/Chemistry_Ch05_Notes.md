## 5.1 Writing Formulae for Ionic Compounds

### 5.1.1 Ions

**Definition:** An ion is a charged particle formed when an atom or group of atoms gains or loses electrons.

**Types of Ions:**
| Type | Formation | Charge | Example |
|------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Cation | Atom loses electron(s) | Positive (+) | Na⁺, Ca²⁺, Al³⁺ |
| Anion | Atom gains electron(s) | Negative (-) | Cl⁻, O²⁻, N³⁻ |

### 5.1.3 Rules for Writing Ionic Formulae

**Key Principle:** The total positive charge must equal the total negative charge (compound must be neutral).

**Method: The Crossing-Over Method**

1. Write the symbols of the ions involved
2. Write the charges as superscripts
3. Cross over the charges (without signs) to become subscripts
4. Simplify if necessary

**Example 1: Sodium Chloride**
```
Na⁺    Cl⁻
  ↓     ↓
Na₁    Cl₁  → NaCl
```

**Example 2: Magnesium Oxide**
```
Mg²⁺   O²⁻
  ↓     ↓
Mg₂    O₂  → MgO (simplified)
```

**Example 3: Calcium Chloride**
```
Ca²⁺   Cl⁻
  ↓     ↓
Ca₁    Cl₂  → CaCl₂
```

**Example 4: Aluminium Oxide**
```
Al³⁺   O²⁻
  ↓     ↓
Al₂    O₃  → Al₂O₃
```

### 5.1.4 Formulae with Polyatomic Ions

**Important Rule:** When you need more than one polyatomic ion, use brackets.

**Example 1: Sodium Hydroxide**
```
Na⁺    OH⁻
  ↓     ↓
Na₁   (OH)₁  → NaOH
```

**Example 2: Calcium Hydroxide**
```
Ca²⁺   OH⁻
  ↓     ↓
Ca₁   (OH)₂  → Ca(OH)₂
```

**Example 3: Aluminium Sulfate**
```
Al³⁺   SO₄²⁻
  ↓      ↓
Al₂   (SO₄)₃  → Al₂(SO₄)₃
```

**Example 4: Ammonium Sulfate**
```
NH₄⁺   SO₄²⁻
  ↓      ↓
(NH₄)₂  SO₄  → (NH₄)₂SO₄
```

## 5.2 Relative Formula Mass (Mᵣ)

### 5.2.1 Definition

**Relative Formula Mass (Mᵣ):** The sum of the relative atomic masses of all the atoms in a formula.

**Key Points:**
- Also called relative molecular mass for molecules
- Has no units (it's a ratio compared to carbon-12)
- Use the Aᵣ values from the Periodic Table

### 5.2.2 Calculating Mᵣ

**Method:**
1. List all atoms in the formula
2. Multiply the Aᵣ of each element by the number of atoms
3. Add all the values together

**Example 1: Water (H₂O)**
$$M_r = (2 \times 1) + (1 \times 16) = 2 + 16 = 18$$

**Example 2: Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)**
$$M_r = (1 \times 12) + (2 \times 16) = 12 + 32 = 44$$

**Example 3: Calcium Carbonate (CaCO₃)**
$$M_r = (1 \times 40) + (1 \times 12) + (3 \times 16) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100$$

**Example 4: Sulfuric Acid (H₂SO₄)**
$$M_r = (2 \times 1) + (1 \times 32) + (4 \times 16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98$$

**Example 5: Hydrated Copper(II) Sulfate (CuSO₄·5H₂O)**
$$M_r = 63.5 + 32 + (4 \times 16) + 5[(2 \times 1) + 16]$$
$$M_r = 63.5 + 32 + 64 + 5[18]$$
$$M_r = 159.5 + 90 = 249.5$$

### 5.2.3 Aᵣ Values (Common Elements)

| Element | Symbol | Aᵣ |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Hydrogen | H | 1 |

| Nitrogen | N | 14 |

## 5.3 The Mole and Avogadro's Constant

### 5.3.1 The Mole Concept

**Definition:** The mole is the amount of substance that contains exactly $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles (atoms, molecules, ions, or electrons).

**Key Points:**
- The mole is the SI unit for amount of substance
- Symbol: mol
- One mole of any substance contains the same number of particles

### 5.3.2 Avogadro's Constant (Nₐ)

**Definition:** Avogadro's constant is the number of particles in one mole of substance.

$$N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}$$

**Examples:**
- 1 mole of carbon atoms = $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ C atoms
- 1 mole of water molecules = $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ H₂O molecules
- 1 mole of sodium ions = $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ Na⁺ ions

### 5.3.3 Molar Mass

**Definition:** The molar mass (M) is the mass of one mole of a substance.

**Key Points:**
- Unit: grams per mole (g/mol)
- Numerically equal to the relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) or relative formula mass (Mᵣ)

**Examples:**

| Substance | Mᵣ | Molar Mass (g/mol) |
|-----------|-----|-------------------|
| Carbon (C) | 12 | 12 g/mol |
| Water (H₂O) | 18 | 18 g/mol |
| Sodium chloride (NaCl) | 58.5 | 58.5 g/mol |
| Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) | 100 | 100 g/mol |

## 5.4 Calculating Moles from Mass

### 5.4.1 The Mole Equation

$$n = \frac{m}{M}$$

Where:
- n = number of moles (mol)
- m = mass (g)
- M = molar mass (g/mol)

**Rearrangements:**
$$m = n \times M$$
$$M = \frac{m}{n}$$

### 5.4.2 Worked Examples

**Example 1: Calculate the number of moles in 24 g of carbon.**
$$n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{24}{12} = 2 \text{ mol}$$

**Example 2: Calculate the number of moles in 36 g of water.**
$$M_r(H_2O) = 18$$
$$n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{36}{18} = 2 \text{ mol}$$

**Example 3: Calculate the mass of 0.5 mol of calcium carbonate.**
$$M_r(CaCO_3) = 100$$
$$m = n \times M = 0.5 \times 100 = 50 \text{ g}$$

**Example 4: Calculate the mass of 2.5 mol of sodium chloride.**
$$M_r(NaCl) = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5$$
$$m = n \times M = 2.5 \times 58.5 = 146.25 \text{ g}$$

### 5.4.3 Calculating Number of Particles

**Formula:**
$$\text{Number of particles} = n \times N_A$$

**Example: How many molecules are in 9 g of water?**
$$n = \frac{9}{18} = 0.5 \text{ mol}$$
$$\text{Number of molecules} = 0.5 \times 6.02 \times 10^{23} = 3.01 \times 10^{23}$$

**Example: How many atoms are in 2.4 g of carbon?**
$$n = \frac{2.4}{12} = 0.2 \text{ mol}$$
$$\text{Number of atoms} = 0.2 \times 6.02 \times 10^{23} = 1.20 \times 10^{23}$$

## 5.5 Empirical and Molecular Formulae

### 5.5.1 Empirical Formula

**Definition:** The empirical formula shows the simplest whole number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.

### 5.5.2 Calculating Empirical Formula from Masses

**Method:**
1. Write the mass of each element
2. Divide by the Aᵣ to get moles
3. Divide all by the smallest number of moles
4. If necessary, multiply to get whole numbers

**Example 1: A compound contains 2.4 g of carbon and 0.6 g of hydrogen. Find the empirical formula.**

| Element | C | H |
|---------|---|---|
| Mass (g) | 2.4 | 0.6 |
| Aᵣ | 12 | 1 |
| Moles | 2.4 ÷ 12 = 0.2 | 0.6 ÷ 1 = 0.6 |
| Ratio | 0.2 ÷ 0.2 = 1 | 0.6 ÷ 0.2 = 3 |

**Empirical formula = CH₃**

**Example 2: A compound contains 40% calcium, 12% carbon and 48% oxygen by mass. Find the empirical formula.**

| Element | Ca | C | O |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|
| Mass (%) | 40 | 12 | 48 |
| Aᵣ | 40 | 12 | 16 |
| Moles | 40 ÷ 40 = 1 | 12 ÷ 12 = 1 | 48 ÷ 16 = 3 |
| Ratio | 1 | 1 | 3 |

**Empirical formula = CaCO₃**

**Example 3: 5.6 g of iron reacts with 2.4 g of oxygen. Find the empirical formula of the iron oxide.**

| Element | Fe | O |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Mass (g) | 5.6 | 2.4 |
| Aᵣ | 56 | 16 |
| Moles | 5.6 ÷ 56 = 0.1 | 2.4 ÷ 16 = 0.15 |
| Ratio | 0.1 ÷ 0.1 = 1 | 0.15 ÷ 0.1 = 1.5 |
| ×2 | 2 | 3 |

**Empirical formula = Fe₂O₃**

### 5.5.3 Molecular Formula

**Definition:** The molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule.

**Relationship:**
$$\text{Molecular formula} = n \times \text{Empirical formula}$$
$$n = \frac{M_r \text{ (molecular)}}{M_r \text{ (empirical)}}$$

### 5.5.4 Calculating Molecular Formula

**Example: A compound has the empirical formula CH₂ and a relative molecular mass of 56. Find the molecular formula.**

**Step 1:** Calculate Mᵣ of empirical formula
$$M_r(CH_2) = 12 + (2 \times 1) = 14$$

**Step 2:** Find the multiplier (n)
$$n = \frac{56}{14} = 4$$

**Step 3:** Multiply the empirical formula by n
$$\text{Molecular formula} = (CH_2) \times 4 = C_4H_8$$

**Example: A hydrocarbon has the empirical formula CH and a Mᵣ of 78. Find the molecular formula.**

$$M_r(CH) = 12 + 1 = 13$$
$$n = \frac{78}{13} = 6$$
$$\text{Molecular formula} = C_6H_6$$

### 5.5.5 Determining Formulae from Combustion Data

**Example: 0.46 g of a hydrocarbon burns to produce 1.32 g of CO₂ and 0.54 g of H₂O. Find the empirical formula.**

**Step 1:** Find moles of C from CO₂
$$M_r(CO_2) = 44$$
$$n(CO_2) = \frac{1.32}{44} = 0.03 \text{ mol}$$
$$n(C) = 0.03 \text{ mol}$$
$$\text{Mass of C} = 0.03 \times 12 = 0.36 \text{ g}$$

**Step 2:** Find moles of H from H₂O
$$M_r(H_2O) = 18$$
$$n(H_2O) = \frac{0.54}{18} = 0.03 \text{ mol}$$
$$n(H) = 0.03 \times 2 = 0.06 \text{ mol}$$
$$\text{Mass of H} = 0.06 \times 1 = 0.06 \text{ g}$$

**Step 3:** Find ratio
$$C:H = 0.03:0.06 = 1:2$$

**Empirical formula = CH₂**

## 5.7 Common Exam Mistakes

### 5.7.1 Formula Writing Errors

❌ **Wrong:** CaCl (missing subscript)
✅ **Correct:** CaCl₂

❌ **Wrong:** AlSO₄ (missing brackets and wrong ratio)
✅ **Correct:** Al₂(SO₄)₃

❌ **Wrong:** NH₄₂SO₄ (missing brackets)
✅ **Correct:** (NH₄)₂SO₄

### 5.7.2 Mᵣ Calculation Errors

❌ **Wrong:** Forgetting to multiply by subscript
✅ **Correct:** For CaCO₃: (1×40) + (1×12) + (3×16)

❌ **Wrong:** Including brackets in the mass calculation
✅ **Correct:** Brackets indicate grouping only; multiply the atoms inside

### 5.7.3 Mole Calculation Errors

❌ **Wrong:** Using Aᵣ instead of Mᵣ for compounds
✅ **Correct:** For compounds, always use Mᵣ (sum of all atoms)

❌ **Wrong:** Forgetting units
✅ **Correct:** n in mol, m in g, M in g/mol

### 5.7.4 Empirical Formula Errors

❌ **Wrong:** Leaving decimals in the ratio (e.g., 1:1.5)
✅ **Correct:** Multiply to get whole numbers (e.g., 2:3)

❌ **Wrong:** Forgetting to divide by the smallest number
✅ **Correct:** Always divide all values by the smallest mole value

## 5.9 Practice Calculations

### Quick Practice Problems

**1. Calculate the Mᵣ of:**
- a) NaOH
- b) Mg(OH)₂
- c) Al₂(SO₄)₃

**2. Calculate the number of moles in:**
- a) 11.5 g of Na
- b) 8 g of CuO
- c) 25 g of CaCO₃

**3. Calculate the mass of:**
- a) 3 mol of H₂O
- b) 0.25 mol of NaCl
- c) 4 mol of CO₂

**4. Find the empirical formula of:**
- a) A compound containing 2.8 g Fe and 1.2 g O
- b) A compound containing 27.3% C and 72.7% O

**Answers:**

**1. Mᵣ calculations:**
- a) NaOH = 23 + 16 + 1 = 40
- b) Mg(OH)₂ = 24 + 2(16+1) = 58
- c) Al₂(SO₄)₃ = 2(27) + 3(32+64) = 342

**2. Moles calculations:**
- a) n = 11.5/23 = 0.5 mol
- b) Mᵣ(CuO) = 79.5; n = 8/79.5 = 0.101 mol
- c) n = 25/100 = 0.25 mol

**3. Mass calculations:**
- a) m = 3 × 18 = 54 g
- b) m = 0.25 × 58.5 = 14.625 g
- c) m = 4 × 44 = 176 g

**4. Empirical formulae:**
- a) Fe₂O₃
- b) CO₂
