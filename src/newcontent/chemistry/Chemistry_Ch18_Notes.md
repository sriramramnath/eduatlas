## 18.1 Tests for Gases

### Introduction

Identifying gases is a fundamental skill in chemistry. Each gas has a characteristic test that produces a unique observation. Understanding these tests is essential for practical work and examinations.

---

### 18.1.1 Test for Oxygen (O₂)

**Test:** Insert a **glowing splint** into the gas.

**Positive Result:** The glowing splint **relights** (bursts into flame).

**Explanation:**
- Oxygen supports combustion
- A glowing splint has embers but no flame
- In the presence of oxygen, the embers reignite

**Common Sources of Oxygen:**
- Decomposition of hydrogen peroxide: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (using MnO₂ catalyst)
- Electrolysis of water: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
- Heating metal oxides (e.g., potassium manganate(VII))

**Exam Tip:** Do NOT confuse with hydrogen test. Oxygen RELIGHTS a glowing splint; hydrogen produces a "pop" with a LIGHTED splint.

---

### 18.1.2 Test for Hydrogen (H₂)

**Test:** Place a **lighted splint** at the mouth of the test tube containing the gas.

**Positive Result:** The gas burns with a **"pop" sound** (squeaky pop).

**Explanation:**
- Hydrogen is highly flammable
- When mixed with air and ignited, it explodes rapidly
- The "pop" is a small explosion

**Common Sources of Hydrogen:**
- Acid + reactive metal: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
- Electrolysis of water: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
- Reaction of alkali metals with water: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂

**Important Distinction:**
| Gas | Splint Type | Observation |
|-----|-------------|-------------|
| Oxygen | Glowing splint | Relights |
| Hydrogen | Lighted splint | "Pop" sound |

---

### 18.1.3 Test for Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

**Test:** Bubble the gas through **limewater** (calcium hydroxide solution, Ca(OH)₂).

**Positive Result:** Limewater turns **milky/cloudy** (white precipitate forms).

**Chemical Equation:**
```
Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)
```

**Explanation:**
- Limewater is a clear, colourless solution of calcium hydroxide
- Carbon dioxide reacts with calcium hydroxide to form calcium carbonate
- Calcium carbonate is a white insoluble solid (precipitate)

**Excess Carbon Dioxide:**
If excess CO₂ is passed through the milky solution, it clears:
```
CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) → Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq)
```
Calcium hydrogen carbonate is soluble.

**Common Sources of Carbon Dioxide:**
- Acid + carbonate: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
- Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O
- Combustion of hydrocarbons
- Thermal decomposition of carbonates: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

---

### 18.1.4 Test for Ammonia (NH₃)

**Test:** Hold **damp red litmus paper** in the gas.

**Positive Result:** Red litmus paper turns **blue**.

**Explanation:**
- Ammonia is an alkaline gas (base)
- It dissolves in water to form an alkaline solution
- Alkaline solutions turn red litmus blue

**Additional Test:** 
- Ammonia has a characteristic **pungent smell** (sharp, irritating odour)
- It turns universal indicator solution **blue or purple**

**Common Sources of Ammonia:**
- Heating ammonium salts with alkali: NH₄Cl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O + NH₃
- Haber process (industrial)
- Decomposition of nitrogenous organic matter

**Important:** The litmus paper must be DAMP - this allows the ammonia to dissolve and form an alkaline solution.

---

### 18.1.5 Test for Chlorine (Cl₂)

**Test:** Hold **damp blue litmus paper** in the gas.

**Positive Result:** Blue litmus paper turns **red**, then **bleaches white**.

**Explanation:**
- Chlorine is an acidic gas
- It reacts with water to form hydrochloric acid and hypochlorous acid:
  ```
  Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl
  ```
- The acid turns blue litmus red
- The hypochlorous acid is a bleaching agent, so the paper then turns white

**Characteristics:**
- Chlorine has a **sharp, choking smell**
- It is **toxic** - must be handled with care
- It is **greenish-yellow** in colour

**Common Sources of Chlorine:**
- Electrolysis of brine (NaCl solution): 2NaCl + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ + Cl₂
- Reaction of HCl with oxidising agents (e.g., MnO₂)

---

### Summary Table: Gas Tests

| Gas | Test | Positive Result |
|-----|------|-----------------|
| **Oxygen (O₂)** | Glowing splint | Relights |
| **Hydrogen (H₂)** | Lighted splint | "Pop" sound |
| **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** | Bubble through limewater | Turns milky |
| **Ammonia (NH₃)** | Damp red litmus paper | Turns blue |
| **Chlorine (Cl₂)** | Damp blue litmus paper | Turns red, then white (bleaches) |

## 18.2 Flame Tests for Metal Ions

### Introduction

Flame tests are used to identify certain metal ions based on the characteristic colour they produce when heated in a Bunsen burner flame. This technique works because the heat excites electrons in the metal ions, and when they return to their ground state, they emit light of specific wavelengths.

---

### 18.2.1 Method for Flame Tests

**Apparatus:**
- Platinum or nichrome wire loop
- Concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl)
- Bunsen burner
- Sample of compound to test

**Procedure:**
1. **Clean the wire loop** by dipping in concentrated HCl and heating in a roaring blue flame until no colour is seen
2. Dip the clean loop in the sample (or moisten with HCl and dip in solid)
3. Hold in the edge of the roaring blue flame
4. Observe the flame colour

**Why use hydrochloric acid?**
- Converts the metal compound to the chloride
- Metal chlorides are more volatile and vaporise more easily
- Gives a clearer, more intense flame colour

---

### 18.2.2 Flame Test Colours

| Metal Ion | Flame Colour | Example Compound |
|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| **Lithium (Li⁺)** | **Crimson red** | Lithium chloride |
| **Sodium (Na⁺)** | **Yellow** (intense) | Sodium chloride |
| **Potassium (K⁺)** | **Lilac** (pale purple) | Potassium chloride |
| **Calcium (Ca²⁺)** | **Orange-red** (brick red) | Calcium chloride |
| **Copper (Cu²⁺)** | **Blue-green** | Copper(II) chloride |

---

### 18.2.3 Important Notes on Flame Tests

**Sodium's Yellow Flame:**
- Sodium produces a very intense yellow flame
- This can mask other colours
- If sodium is present with potassium, the yellow colour dominates
- **Solution:** View through **blue glass** (cobalt glass) which filters out yellow light, allowing the lilac potassium flame to be seen

**Limitations of Flame Tests:**
- Subjective - different people may perceive colours differently
- Some colours are similar (e.g., lithium red and calcium orange-red)
- Sample must be pure - mixtures give confusing results
- Not all metal ions give distinctive colours
- Cannot determine concentration

**Exam Tip:** Learn the exact colour descriptions:
- Lithium = crimson red (deep red)
- Calcium = orange-red OR brick red
- Potassium = lilac (not purple, not pink)
- Copper = blue-green (sometimes described as green)

---

## 18.3 Tests for Aqueous Cations

### Introduction

Aqueous cations (positive ions in solution) can be identified by adding sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or ammonia (NH₃) solution and observing the precipitate formed. The precipitate is usually the metal hydroxide.

---

### 18.3.1 Using Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)

**General Principle:**
Metal ion + Sodium hydroxide → Metal hydroxide precipitate + Sodium ions

Most metal hydroxides are insoluble and form characteristic coloured precipitates.

---

### 18.3.2 Precipitate Colours and Solubility

| Cation | Precipitate Formula | Colour of Precipitate | Solubility in Excess NaOH |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Copper(II) (Cu²⁺)** | Cu(OH)₂ | **Blue** | Insoluble |
| **Iron(II) (Fe²⁺)** | Fe(OH)₂ | **Green** | Insoluble |
| **Iron(III) (Fe³⁺)** | Fe(OH)₃ | **Brown** (rusty) | Insoluble |
| **Aluminium (Al³⁺)** | Al(OH)₃ | **White** | **Soluble** (dissolves) |
| **Zinc (Zn²⁺)** | Zn(OH)₂ | **White** | **Soluble** (dissolves) |
| **Calcium (Ca²⁺)** | Ca(OH)₂ | **White** | Insoluble |

---

### 18.3.3 Detailed Observations

#### Copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺)
```
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cu(OH)₂(s)
```
- **Blue precipitate** forms immediately
- Precipitate is **insoluble in excess NaOH**
- Solution remains blue due to unreacted Cu²⁺ ions

---

#### Iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺)
```
Fe²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₂(s)
```
- **Green precipitate** forms
- On standing, the precipitate may turn brown at the surface (oxidises to Fe(OH)₃)
- Precipitate is **insoluble in excess NaOH**

---

#### Iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺)
```
Fe³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s)
```
- **Brown/rust-coloured precipitate** forms
- Precipitate is **insoluble in excess NaOH**

---

#### Aluminium ions (Al³⁺)
```
Al³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Al(OH)₃(s)
```
- **White precipitate** forms initially
- Precipitate **dissolves in excess NaOH** to form a colourless solution
- This is because Al(OH)₃ is amphoteric - it reacts with both acids and bases:
  ```
  Al(OH)₃(s) + OH⁻(aq) → Al(OH)₄⁻(aq)  [aluminate ion]
  ```

---

#### Zinc ions (Zn²⁺)
```
Zn²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Zn(OH)₂(s)
```
- **White precipitate** forms initially
- Precipitate **dissolves in excess NaOH** to form a colourless solution
- Zn(OH)₂ is also amphoteric:
  ```
  Zn(OH)₂(s) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Zn(OH)₄²⁻(aq)  [zincate ion]
  ```

---

#### Calcium ions (Ca²⁺)
```
Ca²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Ca(OH)₂(s)
```
- **White precipitate** forms
- Precipitate is **insoluble in excess NaOH**

**Distinguishing Al³⁺, Zn²⁺, and Ca²⁺:**
All three give white precipitates with NaOH, but:
- **Al³⁺ and Zn²⁺:** Precipitate dissolves in excess NaOH
- **Ca²⁺:** Precipitate does NOT dissolve in excess NaOH

To distinguish Al³⁺ from Zn²⁺, use ammonia solution.

---

### 18.3.4 Using Ammonia Solution (NH₃)

Ammonia can also be used to test for cations. It provides OH⁻ ions when dissolved:
```
NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻
```

| Cation | Observation with NH₃ | Solubility in Excess NH₃ |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| **Cu²⁺** | Blue precipitate | **Dissolves** to deep blue solution |
| **Fe²⁺** | Green precipitate | Insoluble |
| **Fe³⁺** | Brown precipitate | Insoluble |
| **Al³⁺** | White precipitate | **Insoluble** (unlike with NaOH) |
| **Zn²⁺** | White precipitate | **Dissolves** to colourless solution |
| **Ca²⁺** | No precipitate (or very slight) | N/A |

---

### 18.3.5 Distinguishing Al³⁺ from Zn²⁺

Both Al³⁺ and Zn²⁺ give white precipitates that dissolve in excess NaOH. To distinguish between them:

**Add ammonia solution:**
- **Al³⁺:** White precipitate forms and does NOT dissolve in excess ammonia
- **Zn²⁺:** White precipitate forms and DOES dissolve in excess ammonia

| Test | Al³⁺ | Zn²⁺ |
|------|------|------|
| NaOH (excess) | White ppt dissolves | White ppt dissolves |
| NH₃ (excess) | White ppt does NOT dissolve | White ppt dissolves |

---

### 18.3.6 Identifying Copper(II) ions

Cu²⁺ ions can be identified by:
1. **Blue solution colour** - most Cu²⁺ solutions are blue
2. **NaOH test:** Blue precipitate (insoluble in excess)
3. **NH₃ test:** Blue precipitate that dissolves in excess to form a **deep blue solution**
   ```
   Cu²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Cu(OH)₂(s) [blue]
   Cu(OH)₂ + 4NH₃ → [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ + 2OH⁻ [deep blue solution]
   ```

## 18.4 Tests for Anions

### Introduction

Anions are negative ions. Common tests involve adding specific reagents and observing characteristic precipitates or gas evolution.

---

### 18.4.1 Test for Carbonates (CO₃²⁻)

**Test:** Add dilute hydrochloric acid to the solid or solution.

**Observation:** **Effervescence** (bubbling) occurs - carbon dioxide gas is produced.

**Confirmatory Test:** Bubble the gas through limewater - it turns milky.

**Equations:**
```
CO₃²⁻(s) + 2H⁺(aq) → H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
```

For calcium carbonate:
```
CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
```

**Key Points:**
- All carbonates react with acids to produce CO₂
- Solid carbonates show vigorous effervescence
- Carbonate solutions also fizz but less vigorously

---

### 18.4.2 Test for Sulfates (SO₄²⁻)

**Test:** 
1. Add dilute hydrochloric acid (to remove any carbonates)
2. Add barium chloride solution (BaCl₂)

**Observation:** **White precipitate** of barium sulfate forms.

**Equation:**
```
Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s)
```

**Why add HCl first?**
- Barium carbonate is also insoluble and would give a white precipitate
- Adding HCl dissolves any carbonate present:
  ```
  BaCO₃ + 2HCl → BaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
  ```
- This ensures the white precipitate is due to sulfate, not carbonate

**Properties of BaSO₄:**
- Insoluble in water and dilute acids
- Very stable compound
- Used in "barium meals" for X-rays (non-toxic because insoluble)

---

### 18.4.3 Test for Halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻)

**Test:**
1. Add dilute nitric acid (to remove interfering ions)
2. Add silver nitrate solution (AgNO₃)

**Observations:**

| Halide Ion | Precipitate with AgNO₃ | Colour |
|------------|------------------------|--------|
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | AgCl | **White** |
| **Bromide (Br⁻)** | AgBr | **Cream** (pale yellow) |
| **Iodide (I⁻)** | AgI | **Yellow** |

**Equations:**
```
Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) [white]
Ag⁺(aq) + Br⁻(aq) → AgBr(s) [cream]
Ag⁺(aq) + I⁻(aq) → AgI(s) [yellow]
```

**Why add HNO₃ first?**
- Removes carbonate ions (which would give a white precipitate with Ag⁺)
- Removes hydroxide ions (which would give brown Ag₂O)
- Does not interfere with the halide test

**Important Distinction:**
- Silver chloride = white
- Silver bromide = cream (off-white, pale yellow)
- Silver iodide = yellow

These colours can be hard to distinguish - compare with known samples if possible.

---

### 18.4.4 Summary Table: Anion Tests

| Anion | Reagent | Observation |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Carbonate (CO₃²⁻)** | HCl | Effervescence, gas turns limewater milky |
| **Sulfate (SO₄²⁻)** | HCl, then BaCl₂ | White precipitate |
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | HNO₃, then AgNO₃ | White precipitate |
| **Bromide (Br⁻)** | HNO₃, then AgNO₃ | Cream precipitate |
| **Iodide (I⁻)** | HNO₃, then AgNO₃ | Yellow precipitate |

## 18.5 Instrumental Analysis

### Introduction

While traditional chemical tests are useful for identifying simple substances, instrumental methods offer more accurate, sensitive, and rapid analysis. Flame emission spectroscopy is an important instrumental technique in the IGCSE specification.

---

### 18.5.1 Flame Emission Spectroscopy

**Principle:**
- Atoms of a metal are heated in a flame
- Electrons become excited to higher energy levels
- When electrons return to ground state, they emit light of characteristic wavelengths
- The wavelength (colour) identifies the element
- The intensity of the light is proportional to concentration

---

### 18.5.2 Method

1. **Sample preparation:** The sample is dissolved to make a solution
2. **Introduction:** The solution is sprayed into a flame
3. **Excitation:** Atoms in the flame are excited by the heat
4. **Emission:** Atoms return to ground state and emit light
5. **Analysis:** The emitted light passes through a spectrometer
6. **Detection:** The spectrometer measures the wavelengths and intensities

---

### 18.5.3 Applications

1. **Identification of metal ions:**
   - Each metal produces a unique emission spectrum
   - The wavelengths identify which metals are present

2. **Quantitative analysis (measuring concentration):**
   - The intensity of light at a specific wavelength is measured
   - This is compared to standards of known concentration
   - Allows precise determination of concentration

---

### 18.5.4 Advantages of Instrumental Methods

| Advantage | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Rapid** | Results in minutes, not hours |
| **Accurate** | Less subjective than visual colour comparison |
| **Sensitive** | Can detect very small amounts (trace levels) |
| **Quantitative** | Can measure concentration, not just presence |
| **Multi-element** | Can analyse several elements simultaneously |

---

### 18.5.5 Comparison: Flame Test vs Flame Emission Spectroscopy

| Feature | Flame Test | Flame Emission Spectroscopy |
|---------|------------|----------------------------|
| **Speed** | Quick (seconds) | Rapid (minutes) |
| **Accuracy** | Subjective | Objective, precise |
| **Sensitivity** | Low | High (ppm levels) |
| **Quantitative** | No | Yes (measures concentration) |
| **Sample type** | Solid or solution | Solution only |
| **Multiple ions** | Difficult to distinguish | Can identify several at once |
| **Equipment** | Simple (flame, wire) | Expensive spectrometer |

## Common Exam Mistakes

###  Mistake 1: Confusing oxygen and hydrogen tests
- Oxygen: GLOWING splint RELIGHTS
- Hydrogen: LIGHTED splint gives "POP"
- Remember: "Hydrogen goes pop when lit; Oxygen brings the glow back to life"

###  Mistake 2: Wrong flame colours
- Lithium = crimson red (not just red)
- Calcium = orange-red or brick red (not red)
- Potassium = lilac (not purple)
- Copper = blue-green (not just green)

###  Mistake 3: Forgetting "excess NaOH" observations
- Al³⁺ and Zn²⁺ precipitates dissolve in excess NaOH
- Ca²⁺ precipitate does NOT dissolve in excess NaOH
- Cu²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺ precipitates do NOT dissolve in excess NaOH

###  Mistake 4: Not adding acid before testing for anions
- Always add HCl before BaCl₂ for sulfate test (removes carbonate)
- Always add HNO₃ before AgNO₃ for halide test (removes carbonate and hydroxide)

###  Mistake 5: Confusing silver halide colours
- AgCl = white
- AgBr = cream (pale yellow)
- AgI = yellow
- In order of increasing depth of colour

###  Mistake 6: Damp litmus for chlorine test
- Must be DAMP blue litmus for chlorine test
- Dry litmus paper won't show the proper colour change

###  Mistake 7: State symbols in equations
- Precipitates are solid (s)
- Solutions are aqueous (aq)
- Gases are (g)

###  Mistake 8: Distinguishing Al³⁺ and Zn²⁺
- Both give white precipitate with NaOH that dissolves in excess
- Use ammonia to distinguish: Al(OH)₃ insoluble in excess NH₃; Zn(OH)₂ dissolves
