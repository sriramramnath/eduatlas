## 17.2 Making Soluble Salts

### 17.2.1 What is a Salt?

**Definition:** A salt is an ionic compound formed when the hydrogen in an acid is **replaced by a metal ion** or ammonium ion.

**Examples:**
- From HCl â†’ NaCl (sodium chloride), KCl (potassium chloride)
- From Hâ‚‚SOâ‚„ â†’ Naâ‚‚SOâ‚„ (sodium sulfate), CuSOâ‚„ (copper(II) sulfate)
- From HNOâ‚ƒ â†’ NaNOâ‚ƒ (sodium nitrate), Ca(NOâ‚ƒ)â‚‚ (calcium nitrate)

---

### 17.2.2 Methods for Making Soluble Salts

#### Method 1: Acid + Metal (for reactive metals)

**Suitable for:** Metals above hydrogen in reactivity series (Mg, Zn, Fe)

**Method:**
1. Add excess metal to acid
2. Wait until bubbling stops (acid used up)
3. Filter off excess metal
4. Crystallise the salt solution

**Limitation:** Not suitable for very reactive metals (Na, K, Ca) - too dangerous
**Limitation:** Not suitable for unreactive metals (Cu, Ag, Au) - no reaction

---

#### Method 2: Acid + Insoluble Base (Metal Oxide/Hydroxide/Carbonate)

**Suitable for:** All metals, including copper and other unreactive metals

**Method:**
1. Add the insoluble base to warm acid
2. Stir and add more base until no more dissolves (in excess)
3. Filter off excess base
4. Heat the filtrate to concentrate
5. Leave to crystallise
6. Filter crystals, wash with distilled water, dry

**This is the preferred method for making salts of unreactive metals like copper.**

**Example: Making Copper(II) Sulfate**
```
CuO(s) + Hâ‚‚SOâ‚„(aq) â†’ CuSOâ‚„(aq) + Hâ‚‚O(l)
```

---

#### Method 3: Acid + Alkali (Titration Method)

**Suitable for:** Making soluble salts from soluble reactants (e.g., sodium salts, potassium salts, ammonium salts)

**Method:**
1. Carry out a titration to find exact volumes needed
2. Mix the exact volumes without indicator
3. Heat to evaporate water
4. Crystallise the salt

**Example: Making Sodium Chloride**
```
NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) â†’ NaCl(aq) + Hâ‚‚O(l)
```

---

### 17.2.3 Crystallisation Process

**Steps:**
1. Heat the salt solution to evaporate some water (concentrate)
2. Do NOT evaporate to dryness - this would give powder, not crystals
3. Allow to cool - solubility decreases, so crystals form
4. Filter to collect crystals
5. Wash with small amount of cold distilled water
6. Dry between filter paper or in a low-temperature oven

**Why crystallisation?**
- Produces pure crystals
- Slow cooling gives larger, purer crystals
- Removes impurities which remain in solution

## 17.5 Required Practical: Preparing a Pure, Dry Sample of a Soluble Salt

### Aim
To prepare a pure, dry sample of copper(II) sulfate crystals from copper(II) oxide and dilute sulfuric acid.

---

### Materials
- Copper(II) oxide (black powder)
- Dilute sulfuric acid (1.0 mol/dmآ³)
- Beaker (100 cmآ³)
- Glass rod
- Bunsen burner, tripod, gauze
- Filter funnel and filter paper
- Evaporating basin
- Distilled water

---

### Method

#### Step 1: Reaction
1. Measure approximately 25 cmآ³ of dilute sulfuric acid into a beaker
2. Warm the acid gently (do NOT boil)
3. Add copper(II) oxide powder in small amounts
4. Stir with a glass rod
5. Continue adding until no more reacts (solid remains in excess)
6. The solution turns blue as copper(II) sulfate forms

**Equation:**
```
CuO(s) + Hâ‚‚SOâ‚„(aq) â†’ CuSOâ‚„(aq) + Hâ‚‚O(l)
```

**Why excess base?** To ensure ALL the acid is used up and the salt solution is neutral.

---

#### Step 2: Filtration
1. Set up filter funnel with filter paper
2. Filter the mixture to remove unreacted copper(II) oxide
3. Collect the blue filtrate (copper(II) sulfate solution)

---

#### Step 3: Crystallisation
1. Pour filtrate into an evaporating basin
2. Heat gently to concentrate (do NOT evaporate to dryness)
3. When crystals start to form at edges, stop heating
4. Allow to cool and crystallise fully

---

#### Step 4: Collecting Crystals
1. Filter to collect crystals
2. Wash with a small amount of cold distilled water
3. Dry between sheets of filter paper

---

### Safety Considerations
- Wear eye protection
- Sulfuric acid is corrosive - handle with care
- Copper compounds are toxic - wash hands after use
- Heat carefully to avoid spitting

---

### Why This Method?

| Step | Reason |
|------|--------|
| Warming the acid | Speeds up reaction |
| Adding excess base | Ensures all acid reacts |
| Filtration | Removes unreacted base |
| Not evaporating to dryness | Gives crystals, not powder |
| Cooling slowly | Forms larger, purer crystals |
| Washing with cold water | Removes impurities without dissolving crystals |

- This ensures all acid is neutralised
