## 9.1 Structure of Metals

### The Metallic Lattice

**Definition:** A metallic lattice is a giant structure of metal ions arranged in a regular, repeating pattern, surrounded by a 'sea' of delocalised electrons.

### Key Features of Metallic Structure

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Arrangement** | Metal atoms are packed closely together in regular patterns (often hexagonal close-packed or cubic close-packed) |
| **Particles present** | Positive metal ions (cations) and delocalised electrons |
| **Bonding** | Strong metallic bonds between positive ions and delocalised electrons |
| **Type of structure** | Giant metallic lattice |

### Diagrammatic Representation

```
    ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕
  ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕    ⊕ = Positive metal ion
    ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕      • = Delocalised electron
  ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕
    ⊕   ⊕   ⊕   ⊕
       Sea of delocalised electrons (•)
```

## 9.2 Metallic Bonding

### Definition

**Metallic bonding** is the strong electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and the sea of delocalised electrons.

### How Metallic Bonds Form

1. Metal atoms **lose their outer shell electrons** to form positive ions (cations)
2. The released electrons become **delocalised** (free to move throughout the structure)
3. The positive ions are held together by their attraction to the delocalised electrons
4. This creates a **giant metallic lattice** with strong metallic bonds in all directions

### The Nature of Delocalised Electrons

- **Delocalised electrons** are electrons that are not associated with any particular atom
- They are free to move throughout the entire metallic structure
- They come from the outer shell (valence electrons) of metal atoms
- The number of delocalised electrons per atom equals the number of valence electrons

### Why Metallic Bonding is Strong

- Strong **electrostatic attraction** between positive ions and negative electrons
- The attraction acts in **all directions** throughout the lattice
- Each metal ion is attracted to many delocalised electrons
- Energy is needed to overcome these strong attractions

## 9.3 Properties of Metals and Their Explanations

### Summary Table of Properties

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Good electrical conductivity** | Delocalised electrons can move freely and carry charge through the metal |
| **Good thermal conductivity** | Energy is transferred by collisions between delocalised electrons and ions |
| **Malleable** | Layers of ions can slide over each other while maintaining metallic bonding |
| **Ductile** | Layers can slide past each other without breaking bonds, allowing wires to be drawn |
| **High melting/boiling points** | Strong metallic bonds require large amounts of energy to break |
| **Shiny/lustrous** | Delocalised electrons reflect light from the surface |
| **Sonorous** | When struck, layers can move but strong bonds pull them back, creating vibration |

### Detailed Explanations

#### 1. Electrical Conductivity

- **Observation:** Metals are excellent conductors of electricity
- **Explanation:** When a voltage is applied, the delocalised electrons are attracted towards the positive terminal and move through the metal. This flow of charged particles (electrons) constitutes an electric current
- **Key point:** Unlike ionic compounds, metals conduct in both solid and liquid states because delocalised electrons are always present

#### 2. Thermal Conductivity

- **Observation:** Metals are good conductors of heat
- **Explanation:** When a metal is heated, the delocalised electrons gain kinetic energy and move faster. They collide with other electrons and metal ions, transferring energy throughout the structure quickly
- **Contrast:** Non-metals are poor thermal conductors because they lack delocalised electrons

#### 3. Malleability

- **Definition:** Malleable means can be hammered or rolled into shape without breaking
- **Explanation:** In a metallic lattice, the layers of ions can slide over each other when a force is applied. After sliding, the ions are still surrounded by delocalised electrons, so metallic bonding is maintained
- **Practical application:** This allows metals to be shaped into sheets, car body panels, etc.

```
Before force:          After force:
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕               ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕               ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕               ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕

Layers slide but maintain bonding
```

#### 4. Ductility

- **Definition:** Ductile means can be drawn out into wires
- **Explanation:** When a pulling force is applied, the layers of ions can slide past each other. The metallic bonds are maintained because delocalised electrons continue to hold the ions together
- **Practical application:** Copper wires for electrical cables

#### 5. High Melting and Boiling Points

- **Observation:** Most metals have high melting and boiling points
- **Explanation:** Large amounts of energy are needed to overcome the strong electrostatic attraction between positive ions and delocalised electrons
- **Examples:**
  - Iron: melting point 1538°C
  - Copper: melting point 1085°C
  - Aluminium: melting point 660°C
- **Exceptions:** Mercury (Hg) is a liquid at room temperature (melting point -39°C) because metallic bonds are weak due to its large atomic size

#### 6. Shiny Appearance (Lustre)

- **Explanation:** Delocalised electrons at the surface of a metal can absorb and re-emit light at various frequencies, giving metals their characteristic shiny appearance

## 9.4 Alloys and Their Properties

### What is an Alloy?

**Definition:** An alloy is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (usually other metals or carbon).

### Why Make Alloys?

Alloys are often harder and stronger than pure metals because:
- The different-sized atoms of the added element **disrupt the regular arrangement** of metal ions
- This makes it more difficult for layers to slide over each other
- The alloy is therefore **harder and less malleable** than the pure metal

### Pure Metal vs Alloy Structure

```
PURE METAL:              ALLOY:
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕                ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕                ⊕ ◉ ⊕ ⊕    ◉ = Different sized atom
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕                ⊕ ⊕ ◉ ⊕
⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕                ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕

Regular layers           Disrupted layers
can slide easily         cannot slide as easily
```

### Common Alloys and Their Uses

| Alloy | Composition | Properties | Uses |
|-------|-------------|------------|------|
| **Steel** | Iron + Carbon (small amount) | Harder and stronger than pure iron | Construction, vehicles, tools |
| **Stainless steel** | Iron + Chromium + Nickel | Hard, resistant to corrosion | Cutlery, surgical instruments |
| **Brass** | Copper + Zinc | Attractive gold colour, hard, resistant to corrosion | Musical instruments, ornaments |
| **Bronze** | Copper + Tin | Hard, resistant to corrosion | Statues, medals, bearings |
| **Solder** | Tin + Lead (or tin + silver + copper) | Low melting point | Joining electrical components |
| **Duralumin** | Aluminium + Copper + Magnesium | Strong but lightweight | Aircraft construction |
| **Nitinol** | Nickel + Titanium | Shape memory alloy | Dental braces, medical stents |

### Smart Alloys: Shape Memory Alloys

- **Shape memory alloys** (e.g., Nitinol) can return to their original shape after being deformed when heated
- **Applications:**
  - Dental braces that tighten when warmed by body temperature
  - Medical stents that expand at body temperature
  - Spectacle frames that spring back to shape

## 9.5 Comparing Bonding Types

### Summary Table: Comparison of Bonding Types

| Property | Ionic | Covalent (simple molecular) | Covalent (giant covalent) | Metallic |
|----------|-------|----------------------------|---------------------------|----------|
| **Particles** | Ions | Molecules | Atoms | Ions + delocalised electrons |
| **Bonding** | Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions | Shared electron pairs between atoms | Shared electron pairs in a giant network | Electrostatic attraction between ions and delocalised electrons |
| **Melting point** | High | Low | Very high | Generally high |
| **Boiling point** | High | Low | Very high | Generally high |
| **Electrical conductivity (solid)** | Does not conduct | Does not conduct | Does not conduct (except graphite) | Good conductor |
| **Electrical conductivity (molten/aqueous)** | Conducts | Does not conduct | Does not conduct | Conducts |
| **Solubility in water** | Many are soluble | Generally insoluble | Insoluble | Insoluble |
| **Examples** | NaCl, MgO | H₂O, CO₂, CH₄ | Diamond, graphite, SiO₂ | Fe, Cu, Al |

### Why These Differences Exist

#### Ionic Compounds
- **High melting/boiling points:** Strong electrostatic forces between ions require lots of energy to break
- **Non-conductors when solid:** Ions are held in fixed positions and cannot move
- **Conductors when molten/dissolved:** Ions are free to move and carry charge
- **Brittle:** If layers are displaced, like-charged ions come together and repel, causing the crystal to shatter

#### Simple Molecular Covalent
- **Low melting/boiling points:** Only weak intermolecular forces between molecules, not covalent bonds
- **Non-conductors:** No free-moving charged particles
- **Often soft or waxy:** Weak intermolecular forces

#### Giant Covalent
- **Very high melting/boiling points:** All atoms are connected by strong covalent bonds
- **Non-conductors (except graphite):** No free-moving charged particles
- **Very hard (except graphite):** Strong covalent bonds in a 3D network

#### Metallic
- **High melting/boiling points:** Strong metallic bonds throughout the lattice
- **Good conductors:** Delocalised electrons are free to move
- **Malleable and ductile:** Layers can slide while maintaining bonding

### Graphite - The Exception

Graphite is a giant covalent structure but conducts electricity because:
- Each carbon atom forms only **3 covalent bonds** with other carbon atoms
- This leaves **one delocalised electron per carbon atom**
- These delocalised electrons can move between the layers and carry charge

## Common Exam Mistakes

1. **Confusing delocalised electrons with free electrons**
   - ✓ Correct: "Delocalised electrons are free to move throughout the metal"
   - ✗ Wrong: "Free electrons in the metal"

2. **Saying metals conduct because ions move**
   - ✓ Correct: "Delocalised electrons move and carry charge"
   - ✗ Wrong: "Ions move and carry the current"

3. **Explaining malleability incorrectly**
   - ✓ Correct: "Layers of ions can slide over each other while metallic bonds are maintained"
   - ✗ Wrong: "Bonds break and reform" or "Atoms slide"

4. **Confusing alloys with compounds**
   - ✓ Correct: "Alloys are mixtures of metals"
   - ✗ Wrong: "Alloys are compounds of metals"

5. **Forgetting to mention delocalised electrons in metallic bonding definition**
   - ✓ Correct: "Electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and delocalised electrons"
   - ✗ Wrong: "Attraction between positive and negative ions"

6. **Not explaining WHY alloys are harder**
   - ✓ Correct: "Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular arrangement, preventing layers from sliding"
   - ✗ Wrong: "Alloys are harder because they are mixtures"

7. **Saying metallic bonds are "weak" when explaining malleability**
   - ✓ Correct: "The metallic bonds are maintained when layers slide"
   - ✗ Wrong: "The bonds are weak so layers can slide"
