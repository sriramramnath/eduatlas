## 8.1 Formation of Covalent Bonds

### What is a Covalent Bond?

A **covalent bond** is a chemical bond formed when two atoms **share a pair of electrons**.

### How Covalent Bonds Form

- Occurs between **non-metal atoms**
- Atoms share electrons to achieve a **full outer shell** (usually 8 electrons)
- The shared pair of electrons is attracted to the nuclei of **both** atoms
- This attraction holds the atoms together

### Types of Covalent Bonds

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Single bond** | One shared pair of electrons | H-H, Cl-Cl, H-Cl |
| **Double bond** | Two shared pairs of electrons | O=O, C=O |
| **Triple bond** | Three shared pairs of electrons | N≡N, C≡N |

### Dot and Cross Diagrams

**Example: Hydrogen (H₂)**

```
H•  +  •H   →   H⊛H
               (shared pair)
```
Each hydrogen contributes 1 electron. The shared pair gives each H a full shell (2 electrons).

**Example: Water (H₂O)**

```
     H
     •
     ×
O ××× H•
     ×
     H•
```
Oxygen shares 2 electrons with each of 2 hydrogen atoms. Oxygen achieves 8 electrons in outer shell; each H achieves 2.

**Example: Methane (CH₄)**

```
      H
      •
      ×
H• ××× C ××× •H
      ×
      H•
```
Carbon shares one electron with each of 4 hydrogen atoms. Carbon achieves 8 electrons; each H achieves 2.

### Examples of Common Molecules

| Molecule | Formula | Bond type | Electron pairs shared |
|----------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Hydrogen | H₂ | Single | 1 |
| Chlorine | Cl₂ | Single | 1 |
| Water | H₂O | Single (2 bonds) | 2 |
| Ammonia | NH₃ | Single (3 bonds) | 3 |
| Methane | CH₄ | Single (4 bonds) | 4 |
| Oxygen | O₂ | Double | 2 |
| Carbon dioxide | CO₂ | Double (2 bonds) | 4 |
| Nitrogen | N₂ | Triple | 3 |

### Multiple Bonds in Detail

**Oxygen (O₂):**
```
O::O   or   O=O
```
Each oxygen shares 2 electrons. Total of 4 electrons shared (2 pairs).

**Nitrogen (N₂):**
```
N:::N   or   N≡N
```
Each nitrogen shares 3 electrons. Total of 6 electrons shared (3 pairs).

**Carbon dioxide (CO₂):**
```
O=C=O
```
Carbon forms double bonds with two oxygen atoms.

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Showing only one electron in a bond** - must show both (dot AND cross)

2. **Forgetting to show all outer shell electrons** - all valence electrons should be shown

3. **Drawing bonds as lines when dots/crosses required** - read the question carefully

## 8.2 Simple Molecular Substances

### What are Simple Molecular Substances?

**Simple molecular substances** consist of small, discrete molecules held together by weak **intermolecular forces**.

### Structure

- Individual molecules are held together by strong **covalent bonds**
- Molecules are held together by weak **intermolecular forces**
- Low melting and boiling points because intermolecular forces are easy to overcome

### Examples of Simple Molecular Substances

| Substance | Formula | State at room temp |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Hydrogen | H₂ | Gas |
| Oxygen | O₂ | Gas |
| Nitrogen | N₂ | Gas |
| Chlorine | Cl₂ | Gas |
| Water | H₂O | Liquid |
| Methane | CH₄ | Gas |
| Carbon dioxide | CO₂ | Gas |
| Ammonia | NH₃ | Gas |

### Properties of Simple Molecular Substances

#### 1. Low Melting and Boiling Points

**Why?**
- Weak intermolecular forces between molecules
- Little energy needed to separate molecules
- Most are gases or liquids at room temperature

**Melting points comparison:**
| Substance | Melting point (°C) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| O₂ | -219 |
| H₂O | 0 |
| CH₄ | -182 |
| CO₂ | -78 (sublimes) |

#### 2. Do Not Conduct Electricity

**Why?**
- No free ions or delocalised electrons
- Molecules are neutral overall
- Electrons are held in covalent bonds

**Exception:** Some molecules can ionise in solution (e.g., HCl in water → H⁺ + Cl⁻)

#### 3. Generally Insoluble in Water

**Why?**
- Most covalent molecules are non-polar
- Water is polar
- "Like dissolves like" - non-polar dissolves in non-polar

**Exceptions:**
- Small polar molecules (H₂O, NH₃, HCl) dissolve in water

#### 4. Often Soluble in Organic Solvents

**Why?**
- Similar intermolecular forces
- Organic solvents are non-polar like many molecular substances

### Intermolecular Forces

The three types of intermolecular forces (from weakest to strongest):

1. **Van der Waals forces** (induced dipole-dipole) - present in ALL molecules
2. **Permanent dipole-dipole forces** - in polar molecules
3. **Hydrogen bonding** - strongest type, in molecules with O-H, N-H, or F-H bonds

**Effect on boiling point:**
More/stronger intermolecular forces → Higher boiling point

| Molecule | Mr | Boiling point | Intermolecular forces |
|----------|-----|---------------|----------------------|
| CH₄ | 16 | -161°C | Van der Waals only |
| H₂O | 18 | 100°C | Hydrogen bonding |

### Trend in Boiling Points of Halogens

| Halogen | Formula | Mr | State at r.t.p | Boiling point |
|---------|---------|-----|----------------|---------------|
| Fluorine | F₂ | 38 | Gas | -188°C |
| Chlorine | Cl₂ | 71 | Gas | -34°C |
| Bromine | Br₂ | 160 | Liquid | 59°C |
| Iodine | I₂ | 254 | Solid | 184°C |

**Explanation:**
- Larger molecules have more electrons
- Stronger Van der Waals forces
- Higher boiling points

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Confusing intermolecular forces with covalent bonds**
   - Intermolecular forces = BETWEEN molecules (weak)
   - Covalent bonds = WITHIN molecules (strong)

2. **Saying "weak bonds"** - always specify "weak intermolecular forces"

3. **Not explaining the difference in boiling points of H₂O vs H₂S**
   - H₂O has hydrogen bonding, H₂S does not

## 8.3 Giant Covalent Structures

### What are Giant Covalent Structures?

**Giant covalent structures** (also called **macromolecules**) are huge networks of atoms all joined by covalent bonds in a giant 3D structure.

### Types of Giant Covalent Structures

1. **Diamond**
2. **Graphite**
3. **Silicon dioxide (SiO₂)**

---

### Diamond

**Structure:**
- Each carbon atom forms **4 covalent bonds** with 4 other carbon atoms
- **Tetrahedral arrangement** - bond angle 109.5°
- Forms a **giant 3D network**

**Diagram:**
```
    C
   /|\
  / | \
 C  C  C
 |\/|\/|
 |/ |/ |
 C--C--C
```

**Properties:**

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Very hard** | Strong covalent bonds in all directions |
| **Very high melting point** (3550°C) | Lots of energy needed to break many covalent bonds |
| **Does NOT conduct electricity** | No free electrons - all 4 outer electrons used in bonding |
| **Insoluble in water** | Covalent bonds too strong to be broken by water |

**Uses:**
- Cutting tools (hardness)
- Drill bits
- Jewellery (sparkles due to light refraction)

---

### Graphite

**Structure:**
- Each carbon atom forms **3 covalent bonds** with 3 other carbon atoms
- Forms **layers of hexagonal rings**
- **One electron per carbon is delocalised** (free to move)
- Layers held together by **weak Van der Waals forces**

**Diagram:**
```
Layer structure (top view):
   C---C---C
  / \ / \ / \
 C   C   C   C
  \ / \ / \ /
   C---C---C

Layer structure (side view):
  ○ ○ ○ ○ ○   ← Layer 3
  ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓     (weak forces between layers)
  ○ ○ ○ ○ ○   ← Layer 2
  ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓
  ○ ○ ○ ○ ○   ← Layer 1
```

**Properties:**

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Soft and slippery** | Weak forces between layers allow them to slide over each other |
| **High melting point** (3650°C) | Strong covalent bonds within layers need much energy to break |
| **Conducts electricity** | Delocalised electrons can move through the structure |
| **Insoluble in water** | Covalent bonds too strong |

**Uses:**
- Pencils (layers rub off onto paper)
- Lubricant (slippery)
- Electrodes (conducts electricity)

---

### Comparison: Diamond vs Graphite

| Property | Diamond | Graphite |
|----------|---------|----------|
| Bonding | Each C bonded to 4 others | Each C bonded to 3 others |
| Structure | Giant 3D network | Layered structure |
| Hardness | Very hard | Soft and slippery |
| Melting point | Very high (~3550°C) | Very high (~3650°C) |
| Conducts electricity? | No | Yes |
| Uses | Cutting tools, jewellery | Pencils, electrodes, lubricant |

**Key difference:**
- Diamond: 4 bonds per C, no free electrons
- Graphite: 3 bonds per C, 1 delocalised electron

---

### Silicon Dioxide (Silica, SiO₂)

**Structure:**
- Each silicon atom is bonded to **4 oxygen atoms**
- Each oxygen atom is bonded to **2 silicon atoms**
- Forms a **giant 3D network** similar to diamond

**Diagram:**
```
    O
    |
O--Si--O
    |
    O
    |
    Si
   /|\
  O O O
```

**Properties:**

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Hard** | Strong covalent bonds throughout structure |
| **Very high melting point** (1713°C) | Many strong covalent bonds to break |
| **Does NOT conduct electricity** | No free electrons |
| **Insoluble in water** | Covalent bonds too strong |

**Uses:**
- Glass (main component)
- Sand
- Silicon chips for electronics

---

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Saying graphite has "free electrons"** - say "delocalised electrons"

2. **Not explaining WHY graphite conducts** - must mention the spare electron from each carbon

3. **Forgetting that giant covalent structures have NO intermolecular forces** - the whole structure is covalent bonds

4. **Saying diamond conducts** - it doesn't!

## 8.4 Properties of Covalent Compounds

### Summary Table

| Property | Simple Molecular | Giant Covalent |
|----------|------------------|----------------|
| **Bonding** | Covalent bonds within molecules, weak forces between | Covalent bonds throughout |
| **Melting point** | Low | Very high |
| **Hardness** | Soft (often gases/liquids) | Hard (except graphite) |
| **Electrical conductivity** | Does not conduct | Diamond/SiO₂: No; Graphite: Yes |
| **Solubility** | Variable | Insoluble |

### Explaining Properties in Exams

**For simple molecular substances:**

1. **Low melting point:**
   - "Weak intermolecular forces between molecules"
   - "Little energy needed to separate molecules"

2. **Does not conduct:**
   - "No free ions or delocalised electrons"
   - "Electrons are held in covalent bonds"

**For giant covalent substances:**

1. **High melting point:**
   - "Strong covalent bonds throughout the structure"
   - "Large amounts of energy needed to break many bonds"

2. **Hardness (diamond):**
   - "Strong covalent bonds in all directions"
   - "Rigid 3D structure"

3. **Graphite conducts:**
   - "Each carbon uses 3 electrons for bonding"
   - "One electron per carbon is delocalised"
   - "Delocalised electrons can move through the structure"

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Using "molecules" to describe giant covalent structures**
   - Diamond and graphite do NOT consist of molecules

2. **Saying "intermolecular forces" for giant structures**
   - There are no separate molecules, so no intermolecular forces

3. **Not being specific about which type of covalent substance**

## 8.5 Polymers (Chemistry Only)

### What are Polymers?

**Polymers** are very large molecules made by joining together many small molecules called **monomers**.

- **Monomer**: Small molecule that joins to make a polymer
- **Polymer**: Large molecule made of repeating units

### Polymerisation

The process of joining monomers together to form a polymer.

### Addition Polymerisation

**How it works:**
- Monomers with C=C double bonds join together
- Double bond opens up
- Monomers add to the growing chain
- No other products formed

**Example: Poly(ethene) from ethene**

```
n CH₂=CH₂  →  -[-CH₂-CH₂-]-ₙ-

Ethene monomer     Poly(ethene) polymer
```

**Diagram:**
```
    C=C        C-C-C-C-C-C
   /   \  →   /   /   /   /
  H     H    H   H   H   H

(n monomers)  (polymer chain)
```

### Common Addition Polymers

| Polymer | Monomer | Uses |
|---------|---------|------|
| Poly(ethene) / Polythene | Ethene | Plastic bags, bottles |
| Poly(propene) / Polypropylene | Propene | Ropes, crates |
| Poly(chloroethene) / PVC | Chloroethene | Pipes, window frames |
| Poly(tetrafluoroethene) / PTFE | Tetrafluoroethene | Non-stick coating |

### Drawing Polymers

**Steps:**
1. Draw the monomer with its double bond
2. Open the double bond to a single bond
3. Add continuation bonds to show chain extends
4. Draw brackets around the repeating unit
5. Add subscript 'n' to show many units

**Example: Poly(propene)**
```
Monomer:  CH₂=CH-CH₃

Repeating unit:  -[-CH₂-CH(CH₃)-]-ₙ-

Full structure:
    H   H   H   H
    |   |   |   |
...-C-C-C-C-...
    |   |   |   |
    H  CH₃  H  CH₃
```

### Condensation Polymerisation (Higher Tier)

In condensation polymerisation:
- Two different monomers join
- A small molecule (usually water) is eliminated

**Example: Polyesters**
- Monomer with -OH groups joins with monomer with -COOH groups
- Water is eliminated at each bond formed

### Properties of Polymers

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Solid at room temperature** | Large molecules have stronger intermolecular forces |
| **Can be moulded when heated** | Polymer chains can slide past each other |
| **Electrical insulators** | No free electrons or ions |
| **Lightweight** | Carbon and hydrogen are light atoms |

### Thermosoftening vs Thermosetting Polymers

| Type | Structure | Behaviour when heated |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Thermosoftening** | Linear chains with weak forces between | Soften and can be reshaped |
| **Thermosetting** | Cross-linked 3D network | Do not soften; char instead |

**Thermosoftening polymers:**
```
~~~~   ~~~~   ~~~~
~~~~   ~~~~   ~~~~
~~~~   ~~~~   ~~~~
(Linear chains - can slide)
```

**Thermosetting polymers:**
```
|--|---|--|
|  |   |  |
|--|---|--|
|  |   |  |
|--|---|--|
(Cross-linked - cannot slide)
```

### Uses of Polymers

| Polymer | Properties | Uses |
|---------|-----------|------|
| Poly(ethene) | Flexible, tough | Bags, bottles, toys |
| PVC | Rigid or flexible | Pipes, window frames, cables |
| PTFE | Non-stick, heat resistant | Frying pan coatings |
| Nylon | Strong, flexible | Clothing, ropes |
| Polyester | Strong, can be made into fibres | Clothing, bottles |

### Common Exam Mistakes ❌

1. **Drawing the monomer as the repeating unit** - they're different!

2. **Forgetting the 'n' subscript** - shows many repeating units

3. **Not showing continuation bonds** (- at each end)

4. **Confusing addition and condensation polymerisation**

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Covalent bond** | A shared pair of electrons between two atoms |
| **Molecule** | Two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds |
| **Simple molecular substance** | Substances made of small molecules with weak intermolecular forces |
| **Intermolecular forces** | Forces of attraction between molecules |
| **Giant covalent structure** | A huge network of atoms all joined by covalent bonds |
| **Delocalised electron** | An electron that is free to move through a structure |
| **Polymer** | A large molecule made from many small monomer units |
| **Monomer** | A small molecule that can join to form a polymer |
| **Addition polymerisation** | Monomers with double bonds join without forming other products |
| **Thermosoftening polymer** | Polymer that softens when heated and can be reshaped |
| **Thermosetting polymer** | Polymer that does not soften when heated (cross-linked) |

## Exam Tips ✓

1. **Always distinguish between types of covalent substances**
   - Simple molecular vs giant covalent

2. **Use the word "intermolecular"** when discussing simple molecular properties

3. **For graphite conductivity** - mention delocalised electrons

4. **For polymerisation** - show the repeating unit in brackets with 'n'

5. **Compare diamond and graphite** - both made of carbon but very different properties

6. **Know your diagrams** - practise dot and cross diagrams
