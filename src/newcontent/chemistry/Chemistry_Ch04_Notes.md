## 4.1 Development of the Periodic Table

### 4.1.1 Early Attempts at Classification

**Early 1800s:** Scientists began looking for patterns in element properties.

**Johann Döbereiner (1817) - Triads:**
- Grouped elements in threes (triads)
- The middle element had properties halfway between the other two
- Example: Lithium (7), Sodium (23), Potassium (39)
- **Limitation:** Only worked for a few elements

**John Newlands (1864) - Law of Octaves:**
- Arranged elements in order of atomic weight
- Noticed every 8th element had similar properties
- **Limitation:** Pattern broke down after calcium; included some non-elements

### 4.1.2 Mendeleev's Periodic Table (1869)

**Dmitri Mendeleev** made the breakthrough that led to the modern periodic table.

**Key Features of Mendeleev's Work:**

1. **Arrangement by Atomic Mass:**
   - Elements arranged in order of increasing atomic mass
   - Similar elements placed in same column (group)

2. **Leaving Gaps:**
   - Mendeleev left gaps for undiscovered elements
   - Predicted properties of missing elements (e.g., germanium)
   - These predictions were later confirmed

3. **Changing the Order:**
   - Some elements were swapped to maintain pattern
   - Example: Iodine placed after tellurium despite lower atomic mass
   - This was later explained by atomic number

**Why Mendeleev was Successful:**
- He left gaps for undiscovered elements
- He was willing to change the order when needed
- His predictions were confirmed when new elements were discovered

### 4.1.3 The Modern Periodic Table

**Modern Arrangement:**
- Elements arranged in order of **atomic number** (not atomic mass)
- Elements with similar properties appear in the same groups
- The pattern is explained by electron configuration

**Key Point:** Mendeleev's success helped establish the periodic table as a fundamental tool in chemistry.

## 4.2 Structure of the Periodic Table

### 4.2.1 Basic Organization

```
Groups (vertical columns) → 1  2  3  4  5  6  7  0
                              ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓
Period 1 (horizontal row) →   H              He
Period 2 →                    Li Be B  C  N  O  F  Ne
Period 3 →                    Na Mg Al Si P  S  Cl Ar
Period 4 →                    K  Ca ...transition metals...
```

### 4.2.2 Groups

**Definition:** Vertical columns in the periodic table. Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties.

**Key Points:**
- Groups are numbered 1-7 and 0 (or 1-18 in IUPAC system)
- Elements in the same group have the same number of outer shell electrons
- This gives them similar chemical properties

| Group | Name | Outer Electrons | Charge of Ions |
|-------|------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | Alkali Metals | 1 | +1 |
| 2 | Alkaline Earth Metals | 2 | +2 |
| 3 | Boron Group | 3 | +3 |
| 4 | Carbon Group | 4 | Variable |
| 5 | Nitrogen Group | 5 | -3 (non-metals) |
| 6 | Oxygen Group | 6 | -2 |
| 7 | Halogens | 7 | -1 |
| 0 | Noble Gases | 8 (full) | 0 (no ions) |

### 4.2.3 Periods

**Definition:** Horizontal rows in the periodic table.

**Key Points:**
- Periods are numbered 1-7
- The period number equals the number of electron shells
- Elements show a gradual change in properties across a period

### 4.2.4 Metals and Non-Metals

**The Periodic Table is Divided:**
- **Metals:** Left and center of the table
- **Non-metals:** Right side of the table
- **Metalloids:** Along the diagonal line (e.g., silicon, germanium)

## 4.3 Group 1: Alkali Metals

### 4.3.1 Properties of Alkali Metals

**Elements:** Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Rubidium (Rb), Caesium (Cs)

**Physical Properties:**
| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Appearance | Soft, shiny metals when freshly cut |
| Texture | Can be cut with a knife (soft) |
| Density | Low density (Li, Na, K float on water) |
| Melting point | Low for metals, decrease down group |
| Conductivity | Good conductors of heat and electricity |

**Chemical Properties:**
- Very reactive (stored in oil to prevent reaction with air)
- React vigorously with water
- Form ionic compounds with non-metals
- Always form +1 ions (lose 1 electron)
- Low melting and boiling points (for metals)

### 4.3.2 Reactions with Water

**General Equation:**
$$2M + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2MOH + H_2$$

Where M = any alkali metal

**Observations:**

| Metal | Observations | Equation |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Lithium | Fizzes steadily, moves slowly on surface | $2Li + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2LiOH + H_2$ |
| Sodium | Melts into a ball, fizzes rapidly, moves on surface | $2Na + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH + H_2$ |
| Potassium | Catches fire instantly, lilac flame, violent reaction | $2K + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2KOH + H_2$ |

**Trend:** Reactivity **increases** down the group

### 4.3.3 Reactions with Oxygen

Alkali metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides:

| Metal | Product | Formula |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Lithium | Lithium oxide | Li₂O |
| Sodium | Sodium oxide | Na₂O |
| Potassium | Potassium oxide | K₂O |

**Equation:** $4Na + O_2 \rightarrow 2Na_2O$

### 4.3.4 Reactions with Chlorine

Alkali metals react vigorously with chlorine to form chlorides:

**Equation:** $2Na + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2NaCl$

### 4.3.5 Explaining the Trend in Reactivity

**Why does reactivity increase down Group 1?**

1. Atoms get **larger** down the group (more electron shells)
2. The outer electron is **further from the nucleus**
3. There is increased **shielding** by inner electron shells
4. The outer electron is **less strongly attracted** to the nucleus
5. It becomes **easier to lose** the outer electron
6. Therefore, reactivity **increases** down the group

## 4.4 Group 7: The Halogens

### 4.4.1 Properties of Halogens

**Elements:** Fluorine (F), Chlorine (Cl), Bromine (Br), Iodine (I), Astatine (At)

**Physical Properties:**

| Property | Fluorine | Chlorine | Bromine | Iodine |
|----------|----------|----------|---------|--------|
| Appearance | Pale yellow gas | Green-yellow gas | Red-brown liquid | Grey-black solid |
| State at room temp | Gas | Gas | Liquid | Solid |
| Melting point | -220°C | -101°C | -7°C | 114°C |
| Boiling point | -188°C | -34°C | 59°C | 184°C |

**Trends:**
- Melting and boiling points **increase** down the group
- Colour becomes **darker** down the group

**Chemical Properties:**
- All exist as diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂)
- Form -1 ions (gain 1 electron)
- React with metals to form salts (halides)
- React with hydrogen to form hydrogen halides

### 4.4.2 Reactions of Halogens

**Reaction with Metals:**
$$2Fe + 3Cl_2 \rightarrow 2FeCl_3$$
$$2Na + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2NaCl$$

**Reaction with Hydrogen:**
$$H_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2HCl$$

**Hydrogen halides dissolve in water to form acidic solutions:**
$$HCl(g) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow H^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq)$$

### 4.4.3 Displacement Reactions

**Principle:** A more reactive halogen will displace a less reactive halogen from its compound.

**Reactivity Order:** Fluorine > Chlorine > Bromine > Iodine

**Examples:**

| Reaction | Observation |
|----------|-------------|
| $Cl_2 + 2NaBr \rightarrow 2NaCl + Br_2$ | Orange/brown colour of bromine appears |
| $Cl_2 + 2NaI \rightarrow 2NaCl + I_2$ | Brown colour of iodine appears |
| $Br_2 + 2NaI \rightarrow 2NaBr + I_2$ | Brown colour of iodine appears |
| $Cl_2 + 2NaF \rightarrow$ No reaction | Chlorine cannot displace fluorine |

### 4.4.4 Explaining the Trend in Reactivity

**Why does reactivity decrease down Group 7?**

1. Atoms get **larger** down the group (more electron shells)
2. The incoming electron is **further from the nucleus**
3. There is increased **shielding** by inner electron shells
4. The attraction for an incoming electron **decreases**
5. It becomes **harder to gain** an electron
6. Therefore, reactivity **decreases** down the group

## 4.5 Group 0: Noble Gases

### 4.5.1 Properties of Noble Gases

**Elements:** Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), Radon (Rn)

**Physical Properties:**

| Property | Helium | Neon | Argon | Krypton | Xenon |
|----------|--------|------|-------|---------|-------|
| Appearance | Colourless gas | Colourless gas | Colourless gas | Colourless gas | Colourless gas |
| Boiling point | -269°C | -246°C | -186°C | -152°C | -108°C |

**Trend:** Boiling points **increase** down the group (due to increasing atomic size)

### 4.5.2 Chemical Properties

**Key Features:**
- **Unreactive** (inert) - they have full outer shells (8 electrons, except He with 2)
- **Monatomic** - exist as single atoms (not molecules)
- **Non-metals** - they are all non-metals
- **Do not form ions** readily - they have stable electron configurations

**Why are they unreactive?**
- They have full outer electron shells (He: 2, others: 8)
- This is a stable electron arrangement
- They do not need to gain, lose, or share electrons

### 4.5.3 Uses of Noble Gases

| Gas | Use | Reason |
|-----|-----|--------|
| Helium | Balloons, airships | Low density, non-flammable |
| Helium | Deep-sea diving | Mixed with oxygen to prevent 'the bends' |
| Neon | Advertising signs | Glows red-orange when electricity passed through |
| Argon | Light bulbs | Prevents filament from oxidizing |
| Argon | Welding | Provides inert atmosphere |
| Krypton | Lasers, high-efficiency lighting | Specific emission spectrum |

## 4.6 Properties of Metals and Non-Metals

### 4.6.1 Metals

**Physical Properties:**

| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| State | Usually solid at room temperature (except mercury) |
| Appearance | Shiny (lustrous) |
| Melting/Boiling points | Generally high |
| Density | Generally high |
| Conductivity | Good conductors of heat and electricity |
| Malleability | Malleable (can be hammered into shape) |
| Ductility | Ductile (can be drawn into wires) |
| Sonorous | Make a ringing sound when hit |

**Chemical Properties:**
- Lose electrons to form positive ions (cations)
- Form basic oxides
- React with acids to produce salts and hydrogen

### 4.6.2 Non-Metals

**Physical Properties:**

| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| State | Can be solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature |
| Appearance | Dull (non-lustrous) if solid |
| Melting/Boiling points | Generally low |
| Density | Generally low |
| Conductivity | Poor conductors (insulators) |
| Malleability | Brittle (break when hammered) |
| Ductility | Non-ductile |

**Chemical Properties:**
- Gain or share electrons to form negative ions or covalent bonds
- Form acidic oxides (most non-metals)
- Generally do not react with acids

### 4.6.3 Comparison Table

| Property | Metals | Non-Metals |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Appearance | Shiny (lustrous) | Dull |
| State at room temp | Solid (except Hg) | Solid, liquid, or gas |
| Melting point | High | Low |
| Density | High | Low |
| Heat conductivity | Good | Poor |
| Electrical conductivity | Good | Poor |
| Malleability | Malleable | Brittle |
| Ductility | Ductile | Non-ductile |
| Ions formed | Positive (cations) | Negative (anions) |
| Oxides | Basic | Acidic |

## 4.7 Transition Metals (Chemistry Only)

### 4.7.1 Position in Periodic Table

- Located in the center of the periodic table
- Between Groups 2 and 3
- Examples: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Zinc (Zn), Silver (Ag), Gold (Au)

### 4.7.2 Physical Properties

**Compared to Alkali Metals:**

| Property | Transition Metals | Alkali Metals |
|----------|------------------|---------------|
| Melting point | High | Low |
| Density | High | Low |
| Hardness | Hard | Soft |
| Strength | Strong | Weak |

**Key Physical Properties:**
- High melting and boiling points
- High density
- Hard and strong
- Good conductors of heat and electricity
- Malleable and ductile

### 4.7.3 Chemical Properties

**1. Variable Oxidation States:**
- Transition metals can form ions with different charges
- Example: Iron forms Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺; Copper forms Cu⁺ and Cu²⁺

| Element | Possible Oxidation States |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Iron | +2, +3 |
| Copper | +1, +2 |
| Manganese | +2, +3, +4, +6, +7 |
| Chromium | +2, +3, +6 |

**2. Formation of Coloured Compounds:**
- Transition metal compounds are often coloured

| Compound | Colour |
|----------|--------|
| Copper(II) sulfate (CuSO₄) | Blue |
| Iron(II) sulfate (FeSO₄) | Pale green |
| Iron(III) chloride (FeCl₃) | Yellow/brown |
| Potassium manganate(VII) (KMnO₄) | Purple |
| Potassium dichromate(VI) (K₂Cr₂O₇) | Orange |

**3. Catalytic Properties:**
- Many transition metals and their compounds act as catalysts

| Catalyst | Reaction Catalyzed |
|----------|-------------------|
| Iron (Fe) | Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃) |
| Vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) | Contact process (2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃) |
| Nickel (Ni) | Hydrogenation of alkenes |
| Platinum/Rhodium | Catalytic converters in cars |

### 4.7.4 Uses of Transition Metals

| Metal | Use | Property Used |
|-------|-----|---------------|
| Iron | Construction, vehicles | Strength, abundance |
| Copper | Electrical wiring | Excellent conductivity |
| Copper | Water pipes | Malleability, corrosion resistance |
| Titanium | Aircraft, medical implants | High strength-to-weight ratio, corrosion resistance |
| Gold | Jewellery, electronics | Unreactive, malleable, attractive |
| Silver | Jewellery, photography | Reflectivity, conductivity |

## 4.8 Common Exam Mistakes

### 4.8.1 Trends and Explanations

❌ **Wrong:** "Reactivity increases down Group 7"
✅ **Correct:** "Reactivity decreases down Group 7"

❌ **Wrong explanation:** "The nucleus gets bigger"
✅ **Correct explanation:** "The atomic radius increases, so the outer shell is further from the nucleus"

### 4.8.2 Electron Configuration

❌ **Wrong:** "Elements in the same period have the same number of electrons"
✅ **Correct:** "Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells"

❌ **Wrong:** "Group number = number of electron shells"
✅ **Correct:** "Group number = number of outer shell electrons"

### 4.8.3 Noble Gases

❌ **Wrong:** "Noble gases have 8 electrons in their outer shell"
✅ **Correct:** "Most noble gases have 8 electrons; helium has 2"

❌ **Wrong:** "Noble gases don't react at all"
✅ **Correct:** "Noble gases are very unreactive due to full outer shells"

### 4.8.4 Transition Metals

❌ **Wrong:** "Transition metals are very reactive"
✅ **Correct:** "Transition metals are less reactive than alkali metals"

❌ **Wrong:** "All metals are transition metals"
✅ **Correct:** "Only metals in the d-block between Groups 2 and 3 are transition metals"

## 4.10 Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Alkali metals | Group 1 elements: soft, reactive metals |

| Displacement reaction | A reaction where a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound |
| Group | Vertical column in the periodic table |
| Halogens | Group 7 elements: reactive non-metals |
