## Section C: Short Answer Mark Schemes

### Q1. Ion Formation (4 marks)

a) **Lithium:** Loses 1 electron to form Li⁺ (1 mark for electron loss, positive ion)

b) **Oxygen:** Gains 2 electrons to form O²⁻ (1 mark for electron gain, -2 charge)

c) **Aluminium:** Loses 3 electrons to form Al³⁺ (1 mark)

d) **Fluorine:** Gains 1 electron to form F⁻ (1 mark)

---

### Q2. Formulae (6 marks)

e) **(NH₄)₂CO₃** (1 mark - brackets needed)

### Q3. Dot and Cross Diagrams (4 marks)

a) **NaCl formation:**
- Na atom shown with one outer electron (dot or cross) (1 mark)
- Cl atom shown with seven outer electrons (1 mark)
- Arrow showing electron transfer
- Na⁺ with no outer electrons shown, Cl⁻ with eight outer electrons (different symbols for original and gained)

b) **MgO formation:**
- Mg atom with two outer electrons (1 mark)
- O atom with six outer electrons (1 mark)
- Two electrons transferred from Mg to O
- Mg²⁺ with no outer electrons, O²⁻ with eight outer electrons

---

### Q4. Explanations (6 marks)

a) **High melting points:**
- Strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions (1 mark)
- Large amounts of energy needed to overcome these forces (1 mark)

b) **Conductivity:**
- Solid: ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move (1 mark)
- Molten: ions are free to move and carry charge (1 mark)

c) **Brittle:**
- When hit, layers of ions slide past each other (1 mark)
- This brings like charges together, causing repulsion and the crystal breaks (1 mark)

---

### Q5. Comparing Melting Points (3 marks)

- MgO has a **higher melting point** than NaCl (1 mark)
- Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ have charges of +2 and -2 (higher charges) compared to Na⁺ and Cl⁻ (+1 and -1) (1 mark)
- Stronger electrostatic attraction in MgO requires more energy to overcome (1 mark)

---

### Q6. Identifying Substances (4 marks)

a) **Substance A is ionic** (1 mark)
- Reason: High melting point (801°C), does not conduct when solid but does conduct when molten (1 mark)

b) **Substance B or D could be a metal** (1 mark)
- Reason: Conducts electricity when solid - metals have delocalised electrons that can move (1 mark)
- (Note: D has lower MP, could be an alkali metal)

---

### Q7. Structure Description (4 marks)

- Sodium chloride forms a **giant ionic lattice** (1 mark)
- Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are held together by strong **electrostatic attraction** (1 mark)
- Ions are arranged in a **regular arrangement** with alternating charges (1 mark)
- Each ion has a **coordination number** of 6 (surrounded by 6 ions of opposite charge) (1 mark)

---

### Q8. Limitations (4 marks)

Any two from:

1. **Ions are not perfect spheres**
   - Example: Large anions can be polarised by small cations

2. **Bonds can have covalent character**
   - Example: AgCl shows some covalent character due to polarisation

3. **Some compounds show unexpected behaviour**
   - Example: AlCl₃ sublimes at low temperature, behaving covalently

4. **The model doesn't explain transition metal behaviour well**
   - Example: Variable oxidation states, coloured compounds

(2 marks per limitation with example)

---

### Q9. Aluminium Chloride (3 marks)

- AlCl₃ is expected to be ionic (Al³⁺ and Cl⁻) (1 mark)
- However, it sublimes at a relatively low temperature and behaves like a covalent compound (1 mark)
- This shows that the ionic model has limitations - some "ionic" compounds have covalent character (1 mark)
