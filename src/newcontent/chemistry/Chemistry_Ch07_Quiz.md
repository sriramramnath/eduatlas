### Question 1
What happens when a sodium atom forms a sodium ion?

A) It gains one electron  
B) It loses one electron  
C) It gains one proton  
D) It loses one proton  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Sodium (Group 1) loses 1 electron to form Na⁺

---

### Question 2
Which ion has the same electron configuration as argon?

A) Na⁺  
B) Mg²⁺  
C) Cl⁻  
D) All of the above  

**Answer: D**

**Explanation**: Na⁺ (2,8), Mg²⁺ (2,8), Cl⁻ (2,8,8) all have same config as Ar (2,8,8) or Ne (2,8) - Cl⁻ matches Ar exactly

---

### Question 3
What is the formula of calcium phosphate?

A) CaPO₄  
B) Ca₂PO₄  
C) Ca₃(PO₄)₂  
D) Ca(PO₄)₂  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation**: Ca²⁺ + PO₄³⁻ = Ca₃(PO₄)₂ (swap and balance: 3×2 and 2×3)

### Question 4
In an ionic lattice, each ion is surrounded by:

A) Ions of the same charge  
B) Ions of the opposite charge  
C) Neutral atoms  
D) Molecules  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation**: Each ion is surrounded by ions of opposite charge

### Question 28 (4 marks)
Draw dot and cross diagrams to show the formation of: a) Sodium chloride from sodium and chlorine atoms, b) Magnesium oxide from magnesium and oxygen atoms.

**Answer**: 
a) NaCl formation: Na atom with one outer electron (dot or cross); Cl atom with seven outer electrons; arrow showing electron transfer; Na⁺ with no outer electrons, Cl⁻ with eight outer electrons (different symbols) (2 marks)
b) MgO formation: Mg atom with two outer electrons; O atom with six outer electrons; two electrons transferred; Mg²⁺ with no outer electrons, O²⁻ with eight outer electrons (2 marks)

### Question 29 (6 marks)
Explain, in terms of structure and bonding, why: a) Ionic compounds have high melting points, b) Ionic compounds conduct electricity when molten but not when solid, c) Ionic crystals are brittle.

**Answer**: 
a) High melting points: Strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions; large amounts of energy needed to overcome these forces (2 marks)
b) Conductivity: Solid - ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move; Molten - ions are free to move and carry charge (2 marks)
c) Brittle: When hit, layers of ions slide past each other; this brings like charges together, causing repulsion and the crystal breaks (2 marks)

---

### Question 30 (3 marks)
Compare the melting points of sodium chloride (NaCl) and magnesium oxide (MgO). Explain your answer in terms of the ions involved.

**Answer**: MgO has a higher melting point than NaCl; Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ have charges of +2 and -2 (higher charges) compared to Na⁺ and Cl⁻ (+1 and -1); stronger electrostatic attraction in MgO requires more energy to overcome.

### Question 31 (4 marks)
The table shows some properties of four substances:

| Substance | Melting point (°C) | Conducts when solid? | Conducts when molten? |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| A | 801 | No | Yes |
| B | 1610 | Yes | Yes |
| C | -78 | No | No |
| D | 98 | Yes | Yes |

a) Which substance is ionic? Explain your answer.
b) Which substance could be a metal? Explain your answer.

**Answer**: 
a) Substance A is ionic - Reason: High melting point (801°C), does not conduct when solid but does conduct when molten (2 marks)
b) Substance B or D could be a metal - Reason: Conducts electricity when solid - metals have delocalised electrons that can move (2 marks)

### Question 32 (4 marks)
Describe the structure of a sodium chloride crystal. Include the following terms in your answer: Lattice, Electrostatic attraction, Coordination number, Regular arrangement.

**Answer**: Sodium chloride forms a giant ionic lattice; Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are held together by strong electrostatic attraction; ions are arranged in a regular arrangement with alternating charges; each ion has a coordination number of 6 (surrounded by 6 ions of opposite charge).

### Question 33 (4 marks)
State two limitations of the simple ionic model and give an example for each.

**Answer**: 
1. Ions are not perfect spheres: Example - Large anions can be polarised by small cations (2 marks)
2. Bonds can have covalent character: Example - AgCl shows some covalent character due to polarisation (2 marks)

### Question 34 (3 marks)
Aluminium chloride (AlCl₃) sublimes at 178°C and behaves more like a covalent compound than an ionic compound. Explain why this is unexpected and what it tells us about the limitations of the ionic model.

**Answer**: AlCl₃ is expected to be ionic (Al³⁺ and Cl⁻); however, it sublimes at a relatively low temperature and behaves like a covalent compound; this shows that the ionic model has limitations - some ionic compounds have covalent character.

### Question 37 (3 marks)
A student investigates the electrical conductivity of sodium chloride. They set up a circuit with solid NaCl between two electrodes and observe that the bulb does not light. They then heat the NaCl until it melts and the bulb lights up.
a) Explain why the bulb does not light with solid NaCl.
b) Explain why the bulb lights up when the NaCl melts.
c) What particles carry the charge in molten NaCl?

**Answer**: 
a) Solid NaCl does not conduct because: ions are held fixed in the lattice and cannot move (1 mark)
b) Molten NaCl conducts because: ions are free to move and carry charge (1 mark)
c) Ions (Na⁺ and Cl⁻) carry the charge (1 mark)
