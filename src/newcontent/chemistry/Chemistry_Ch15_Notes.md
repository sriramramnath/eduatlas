## 1. Introduction to Metal Extraction

### Why Extraction is Necessary
Most metals are found in the Earth's crust combined with other elements, usually as **ores**. An ore is a rock containing enough metal compound to make it economically viable to extract the metal.

### Native Metals
Some unreactive metals (low in reactivity series) are found **native** (uncombined) because they do not readily react with other elements:
- **Gold** - found native
- **Silver** - sometimes found native
- **Platinum** - found native

### Relationship Between Reactivity and Extraction Method

| Reactivity | Metals | Extraction Method |
|------------|--------|-------------------|
| Very High | K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al | Electrolysis of molten compounds |
| Medium | Zn, Fe, Sn, Pb, Cu | Reduction with carbon (smelting) |
| Low | Ag, Au, Pt | Found native or simple heating |

**Why different methods?**
- **Very reactive metals** form very stable compounds that cannot be reduced by carbon
- **Medium reactivity metals** can be reduced by carbon (carbon is more reactive)
- **Unreactive metals** are often found uncombined

## 2. Extraction of Iron

### The Blast Furnace

Iron is extracted from its ore (haematite, Fe₂O₃) in a **blast furnace**.

### Raw Materials

| Material | Chemical Name | Formula | Purpose |
|----------|---------------|---------|---------|
| Haematite | Iron(III) oxide | Fe₂O₃ | Iron ore - source of iron |
| Coke | Carbon | C | Fuel and reducing agent |
| Limestone | Calcium carbonate | CaCO₃ | Removes acidic impurities |
| Hot air | Oxygen | O₂ | Supports combustion |

### The Process

#### Step 1: Combustion of Coke
```
C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)
```
- Coke burns in hot air
- Produces carbon dioxide
- Temperature reaches ~1500°C

#### Step 2: Formation of Carbon Monoxide
```
CO₂(g) + C(s) → 2CO(g)
```
- Carbon dioxide reacts with more coke
- Produces carbon monoxide (the reducing agent)

#### Step 3: Reduction of Iron Ore
```
Fe₂O₃(s) + 3CO(g) → 2Fe(l) + 3CO₂(g)
```
- Carbon monoxide reduces iron(III) oxide
- Iron is produced as molten liquid
- Iron is dense and sinks to the bottom

### Role of Limestone

#### Thermal Decomposition
```
CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
```
- Limestone decomposes in the heat
- Produces calcium oxide (quicklime) and carbon dioxide

#### Removal of Impurities
```
CaO(s) + SiO₂(s) → CaSiO₃(l)
```
- Calcium oxide reacts with silica (acidic impurity)
- Produces calcium silicate (slag)
- Slag is molten and floats on molten iron

### Products of the Blast Furnace

| Product | Composition | Use |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Molten iron | Fe with ~4% carbon | Cast into molds or converted to steel |
| Slag | Calcium silicate | Used in road construction, cement manufacture |

### Diagram Labels (Blast Furnace)
- **Top:** Charging hopper (where raw materials enter)
- **Upper section:** Where CO is formed
- **Middle section:** Where iron ore is reduced
- **Lower section:** Where combustion occurs
- **Bottom:** Tapping holes for iron and slag

## 3. Extraction of Aluminium

### The Ore
Aluminium is extracted from **bauxite**, which is mainly aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) with impurities.

### Why Electrolysis?
- Aluminium is very reactive (high in reactivity series)
- Carbon cannot reduce aluminium oxide
- Electrolysis is required to extract aluminium

### Purification of Bauxite
Before electrolysis, bauxite is purified to obtain pure aluminium oxide (alumina).

### The Electrolysis Process

#### The Electrolyte
- **Molten aluminium oxide** (Al₂O₃)
- Melting point is very high (~2050°C)
- Mixed with **cryolite** (Na₃AlF₆) to lower melting point to ~950°C

#### The Cell
- **Cathode (negative):** Carbon-lined steel container
- **Anode (positive):** Carbon blocks
- **Electrolyte:** Molten Al₂O₃ in cryolite

#### Electrode Reactions

**At the cathode (negative electrode):**
```
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
```
- Aluminium ions gain electrons (reduction)
- Molten aluminium collects at the bottom
- Tapped off periodically

**At the anode (positive electrode):**
```
2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
```
- Oxide ions lose electrons (oxidation)
- Oxygen gas is produced

**Anode Problem:**
```
C + O₂ → CO₂
```
- The oxygen produced reacts with the carbon anodes
- Anodes are gradually worn away
- Anodes must be replaced regularly

### Why Cryolite is Used
1. Lowers the melting point (from 2050°C to ~950°C)
2. Saves energy and reduces costs
3. Improves conductivity of the electrolyte

### Summary Table

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Ore | Bauxite (Al₂O₃) |
| Method | Electrolysis |
| Electrolyte | Molten Al₂O₃ in cryolite |
| Cathode | Carbon-lined steel (Al forms here) |
| Anode | Carbon blocks (wear away) |
| Cathode reaction | Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al |
| Anode reaction | 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ |
| Temperature | ~950°C |

## 4. Extraction of Copper

### Methods of Copper Extraction

#### 1. From Copper-Rich Ores

**Smelting (Traditional Method):**
```
CuFeS₂ (copper pyrites) → Cu₂S + FeS + SO₂
2Cu₂S + 3O₂ → 2Cu₂O + 2SO₂
2Cu₂O + Cu₂S → 6Cu + SO₂
```
- Copper sulfide ores are heated in air
- Produces impure copper

**Electrolytic Refining:**
- Impure copper is purified by electrolysis
- Produces 99.99% pure copper

#### 2. From Low-Grade Ores (Phytomining and Bioleaching)

**Phytomining:**
1. Plants are grown on land containing low-grade copper ore
2. Plants absorb copper compounds through their roots
3. Plants are harvested and burned
4. Ash contains copper compounds
5. Copper is extracted from the ash by electrolysis or displacement

**Bioleaching:**
1. Bacteria feed on low-grade copper ores
2. Bacteria produce a solution (leachate) containing copper ions
3. Copper is extracted from the leachate by:
   - Displacement with scrap iron: Cu²⁺ + Fe → Cu + Fe²⁺
   - Electrolysis

### Advantages of Phytomining and Bioleaching

| Method | Advantages |
|--------|------------|
| Phytomining | Uses low-grade ores; environmentally friendly; can restore degraded land |
| Bioleaching | Uses low-grade ores; low energy consumption; works at ambient temperature |

### Disadvantages

| Method | Disadvantages |
|--------|---------------|
| Phytomining | Slow process; depends on climate; large land area needed |
| Bioleaching | Slow process; can produce acidic waste; limited control |

## 5. Uses of Metals

### Aluminium

| Property | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Low density | Aircraft bodies, drinks cans |
| Resistant to corrosion | Window frames, greenhouses |
| Good conductor of electricity | Overhead power cables |
| Malleable and ductile | Kitchen foil, food packaging |
| Attractive appearance | Car bodies, bicycles |

**Why aluminium resists corrosion:**
- Reacts rapidly with oxygen in air
- Forms a thin, protective layer of aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃)
- This layer prevents further oxidation

### Copper

| Property | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Excellent electrical conductor | Electrical wiring, circuit boards |
| Good thermal conductor | Cooking pans, heat exchangers |
| Malleable and ductile | Water pipes, plumbing |
| Resistant to corrosion | Roofing, statues |
| Attractive color | Decorative items, jewelry |
| Antibacterial properties | Door handles, cookware |

### Iron and Steel

**Pure iron is too soft** for most uses. It is usually converted to **steel** (alloy of iron with carbon and other elements).

#### Types of Steel

| Type | Composition | Properties | Uses |
|------|-------------|------------|------|
| Mild steel | Fe + <1% C | Malleable, ductile | Car bodies, buildings, ships |
| High-carbon steel | Fe + 1-1.5% C | Hard, brittle | Cutting tools, knives |
| Stainless steel | Fe + Cr + Ni | Resistant to corrosion | Cutlery, surgical instruments, chemical plants |

### Summary Table of Metal Uses

| Metal | Key Properties | Main Uses |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| Aluminium | Low density, corrosion-resistant | Aircraft, cans, power cables |
| Copper | Excellent conductor, ductile | Wires, pipes |
| Iron/Steel | Strong, versatile | Construction, vehicles, tools |
| Gold | Unreactive, attractive, conducts | Jewelry, electronics |
| Titanium | Strong, low density, corrosion-resistant | Aircraft, medical implants |

## 6. Alloys and Their Uses

### Definition
An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (usually metals, sometimes non-metals like carbon).

### Why Alloys are Useful
- Alloys are often **harder** than pure metals
- Alloys can have improved properties
- Alloying allows tailoring of properties for specific applications

### Why Alloys are Harder than Pure Metals

**In pure metals:**
- Atoms are all the same size
- Arranged in regular layers
- Layers can slide over each other easily
- Metal is soft and malleable

**In alloys:**
- Different sized atoms disrupt the regular structure
- Layers cannot slide easily
- Alloy is harder and stronger

### Common Alloys

| Alloy | Main Metals | Added Elements | Properties | Uses |
|-------|-------------|----------------|------------|------|
| Brass | Copper | Zinc (Zn) | Hard, golden color, corrosion-resistant | Musical instruments, door fittings |
| Bronze | Copper | Tin (Sn) | Hard, corrosion-resistant | Statues, medals, bearings |
| Stainless steel | Iron | Chromium, Nickel | Resistant to corrosion | Cutlery, surgical instruments |
| Mild steel | Iron | Carbon (<1%) | Malleable, strong | Construction, car bodies |
| High-carbon steel | Iron | Carbon (1-1.5%) | Hard, brittle | Cutting tools, springs |
| Solder | Tin | Lead (Pb) or Silver | Low melting point | Joining electrical components |
| Amalgam | Mercury | Silver, Tin | Hardens at body temperature | Dental fillings |
| Duralumin | Aluminium | Copper, Magnesium | Strong, low density | Aircraft structures |
| Nitinol | Nickel | Titanium | Shape memory alloy | Medical stents, glasses frames |

### Special Alloys

#### Shape Memory Alloys (e.g., Nitinol)
- "Remember" their original shape
- Can be bent and then return to original shape when heated
- Used in medical stents, glasses frames, orthodontics

#### Smart Alloys
- Respond to changes in environment (temperature, stress)
- Return to original shape after deformation

## 7. Corrosion and Rusting Prevention

### What is Corrosion?
**Corrosion** is the gradual destruction of metals by chemical reactions with substances in the environment.

### Rusting of Iron

**Rusting** is the specific corrosion of iron (and steel).

#### Requirements for Rusting
Both **oxygen** and **water** must be present for rusting to occur.

#### Equation for Rusting
```
Iron + Oxygen + Water → Hydrated iron(III) oxide
4Fe(s) + 3O₂(g) + 2xH₂O(l) → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O(s)
```
- x = variable number of water molecules
- Rust is a reddish-brown, flaky solid
- Rust does not protect the iron underneath

### Why Aluminium Doesn't Rust
- Aluminium reacts with oxygen
- Forms a thin, **impervious** layer of aluminium oxide
- This layer protects the metal underneath from further corrosion

### Methods of Preventing Rusting

#### 1. Barrier Methods
These prevent oxygen and water from reaching the iron surface.

| Method | Description | Uses |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Painting | Covers surface with paint | Cars, bridges, ships |
| Oiling/Greasing | Applies oil or grease to surface | Tools, bicycle chains |
| Plastic coating | Covers with plastic layer | Garden furniture, wire racks |
| Galvanizing | Coating with zinc | Dustbins, lamp posts |
| Tin plating | Coating with tin | Food cans |

#### 2. Sacrificial Protection

**Principle:** A more reactive metal is attached to or coated on the iron. The more reactive metal corrodes instead of the iron.

| Method | How it Works | Uses |
|--------|--------------|------|
| Galvanizing | Zinc coating corrodes sacrificially | Roofs, buckets, fences |
| Sacrificial anodes | Magnesium or zinc blocks attached | Ship hulls, underground pipes, oil rigs |

**Why it works:**
- Zinc/magnesium is more reactive than iron
- These metals lose electrons more readily
- They corrode instead of the iron
- Even if the coating is scratched, protection continues

#### 3. Alloying

- Iron is mixed with other metals (chromium, nickel) to make stainless steel
- Stainless steel does not rust
- Used for cutlery, surgical instruments, chemical plants

### Comparing Protection Methods

| Method | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| Painting | Cheap, decorative, easy to apply | Needs regular repainting, can chip |
| Oiling/Greasing | Good for moving parts, easy to apply | Needs regular reapplication, messy |
| Galvanizing | Long-lasting, self-healing if scratched | More expensive than painting |
| Sacrificial anodes | Very effective, no coating needed | Anodes need replacing periodically |
| Stainless steel | No additional protection needed | Expensive |

## 8. Environmental and Economic Considerations

### Recycling Metals

**Advantages:**
- Conserves natural resources (ores are finite)
- Uses less energy than extracting from ores
- Reduces waste sent to landfill
- Reduces environmental impact of mining

**Comparison of Energy Use:**
| Metal | Energy for Extraction | Energy for Recycling |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Aluminium | Very high (electrolysis) | ~5% of extraction energy |
| Iron/Steel | High (blast furnace) | ~25% of extraction energy |
| Copper | Moderate to high | ~15% of extraction energy |

### Environmental Impact of Metal Extraction

| Issue | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mining | Destroys habitats, produces large amounts of waste rock |
| Air pollution | Blast furnaces produce CO₂, SO₂ |
| Water pollution | Mine drainage can contaminate water sources |
| Energy consumption | High energy demand contributes to carbon emissions |

### Sustainable Metal Use

- Recycling programs
- Using alternative materials where possible
- Improving extraction efficiency
- Restoring mined land

## 9. Common Exam Mistakes

### 1. Confusing Reduction with Oxidation
❌ "In the blast furnace, iron oxide is oxidized to iron"
✓ "In the blast furnace, iron oxide is reduced to iron"

**Remember:** Reduction = removal of oxygen; Iron OXIDE → IRON

### 2. Wrong Reducing Agent in Blast Furnace
❌ "Carbon reduces iron oxide"
✓ "Carbon MONOXIDE reduces iron oxide"

**Remember:** C → CO₂ → CO; CO is the reducing agent

### 3. Forgetting Cryolite
❌ "Aluminium oxide is electrolysed at 2050°C"
✓ "Aluminium oxide is dissolved in cryolite and electrolysed at ~950°C"

### 4. Electrode Confusion in Aluminium Extraction
❌ "Aluminium forms at the anode"
✓ "Aluminium forms at the cathode"

**Remember:** 
- Cathode = negative electrode = where reduction occurs
- Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (gain electrons = reduction = cathode)

### 5. Phytomining vs Bioleaching
❌ "In phytomining, bacteria extract copper"
✓ "In bioleaching, bacteria extract copper; phytomining uses plants"

### 6. Slag Formation
❌ "Limestone removes carbon impurities"
✓ "Limestone removes acidic impurities like silica (SiO₂)"

### 7. Why Alloys are Harder
❌ "Alloys are harder because they contain different metals"
✓ "Alloys are harder because the different-sized atoms disrupt the regular structure, preventing layers from sliding"

### 8. Sacrificial Protection
❌ "Zinc protects iron by forming a barrier"
✓ "Zinc protects iron sacrificially - it corrodes instead of the iron"

## 10. Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Ore | A rock containing enough metal compound to make extraction economically viable |
| Native metal | A metal found uncombined in the Earth's crust |
| Electrolysis | Decomposition of a compound using electricity |
| Reduction | Loss of oxygen or gain of electrons |
| Oxidation | Gain of oxygen or loss of electrons |
| Alloy | A mixture of a metal with one or more other elements |
| Corrosion | Gradual destruction of a metal by chemical reaction with its environment |
| Rusting | Corrosion of iron in the presence of oxygen and water |
| Galvanizing | Coating iron with zinc to prevent rusting |
| Sacrificial protection | Using a more reactive metal to protect iron from corrosion |
| Slag | Waste material formed when limestone reacts with acidic impurities in the blast furnace |
| Smelting | Heating an ore with a reducing agent to extract the metal |

## 11. Equations Summary

### Blast Furnace
```
C + O₂ → CO₂              (combustion)
CO₂ + C → 2CO             (formation of reducing agent)
Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂  (reduction of iron ore)
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂         (limestone decomposition)
CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃       (slag formation)
```

### Aluminium Extraction
```
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al           (cathode - reduction)
2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻           (anode - oxidation)
C + O₂ → CO₂              (anode wear)
```

### Rusting
```
4Fe + 3O₂ + 2xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O
```
