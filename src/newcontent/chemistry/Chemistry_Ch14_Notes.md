## 1. Introduction to the Reactivity Series

### Definition
The **reactivity series** is a list of metals arranged in order of their reactivity, from the most reactive to the least reactive. This order determines how metals behave in chemical reactions and helps predict the outcomes of reactions involving metals.

### The Reactivity Series (Most to Least Reactive)

```
MOST REACTIVE
    Potassium (K)
    Sodium (Na)
    Lithium (Li)
    Calcium (Ca)
    Magnesium (Mg)
    Aluminium (Al)
    (Carbon) - reference point
    Zinc (Zn)
    Iron (Fe)
    Tin (Sn)
    Lead (Pb)
    (Hydrogen) - reference point
    Copper (Cu)
    Silver (Ag)
    Gold (Au)
    Platinum (Pt)
LEAST REACTIVE
```

## 2. Reactions of Metals with Water

### General Pattern
- **More reactive metals** react vigorously with cold water
- **Less reactive metals** react slowly or not at all with cold water
- Products: Metal hydroxide + Hydrogen gas

### General Equation
```
Metal + Water → Metal Hydroxide + Hydrogen
```

### Specific Reactions

#### Potassium, Sodium, Lithium (Very Reactive)
| Metal | Reaction with Cold Water | Observations |
|-------|-------------------------|--------------|
| Potassium | 2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂ | Violent explosion, lilac flame, floats, melts into a ball |
| Sodium | 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ | Vigorous fizzing, orange flame, floats, melts into a ball |
| Lithium | 2Li + 2H₂O → 2LiOH + H₂ | Steady fizzing, floats, does not melt |

**Key Points:**
- These metals are stored under oil to prevent reaction with air/moisture
- They float because they are less dense than water
- Potassium and sodium melt due to the heat of reaction
- The metal hydroxides produced are alkaline solutions (turn litmus blue)

#### Calcium
```
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂
```
- Reacts steadily with cold water
- Bubbles of hydrogen gas
- Sinks (denser than water)
- Calcium hydroxide is slightly soluble (limewater)

#### Magnesium
```
Mg + 2H₂O → Mg(OH)₂ + H₂
```
- Very slow reaction with cold water
- Reacts more quickly with hot water
- Produces magnesium hydroxide (milk of magnesia)

#### Zinc, Iron, Lead
- **No reaction** with cold water
- React very slowly with steam (if at all)

## 3. Reactions of Metals with Steam

### General Equation
```
Metal + Steam → Metal Oxide + Hydrogen
```

**Important:** With steam, the product is a metal **oxide** (not hydroxide) because the temperature is higher.

### Specific Reactions

#### Magnesium + Steam
```
Mg(s) + H₂O(g) → MgO(s) + H₂(g)
```
- Burns with a bright white flame
- Produces white magnesium oxide
- Hydrogen gas produced (burns with a squeaky pop)

#### Zinc + Steam
```
Zn(s) + H₂O(g) → ZnO(s) + H₂(g)
```
- Reacts when heated strongly
- Produces yellow zinc oxide (white when cold)

#### Iron + Steam
```
3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) → Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g)
```
- Reacts when heated strongly
- Produces triiron tetroxide (black solid)

#### Copper and Below
- **No reaction** with steam
- Too unreactive

### Experimental Setup for Steam Reaction
1. Wet sand or mineral wool soaked in water at the end of a boiling tube
2. Metal sample in the middle of the tube
3. Heat the water source first to produce steam
4. Then heat the metal
5. Collect hydrogen gas over water or test with a lit splint

## 4. Reactions of Metals with Acids

### General Pattern
- Metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series react with dilute acids
- Metals below hydrogen do not react with dilute acids

### General Equations
```
Metal + Dilute Hydrochloric Acid → Metal Chloride + Hydrogen
Metal + Dilute Sulfuric Acid → Metal Sulfate + Hydrogen
```

### Specific Reactions

#### Magnesium + Hydrochloric Acid
```
Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
```
- Rapid fizzing
- Exothermic reaction

#### Magnesium + Sulfuric Acid
```
Mg(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
```

#### Zinc + Hydrochloric Acid
```
Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
```
- Moderate reaction rate

#### Iron + Hydrochloric Acid
```
Fe(s) + 2HCl(aq) → FeCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
```
- Slow reaction, bubbles gradually

#### Iron + Sulfuric Acid
```
Fe(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
```

#### Copper, Silver, Gold
- **No reaction** with dilute acids
- Below hydrogen in reactivity series

### Reaction Rate Pattern
The more reactive the metal, the **faster** the reaction with acid:
- Potassium, Sodium, Lithium: EXPLOSIVE (too dangerous to test!)
- Calcium: Very rapid
- Magnesium: Rapid
- Zinc: Moderate
- Iron: Slow
- Lead: Very slow
- Copper and below: No reaction

## 5. Displacement Reactions

### Definition
A **displacement reaction** occurs when a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its compound (salt solution or oxide).

### General Principle
A more reactive metal will displace a less reactive metal from its compound because the more reactive metal has a stronger tendency to lose electrons and form positive ions.

### General Equation
```
More Reactive Metal + Salt of Less Reactive Metal → 
    Salt of More Reactive Metal + Less Reactive Metal
```

### Examples

#### Iron + Copper(II) Sulfate
```
Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
```
**Observations:**
- Blue copper(II) sulfate solution fades to pale green iron(II) sulfate
- Red-brown copper metal deposits on the iron
- Iron displaces copper because iron is more reactive

#### Zinc + Copper(II) Sulfate
```
Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
```
- Blue solution becomes colorless
- Red-brown copper deposits on zinc

#### Magnesium + Zinc Sulfate
```
Mg(s) + ZnSO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + Zn(s)
```
- Colorless solution remains colorless
- Grey zinc deposits on magnesium

#### No Reaction Example
```
Cu(s) + FeSO₄(aq) → NO REACTION
```
- Copper cannot displace iron because copper is less reactive than iron

### Summary Table
| Metal Added | Copper(II) Sulfate Solution | Iron(II) Sulfate Solution |
|-------------|----------------------------|---------------------------|
| Magnesium | ✓ Displaces copper | ✓ Displaces iron |
| Zinc | ✓ Displaces copper | ✓ Displaces iron |
| Iron | ✓ Displaces copper | ✗ No reaction |
| Copper | ✗ No reaction | ✗ No reaction |

## 6. Determining Position in the Reactivity Series

### Experimental Methods

#### Method 1: Reaction with Water
- Add metal to cold water
- Observe the rate of hydrogen production (bubbling)
- More reactive metals react more vigorously

#### Method 2: Reaction with Dilute Acid
- Add metal to dilute hydrochloric or sulfuric acid
- Measure the rate of hydrogen production
- Compare with known metals

#### Method 3: Displacement Reactions
- Add the unknown metal to solutions of salts of known metals
- Or add known metals to solutions containing the unknown metal ions
- Observe whether displacement occurs

### Example: Determining the Position of Metal X

**Experiments:**
1. Metal X + water → slow fizzing
2. Metal X + HCl → rapid bubbling
3. Metal X + CuSO₄ → blue color fades, copper deposited
4. Metal X + FeSO₄ → no reaction

**Conclusion:**
- Reacts with water (above lead)
- Reacts with acid (above hydrogen)
- Displaces copper (above copper)
- Does NOT displace iron (below iron)

**Position:** Metal X is between magnesium and iron, but above copper and below iron. Most likely **zinc**.

## 7. The Thermit Reaction

### Definition
The **Thermit reaction** is a highly exothermic displacement reaction between aluminium and iron(III) oxide, used to weld railway tracks.

### Equation
```
2Al(s) + Fe₂O₃(s) → Al₂O₃(s) + 2Fe(l)
```

### Why It Works
- Aluminium is more reactive than iron
- Aluminium displaces iron from iron(III) oxide
- The reaction releases a large amount of heat energy

### Observations
- Mixture is ignited using a magnesium ribbon fuse
- Very bright, intense white light
- Temperatures reach around 2500°C
- Molten iron is produced

### Applications
1. **Welding railway tracks:** The molten iron fills the gap between rail ends
2. **Thermite welding:** Used for joining heavy steel sections
3. **Metal extraction:** Can extract chromium, manganese from their oxides

### Why Use Aluminium?
- Aluminium is more reactive than iron (and many other metals)
- Forms aluminium oxide which is very stable
- Reaction is highly exothermic
- Aluminium oxide forms a protective layer, making aluminium safe to handle

## 8. Using the Reactivity Series for Predictions

### Predicting Reactions with Water
- Metals above calcium: React violently with cold water
- Calcium: Reacts steadily with cold water
- Magnesium: Reacts slowly with cold water, faster with hot water/steam
- Zinc, Iron: Only react with steam (when heated strongly)
- Metals below iron: No reaction with water or steam

### Predicting Reactions with Acids
- Metals above hydrogen: React with dilute acids
- Metals below hydrogen: No reaction with dilute acids

### Predicting Displacement Reactions
A metal will displace another metal from its compound ONLY IF:
- The metal is higher (more reactive) in the reactivity series
- The other metal is lower (less reactive)

### Predicting Extraction Methods
- **Very reactive metals (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al):** Extracted by electrolysis
- **Moderately reactive metals (Zn, Fe):** Extracted by reduction with carbon
- **Unreactive metals (Cu, Ag, Au):** Found native or extracted by heating

### Predicting Corrosion
- More reactive metals oxidize more readily
- Iron (steel) corrodes (rusts) in presence of water and oxygen
- Aluminium forms a protective oxide layer
- Gold and platinum do not corrode (unreactive)

## 9. Oxidation and Reduction (Higher Tier)

### Definitions
- **Oxidation:** Loss of electrons (gain of oxygen)
- **Reduction:** Gain of electrons (loss of oxygen)
- **Redox reaction:** A reaction involving both oxidation and reduction

### In Displacement Reactions

**Example:** Iron + Copper(II) Sulfate
```
Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu

Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻  (oxidation - iron loses electrons)
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu  (reduction - copper gains electrons)
```

- Iron is **oxidized** (loses electrons, oxidation number increases)
- Copper ions are **reduced** (gain electrons, oxidation number decreases)
- Iron acts as the **reducing agent**
- Copper ions act as the **oxidizing agent**

## 10. Common Exam Mistakes

### 1. Confusing Metal Hydroxide and Metal Oxide
❌ "Magnesium reacts with steam to produce magnesium hydroxide"
✓ "Magnesium reacts with steam to produce magnesium oxide"

**Remember:** With cold water → hydroxide; with steam → oxide

### 2. Incorrect Products for Acid Reactions
❌ "Magnesium + sulfuric acid → magnesium chloride + hydrogen"
✓ "Magnesium + sulfuric acid → magnesium sulfate + hydrogen"

**Remember:** The anion from the acid appears in the product (chloride from HCl, sulfate from H₂SO₄)

### 3. Forgetting State Symbols
❌ "Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄ + Cu"
✓ "Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)"

### 4. Displacement Reaction Direction
❌ "Copper displaces iron from iron sulfate"
✓ "Iron displaces copper from copper sulfate"

**Remember:** The MORE reactive metal displaces the LESS reactive metal

### 5. Explaining "Why" in Displacement
❌ "Iron displaces copper because iron is higher in the reactivity series"
✓ "Iron displaces copper because iron is more reactive than copper"

### 6. Thermit Reaction
❌ "The Thermit reaction is an oxidation reaction"
✓ "The Thermit reaction is a displacement reaction (and also a redox reaction)"

### 7. Missing Observations
Always include:
- Color changes (e.g., blue to pale green)
- Gas production (effervescence/bubbling)
- Solid formation (metal deposited)
- Temperature changes (exothermic/endothermic)

### 8. Word Equations vs Symbol Equations
- Learn both forms
- Balance symbol equations
- Include state symbols when asked
