## 1. Introduction to Online Information

The internet has transformed information access from a privilege to a ubiquitous resource. With billions of websites, countless databases, and real-time news streams, information is available on virtually any topic at any time. However, this abundance brings challenges: not all online information is accurate, reliable, or appropriate. Learning to find, evaluate, and use online information effectively has become an essential skill for education, work, and everyday life.

This chapter explores how to effectively search for information online, critically evaluate sources, and use information ethically. Understanding these skills is crucial for academic success, professional competence, and informed citizenship in the digital age.

## 2. Searching for Information Online

### 2.1 Search Engines

Search engines are software systems designed to find information on the World Wide Web.

**How Search Engines Work:**

**1. Crawling:**
Search engines use automated programs called "bots" or "spiders" to systematically browse the web, following links from page to page to discover content.

**2. Indexing:**
Discovered pages are indexed—analysed and stored in massive databases. The index includes keywords, content, metadata, and links.

**3. Ranking:**
When users search, algorithms determine which results to show. Ranking factors include:
- Relevance to search terms
- Quality and authority of the website
- Number and quality of links to the page
- User engagement metrics
- Freshness of content
- Location and personalisation factors

**Major Search Engines:**
- **Google:** Dominant global search engine (~90% market share)
- **Bing:** Microsoft's search engine
- **DuckDuckGo:** Privacy-focused, no tracking
- **Yahoo:** Combined with Bing results

### 2.2 Effective Search Techniques

**Basic Search Operators:**

| Operator | Function | Example |
|----------|----------|---------|
| "quotes" | Exact phrase match | "climate change effects" |
| AND | Both terms required | cats AND dogs |
| OR | Either term accepted | football OR soccer |
| NOT | Excludes term | apple NOT fruit |
| * | Wildcard | comput* (computer, computing, etc.) |
| site: | Search specific website | site:bbc.com news |

**Advanced Search Techniques:**

**Boolean Operators:**
- AND: Narrows search (both terms must appear)
- OR: Broadens search (either term can appear)
- NOT: Excludes unwanted results

**Phrase Searching:**
Using quotation marks finds exact phrases:
- `"global warming"` finds only pages with this exact phrase
- Without quotes, pages with both words anywhere appear

**Wildcards:**
The asterisk (*) replaces unknown words:
- `"to be or * to be"` finds the Shakespeare quote
- `comput*` finds computer, computing, computational

**Domain Filtering:**
Limit to specific domains:
- `site:.edu` - educational institutions
- `site:.gov` - government websites
- `site:.org` - organisations

**Date Filtering:**
Most search engines allow filtering by:
- Past hour, day, week, month, year
- Custom date ranges

### 2.3 Types of Search Results

**Organic Results:**
Natural results based on relevance to search terms, determined by algorithms rather than payment.

**Paid Results (Advertisements):**
Sponsored results appearing at the top of search results, labelled as "Ad" or "Sponsored." Advertisers pay when users click these links.

**Featured Snippets:**
Highlighted boxes at the top of results showing direct answers to queries, extracted from websites.

**Knowledge Panels:**
Information boxes appearing for entities like people, places, or organisations, compiled from various sources.

**Local Results:**
Map-based results for location-specific searches, showing nearby businesses with ratings and reviews.

### 2.4 Beyond Search Engines

**Specialised Information Sources:**

**Online Encyclopaedias:**
- **Wikipedia:** Collaborative encyclopaedia with user-edited content
- **Britannica Online:** Expert-written, professionally edited
- **Scholarpedia:** Peer-reviewed articles

**Academic Databases:**

## 3. Evaluating Online Information

### 3.1 Why Evaluation Matters

Anyone can publish online, regardless of expertise or accuracy. Unlike traditional publishing with editorial review, online content ranges from authoritative research to deliberate misinformation. Critical evaluation is essential to distinguish reliable information from unreliable or misleading content.

**Risks of Unevaluated Information:**
- Making decisions based on false information
- Spreading misinformation to others
- Academic penalties for using unreliable sources
- Health risks from medical misinformation
- Financial losses from scams

### 3.2 The CRAAP Test

A systematic approach to evaluating sources:

**C - Currency:**
- When was the information published or posted?
- When was it last updated?
- Is the information outdated for your purpose?
- Do links still work?

**R - Relevance:**
- Does the information answer your question?
- Who is the intended audience?
- Is the information at an appropriate level?

**A - Authority:**
- Who is the author/publisher?
- What are their credentials or qualifications?
- Is contact information provided?
- What does the URL reveal (.edu, .gov, .com)?

**A - Accuracy:**
- Is the information supported by evidence?
- Has it been reviewed or refereed?
- Can you verify the information elsewhere?
- Are there spelling or grammatical errors?

**P - Purpose:**
- Why does this information exist? (inform, teach, sell, entertain, persuade?)
- Is the purpose clear?
- Is the information fact, opinion, or propaganda?
- Are there potential biases?

### 3.3 Domain Name Analysis

URL domains can indicate source type:

| Domain | Type | Typical Reliability |
|--------|------|---------------------|
| .edu | Educational institution | Generally reliable for academic content |
| .gov | Government | Official information, generally reliable |
| .org | Organisation | Varies; check the organisation |
| .com | Commercial | May have commercial bias |
| .net | Network | Varies widely |
| .co.uk | UK commercial | Similar to .com |
| .ac.uk | UK academic | Generally reliable |

**Warning:** Domain extensions can be misleading. Anyone can register .org or .com domains.

### 3.4 Identifying Misinformation

**Types of False Information:**

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| Misinformation | False information shared without intent to harm | Sharing incorrect health advice thinking it's true |
| Disinformation | Deliberately created false information | Fake news stories designed to mislead |
| Malinformation | True information shared to cause harm | Leaking private information maliciously |

**Signs of Unreliable Sources:**
- Sensational or emotional headlines
- No author or credentials listed
- No citations or references
- Outdated information
- Extreme bias or one-sided presentation
- Spelling and grammar errors
- No contact information
- Only found on one website
- Contradicts established reliable sources

**Clickbait:**
Headlines designed to attract clicks regardless of content quality:
- "You won't believe what happened..."
- "Doctors hate this one trick..."
- Shocking claims that seem too extreme

**Deepfakes and AI-Generated Content:**
- AI can create realistic fake images and videos
- Voice synthesis can mimic real people
- Text generators can create fake articles
- Verify through multiple reliable sources

### 3.5 Fact-Checking

**Fact-Checking Websites:**
- **Snopes:** Original fact-checking site
- **Full Fact:** UK-based independent fact-checker
- **Politifact:** US political fact-checking
- **BBC Reality Check:** BBC's fact-checking service

**Verification Techniques:**
- Cross-reference with multiple reliable sources
- Check original sources for claims
- Look for official statements
- Verify images using reverse image search
- Check when accounts/content were created

## 4. Using Information Ethically

### 4.1 Copyright and Fair Use

**Copyright Basics:**
- Automatically protects original creative works
- Includes text, images, music, video, software
- Owner has exclusive rights to copy, distribute, adapt

**Fair Use (UK: Fair Dealing):**
Limited use of copyrighted material without permission for:
- Criticism and review
- News reporting
- Education and research
- Parody and satire

**Fair Use Factors:**
- Purpose of use (commercial vs educational)
- Nature of copyrighted work
- Amount used
- Effect on market value

### 4.2 Plagiarism

**What is Plagiarism?**
Using others' work without proper attribution:
- Copying text without quotation marks and citation
- Paraphrasing without credit
- Using ideas without attribution
- Submitting others' work as your own
- Self-plagiarism (resubmitting your own previous work)

**Avoiding Plagiarism:**
- Always cite sources
- Use quotation marks for direct quotes
- Paraphrase properly with attribution
- Keep track of sources during research
- Use plagiarism checkers before submission

### 4.3 Citing Sources

**Why Cite?**
- Acknowledge others' work
- Allow readers to find sources
- Demonstrate research breadth
- Avoid plagiarism
- Build credibility

**Common Citation Styles:**
- **Harvard:** Author-date format
- **APA:** American Psychological Association
- **MLA:** Modern Language Association
- **Chicago:** Notes and bibliography

**Citation Elements:**
- Author name(s)
- Title of work
- Publication date
- Publisher/website
- URL (for online sources)
- Date accessed (for online sources)

### 4.4 Creative Commons

Creative Commons licences allow creators to share work with specified permissions.

**Licence Types:**

| Symbol | Meaning | Permission |
|--------|---------|------------|
| BY | Attribution | Must credit creator |
| SA | ShareAlike | Same licence for derivatives |
| NC | NonCommercial | No commercial use |
| ND | NoDerivatives | No modifications allowed |

**Common Combinations:**
- CC BY: Use freely with attribution
- CC BY-SA: Use with attribution, share alike
- CC BY-NC: Non-commercial use with attribution
- CC BY-NC-ND: Most restrictive

## 5. Common Exam Mistakes and Key Points

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Assuming All Online Information is True:** Always evaluate sources critically.

2. **Using Only One Source:** Cross-reference information across multiple sources.

3. **Ignoring Domain Extensions:** .edu and .gov generally more reliable than .com.

4. **Forgetting to Cite:** All borrowed ideas must be attributed.

5. **Relying on Wikipedia Alone:** Use as starting point, verify elsewhere.

### Key Points for Exams

- Search engines crawl, index, and rank web pages
- Use search operators for more effective searches
- Evaluate sources using CRAAP or similar frameworks
- Domain extensions can indicate reliability
- Misinformation spreads easily; verify before sharing
- Always cite sources to avoid plagiarism
- Creative Commons allows specified sharing permissions
- Cross-reference information across multiple sources
