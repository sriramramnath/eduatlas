## SECTION A: Multiple Choice Questions

### Question 1
What do search engine "bots" or "spiders" do?

A) Block unwanted websites from results  
B) Systematically browse the web to discover content  
C) Create website content  
D) Delete outdated websites  

**Correct Answer: B) Systematically browse the web to discover content**

**Explanation:** Search engine bots (also called spiders or crawlers) systematically browse the web, following links from page to page to discover and collect content for indexing.

---

### Question 2
What does using quotation marks in a search query do?

A) Searches for synonyms  
B) Finds an exact phrase match  
C) Excludes certain words  
D) Limits to news articles  

**Correct Answer: B) Finds an exact phrase match**

**Explanation:** Quotation marks around a phrase tell the search engine to find that exact phrase in that exact word order. For example, "climate change" finds only pages with those words together.

---

### Question 3
In the CRAAP test, what does the "A" stand for?

A) Accuracy  
B) Accessibility  
C) Availability  
D) Authority  

**Correct Answer: D) Authority** (or Accuracy—both are part of CRAAP)

**Explanation:** The CRAAP test stands for Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose. Authority examines who created the information and their credentials; Accuracy examines correctness and verification.

---

### Question 4
Which domain extension typically indicates a UK educational institution?

A) .edu  
B) .ac.uk  
C) .school.uk  
D) .education  

**Correct Answer: B) .ac.uk**

**Explanation:** .ac.uk is the domain extension for UK academic and educational institutions. .edu is primarily used by US educational institutions. UK schools typically use .sch.uk.

---

### Question 5
What is the difference between misinformation and disinformation?

A) There is no difference  
B) Misinformation is deliberately false; disinformation is accidentally false  
C) Misinformation is accidentally false; disinformation is deliberately false  
D) Both are always true  

**Correct Answer: C) Misinformation is accidentally false; disinformation is deliberately false**

**Explanation:** Misinformation is false information shared without intent to harm (shared by mistake). Disinformation is deliberately created and spread false information intended to deceive.

---

### Question 6
What is plagiarism?

A) Properly citing sources in your work  
B) Using others' work without proper attribution  
C) Creating original content  
D) Paraphrasing with credit  

**Correct Answer: B) Using others' work without proper attribution**

**Explanation:** Plagiarism is using someone else's work, ideas, or words without giving them proper credit. This includes copying text, paraphrasing without attribution, or submitting others' work as your own.

---

### Question 7
What does the Creative Commons "BY" licence require?

A) Non-commercial use only  
B) No modifications allowed  
C) Attribution to the creator  
D) Sharing under same licence  

**Correct Answer: C) Attribution to the creator**

**Explanation:** The Creative Commons "BY" (Attribution) licence requires users to give appropriate credit to the original creator. It's the most permissive CC licence, allowing use with attribution.

---

### Question 8
What does the search operator "site:bbc.com" do?

A) Searches all news websites  
B) Finds websites similar to BBC  
C) Limits search results to the BBC website  
D) Blocks BBC from results  

**Correct Answer: C) Limits search results to the BBC website**

**Explanation:** The "site:" operator restricts search results to a specific website or domain. "site:bbc.com" will only show results from pages on the BBC website.

---

### Question 9
Which of these is a sign that an online source might be unreliable?

A) The author's credentials are clearly stated  
B) The article includes citations to research  
C) The headline uses extreme emotional language  
D) The article was published recently  

**Correct Answer: C) The headline uses extreme emotional language**

**Explanation:** Sensational or emotionally charged headlines ("SHOCKING truth they don't want you to know!") are often signs of clickbait or unreliable sources. Reliable sources typically use neutral, informative headlines.

---

### Question 10
What is the purpose of fact-checking websites like Snopes?

A) To create fake news  
B) To verify the accuracy of claims and stories  
C) To sell subscriptions  
D) To promote certain political views  

**Correct Answer: B) To verify the accuracy of claims and stories**

**Explanation:** Fact-checking websites like Snopes, Full Fact, and Politifact investigate claims, rumours, and viral stories to determine their accuracy and provide evidence-based conclusions.

---

### Question 11
What does "organic results" mean in search engine results?

A) Results about farming and nature  
B) Non-paid results based on relevance  
C) Results from government websites  
D) Paid advertisements  

**Correct Answer: B) Non-paid results based on relevance**

**Explanation:** Organic results are the natural search results that appear based on their relevance to the search terms, as determined by the search engine's algorithm. They are not paid advertisements.

---

### Question 12
When citing an online source, which element should you include?

A) Only the website name  
B) The weather when you accessed it  
C) The date you accessed the website  
D) The website owner's home address  

**Correct Answer: C) The date you accessed the website**

**Explanation:** When citing online sources, include the date accessed because web content can change or be removed. This helps readers find the version you used.

## SECTION B: True/False Questions

### Statement 1
**All information found on .edu websites is completely reliable.**

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** While .edu domains are restricted to educational institutions and generally more reliable, the content can still include personal opinions, outdated information, or student work that hasn't been peer-reviewed. Always evaluate content critically.

---

### Statement 2
**Using quotation marks around a search phrase will narrow your search results.**

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Quotation marks narrow search results by requiring the exact phrase in that specific order. Without quotes, search engines return pages containing the words in any order, resulting in more (but less precise) results.

---

### Statement 3
**Wikipedia is a reliable primary source for academic research.**

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** Wikipedia is useful as a starting point but should not be used as a primary source for academic research. Anyone can edit it, and information may be inaccurate. Use Wikipedia to find leads, then verify information in reliable sources.

---

### Statement 4
**Cross-referencing information across multiple sources helps verify accuracy.**

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Cross-referencing involves checking the same information across multiple independent, reliable sources. If multiple credible sources report the same facts, the information is more likely to be accurate.

---

### Statement 5
**Paraphrasing someone else's ideas without citation is acceptable.**

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** Paraphrasing without attribution is still plagiarism. Even if you reword the information in your own language, the ideas came from someone else and must be credited with a citation.

---

### Statement 6
**A website with spelling and grammar errors is likely to be less reliable.**

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Professional, authoritative sources typically have editorial standards that catch errors. Frequent spelling and grammar mistakes suggest the content hasn't been professionally reviewed, which may indicate unreliability.

---

### Statement 7
**Creative Commons licences allow anyone to use the work however they want.**

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** Creative Commons licences specify particular conditions for use. Each licence has different requirements (attribution, non-commercial use, no derivatives, etc.). Users must follow the specific licence terms.

---

### Statement 8
**The date an article was published is important when evaluating its relevance.**

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Currency matters because information can become outdated, especially in fields like technology, medicine, and current affairs. The publication date helps determine if information is still relevant and accurate.

---

### Statement 9
**Paid search results are always more relevant than organic results.**

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** Paid results appear because advertisers paid for placement, not because they're most relevant to your search. Organic results are ranked by relevance to your search terms. Paid results should be viewed with awareness of commercial intent.

---

### Statement 10
**You should evaluate the purpose of a website when assessing reliability.**

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Understanding why content was created—to inform, sell, persuade, entertain—helps assess its reliability. Content designed to sell products may present biased information, while educational or news content should aim for accuracy.

---

### Statement 11
**Clickbait headlines typically provide accurate, useful information.**

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** Clickbait headlines are designed to attract clicks rather than accurately represent content. They often exaggerate, sensationalise, or mislead. The actual content rarely lives up to the headline's promises.

---

### Statement 12
**Reverse image search can help verify if an image is authentic.**

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Reverse image search (uploading an image to find where it appears online) can reveal an image's original source, when it first appeared, and whether it has been manipulated or used out of context.

## SECTION C: Short Answer Questions

### Question 1 (1 mark)
**State one way to narrow search results using search operators.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
Any one of the following:
- Use quotation marks for exact phrase matching [1 mark]
- Use AND to require both terms [1 mark]
- Use site: to limit to specific website [1 mark]
- Use NOT to exclude unwanted terms [1 mark]
- Use filetype: to find specific document types [1 mark]

### Question 2 (2 marks)
**Explain two factors from the CRAAP test for evaluating online sources.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
Any two factors:

**Currency:** When was it published/updated? Is it current enough? [1 mark]

**Relevance:** Does it answer your question? Is it appropriate for your needs? [1 mark]

**Authority:** Who created it? What are their credentials? [1 mark]

**Accuracy:** Is it supported by evidence? Can it be verified? [1 mark]

**Purpose:** Why was it created? Is there bias? [1 mark]

---

### Question 3 (2 marks)
**Describe two warning signs that an online source might be unreliable.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
Any two signs:
- No author or credentials listed [1 mark]
- No citations or evidence for claims [1 mark]
- Sensational or emotional headlines [1 mark]
- Extreme bias or one-sided presentation [1 mark]
- Spelling and grammar errors [1 mark]
- Outdated information [1 mark]
- Cannot verify information elsewhere [1 mark]

---

### Question 4 (3 marks)
**Explain three ways to avoid plagiarism when using online information.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
Any three methods:

1. **Always cite sources** — Include author, title, date, URL for all borrowed ideas [1 mark]

2. **Use quotation marks** — Put direct quotes in quotation marks with citation [1 mark]

3. **Paraphrase properly** — Rewrite in own words but still cite original source [1 mark]

4. **Track sources** — Keep records of all sources during research [1 mark]

5. **Use plagiarism checkers** — Check work before submission [1 mark]

---

### Question 5 (3 marks)
**Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using Wikipedia for research.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
**Advantages:**
- Good starting point for research [1 mark]
- Links to primary sources at bottom of articles [1 mark]
- Covers wide range of topics [1 mark]
- Regularly updated [1 mark]

**Disadvantages:**
- Anyone can edit, accuracy not guaranteed [1 mark]
- Not considered reliable academic source [1 mark]
- Information may change without notice [1 mark]
- Some articles may have bias or errors [1 mark]

*Award up to 3 marks for balanced discussion.*

---

### Question 6 (4 marks)
**Explain how to verify whether a news story found online is accurate.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
Any four methods:

1. **Cross-reference** — Check if multiple reliable news sources report the same story [1 mark]

2. **Check source reputation** — Is the website a known reliable news outlet? [1 mark]

3. **Look for original sources** — Find where the story originated [1 mark]

4. **Check fact-checking sites** — Use Snopes, Full Fact, or similar [1 mark]

5. **Verify author credentials** — Who wrote it? Are they a journalist? [1 mark]

6. **Check date** — Is it recent? Is it being shared out of context? [1 mark]

7. **Reverse image search** — If images are included, verify their authenticity [1 mark]

---

### Question 7 (4 marks)
**A student finds conflicting information on two websites about a historical event. Explain how they should determine which source to trust.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
**Evaluate authority:**
- Check author credentials on each site [1 mark]
- Determine if authors are historians or experts [1 mark]

**Check sources:**
- Which site provides citations and references? [1 mark]
- Are primary sources cited? [1 mark]

**Assess domain reliability:**
- Is one an educational (.edu/.ac.uk) or official source? [1 mark]
- Is one a personal blog or unknown website? [1 mark]

**Cross-reference:**
- Check reliable history sources (textbooks, encyclopaedias) [1 mark]
- Look for consensus among multiple authoritative sources [1 mark]

*Award up to 4 marks for comprehensive evaluation approach.*

---

### Question 8 (4 marks)
**Describe four elements that should be included when citing an online source.**

**Mark Scheme Answer:**
Any four elements:

1. **Author name(s)** — Who wrote the content [1 mark]

2. **Title of work** — Article or page title [1 mark]

3. **Website name** — Name of the website or publication [1 mark]

4. **Publication date** — When it was published or updated [1 mark]

5. **URL** — Full web address [1 mark]

6. **Date accessed** — When you viewed the content [1 mark]

7. **Publisher** — Organisation responsible for the site [1 mark]
