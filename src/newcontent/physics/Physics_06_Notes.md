## 6.1 Electrical Charges

### Types of Charge

| Charge | Symbol | Description |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Positive** | + | Deficiency of electrons |
| **Negative** | - | Excess of electrons |

### Charging by Friction

When two insulating materials are rubbed together:
- Electrons transfer from one material to the other
- One becomes positively charged (lost electrons)
- One becomes negatively charged (gained electrons)

### Common Charging Pairs

| Material Rubbed With | Becomes |
|---------------------|---------|
| Polythene rod + cloth | Polythene negative, cloth positive |
| Perspex/acetate rod + cloth | Perspex positive, cloth negative |
| Ebonite rod + fur | Ebonite negative, fur positive |

### Attraction and Repulsion

| Interaction | Result |
|-------------|--------|
| Positive + Positive | Repel |
| Negative + Negative | Repel |
| Positive + Negative | Attract |

**Rule:** Like charges repel, opposite charges attract.

## 6.2 Electric Current

### Definition

**Electric current** is the rate of flow of electric charge.

```
current = charge ÷ time

I = Q ÷ t

Where:
I = current (A)
Q = charge (C)
t = time (s)
```

### Units

| Quantity | Unit | Symbol |
|----------|------|--------|
| Current | Ampere (Amp) | A |
| Charge | Coulomb | C |
| Time | Second | s |

### Direction of Current

**Conventional current** flows from **positive to negative**.

**Electron flow** is from **negative to positive** (opposite to conventional current).

### Charge Carriers

| Material | Charge Carrier |
|----------|---------------|
| Metals | Electrons (negative) |
| Electrolytes | Ions (positive and negative) |
| Semiconductors | Electrons and holes |

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** Calculate the charge when 3 A flows for 10 s.
```
Q = I × t = 3 × 10 = 30 C
```

**Example 2:** Calculate the current when 60 C passes in 15 s.
```
I = Q ÷ t = 60 ÷ 15 = 4 A
```

## 6.3 AC and DC

### Direct Current (DC)

- Flows in **one direction only**
- Provided by batteries and cells
- Graph: horizontal line (constant value)

```
Current
  │
  ├──────────────────
  │
  └──────────────────→ Time
```

### Alternating Current (AC)

- Changes **direction periodically**
- Provided by mains electricity
- Graph: sine wave

```
Current
  │    ╭─╮         ╭─╮
  │   ╱   ╲       ╱   ╲
  │──╱─────╲─────╱─────╲──→ Time
  │         ╲───╱       ╲──
  │
```

### Mains Electricity (UK)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Voltage | 230 V |
| Frequency | 50 Hz |
| Type | AC |

### Frequency

**Frequency** is the number of complete cycles per second.

Unit: **Hertz (Hz)**

For mains electricity at 50 Hz:
- One complete cycle takes 1/50 = 0.02 s
- Direction changes 100 times per second (2 per cycle)

## 6.4 Voltage (Potential Difference)

### Definition

**Voltage** (potential difference) is the energy transferred per unit charge.

```
voltage = energy ÷ charge

V = E ÷ Q

Where:
V = voltage (V)
E = energy (J)
Q = charge (C)
```

### Alternative Definition

Voltage is the "push" that drives current around a circuit.

### Units

| Quantity | Unit | Symbol |
|----------|------|--------|
| Voltage | Volt | V |
| Energy | Joule | J |
| Charge | Coulomb | C |

### Worked Example

Calculate the energy transferred when 5 C of charge passes through a 12 V battery.

```
E = V × Q = 12 × 5 = 60 J
```

## 6.5 Mains Supply and Safety

### Three-Wire System

| Wire | Colour | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Live** | Brown | Carries AC at 230 V |
| **Neutral** | Blue | Completes circuit at 0 V |
| **Earth** | Green/Yellow | Safety wire at 0 V |

### The Live Wire

- Carries alternating voltage (±230 V relative to neutral)
- **Dangerous** - can cause electric shock
- Connected to fuse/circuit breaker

### The Neutral Wire

- At or near 0 V
- Completes the circuit
- Less dangerous than live

### The Earth Wire

- Safety wire at 0 V
- Connected to metal casing of appliances
- Provides safe path for current if fault occurs

### Fuses

**Function:** Protect circuit by melting if current is too high.

| Fuse Rating | Use |
|-------------|-----|
| 3 A | Low power appliances (lamps, TVs) |
| 5 A | Medium power appliances |
| 13 A | High power appliances (heaters, kettles) |

**How to choose a fuse:**
- Fuse rating should be slightly higher than normal operating current
- Next standard value above operating current

### Circuit Breakers

| Type | How it Works |
|------|--------------|
| **MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker)** | Electromagnetic switch trips when current too high |
| **RCD (Residual Current Device)** | Detects current imbalance (leakage to earth) |

**Advantages over fuses:**
- Can be reset (no need to replace)
- Faster response
- More sensitive

### Double Insulation

Some appliances have **no earth wire** because:
- They are made entirely of insulating materials (plastic)
- No metal parts that could become live
- Marked with double square symbol (⧈)

## 6.6 Electrical Power

### Definition

**Power** is the rate of energy transfer.

### Equations

```
Power = current × voltage

P = I × V

Where:
P = power (W)
I = current (A)
V = voltage (V)
```

### Alternative Forms

Using V = IR and P = IV:

```
P = I² × R
P = V² ÷ R
```

### Units

| Quantity | Unit |
|----------|------|
| Power | Watt (W) |
| Energy | Kilowatt-hour (kWh) for electricity bills |

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** Calculate the power of a 230 V kettle that draws 10 A.
```
P = I × V = 10 × 230 = 2300 W = 2.3 kW
```

**Example 2:** Calculate the current drawn by a 60 W lamp at 230 V.
```
I = P ÷ V = 60 ÷ 230 = 0.26 A
```

## 6.7 Calculating Electrical Energy

### Equation

```
energy = power × time

E = P × t

Where:
E = energy (J or kWh)
P = power (W or kW)
t = time (s or h)
```

### Units

| Power | Time | Energy |
|-------|------|--------|
| W | s | J (joules) |
| kW | h | kWh (kilowatt-hours) |

### Kilowatt-hours (kWh)

The **kilowatt-hour** is the unit of energy used on electricity bills.

```
1 kWh = 1 kW × 1 h = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3 600 000 J = 3.6 MJ
```

### Cost of Electricity

```
cost = energy (kWh) × price per kWh
```

### Worked Example

Calculate the cost of running a 2 kW heater for 3 hours at 15p per kWh.

```
Energy = 2 × 3 = 6 kWh
Cost = 6 × 15 = 90p
```

## 6.8 Domestic Wiring

### Ring Main Circuit

- Powers sockets around the house
- Wired in a ring (loop)
- Can carry more current than radial circuit
- Protected by 30 A or 32 A fuse/circuit breaker

### Lighting Circuit

- Powers ceiling lights
- Lower current rating (5 A or 6 A)
- Wired in parallel

### cooker Circuit

- High current circuit for electric cookers
- Separate circuit with higher rating (30-45 A)

### Switches and Fuses

| Component | Position | Reason |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Switch** | Live wire | Breaks circuit completely when off |
| **Fuse** | Live wire | Cuts off live supply if fault occurs |

## Exam Tips for Chapter 6

1. **AC vs DC** - Know the difference and examples
2. **Wire colours** - Live = brown, Neutral = blue, Earth = green/yellow
3. **Safety** - Know why earth wire and fuses are needed
4. **Units** - Use correct units (A, V, W, C, J, kWh)
5. **Fuse selection** - Next standard value above operating current
6. **Power calculations** - P = IV, can rearrange to find I or V

## Common Student Errors

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| Confusing AC and DC | AC changes direction, DC is one direction |
| Wrong wire colours | Learn: Brown = Live, Blue = Neutral, Green/Yellow = Earth |
| Using wrong units for energy | kWh for bills, J for scientific calculations |
| Forgetting earth wire purpose | Safety - provides path for fault current |
| Confusing charge and current | Current is flow rate, charge is quantity |
