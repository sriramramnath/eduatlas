## 1.1 Distance and Displacement

### Definitions

| Term | Definition | Symbol | Unit |
|------|------------|--------|------|
| **Distance** | The total length of the path travelled by an object | d or s | metres (m) |
| **Displacement** | The straight-line distance from start to finish, including direction | s | metres (m) |

### Key Differences

**Distance** is a **scalar quantity** - it has magnitude only.
- Example: Walking 5 m north then 5 m south = 10 m distance travelled

**Displacement** is a **vector quantity** - it has magnitude and direction.
- Example: Walking 5 m north then 5 m south = 0 m displacement (back at start)

### Common Exam Mistake
Students often confuse distance and displacement. Remember:
- Distance = total path length (always positive)
- Displacement = final position - initial position (can be positive, negative, or zero)

## 1.2 Speed and Velocity

### Definitions

| Term | Definition | Formula | Unit |
|------|------------|---------|------|
| **Speed** | Rate of change of distance | speed = distance ÷ time | m/s |
| **Velocity** | Rate of change of displacement | velocity = displacement ÷ time | m/s |

### Average Speed vs Instantaneous Speed

**Average speed** = total distance ÷ total time

**Instantaneous speed** = speed at a particular moment (found from speedometer or gradient of distance-time graph)

### Calculating Speed from Distance-Time Graphs

The **gradient** of a distance-time graph gives speed:

```
speed = gradient = Δdistance ÷ Δtime = rise ÷ run
```

### Velocity as a Vector

Velocity includes direction. For example:
- 10 m/s north is different from 10 m/s south
- If velocity changes from +10 m/s to -10 m/s, the object has reversed direction

## 1.3 Acceleration

### Definition

**Acceleration** is the rate of change of velocity.

```
acceleration = change in velocity ÷ time taken

a = (v - u) ÷ t

Where:
a = acceleration (m/s²)
v = final velocity (m/s)
u = initial velocity (m/s)
t = time (s)
```

### Units
- Acceleration: **metres per second squared (m/s²)**
- This means "metres per second, per second"

### Types of Acceleration

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Positive acceleration** | Speeding up in positive direction | Car accelerating forwards |
| **Negative acceleration (deceleration)** | Slowing down | Car braking |
| **Constant acceleration** | Velocity changes at steady rate | Free fall (ignoring air resistance) |

### The Equation of Motion

From the acceleration formula, we can derive:

```
v = u + at
```

This is one of the **suvat equations** (though only this one is required at IGCSE).

## 1.4 Distance-Time Graphs

### Key Features

| Feature | What it Shows |
|---------|---------------|
| **Gradient** | Speed |
| **Steeper gradient** | Faster speed |
| **Zero gradient (horizontal)** | Stationary (not moving) |
| **Curved line** | Changing speed (acceleration) |

### Interpreting Distance-Time Graphs

**Example Analysis:**

```
Distance (m)
    │
 50 ┤        ╭────── (constant speed)
    │       ╱
 30 ┤      ╱
    │     ╱
 10 ┤────╱ (constant speed)
    │
    0────┬────┬────┬────→ Time (s)
         0    5   10   15
```

- **0-5 s**: Straight line with positive gradient = constant speed
- **5-10 s**: Horizontal line = stationary (object stopped)
- **10-15 s**: Straight line with positive gradient = moving again at constant speed

### Calculating Speed from Graph

**Step 1:** Identify a straight-line section
**Step 2:** Calculate gradient = (change in distance) ÷ (change in time)

Example: Between 0-5 s, distance changes from 0 to 10 m
Speed = (10 - 0) ÷ (5 - 0) = 2 m/s

## 1.5 Velocity-Time Graphs

### Key Features

| Feature | What it Shows |
|---------|---------------|
| **Gradient** | Acceleration |
| **Area under graph** | Displacement (or distance if all positive) |
| **Horizontal line** | Constant velocity (zero acceleration) |
| **Line through origin** | Starting from rest |

### Interpreting Velocity-Time Graphs

**Example Analysis:**

```
Velocity (m/s)
    │
 20 ┤           ╭────── (constant velocity)
    │          ╱
 10 ┤         ╱
    │        ╱ (constant acceleration)
  0 ┤───────╱──────────→ Time (s)
    0       5   10   15
```

- **0-5 s**: Straight line with positive gradient = constant acceleration
- **5-15 s**: Horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration)

### Calculating Acceleration from Graph

Acceleration = gradient = Δvelocity ÷ Δtime

Example: Between 0-5 s, velocity changes from 0 to 20 m/s
Acceleration = (20 - 0) ÷ (5 - 0) = 4 m/s²

### Calculating Displacement from Graph

**Displacement = area under velocity-time graph**

For the example above:
- **0-5 s**: Area = ½ × base × height = ½ × 5 × 20 = 50 m
- **5-15 s**: Area = base × height = 10 × 20 = 200 m
- **Total displacement** = 50 + 200 = 250 m

### Common Exam Mistake
Students often forget that:
- Distance-time graph gradient = speed
- Velocity-time graph gradient = acceleration
- Velocity-time graph area = displacement

## 1.6 Motion Under Gravity

### Free Fall

When an object falls under gravity (ignoring air resistance):
- It accelerates at **g = 10 m/s²** (or 9.8 m/s² if specified)
- This acceleration is constant and directed downwards

### Key Values for Free Fall

| Quantity | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Acceleration due to gravity (g) | 10 m/s² (downwards) |
| Initial velocity (dropped from rest) | 0 m/s |

### Example Calculation

A ball is dropped from rest. Calculate its velocity after 3 seconds.

```
u = 0 m/s
a = g = 10 m/s²
t = 3 s
v = u + at
v = 0 + (10 × 3)
v = 30 m/s (downwards)
```

## 1.7 Terminal Velocity

### What is Terminal Velocity?

When an object falls through a fluid (air, water), it eventually reaches a **constant maximum velocity** called terminal velocity.

### Why Terminal Velocity Occurs

1. **Initially**: Object accelerates due to gravity (weight > air resistance)
2. **As speed increases**: Air resistance increases
3. **Eventually**: Air resistance = weight (forces balanced)
4. **Result**: No resultant force, so constant velocity (terminal velocity)

### Velocity-Time Graph for Terminal Velocity

```
Velocity
    │
    │              ╭────── (terminal velocity)
    │             ╱
    │            ╱
    │           ╱
    │          ╱
    │         ╱
    │        ╱
    │       ╱
    │      ╱
    │     ╱
    │    ╱
    │   ╱
    │  ╱
    │ ╱
    │╱
    └──────────────────→ Time
```

### Factors Affecting Terminal Velocity

| Factor | Effect on Terminal Velocity |
|--------|----------------------------|
| **Mass** | Greater mass → higher terminal velocity |
| **Surface area** | Larger area → lower terminal velocity |
| **Shape** | Streamlined shape → higher terminal velocity |
| **Fluid density** | Denser fluid → lower terminal velocity |

### Skydiver Example

A skydiver:
1. Jumps from plane → accelerates at ~10 m/s²
2. As speed increases, air resistance increases
3. Reaches terminal velocity (~50-60 m/s in free fall position)
4. Opens parachute → large surface area → air resistance increases dramatically
5. New, lower terminal velocity reached (~5 m/s)

## 1.8 Measuring Motion

### Methods for Measuring Speed

| Method | Description | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Stopwatch + ruler** | Measure time to travel known distance | Slow, predictable motion |
| **Light gates** | Beam broken by object, timer records time | Precise timing, fast motion |
| **Ticker timer** | Dots on tape at regular intervals (e.g., 50 Hz = 0.02 s) | Detailed motion analysis |
| **Video analysis** | Frame-by-frame position measurement | Complex motion |

### Ticker Timer Analysis

A ticker timer produces 50 dots per second (frequency = 50 Hz).
- Time interval between dots = 1/50 = 0.02 s

**Calculating speed:**
```
speed = distance between dots ÷ time interval
```

- **Evenly spaced dots** = constant speed
- **Increasing spacing** = acceleration
- **Decreasing spacing** = deceleration

## Exam Tips for Chapter 1

1. **Always check units** - convert to metres and seconds before calculating
2. **Read graph axes carefully** - distance-time vs velocity-time
3. **Show working** - method marks available even if final answer wrong
4. **State direction** for vector quantities (velocity, displacement, acceleration)
5. **Area under velocity-time graph** gives displacement, not distance (unless all positive)
6. **Gradient calculations** - use large triangles for accuracy

## Common Student Errors

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| Confusing distance and displacement | Distance = path length; Displacement = straight-line with direction |
| Using wrong graph gradient | Distance-time → speed; Velocity-time → acceleration |
| Forgetting area under v-t graph | Area = displacement |
| Not converting units | Always use m and s for calculations |
| Ignoring direction for vectors | Velocity and displacement need direction |
