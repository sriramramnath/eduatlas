## 18.1 Density

### Definition

**Density** is the mass per unit volume of a substance.

### Equation

```
density = mass ÷ volume

ρ = m ÷ V

Where:
ρ = density (kg/m³ or g/cm³)
m = mass (kg or g)
V = volume (m³ or cm³)
```

### Units

| Unit | Use |
|------|-----|
| kg/m³ | SI unit, for larger objects |
| g/cm³ | Common for solids and liquids |

### Conversion

```
1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³
```

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** Calculate the density of a 500 g object with volume 250 cm³.
```
ρ = m ÷ V = 500 ÷ 250 = 2 g/cm³
```

**Example 2:** Calculate the mass of 3 m³ of water (density = 1000 kg/m³).
```
m = ρ × V = 1000 × 3 = 3000 kg
```

### Typical Densities

| Substance | Density (kg/m³) | Density (g/cm³) |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Air | 1.2 | 0.0012 |
| Water | 1000 | 1.0 |
| Ice | 920 | 0.92 |
| Aluminum | 2700 | 2.7 |
| Iron | 7800 | 7.8 |
| Lead | 11300 | 11.3 |

### Floating and Sinking

| Comparison | Result |
|------------|--------|
| Object density < liquid density | Object floats |
| Object density > liquid density | Object sinks |
| Object density = liquid density | Object remains suspended |

**Ice floats on water** because ice (920 kg/m³) is less dense than water (1000 kg/m³).

## 18.2 Measuring Density

### Regular Solids

1. Measure mass using balance
2. Calculate volume from dimensions
3. Calculate density: ρ = m/V

### Irregular Solids

1. Measure mass using balance
2. Measure volume by displacement (submerge in water, measure volume change)
3. Calculate density: ρ = m/V

### Liquids

1. Measure mass of empty measuring cylinder
2. Add known volume of liquid
3. Measure mass of cylinder + liquid
4. Calculate liquid mass and density

## 18.3 Pressure

### Definition

**Pressure** is the force per unit area.

### Equation

```
pressure = force ÷ area

p = F ÷ A

Where:
p = pressure (Pa or N/m²)
F = force (N)
A = area (m²)
```

### Unit

The unit of pressure is the **pascal (Pa)**.

```
1 Pa = 1 N/m²
```

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** Calculate the pressure when a 500 N force acts on 2 m².
```
p = F ÷ A = 500 ÷ 2 = 250 Pa
```

**Example 2:** Calculate the force exerted by a pressure of 1000 Pa on 0.5 m².
```
F = p × A = 1000 × 0.5 = 500 N
```

---

## 18.4 Pressure in Liquids

### Equation

```
pressure = density × g × depth

p = ρ × g × h

Where:
p = pressure (Pa)
ρ = density (kg/m³)
g = 10 N/kg
h = depth (m)
```

### Key Points

- Pressure increases with depth
- Pressure acts equally in all directions
- Pressure depends only on depth and density, not container shape

### Worked Example

Calculate the pressure at 10 m depth in water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³).

```
p = ρ × g × h = 1000 × 10 × 10 = 100,000 Pa = 100 kPa
```

## 18.5 Atmospheric Pressure

### Definition

**Atmospheric pressure** is the pressure exerted by the weight of the atmosphere.

### Value

| Location | Pressure |
|----------|----------|
| Sea level | ~101,000 Pa (~101 kPa) |
| Mountain top | Lower |

### Effects

- Decreases with altitude
- Causes weather patterns
- Enables drinking through a straw
- Crushes empty can when air removed

## 18.6 Hydraulic Systems

### Principle

Pressure is transmitted equally throughout a liquid (Pascal's principle).

### Hydraulic Jack

```
    Small piston          Large piston
    Area = A₁             Area = A₂
    
    │    ↓ F₁             │    ↑ F₂
    │                     │
    ├──────────┐          ├──────────┐
    │░░░░░░░░░░│          │░░░░░░░░░░│
    │░░░░░░░░░░│══════════│░░░░░░░░░░│
    │░░░░░░░░░░│  Liquid   │░░░░░░░░░░│
    └──────────┘          └──────────┘
    
    p = F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂
    
    F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁)
```

### Force Multiplication

A small force on the small piston creates a larger force on the large piston.

```
F₂ = F₁ × (A₂ ÷ A₁)
```

### Worked Example

A hydraulic system has small piston area 0.01 m² and large piston area 0.5 m². A 100 N force is applied to the small piston. Calculate the force on the large piston.

```
F₂ = F₁ × (A₂ ÷ A₁)
F₂ = 100 × (0.5 ÷ 0.01)
F₂ = 100 × 50
F₂ = 5000 N
```

## 18.7 Applications

### Hydraulic Brakes

- Foot applies small force to brake pedal
- Hydraulic fluid transmits pressure
- Larger force applied to brake pads

### Hydraulic Press

- Small effort force creates large pressing force
- Used for crushing, shaping metal

### Syringes

- Pressure applied to liquid
- Liquid transmits pressure to move piston

3. **Liquid pressure** - p = ρgh

5. **Hydraulics** - Pressure same throughout, force varies with area
6. **Floating** - Less dense objects float

## Common Student Errors

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| Confusing mass and weight | Mass in kg, weight in N |
| Wrong units for density | Use kg/m³ or g/cm³ consistently |
| Forgetting g in liquid pressure | p = ρgh, include g = 10 |
| Wrong area in hydraulics | Area, not diameter or radius |
| Confusing pressure and force | Pressure is force/area |
