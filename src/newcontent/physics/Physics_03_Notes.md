## 3.1 Newton's First Law of Motion

### Statement

**Newton's First Law:** An object will remain at rest or move with constant velocity unless acted upon by a resultant force.

### Understanding the Law

| Situation | Resultant Force | Motion |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| Object at rest | Zero | Stays at rest |
| Object moving | Zero | Constant velocity (same speed and direction) |
| Object acted on by force | Non-zero | Acceleration |

### Inertia

**Inertia** is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion.
- Objects with more mass have more inertia
- Greater mass = harder to start moving or stop

### Everyday Examples

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Passenger thrown forward when car brakes | Body tends to continue moving (inertia) |
| Seat belts needed | Prevent passengers continuing forward motion |
| Dust falls when beating carpet | Carpet moves, dust stays (inertia) |
| Satellite in space | Continues moving without engine (no resultant force) |

## 3.2 Newton's Second Law of Motion

### Statement

**Newton's Second Law:** The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force and inversely proportional to its mass.

```
resultant force = mass × acceleration

F = m × a

Where:
F = resultant force (N)
m = mass (kg)
a = acceleration (m/s²)
```

### Rearranging the Equation

```
F = m × a
m = F ÷ a
a = F ÷ m
```

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** A 500 kg car accelerates at 2 m/s². What is the resultant force?
```
F = m × a = 500 × 2 = 1000 N
```

**Example 2:** A 60 kg skater experiences a 120 N force. What is her acceleration?
```
a = F ÷ m = 120 ÷ 60 = 2 m/s²
```

**Example 3:** What mass would accelerate at 5 m/s² with a 250 N force?
```
m = F ÷ a = 250 ÷ 5 = 50 kg
```

### Key Relationships

| If mass is constant | If force is constant |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| F ∝ a (double force = double acceleration) | a ∝ 1/m (double mass = half acceleration) |

## 3.3 Newton's Third Law of Motion

### Statement

**Newton's Third Law:** When two objects interact, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other.

### Characteristics of Action-Reaction Pairs

| Feature | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Equal magnitude** | Both forces have same size |
| **Opposite direction** | Forces act in opposite directions |
| **Same type** | Both are same type of force (e.g., both contact) |
| **Different objects** | Each force acts on a different object |
| **Simultaneous** | Both forces occur at the same time |

### Common Examples

| Situation | Action Force | Reaction Force |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Walking | Foot pushes backward on ground | Ground pushes forward on foot |
| Rocket launch | Rocket pushes gas downward | Gas pushes rocket upward |
| Swimming | Hands push water backward | Water pushes swimmer forward |
| Book on table | Book pushes down on table | Table pushes up on book |

### Common Misconception

Action and reaction forces do NOT cancel out because they act on **different objects**.

Example: When you push a wall, the wall pushes back with equal force. You don't move because other forces (friction with ground) act on you.

## 3.4 Resultant Force and Motion

### Finding Resultant Force

**Step 1:** Identify all forces acting on the object
**Step 2:** Determine direction of each force
**Step 3:** Add forces in same direction, subtract opposite forces
**Step 4:** Use F = ma to find acceleration

### Worked Example: Object on Slope

A 10 kg box is pulled up a slope with 80 N force. Friction is 20 N down the slope.

```
Resultant force up slope = 80 - 20 = 60 N
Using F = ma:
60 = 10 × a
a = 6 m/s² up the slope
```

### Worked Example: Horizontal Motion

A 500 kg car has driving force 2000 N and experiences 800 N air resistance.

```
Resultant force = 2000 - 800 = 1200 N
Using F = ma:
1200 = 500 × a
a = 2.4 m/s²
```

## 3.5 Force, Mass and Acceleration Experiments

### Investigating F = ma

**Method 1: Constant mass, varying force**
- Use a trolley on a runway
- Add masses to hanging weight (increasing force)
- Keep total mass constant by removing masses from trolley
- Measure acceleration using ticker timer or light gates
- Plot F against a - should be straight line through origin

**Method 2: Constant force, varying mass**
- Keep hanging mass constant (constant force)
- Add masses to trolley (increasing total mass)
- Measure acceleration
- Plot a against 1/m - should be straight line through origin

### Expected Results

**F vs a (constant m):**
- Straight line through origin
- Gradient = mass

**a vs 1/m (constant F):**
- Straight line through origin
- Gradient = force

## 3.6 Terminal Velocity Revisited

### Applying Newton's Laws

**Stage 1: Initial fall**
- Weight > Air resistance
- Resultant force downward
- Acceleration downward (Newton's Second Law)

**Stage 2: Speed increases**
- Air resistance increases with speed
- Resultant force decreases
- Acceleration decreases

**Stage 3: Terminal velocity**
- Weight = Air resistance
- Resultant force = 0
- Acceleration = 0 (constant velocity - Newton's First Law)

## 3.7 Stopping Distance

### Definition

**Stopping distance** = thinking distance + braking distance

### Thinking Distance

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Speed** | Higher speed = longer thinking distance |
| **Reaction time** | Slower reactions = longer thinking distance |
| **Tiredness/alcohol/drugs** | Increase reaction time |
| **Distractions** | Increase reaction time |

```
thinking distance = speed × reaction time
```

### Braking Distance

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Speed** | Higher speed = much longer braking distance (v² relationship) |
| **Mass** | Greater mass = longer braking distance |
| **Brake condition** | Worn brakes = longer braking distance |
| **Tyre condition** | Worn tyres = longer braking distance |
| **Road surface** | Wet/icy roads = longer braking distance |
| **Tyre pressure** | Incorrect pressure = longer braking distance |

### Typical Values (dry road, good tyres)

| Speed (mph) | Thinking Distance (m) | Braking Distance (m) | Total (m) |
|-------------|----------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| 20 | 6 | 6 | 12 |
| 30 | 9 | 14 | 23 |
| 40 | 12 | 24 | 36 |
| 50 | 15 | 38 | 53 |
| 60 | 18 | 55 | 73 |
| 70 | 21 | 75 | 96 |

### Why Speed Matters So Much

Kinetic energy = ½mv²

- Double speed → 4× kinetic energy → 4× braking distance
- Triple speed → 9× kinetic energy → 9× braking distance

## 3.8 Safety Features in Vehicles

### Crumple Zones

| Feature | How it Works |
|---------|--------------|
| **Crumple zones** | Collapse gradually, increasing stopping time, reducing force |
| **Seat belts** | Stretch slightly, increasing stopping time |
| **Air bags** | Increase stopping time and spread force over larger area |
| **Helmets** | Increase stopping time, reduce force on head |

### The Physics

From F = ma and a = (v - u) ÷ t:

```
F = m × (v - u) ÷ t
```

To reduce force:
- Increase stopping time (t)
- This is what safety features do

## Exam Tips for Chapter 3

1. **Always draw force diagrams** - helps visualize the problem
2. **Identify the direction of acceleration** - resultant force is in this direction
3. **Newton's Third Law pairs** - act on different objects, never cancel
4. **Stopping distance** - know factors affecting thinking and braking distances
5. **Show all working** - F = ma calculations need clear substitution
6. **Terminal velocity** - forces balanced, Newton's First Law applies

## Common Student Errors

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| Thinking action-reaction forces cancel | They act on different objects |
| Using weight instead of mass in F=ma | Mass in kg, weight in N |
| Confusing Newton's First and Second Law | First: F=0 means a=0; Second: F=ma |
| Forgetting direction matters | Forces are vectors |
| Not converting units | Use kg for mass, m/s for speed |
