## 4.1 What is Momentum?

### Definition

**Momentum** is a measure of how difficult it is to stop a moving object. It depends on both the mass and velocity of the object.

```
momentum = mass × velocity

p = m × v

Where:
p = momentum (kg m/s or Ns)
m = mass (kg)
v = velocity (m/s)
```

### Momentum as a Vector

Momentum is a **vector quantity**:
- Has magnitude (size)
- Has direction (same direction as velocity)

### Units of Momentum

| Unit | Equivalent |
|------|------------|
| kg m/s | kilograms metres per second |
| Ns | newton seconds |

Note: kg m/s = Ns (both are correct)

---

## 4.2 Calculating Momentum

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** A 70 kg person runs at 5 m/s. What is their momentum?
```
p = m × v = 70 × 5 = 350 kg m/s
```

**Example 2:** A 0.05 kg bullet travels at 400 m/s. What is its momentum?
```
p = m × v = 0.05 × 400 = 20 kg m/s
```

**Example 3:** A 1500 kg car moves at 20 m/s north. What is its momentum?
```
p = m × v = 1500 × 20 = 30000 kg m/s north
```

### Common Exam Mistake
Always use velocity, not speed, for momentum calculations. Momentum has direction!

---

## 4.3 Conservation of Momentum

### Principle

**The total momentum before a collision equals the total momentum after a collision**, provided no external forces act.

```
total momentum before = total momentum after
```

### Why Momentum is Conserved

During a collision:
- Forces on the two objects are equal and opposite (Newton's Third Law)
- These forces act for the same time
- Therefore, changes in momentum are equal and opposite
- Total momentum remains unchanged

### Types of Collisions

| Type | Description | Kinetic Energy |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Elastic collision** | Objects bounce apart | Conserved (no loss) |
| **Inelastic collision** | Objects stick together | Not conserved (some lost as heat/sound) |

### Collision Formula

For two objects colliding:

```
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

Where:
m₁, m₂ = masses of objects 1 and 2
u₁, u₂ = velocities before collision
v₁, v₂ = velocities after collision
```

---

## 4.4 Solving Collision Problems

### Type 1: Objects Bounce Apart (Elastic)

**Example:** A 2 kg ball moving at 3 m/s collides with a stationary 1 kg ball. After collision, the 2 kg ball moves at 1 m/s. Find the velocity of the 1 kg ball.

```
Before: p = (2 × 3) + (1 × 0) = 6 kg m/s
After:  p = (2 × 1) + (1 × v) = 2 + v

Conservation: 6 = 2 + v
v = 4 m/s
```

### Type 2: Objects Stick Together (Inelastic)

**Example:** A 3 kg trolley moving at 4 m/s collides with a 2 kg stationary trolley. They stick together. Find their common velocity.

```
Before: p = (3 × 4) + (2 × 0) = 12 kg m/s
After:  p = (3 + 2) × v = 5v

Conservation: 12 = 5v
v = 2.4 m/s
```

### Type 3: Explosions/Recoil

**Example:** A 2 kg gun fires a 0.01 kg bullet at 500 m/s. Find the recoil velocity of the gun.

```
Before: p = 0 (everything stationary)
After:  p = (2 × v) + (0.01 × 500) = 2v + 5

Conservation: 0 = 2v + 5
v = -2.5 m/s

The negative sign means the gun moves in opposite direction to bullet.
```

## 4.5 Force and Momentum

### The Relationship

```
force = change in momentum ÷ time taken

F = (mv - mu) ÷ t

or

F = Δp ÷ t

Where:
F = force (N)
mv = final momentum (kg m/s)
mu = initial momentum (kg m/s)
t = time (s)
Δp = change in momentum (kg m/s)
```

### Alternative Form

```
F × t = mv - mu

Force × time = change in momentum
```

### Impulse

**Impulse** = force × time = change in momentum

Unit: Ns (newton seconds)

### Worked Example

A 0.1 kg ball hits a wall at 8 m/s and rebounds at 6 m/s. The collision lasts 0.05 s. Calculate the average force.

```
Taking direction toward wall as positive:
Initial momentum = 0.1 × 8 = 0.8 kg m/s
Final momentum = 0.1 × (-6) = -0.6 kg m/s (rebounds, so negative)

Change in momentum = (-0.6) - 0.8 = -1.4 kg m/s

F = Δp ÷ t = 1.4 ÷ 0.05 = 28 N

The force is 28 N away from the wall.
```

## 4.6 Safety Applications

### Crumple Zones and Air Bags

| Feature | How it Works |
|---------|--------------|
| **Crumple zones** | Increase collision time, reducing force |
| **Air bags** | Increase stopping time, reducing force |
| **Seat belts** | Stretch to increase stopping time |
| **Helmets** | Increase impact time, reducing force |

### The Physics

From F = Δp ÷ t:

- For a given change in momentum (Δp)
- Increasing time (t) decreases force (F)
- This reduces injury

### Example Calculation

A 70 kg passenger travelling at 20 m/s is stopped by an air bag in 0.2 s instead of hitting the dashboard in 0.02 s.

**Without air bag:**
```
F = Δp ÷ t = (70 × 20) ÷ 0.02 = 70000 N
```

**With air bag:**
```
F = Δp ÷ t = (70 × 20) ÷ 0.2 = 7000 N
```

The force is reduced by a factor of 10!

## 4.7 Momentum in Two Dimensions

### Principle

Momentum is conserved separately in each direction (horizontal and vertical).

### Example: Ball Rebounding at an Angle

If a ball hits a wall at an angle, the component of momentum parallel to the wall is unchanged, while the perpendicular component reverses.

## Exam Tips for Chapter 4

1. **Direction matters** - momentum is a vector, use + and - signs
2. **Conservation** - total momentum before = total momentum after
3. **Units** - use kg for mass, m/s for velocity
4. **Show working** - write out "before" and "after" momentum clearly
5. **Inelastic collisions** - combine masses after collision
6. **Explosions** - total momentum before = 0 (usually)

---

## Common Student Errors

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| Forgetting momentum is a vector | Use + and - for opposite directions |
| Using speed instead of velocity | Velocity includes direction |
| Not converting units | Mass must be in kg |
| Adding momenta incorrectly | Consider direction carefully |
| Confusing elastic and inelastic | Elastic = bounce, inelastic = stick |
