# PART 1: UNIT CONVERSIONS

## 1.1 Basic Length Conversions

### Key Conversion Factors

| From | To | Multiply by |
|------|-----|-------------|
| kilometres (km) | metres (m) | 1000 |
| metres (m) | centimetres (cm) | 100 |
| centimetres (cm) | millimetres (mm) | 10 |
| metres (m) | millimetres (mm) | 1000 |

### Complete Length Conversion Chart

$$\boxed{1 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m} = 100000 \text{ cm} = 1000000 \text{ mm}}$$

###  Common Mistake
> Students often confuse when to multiply or divide. Remember: **To go from a larger unit to a smaller unit, multiply** (you need more of the smaller units). **To go from a smaller unit to a larger unit, divide** (you need fewer of the larger units).

**Example 1:** Convert 3.5 km to metres.

**Solution:**
- Kilometres are LARGER than metres
- So we MULTIPLY by 1000
- $3.5 \times 1000 = 3500$ m

**Example 2:** Convert 4750 mm to metres.

**Solution:**
- Millimetres are SMALLER than metres
- So we DIVIDE by 1000
- $4750 \div 1000 = 4.75$ m

## 1.2 Area Conversions

###  KEY CONCEPT: Area Conversion Factors

Area is measured in **square units**. When converting between units, we must square the linear conversion factor.

$$\text{Area conversion factor} = (\text{linear conversion factor})^2$$

### Area Conversion Table

| From | To | Multiply by |
|------|-----|-------------|
| km² | m² | 1,000,000 (or $1000^2$) |
| m² | cm² | 10,000 (or $100^2$) |
| cm² | mm² | 100 (or $10^2$) |
| m² | mm² | 1,000,000 (or $1000^2$) |

### Why Do We Square the Factor?

Consider converting 1 m to cm:
- Linear: $1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm}$

Now consider 1 m² to cm²:
- $1 \text{ m}^2 = 1 \text{ m} \times 1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} = 10000 \text{ cm}^2$

$$\boxed{1 \text{ m}^2 = 10000 \text{ cm}^2}$$

**Example 3:** Convert 5.2 m² to cm².

**Solution:**
- Area conversion: $1 \text{ m}^2 = 10000 \text{ cm}^2$
- $5.2 \times 10000 = 52000 \text{ cm}^2$

**Example 4:** Convert 35000 cm² to m².

**Solution:**
- Going from smaller (cm²) to larger (m²), we divide
- $35000 \div 10000 = 3.5 \text{ m}^2$

**Example 5:** A rectangle has dimensions 2.5 m by 1.8 m. Find its area in cm².

**Solution:**
- Method 1: Calculate area first, then convert
  - Area = $2.5 \times 1.8 = 4.5 \text{ m}^2$
  - Convert: $4.5 \times 10000 = 45000 \text{ cm}^2$

- Method 2: Convert dimensions first, then calculate
  - $2.5 \text{ m} = 250 \text{ cm}$, $1.8 \text{ m} = 180 \text{ cm}$
  - Area = $250 \times 180 = 45000 \text{ cm}^2$

###  Common Mistake
> Do NOT multiply by 100 when converting m² to cm². This is a very common error! Always remember: area conversions involve squaring.

## 1.3 Volume Conversions

###  KEY CONCEPT: Volume Conversion Factors

Volume is measured in **cubic units**. When converting between units, we must cube the linear conversion factor.

$$\text{Volume conversion factor} = (\text{linear conversion factor})^3$$

### Volume Conversion Table

| From | To | Multiply by |
|------|-----|-------------|
| km³ | m³ | 1,000,000,000 (or $1000^3$) |
| m³ | cm³ | 1,000,000 (or $100^3$) |
| cm³ | mm³ | 1000 (or $10^3$) |
| m³ | mm³ | 1,000,000,000 (or $1000^3$) |

### Why Do We Cube the Factor?

Consider converting 1 m to cm:
- Linear: $1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm}$

Now consider 1 m³ to cm³:
- $1 \text{ m}^3 = 1 \text{ m} \times 1 \text{ m} \times 1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} = 1000000 \text{ cm}^3$

$$\boxed{1 \text{ m}^3 = 1000000 \text{ cm}^3}$$

**Example 6:** Convert 0.003 m³ to cm³.

**Solution:**
- $0.003 \times 1000000 = 3000 \text{ cm}^3$

**Example 7:** Convert 5400000 cm³ to m³.

**Solution:**
- $5400000 \div 1000000 = 5.4 \text{ m}^3$

---

## 1.4 Capacity and Volume Connection

###  IMPORTANT RELATIONSHIP

There is a crucial connection between volume and capacity:

$$\boxed{1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ ml}}$$

$$\boxed{1000 \text{ cm}^3 = 1000 \text{ ml} = 1 \text{ litre}}$$

$$\boxed{1 \text{ m}^3 = 1000 \text{ litres}}$$

### Capacity Conversion Table

| From | To | Multiply by |
|------|-----|-------------|
| litres | millilitres (ml) | 1000 |
| kilolitres (kl) | litres | 1000 |
| cm³ | ml | 1 (same value) |
| m³ | litres | 1000 |

**Example 8:** A water tank has a volume of 2.5 m³. How many litres of water can it hold?

**Solution:**
- $1 \text{ m}^3 = 1000 \text{ litres}$
- $2.5 \times 1000 = 2500 \text{ litres}$

**Example 9:** A bottle contains 750 ml of juice. What is this in cm³?

**Solution:**
- $1 \text{ ml} = 1 \text{ cm}^3$
- Therefore, $750 \text{ ml} = 750 \text{ cm}^3$

**Example 10:** Convert 3500 cm³ to litres.

**Solution:**
- $1000 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ litre}$
- $3500 \div 1000 = 3.5 \text{ litres}$

## 1.5 Imperial-Metric Conversions

### Key Imperial-Metric Conversions (Given in Exam)

| Imperial | Metric |
|----------|--------|
| 1 inch | ≈ 2.54 cm |
| 1 foot | ≈ 30 cm |
| 1 mile | ≈ 1.6 km (or 8 km = 5 miles) |
| 1 lb (pound) | ≈ 450 g |
| 1 gallon | ≈ 4.5 litres |
| 1 pint | ≈ 570 ml (or 1 litre ≈ 1.75 pints) |

### Working with Approximations

**Example 11:** Convert 8 miles to km.

**Solution:**
- Using $1 \text{ mile} \approx 1.6 \text{ km}$
- $8 \times 1.6 = 12.8 \text{ km}$

**Alternative Method:**
- Using $8 \text{ km} = 5 \text{ miles}$
- Ratio: $\frac{8}{5} = \frac{x}{8}$
- $x = \frac{8 \times 8}{5} = \frac{64}{5} = 12.8 \text{ km}$

**Example 12:** Convert 3 gallons to litres.

**Solution:**
- $1 \text{ gallon} \approx 4.5 \text{ litres}$
- $3 \times 4.5 = 13.5 \text{ litres}$

###  Exam Tip
> The conversions given are approximations. If the question asks for an exact conversion, use the conversion factor stated in the question. If none is given, use the standard approximations above.

## 1.6 Multi-Step Conversions

**Example 13:** Convert 0.05 km² to cm².

**Solution:**
This is a two-step conversion: km² → m² → cm²

- Step 1: km² to m²
  - $0.05 \times 1000000 = 50000 \text{ m}^2$

- Step 2: m² to cm²
  - $50000 \times 10000 = 500000000 \text{ cm}^2$ or $5 \times 10^8 \text{ cm}^2$

**Alternative single-step method:**
- $1 \text{ km} = 100000 \text{ cm}$
- $1 \text{ km}^2 = (100000)^2 \text{ cm}^2 = 10^{10} \text{ cm}^2$
- $0.05 \times 10^{10} = 5 \times 10^8 \text{ cm}^2$

**Example 14:** A cuboid has dimensions 2 m × 150 cm × 80 cm. Find its volume in m³.

**Solution:**
First, convert all dimensions to the same unit (metres):
- $150 \text{ cm} = 1.5 \text{ m}$
- $80 \text{ cm} = 0.8 \text{ m}$

Volume = $2 \times 1.5 \times 0.8 = 2.4 \text{ m}^3$

# PART 2: COMPOUND MEASURES

## 2.1 Understanding Compound Measures

### Definition
A **compound measure** is a quantity that is made up of two or more other quantities. They are expressed as a combination of units.

### The Three Key Compound Measures

| Measure | Formula | Units |
|---------|---------|-------|
| **Speed** | $\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}$ | m/s, km/h, mph |
| **Density** | $\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}$ | g/cm³, kg/m³ |
| **Pressure** | $\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}$ | N/m², Pa, N/cm² |

###  The Triangle Method

For all three compound measures, use the formula triangle:

```
        D (Distance)
       ┌─────────────┐
       │             │
       │      ÷      │
       │             │
  S ───┼─────────────┼─── T
(Speed)│             │ (Time)
       │      ×      │
       │             │
       └─────────────┘
```

**How to use:**
- Cover what you want to find
- What remains gives you the formula

## 2.2 Speed

### Definition
Speed is the rate at which distance is covered over time.

$$\boxed{\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}}$$

### Rearranged Formulae

$$\boxed{\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}}$$

### Common Units
- metres per second (m/s or ms⁻¹)
- kilometres per hour (km/h or kmh⁻¹)
- miles per hour (mph)

**Example 15:** A car travels 240 km in 3 hours. Calculate its average speed.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{240}{3} = 80 \text{ km/h}$$

**Example 16:** A cyclist travels at an average speed of 15 km/h for 2.5 hours. How far does she travel?

**Solution:**
$$\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time} = 15 \times 2.5 = 37.5 \text{ km}$$

**Example 17:** How long does it take to travel 180 miles at 60 mph?

**Solution:**
$$\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}} = \frac{180}{60} = 3 \text{ hours}$$

### Converting Between Speed Units

**Example 18:** Convert 72 km/h to m/s.

**Solution:**
- $72 \text{ km/h} = \frac{72 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ hour}}$
- Convert km to m: $72 \text{ km} = 72000 \text{ m}$
- Convert hours to seconds: $1 \text{ hour} = 3600 \text{ seconds}$

$$72 \text{ km/h} = \frac{72000 \text{ m}}{3600 \text{ s}} = 20 \text{ m/s}$$

###  Quick Conversion Method
To convert km/h to m/s: **divide by 3.6** (or multiply by $\frac{5}{18}$)
To convert m/s to km/h: **multiply by 3.6** (or multiply by $\frac{18}{5}$)

**Verification:**
- $72 \div 3.6 = 20 \text{ m/s}$ ✓

**Example 19:** Convert 25 m/s to km/h.

**Solution:**
- $25 \times 3.6 = 90 \text{ km/h}$

### Average Speed Problems

**Example 20:** A person travels from A to B at 40 km/h and returns from B to A at 60 km/h. Find the average speed for the entire journey.

**Solution:**
Let distance A to B = $d$ km

- Time A to B: $\frac{d}{40}$ hours
- Time B to A: $\frac{d}{60}$ hours
- Total distance = $2d$ km
- Total time = $\frac{d}{40} + \frac{d}{60} = \frac{3d + 2d}{120} = \frac{5d}{120} = \frac{d}{24}$ hours

$$\text{Average Speed} = \frac{2d}{\frac{d}{24}} = 2d \times \frac{24}{d} = 48 \text{ km/h}$$

###  Common Mistake
> Average speed is NOT the average of the two speeds! It must be calculated using total distance ÷ total time.

## 2.3 Density

### Definition
Density is the mass per unit volume of a substance.

$$\boxed{\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}}$$

### Rearranged Formulae

$$\boxed{\text{Mass} = \text{Density} \times \text{Volume}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Volume} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Density}}}$$

### Common Units
- grams per cubic centimetre (g/cm³)
- kilograms per cubic metre (kg/m³)

### Density Triangle

```
        M (Mass)
       ┌─────────────┐
       │             │
       │      ÷      │
       │             │
  D ───┼─────────────┼─── V
(Density)            │ (Volume)
       │      ×      │
       └─────────────┘
```

**Example 21:** A block of wood has a mass of 120 g and a volume of 150 cm³. Calculate its density.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{120}{150} = 0.8 \text{ g/cm}^3$$

**Example 22:** Gold has a density of 19.3 g/cm³. What is the mass of 5 cm³ of gold?

**Solution:**
$$\text{Mass} = \text{Density} \times \text{Volume} = 19.3 \times 5 = 96.5 \text{ g}$$

**Example 23:** An object has a mass of 270 g and a density of 2.7 g/cm³. Calculate its volume.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Volume} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Density}} = \frac{270}{2.7} = 100 \text{ cm}^3$$

### Finding Volume from Dimensions

**Example 24:** A cuboid measures 8 cm × 5 cm × 3 cm and has a density of 4.5 g/cm³. Find its mass.

**Solution:**
- Volume = $8 \times 5 \times 3 = 120 \text{ cm}^3$
- Mass = Density × Volume = $4.5 \times 120 = 540 \text{ g}$

### Unit Conversions with Density

**Example 25:** The density of water is 1000 kg/m³. Express this in g/cm³.

**Solution:**
- $1000 \text{ kg} = 1000000 \text{ g}$
- $1 \text{ m}^3 = 1000000 \text{ cm}^3$

$$\text{Density} = \frac{1000000 \text{ g}}{1000000 \text{ cm}^3} = 1 \text{ g/cm}^3$$

###  Key Fact
$$\boxed{1000 \text{ kg/m}^3 = 1 \text{ g/cm}^3}$$

This makes sense because:
- The numerator (mass) is multiplied by 1000 (kg to g)
- The denominator (volume) is multiplied by 1,000,000 (m³ to cm³)
- Overall: $\frac{1000}{1000000} = \frac{1}{1000}$

## 2.4 Pressure

### Definition
Pressure is the force applied per unit area.

$$\boxed{\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}}$$

### Rearranged Formulae

$$\boxed{\text{Force} = \text{Pressure} \times \text{Area}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Area} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Pressure}}}$$

### Common Units
- Newtons per square metre (N/m²) - also called **Pascals (Pa)**
- Newtons per square centimetre (N/cm²)

### Pressure Triangle

```
        F (Force)
       ┌─────────────┐
       │             │
       │      ÷      │
       │             │
  P ───┼─────────────┼─── A
(Pressure)           │ (Area)
       │      ×      │
       └─────────────┘
```

**Example 26:** A force of 500 N acts on an area of 25 m². Calculate the pressure.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}} = \frac{500}{25} = 20 \text{ N/m}^2 \text{ (or } 20 \text{ Pa)}$$

**Example 27:** A box exerts a pressure of 2000 Pa on the ground. If the base area is 0.5 m², find the weight of the box.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Force} = \text{Pressure} \times \text{Area} = 2000 \times 0.5 = 1000 \text{ N}$$

**Example 28:** A woman weighing 600 N stands on the floor. The area of one shoe is 150 cm². Calculate the pressure when she stands on:
(a) both feet
(b) one foot

**Solution:**
(a) Both feet: Area = $150 \times 2 = 300 \text{ cm}^2 = 0.03 \text{ m}^2$
$$\text{Pressure} = \frac{600}{0.03} = 20000 \text{ N/m}^2$$

(b) One foot: Area = $150 \text{ cm}^2 = 0.015 \text{ m}^2$
$$\text{Pressure} = \frac{600}{0.015} = 40000 \text{ N/m}^2$$

### Real-World Applications

**Example 29:** Why do stiletto heels sink into soft ground but flat shoes don't?

**Explanation:** Stiletto heels have a very small area of contact with the ground. For the same force (weight), a smaller area means a much higher pressure ($P = \frac{F}{A}$). This high pressure causes the heels to sink. Flat shoes have a larger area, distributing the force over more surface area, resulting in lower pressure.

## 2.5 Mixed Compound Measure Problems

### Complex Word Problems

**Example 30:** A rectangular tank measures 2 m by 1.5 m by 80 cm. It is filled with water (density 1000 kg/m³). Calculate the mass of water in kg.

**Solution:**
- Convert dimensions to metres: $80 \text{ cm} = 0.8 \text{ m}$
- Volume = $2 \times 1.5 \times 0.8 = 2.4 \text{ m}^3$
- Mass = Density × Volume = $1000 \times 2.4 = 2400 \text{ kg}$

**Example 31:** A car travels 45 km at 60 km/h, then 30 km at 40 km/h. Find:
(a) The total time for the journey
(b) The average speed

**Solution:**
(a) Time for first part: $t_1 = \frac{45}{60} = 0.75$ hours
Time for second part: $t_2 = \frac{30}{40} = 0.75$ hours
Total time = $0.75 + 0.75 = 1.5$ hours

(b) Total distance = $45 + 30 = 75$ km
Average speed = $\frac{75}{1.5} = 50$ km/h

**Example 32:** A metal cylinder has radius 3 cm and height 10 cm. It has a mass of 2.124 kg. Find its density in:
(a) g/cm³
(b) kg/m³

**Solution:**
- Volume of cylinder = $\pi r^2 h = \pi \times 3^2 \times 10 = 90\pi \text{ cm}^3$
- Mass = 2.124 kg = 2124 g

(a) Density = $\frac{2124}{90\pi} = \frac{2124}{282.74} \approx 7.51 \text{ g/cm}^3$

(b) Since 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³:
Density = $7.51 \times 1000 = 7510 \text{ kg/m}^3$

## 2.6 Best Value Problems

**Example 33:** Two sizes of cooking oil are available:
- Size A: 2 litres for £3.20
- Size B: 5 litres for £7.50

Which is better value?

**Solution:**
Compare price per litre:
- Size A: $\frac{£3.20}{2 \text{ litres}} = £1.60 \text{ per litre}$
- Size B: $\frac{£7.50}{5 \text{ litres}} = £1.50 \text{ per litre}$

**Size B is better value** as it costs less per litre.

##  COMMON EXAM MISTAKES

1. **Confusing area and volume conversion factors**
   - Area: square the linear factor
   - Volume: cube the linear factor

2. **Wrong direction in conversions**
   - Larger to smaller = multiply
   - Smaller to larger = divide

3. **Inconsistent units in calculations**
   - Always convert all quantities to the same unit before calculating

4. **Average speed errors**
   - Average speed ≠ average of speeds
   - Always use: Total Distance ÷ Total Time

5. **Unit conversion in speed problems**
   - km/h to m/s: divide by 3.6
   - m/s to km/h: multiply by 3.6

6. **Forgetting to convert area units for pressure**
   - If pressure is in N/m², area must be in m²
   - 1 m² = 10,000 cm²

## EXAM TIPS FOR A* STUDENTS

1. **Always check units first** – Convert all quantities to consistent units before starting calculations.

2. **Show all working** – Even if a question is worth few marks, showing method helps gain partial credit.

3. **Use the triangle** – Draw the formula triangle if unsure which formula to use.

4. **Check answers are reasonable** – A car travelling at 500 m/s is unrealistic!

5. **For multi-step problems** – Write down intermediate answers as they may earn method marks.

6. **Round appropriately** – Usually 3 significant figures unless specified otherwise.

7. **Include units in your answer** – A numerical answer without units may lose a mark.
