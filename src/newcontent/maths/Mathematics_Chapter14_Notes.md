## 1. USING FORMULAE

### 1.1 What is a Formula?

> **KEY DEFINITION**: A **formula** is a mathematical rule that expresses the relationship between two or more variables. It is an equation that shows how to calculate one quantity from others.

**Key Features of a Formula:**
- Contains two or more variables
- Shows a relationship that is always true
- Can be used to calculate unknown values
- Often derived from real-world situations

**Examples of Common Formulae:**

| Formula | Meaning | Variables |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| $A = lw$ | Area of rectangle | $A$ = area, $l$ = length, $w$ = width |
| $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$ | Area of triangle | $A$ = area, $b$ = base, $h$ = height |
| $C = 2\pi r$ | Circumference of circle | $C$ = circumference, $r$ = radius |
| $A = \pi r^2$ | Area of circle | $A$ = area, $r$ = radius |
| $v = \frac{d}{t}$ | Speed formula | $v$ = speed, $d$ = distance, $t$ = time |
| $F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32$ | Temperature conversion | $F$ = Fahrenheit, $C$ = Celsius |

### 1.2 Understanding Subject of a Formula

> **KEY DEFINITION**: The **subject** of a formula is the variable that is expressed in terms of other variables. It appears on its own on one side (usually the left) of the equals sign.

**Examples:**
- In $A = lw$, **A** is the subject (area expressed in terms of length and width)
- In $v = u + at$, **v** is the subject (final velocity expressed in terms of initial velocity, acceleration, and time)
- In $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$, **x** is the subject

### 1.3 Types of Formulae

#### **Physical Formulae**
Derived from scientific laws and principles:
- $F = ma$ (Newton's Second Law)
- $E = mc^2$ (Einstein's mass-energy equation)
- $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (Displacement under constant acceleration)

#### **Geometric Formulae**
Relating to shapes and measurements:
- Volume: $V = lwh$ (cuboid), $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ (sphere)
- Surface area: $S = 4\pi r^2$ (sphere)
- Pythagoras: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$

#### **Statistical Formulae**
For data analysis:
- Mean: $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n}$
- Standard deviation: $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(x-\bar{x})^2}{n}}$

### 1.4 Worded Formulae

>  **EXAM INSIGHT**: Many exam questions present formulae in words before asking you to work with them algebraically.

**Example:**
> "The cost, $C$, of hiring a car is calculated by multiplying the number of days, $d$, by £35 and adding a fixed insurance fee of £50."

**Converting to a formula:**
- Cost per day × number of days: $35d$
- Add insurance fee: $35d + 50$
- Formula: $C = 35d + 50$

**Worked Example:**
> A taxi company charges a base fare of £3 plus £1.50 per mile travelled.
> (a) Write a formula for the total fare, $F$, in terms of miles, $m$.
> (b) Calculate the fare for a 12-mile journey.

**Solution:**
(a) $F = 3 + 1.50m$ or $F = 1.5m + 3$

(b) Substituting $m = 12$:
$$F = 3 + 1.50 \times 12$$
$$F = 3 + 18$$
$$F = £21$$

## 2. CHANGE OF SUBJECT (REARRANGING FORMULAE)

### 2.1 What is Change of Subject?

> **KEY DEFINITION**: **Change of subject** means rearranging a formula to make a different variable the subject. This is also called "transposing" or "rearranging" the formula.

**Why is this important?**
- Allows calculation of any variable in a formula
- Essential when the required variable is not the subject
- Tests algebraic manipulation skills

### 2.2 The Golden Rules of Rearranging

> **FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE**: Whatever you do to one side of the equation, you must do to the other side.

#### **Rule 1: Inverse Operations**
To "undo" an operation, apply its inverse:

| Operation | Inverse Operation |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Addition ($+a$) | Subtraction ($-a$) |
| Subtraction ($-a$) | Addition ($+a$) |
| Multiplication ($×a$) | Division ($÷a$) |
| Division ($÷a$) | Multiplication ($×a$) |
| Power ($x^2$) | Square root ($\sqrt{x}$) |
| Square root ($\sqrt{x}$) | Square ($x^2$) |

#### **Rule 2: Order of Operations (Reverse)**
When rearranging, work in **reverse order** of BIDMAS/BODMAS:
1. **First**: Deal with additions/subtractions
2. **Then**: Deal with multiplications/divisions
3. **Finally**: Deal with powers/roots

>  **TIP**: Think about what was done last to the new subject – undo that first!

### 2.3 Step-by-Step Method

**Systematic Approach:**
1. Identify the variable that needs to become the new subject
2. Move all terms containing that variable to one side
3. Isolate the variable by undoing operations in reverse order
4. Simplify if necessary

### 2.4 Worked Examples by Difficulty Level

---

#### **Level 1: Single Step Rearrangement**

**Example 1:** Make $r$ the subject of $C = 2\pi r$

**Solution:**
$$C = 2\pi r$$

Divide both sides by $2\pi$:
$$r = \frac{C}{2\pi}$$

> ✅ **Check**: Substitute back: $C = 2\pi \times \frac{C}{2\pi} = C$ ✓

---

**Example 2:** Make $h$ the subject of $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$

**Solution:**
$$A = \frac{1}{2}bh$$

Multiply both sides by 2:
$$2A = bh$$

Divide both sides by $b$:
$$h = \frac{2A}{b}$$

---

#### **Level 2: Two-Step Rearrangement**

**Example 3:** Make $t$ the subject of $v = u + at$

**Solution:**
$$v = u + at$$

**Step 1:** Subtract $u$ from both sides:
$$v - u = at$$

**Step 2:** Divide both sides by $a$:
$$t = \frac{v - u}{a}$$

> 📝 **EXAM TIP**: The answer $t = \frac{v-u}{a}$ is preferred over $t = \frac{-u+v}{a}$

---

**Example 4:** Make $u$ the subject of $v = u + at$

**Solution:**
$$v = u + at$$

Subtract $at$ from both sides:
$$v - at = u$$

$$u = v - at$$

---

**Example 5:** Make $r$ the subject of $A = \pi r^2 + 2\pi rh$ (surface area of cylinder)

**Solution:**
This requires factoring!

$$A = \pi r^2 + 2\pi rh$$

Factor out $\pi r$:
$$A = \pi r(r + 2h)$$

Divide by $\pi(r + 2h)$:
$$r = \frac{A}{\pi(r + 2h)}$$

>  **COMMON MISTAKE**: Trying to solve this without factoring leads to incorrect answers!

---

#### **Level 3: Powers and Roots**

**Example 6:** Make $r$ the subject of $A = \pi r^2$

**Solution:**
$$A = \pi r^2$$

Divide by $\pi$:
$$\frac{A}{\pi} = r^2$$

Take the square root of both sides:
$$r = \sqrt{\frac{A}{\pi}}$$

>  **NOTE**: We use only the positive root since $r$ represents a radius (positive quantity).

---

**Example 7:** Make $r$ the subject of $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$

**Solution:**
$$V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$$

Multiply both sides by 3:
$$3V = 4\pi r^3$$

Divide by $4\pi$:
$$\frac{3V}{4\pi} = r^3$$

Take the cube root:
$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3V}{4\pi}}$$

---

#### **Level 4: Subject Appears Twice**

**Example 8:** Make $x$ the subject of $ax + b = cx - d$

**Solution:**
$$ax + b = cx - d$$

**Step 1:** Collect $x$ terms on one side
- Subtract $cx$ from both sides: $ax - cx + b = -d$
- Subtract $b$ from both sides: $ax - cx = -d - b$

**Step 2:** Factor out $x$
$$x(a - c) = -d - b$$

**Step 3:** Divide by $(a - c)$
$$x = \frac{-d - b}{a - c}$$

**Alternative form:**
$$x = \frac{-b - d}{a - c} = \frac{-(b+d)}{a-c} = \frac{b+d}{c-a}$$

---

**Example 9:** Make $x$ the subject of $y = mx + c$

**Solution:**
$$y = mx + c$$

Subtract $c$: $y - c = mx$

Divide by $m$: $x = \frac{y - c}{m}$

---

#### **Level 5: Advanced Rearrangement**

**Example 10:** Make $t$ the subject of $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$

**Solution:**
This is a quadratic equation. Rearranging gives:
$$\frac{1}{2}at^2 + ut - s = 0$$

Multiply by 2:
$$at^2 + 2ut - 2s = 0$$

Using the quadratic formula where:
- $a_{quad} = a$ (coefficient of $t^2$)
- $b_{quad} = 2u$ (coefficient of $t$)
- $c_{quad} = -2s$ (constant)

$$t = \frac{-2u \pm \sqrt{(2u)^2 - 4(a)(-2s)}}{2a}$$

$$t = \frac{-2u \pm \sqrt{4u^2 + 8as}}{2a}$$

Simplifying:
$$t = \frac{-u \pm \sqrt{u^2 + 2as}}{a}$$

---

### 2.5 Fraction Formulae

**Example 11:** Make $h$ the subject of $\frac{1}{h} = \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b}$

**Solution:**
$$\frac{1}{h} = \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b}$$

**Step 1:** Add the fractions on the right:
$$\frac{1}{h} = \frac{b + a}{ab}$$

**Step 2:** Cross-multiply:
$$ab = h(a + b)$$

**Step 3:** Divide by $(a + b)$:
$$h = \frac{ab}{a + b}$$

> 📝 **MEMORISE THIS**: This is the formula for combining resistors in parallel or lenses in contact!

---

### 2.6 Common Mistakes to Avoid

>  **EXAM WARNING – TOP ERRORS:**

| Mistake | Wrong | Correct |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Forgetting to apply operation to all terms | $v = u + at$ → $v - at = u$ | $v - at = u$ ✓ |
| Incorrect expansion | $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + b^2$ | $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ |
| Wrong order of operations | Dividing before adding | Follow reverse BIDMAS |
| Sign errors | $-x = -a$ → $x = -a$ | $-x = -a$ → $x = a$ |
| Forgetting brackets | $\frac{a+b}{c} = \frac{a}{c} + b$ | $\frac{a+b}{c} = \frac{a}{c} + \frac{b}{c}$ |

## 3. SUBSTITUTION

### 3.1 What is Substitution?

> **KEY DEFINITION**: **Substitution** is the process of replacing variables with their numerical values to evaluate an expression or formula.

### 3.2 Types of Substitution

#### **Positive Number Substitution**
**Example:** Evaluate $A = lw$ when $l = 8$ and $w = 5$

$$A = 8 \times 5 = 40$$

---

#### **Negative Number Substitution**

>  **CRITICAL**: Use brackets around negative numbers!

**Example:** Evaluate $v = u + at$ when $u = -3$, $a = 4$, $t = 2$

$$v = (-3) + 4 \times 2$$
$$v = -3 + 8$$
$$v = 5$$

**Example:** Evaluate $x^2$ when $x = -5$

$$x^2 = (-5)^2 = 25$$

> ❌ **COMMON MISTAKE**: $-5^2 = -25$ (This is wrong! The negative is not squared.)

---

#### **Fraction Substitution**

**Example:** Evaluate $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$ when $b = \frac{3}{4}$ and $h = 8$

$$A = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{3}{4} \times 8$$
$$A = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{3 \times 8}{4}$$
$$A = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{24}{4}$$
$$A = \frac{1}{2} \times 6$$
$$A = 3$$

---

#### **Decimal Substitution**

**Example:** The formula for kinetic energy is $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Calculate $E$ when $m = 2.5$ kg and $v = 4$ m/s.

$$E = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.5 \times 4^2$$
$$E = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.5 \times 16$$
$$E = 1.25 \times 16$$
$$E = 20 \text{ Joules}$$

---

### 3.3 Substitution with Powers

**Example:** Evaluate $y = 3x^2 - 2x + 5$ when $x = -2$

**Solution:**
$$y = 3(-2)^2 - 2(-2) + 5$$
$$y = 3(4) - (-4) + 5$$
$$y = 12 + 4 + 5$$
$$y = 21$$

> 📝 **WORKING TIP**: Show all intermediate steps clearly for method marks!

---

### 3.4 Substitution in Complex Formulae

**Example:** The formula for compound interest is $A = P(1 + \frac{r}{100})^n$

Calculate $A$ when $P = 1000$, $r = 5$, and $n = 3$.

**Solution:**
$$A = 1000\left(1 + \frac{5}{100}\right)^3$$
$$A = 1000(1 + 0.05)^3$$
$$A = 1000(1.05)^3$$
$$A = 1000 \times 1.157625$$
$$A = 1157.625$$

Rounding to 2 d.p.: $A = £1157.63$

---

### 3.5 Worded Substitution Problems

**Example:** The height $h$ metres of a ball thrown upwards after $t$ seconds is given by:
$$h = 20t - 5t^2$$

(a) Find the height after 2 seconds.
(b) After how many seconds is the ball at height 15 metres?

**Solution:**

(a) Substituting $t = 2$:
$$h = 20(2) - 5(2)^2$$
$$h = 40 - 5(4)$$
$$h = 40 - 20$$
$$h = 20 \text{ metres}$$

(b) Setting $h = 15$:
$$15 = 20t - 5t^2$$
$$5t^2 - 20t + 15 = 0$$
Dividing by 5: $t^2 - 4t + 3 = 0$
$$(t - 3)(t - 1) = 0$$
$$t = 1 \text{ or } t = 3 \text{ seconds}$$

## 4. COMBINED TECHNIQUES

### 4.1 Rearranging Then Substituting

Many exam questions require you to first rearrange a formula, then substitute values.

**Example:** The area of a trapezium is given by $A = \frac{1}{2}(a + b)h$.

(a) Rearrange to make $a$ the subject.
(b) Calculate $a$ when $A = 45$, $b = 8$, and $h = 6$.

**Solution:**

(a) Rearranging:
$$A = \frac{1}{2}(a + b)h$$
Multiply by 2: $2A = (a + b)h$
Divide by $h$: $\frac{2A}{h} = a + b$
Subtract $b$: $a = \frac{2A}{h} - b$

(b) Substituting:
$$a = \frac{2 \times 45}{6} - 8$$
$$a = \frac{90}{6} - 8$$
$$a = 15 - 8$$
$$a = 7$$

---

### 4.2 Multi-Step Problems

**Example:** The volume of a cone is $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$.

Given that $V = 100\pi$ and $h = 12$, find the radius $r$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Substitute the known values:
$$100\pi = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 \times 12$$

**Step 2:** Simplify:
$$100\pi = 4\pi r^2$$

**Step 3:** Divide by $4\pi$:
$$r^2 = \frac{100\pi}{4\pi} = 25$$

**Step 4:** Take the square root:
$$r = 5 \text{ units}$$

## 5. EXAM FOCUS & STRATEGY

### 5.1 Frequently Tested Question Types

| Question Type | Marks | Frequency |
|---------------|-------|-----------|
| Simple substitution | 1-2 | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High |
| Two-step rearrangement | 2-3 | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High |
| Worded formula creation | 2-3 | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ High |
| Complex rearrangement (powers/fractions) | 3-4 | ⭐⭐⭐ Medium |
| Rearrange + substitute | 3-5 | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ High |
| Subject appears twice | 3-4 | ⭐⭐ Low-Medium |

### 5.2 Command Word Guidance

| Command Word | What to Do |
|--------------|------------|
| **Evaluate** | Substitute values and calculate the numerical answer |
| **Express** | Write in terms of variables (algebraic answer) |
| **Make ... the subject** | Rearrange the formula |
| **Find** | Calculate or determine a value |
| **Show that** | Provide full working to demonstrate the result |
| **Hence** | Use your previous answer |

### 5.3 How to Gain Full Marks

> 🎯 **EXAM STRATEGIES:**

**For Substitution Questions:**
1. ✅ Write the original formula first
2. ✅ Show the substitution clearly: $A = \frac{1}{2}(6+8)(5)$
3. ✅ Show all working steps
4. ✅ Give final answer with units (if applicable)
5. ✅ Check your answer is sensible

**For Change of Subject Questions:**
1. ✅ Write the formula clearly
2. ✅ Show each step on a new line
3. ✅ Write what you're doing: "Divide both sides by 2"
4. ✅ Check your answer by substitution
5. ✅ Simplify your final answer

### 5.4 Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

**Pattern 1: The "Real-World" Formula**
```
Worded situation → Extract formula → Substitute → Calculate → Units
```

**Pattern 2: The "Two-Part" Question**
```
Part (a): Rearrange the formula
Part (b): Use your rearranged formula with values
```

**Pattern 3: The "Inverse" Problem**
```
Given result → Find unknown input → May need rearrangement
```

### 5.5 Mark Scheme Insight

**Typical Mark Allocation for 3-mark rearrangement:**

| Mark | Criteria |
|------|----------|
| M1 | Correct first step shown |
| M1 | Correct method throughout |
| A1 | Correct final answer |

**Example Mark Scheme:**
```
Question: Make h the subject of A = ½bh
Answer: h = 2A/b

Mark allocation:
M1: For multiplying both sides by 2 (2A = bh seen)
M1: For dividing both sides by b
A1: For h = 2A/b (or equivalent)
```

## 7. PRACTICE TIPS FOR A* STUDENTS

### 7.1 Quick Self-Check Questions
Before attempting any question, ask yourself:
1. What is being asked?
2. What formula is needed?
3. What values do I have?
4. Do I need to rearrange first?
5. What units should my answer have?

### 7.2 Time-Saving Techniques
- ✅ Memorise common formulae
- ✅ Practise mental substitution
- ✅ Learn to spot which variable is the subject
- ✅ Check if rearrangement is necessary before substituting

### 7.3 Verification Methods
- **Dimension check**: Do the units make sense?
- **Substitution check**: Substitute your answer back
- **Estimation**: Is the answer reasonable?
- **Alternative method**: Try solving differently
