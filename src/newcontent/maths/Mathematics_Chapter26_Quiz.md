### Question 1

The graph of $y = x^3$ passes through which of these points?

A) $(2, 6)$  
B) $(-2, -8)$  
C) $(3, 9)$  
D) $(-1, 1)$

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** For $y = x^3$:
- At $x = -2$: $y = (-2)^3 = -8$
- Therefore the point $(-2, -8)$ lies on the graph.

Checking other options:
- $(2, 6)$: $y$ should be $2^3 = 8$, not $6$ ❌
- $(3, 9)$: $y$ should be $3^3 = 27$, not $9$ ❌
- $(-1, 1)$: $y$ should be $(-1)^3 = -1$, not $1$ ❌

### Question 2

Which transformation maps $y = x^3$ to $y = (x + 2)^3$?

A) Translation 2 units right  
B) Translation 2 units left  
C) Translation 2 units up  
D) Reflection in y-axis

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** For $y = (x + 2)^3$:
- The form $(x + h)^3$ represents a translation LEFT by $h$ units
- So $y = (x + 2)^3$ is a translation 2 units LEFT
- Key rule: $(x - h)$ → RIGHT, $(x + h)$ → LEFT

### Question 3

The graph of $y = \frac{1}{x}$ has asymptotes:

A) $x = 1$ and $y = 1$  
B) $x = 0$ and $y = 0$  
C) $x = 1$ and $y = 0$  
D) $x = 0$ and $y = 1$

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The graph $y = \frac{1}{x}$:
- Cannot have $x = 0$ (division by zero) → vertical asymptote $x = 0$
- As $x \to \infty$, $y \to 0$ → horizontal asymptote $y = 0$
- Both axes are the asymptotes

### Question 4

How many turning points can a cubic graph have at most?

A) 1  
B) 2  
C) 3  
D) 4

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** 
- A cubic function is of degree 3
- Maximum number of turning points = degree - 1 = 3 - 1 = 2
- Cubics can have 0, 1, or 2 turning points
- The basic $y = x^3$ has a point of inflection (not a turning point)

### Question 5

For $y = \frac{3}{x-2} + 1$, what are the asymptotes?

A) $x = 2$, $y = 1$  
B) $x = -2$, $y = 1$  
C) $x = 2$, $y = -1$  
D) $x = -2$, $y = -1$

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** For $y = \frac{a}{x-h} + k$:
- Vertical asymptote: $x = h = 2$
- Horizontal asymptote: $y = k = 1$
- The denominator is zero when $x = 2$

### Question 6

The graph of $y = -x^3$ is:

A) The same as $y = x^3$  
B) A reflection of $y = x^3$ in the x-axis  
C) A reflection of $y = x^3$ in the y-axis  
D) A translation of $y = x^3$

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- $y = -x^3$ means all y-values are negated
- When $x = 1$: $y = -1$ (instead of $1$)
- When $x = -1$: $y = 1$ (instead of $-1$)
- This is a reflection in the x-axis

### Question 7

Which quadrant does NOT contain any part of the graph $y = \frac{1}{x}$?

A) Quadrant I  
B) Quadrant II  
C) Quadrant III  
D) Quadrant IV

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
- $y = \frac{1}{x}$ when $x > 0$: $y > 0$ → Quadrant I
- $y = \frac{1}{x}$ when $x < 0$: $y < 0$ → Quadrant III
- The graph is never in Quadrant II ($x < 0, y > 0$) or Quadrant IV ($x > 0, y < 0$)
- For $y = \frac{-1}{x}$, the graph would be in Quadrants II and IV

### Question 8

The y-intercept of $y = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2x - 5$ is:

A) $(0, -5)$  
B) $(0, 5)$  
C) $(-5, 0)$  
D) $(5, 0)$

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:**
- To find y-intercept, set $x = 0$
- $y = 0^3 - 3(0)^2 + 2(0) - 5 = -5$
- y-intercept is $(0, -5)$
- In general, for $y = ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$, y-intercept is $(0, d)$

### Question 9

The graph of $y = \frac{1}{x^2}$ lies in which quadrants?

A) I and II only  
B) I and III only  
C) II and IV only  
D) I, II, III, and IV

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:**
- $x^2$ is always positive for $x \neq 0$
- $\frac{1}{x^2}$ is always positive
- When $x > 0$: $y > 0$ → Quadrant I
- When $x < 0$: $y > 0$ → Quadrant II
- Never in Quadrants III or IV (y cannot be negative)

### Question 10

If $y = x^3$ is stretched vertically by factor 2, the new equation is:

A) $y = x^3 + 2$  
B) $y = (x+2)^3$  
C) $y = 2x^3$  
D) $y = \frac{1}{2}x^3$

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:**
- A vertical stretch by factor $k$ multiplies the function by $k$
- $y = x^3$ stretched by factor 2 becomes $y = 2x^3$
- Note: $y = x^3 + 2$ is a translation up by 2
- Note: $y = (x+2)^3$ is a translation left by 2

## SECTION B: TRUE/FALSE QUESTIONS (10 marks)

**State whether each statement is TRUE or FALSE. If FALSE, provide the correction.**

---

### Question 11

**Statement:** A cubic graph always passes through the origin.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** A cubic graph only passes through the origin if $d = 0$ in the equation $y = ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$. For example, $y = x^3 + 1$ passes through $(0, 1)$, not the origin.

---

### Question 12

**Statement:** The graph of $y = \frac{1}{x}$ is symmetric about the origin.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** The graph has rotational symmetry of order 2 about the origin. If you rotate it 180° around the origin, it maps onto itself. This is because $f(-x) = -f(x)$ for this function.

---

### Question 13

**Statement:** A cubic graph can have exactly three turning points.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** A cubic graph can have at most TWO turning points (or 0 or 1). A cubic is degree 3, so maximum turning points = degree - 1 = 2.

---

### Question 14

**Statement:** The graph of $y = \frac{2}{x}$ is a stretched version of $y = \frac{1}{x}$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** $y = \frac{2}{x} = 2 \times \frac{1}{x}$ represents a vertical stretch by factor 2. All y-values are doubled while x-values remain the same.

---

### Question 15

**Statement:** For $y = (x-3)^3 + 4$, the point of inflection is at $(3, 4)$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** The basic cubic $y = x^3$ has an inflection point at $(0, 0)$. Translating right by 3 and up by 4 moves this to $(3, 4)$.

---

### Question 16

**Statement:** The graph of $y = \frac{1}{x}$ will eventually cross the x-axis for very large values of x.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** The graph of $y = \frac{1}{x}$ NEVER crosses either axis. The x-axis ($y = 0$) is a horizontal asymptote - the curve gets closer and closer but never touches or crosses it.

---

### Question 17

**Statement:** If $a < 0$ in $y = ax^3$, the graph rises as $x$ increases to the right.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** If $a < 0$, the graph FALLS as $x$ increases to the right. For example, $y = -x^3$ has $y \to -\infty$ as $x \to +\infty$.

---

### Question 18

**Statement:** The graph of $y = \frac{1}{x^2}$ is symmetric about the y-axis.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** For any $x$, $(-x)^2 = x^2$, so $\frac{1}{(-x)^2} = \frac{1}{x^2}$. This means $f(-x) = f(x)$, which is the definition of an even function with y-axis symmetry.

---

### Question 19

**Statement:** To find the x-intercepts of a cubic, we substitute $x = 0$.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** To find x-intercepts, we substitute $y = 0$ and solve $ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0$. Substituting $x = 0$ gives the y-intercept, not x-intercepts.

---

### Question 20

**Statement:** The equation $y = \frac{1}{x+2}$ has a vertical asymptote at $x = -2$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** The denominator is zero when $x + 2 = 0$, i.e., $x = -2$. This is the vertical asymptote. The transformation shifts the basic graph $y = \frac{1}{x}$ left by 2 units.

### Question 27 (4 marks)

A cubic function has equation $y = x^3 + x^2 - 6x$.

(a) Factorise $x^3 + x^2 - 6x$ completely. (2 marks)

(b) Hence find the x-intercepts of the graph. (2 marks)

**Solution:**

(a) **Factorisation:**
$$x^3 + x^2 - 6x = x(x^2 + x - 6) = x(x + 3)(x - 2)$$

**Step-by-step:**
1. Factor out $x$: $x(x^2 + x - 6)$
2. Factorise the quadratic: $x^2 + x - 6 = (x + 3)(x - 2)$
3. Complete factorisation: $x(x + 3)(x - 2)$

(b) **x-intercepts:** Set $y = 0$
$$x(x + 3)(x - 2) = 0$$

Therefore:
- $x = 0$
- $x + 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -3$
- $x - 2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$

**x-intercepts are at $x = -3$, $x = 0$, and $x = 2$**

**Mark Scheme:**
(a)
- M1: Factor out $x$ or attempt factorisation
- A1: $x(x + 3)(x - 2)$

(b)
- M1: Setting $y = 0$ and using factorised form
- A1: All three x-intercepts correct

### Question 29 (5 marks)

The graph of $y = \frac{a}{x}$ passes through the point $(2, -3)$.

(a) Find the value of $a$. (2 marks)

(b) Write down the equations of the asymptotes. (1 mark)

(c) On which side of the asymptotes does each branch of the curve lie? (2 marks)

**Solution:**

(a) **Finding $a$:**
Since $(2, -3)$ lies on the curve:
$$-3 = \frac{a}{2}$$
$$a = -3 \times 2 = -6$$

**$a = -6$**

(b) **Asymptotes:**
- Vertical asymptote: $x = 0$
- Horizontal asymptote: $y = 0$

(c) **Location of branches:**

Since $a = -6 < 0$, the graph is a reflection of $y = \frac{6}{x}$ in the x-axis.

- When $x > 0$: $y = \frac{-6}{x} < 0$ → Quadrant IV
- When $x < 0$: $y = \frac{-6}{x} > 0$ → Quadrant II

**The branches lie in Quadrants II and IV.**

**Mark Scheme:**
(a)
- M1: Correct substitution
- A1: $a = -6$

(b)
- B1: Both asymptotes correct ($x = 0$, $y = 0$)

(c)
- B1: Identifies $a < 0$ affects position
- B1: Quadrants II and IV stated

# EXAM FOCUS & STRATEGY

---

## Frequently Tested Question Types

### Type 1: Table Completion and Graph Drawing
**Frequency:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Very Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- Complete a table of values for a cubic or reciprocal function
- Draw the graph on graph paper
- Use appropriate scale

**Strategy:**
- Show all working for calculations
- Plot points accurately (use × or dots)
- Draw smooth curves (no straight-line segments)
- Label the graph with its equation

---

### Type 2: Reading Values from Graphs
**Frequency:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Very Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- Find y given x (or x given y)
- Find turning points
- Find intercepts

**Strategy:**
- Draw construction lines lightly
- Read to nearest 0.5 small square
- State coordinates as $(x, y)$

---

### Type 3: Solving Equations Graphically
**Frequency:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- "Use your graph to solve..."
- Find intersections with lines
- Estimate solutions

**Strategy:**
- Identify what line to draw
- Draw the line carefully (use a ruler)
- Mark intersection points clearly
- Read x-coordinates accurately

---

### Type 4: Transformations
**Frequency:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- Describe transformations
- Write new equations after transformation
- Find asymptotes after transformation

**Strategy:**
- Use correct vocabulary (translation, stretch, reflection)
- Give direction and magnitude
- For translations, use vector notation or describe fully

---

### Type 5: Asymptotes
**Frequency:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Common for reciprocals)

**What examiners ask:**
- Write down equations of asymptotes
- Explain why asymptotes exist
- Identify from equation form

**Strategy:**
- Always write as equations (e.g., "$x = 2$")
- Draw as dashed lines on graphs
- Identify from form $\frac{a}{x-h} + k$

## Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

### Pattern 1: Find x-intercepts
1. Set $y = 0$
2. Factorise (if possible)
3. Solve each factor = 0

### Pattern 2: Find turning points
1. Draw accurate graph
2. Identify highest/lowest points
3. Read coordinates

### Pattern 3: Solve $f(x) = k$
1. Draw line $y = k$
2. Find intersections
3. Read x-coordinates

### Pattern 4: Identify asymptotes
1. Look at equation form
2. For $y = \frac{a}{x-h} + k$: asymptotes at $x = h$, $y = k$
3. State clearly
