### Question 1
What is the prime factorisation of 72?

A) $2^3 \times 3^2$  
B) $2^2 \times 3^3$  
C) $2^3 \times 3^3$  
D) $2^2 \times 3^2$

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:**
$$72 = 2 \times 36 = 2 \times 2 \times 18 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 9 = 2^3 \times 3^2$$

Verification: $2^3 \times 3^2 = 8 \times 9 = 72$ ✓

### Question 2
Find the HCF of 48 and 72.

A) 12  
B) 24  
C) 36  
D) 48

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
$$48 = 2^4 \times 3$$
$$72 = 2^3 \times 3^2$$

HCF = lowest powers of common primes = $2^3 \times 3 = 8 \times 3 = 24$

LCM = highest powers of all primes = $2^2 \times 3^2 = 4 \times 9 = 36$

## SECTION B: TRUE/FALSE QUESTIONS (10 Statements)

**Instructions:** State whether each statement is TRUE or FALSE. If FALSE, provide the correct statement.

---

### Statement 1
"The number 1 is the smallest prime number."

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** 1 is NOT a prime number. A prime number must have exactly two factors (1 and itself). The number 1 has only one factor. The smallest prime number is 2.

---

### Statement 2
"The HCF of two numbers is always a factor of their LCM."

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Since HCF divides both numbers, and both numbers divide LCM, HCF must divide LCM.

---

### Statement 3
"When finding the LCM using prime factorisation, you take the lowest power of each prime factor."

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** When finding the LCM, you take the **HIGHEST** power of each prime factor. The lowest powers are used for finding the HCF.

---

### Statement 4
"The ratio 2 : 3 can be written as the fraction 2/3."

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** A ratio a : b can be expressed as the fraction a/b. Both represent the same comparison between two quantities.

---

### Statement 5
"The product of HCF and LCM of two numbers equals the product of those two numbers."

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** For two numbers a and b: HCF(a,b) × LCM(a,b) = a × b

---

### Statement 6
"The formula HCF × LCM = Product works for any number of values."

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** The formula HCF × LCM = Product **only works for TWO numbers**. It does NOT apply to three or more numbers.

---

### Statement 7
"When sharing an amount in ratio 3 : 5, the smaller share is 3/8 of the total."

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Total parts = 3 + 5 = 8. The smaller share corresponds to 3 parts out of 8 total parts, so it equals 3/8 of the total.

---

### Statement 8
"The number 2 is the only even prime number."

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** All other even numbers are divisible by 2 (as well as 1 and themselves), so they have at least three factors and are therefore not prime.

---

### Statement 9
"When simplifying a ratio with different units, you should first convert all values to the larger unit."

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** You can convert to ANY same unit (either all to the smaller OR all to the larger unit). Converting to the smaller unit often avoids decimal values and is generally easier.

---

### Statement 10
"If A : B = 2 : 3, then A = 2x and B = 3x for some positive number x."

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** This is the standard way to express a ratio algebraically. The actual values of A and B are multiples of the ratio parts, where x represents the value of one part.

### Question 11 (4 marks)
(a) Express 252 as a product of its prime factors. **[2 marks]**

(b) Hence find the smallest integer $k$ such that $252k$ is a perfect square. **[2 marks]**

---

**Mark Scheme Solution:**

**(a) Express 252 as a product of prime factors [2 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Clear division process shown: $252 \div 2 = 126$, $126 \div 2 = 63$, $63 \div 3 = 21$, $21 \div 3 = 7$, $7 \div 7 = 1$ |
| A1 | $252 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7$ |

**Alternative factor tree method:**
```
        252
       /    \
      4     63
     / \   /  \
    2   2 9    7
          /\
         3  3
```
$252 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 7 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7$

**(b) Find smallest $k$ such that $252k$ is a perfect square [2 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Recognises that all prime factors need even powers OR identifies that 7 has odd power |
| A1 | $k = 7$ |

**Explanation:**
For a perfect square, all prime factors must have even powers.

$252 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7^1$

- 2 has power 2 (even) ✓
- 3 has power 2 (even) ✓
- 7 has power 1 (odd) ✗

We need another 7 to make the power even: $252 \times 7 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7^2 = (2 \times 3 \times 7)^2 = 42^2$

Therefore $k = 7$

**Answer:** (a) $252 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7$, (b) $k = 7$

### Question 12 (5 marks)
Find the HCF and LCM of 120, 180, and 300.

---

**Mark Scheme Solution:**

| Step | Working | Marks |
|------|---------|-------|
| Prime factorisation of 120 | $120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5$ | M1 |
| Prime factorisation of 180 | $180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$ | M1 |
| Prime factorisation of 300 | $300 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5^2$ | M1 |
| HCF (lowest powers of common primes) | HCF $= 2^2 \times 3 \times 5 = 60$ | A1 |
| LCM (highest powers of all primes) | LCM $= 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5^2 = 8 \times 9 \times 25 = 1800$ | A1 |

**Working:**

**Step 1: Prime factorisations**
$$120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5$$
$$180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$$
$$300 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5^2$$

**Step 2: Find HCF**
- Common primes: 2, 3, 5
- Lowest powers: $2^2$, $3^1$, $5^1$
- HCF $= 2^2 \times 3 \times 5 = 4 \times 3 \times 5 = 60$

**Step 3: Find LCM**
- All primes: 2, 3, 5
- Highest powers: $2^3$, $3^2$, $5^2$
- LCM $= 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5^2 = 8 \times 9 \times 25 = 1800$

**Answer: HCF = 60, LCM = 1800**

### Question 13 (5 marks)
Two buses leave a station at the same time. Bus A returns to the station every 15 minutes. Bus B returns to the station every 24 minutes.

(a) After how many minutes will both buses next be at the station together? **[2 marks]**

(b) If they first leave at 8:00 am, at what time will they next be together at the station? **[1 mark]**

(c) How many times in a 12-hour period will both buses be at the station together? **[2 marks]**

---

**Mark Scheme Solution:**

**(a) Time until next together [2 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Recognises this is a LCM problem and shows method to find LCM of 15 and 24 |
| A1 | 120 minutes |

**Working:**
$$15 = 3 \times 5$$
$$24 = 2^3 \times 3$$

LCM $= 2^3 \times 3 \times 5 = 8 \times 3 \times 5 = 120$ minutes

**(b) Time of day [1 mark]**

| A1 | 10:00 am |

**Working:**
8:00 am + 120 minutes = 8:00 am + 2 hours = **10:00 am**

**(c) Number of times in 12 hours [2 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Calculates number of 120-minute periods in 12 hours OR divides 12 hours by 2 hours |
| A1 | 7 times (including the start at 8:00 am) |

**Working:**
12 hours = 720 minutes
Number of coincidences = $\frac{720}{120} = 6$ intervals

However, they are together at the start (8:00 am) and after each 120-minute interval.

Times together: 8:00 am, 10:00 am, 12:00 pm, 2:00 pm, 4:00 pm, 6:00 pm, 8:00 pm

**Total: 7 times**

**Answer:** (a) 120 minutes, (b) 10:00 am, (c) 7 times

### Question 14 (6 marks)
A sum of money is shared between Alice, Bob, and Carol in the ratio 3 : 5 : 7.

(a) If Carol receives £84 more than Alice, find how much each person receives. **[4 marks]**

(b) What percentage of the total does Bob receive? Give your answer to 1 decimal place. **[2 marks]**

---

**Mark Scheme Solution:**

**(a) Find each person's share [4 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Identifies difference in parts: 7 - 3 = 4 parts |
| M1 | Finds value of one part: £84 ÷ 4 = £21 |
| M1 | Calculates each share correctly |
| A1 | Alice = £63, Bob = £105, Carol = £147 |

**Working:**
- Ratio: Alice : Bob : Carol = 3 : 5 : 7
- Difference between Carol and Alice = 7 - 3 = 4 parts
- 4 parts = £84
- 1 part = £84 ÷ 4 = £21

Shares:
- Alice = 3 × £21 = **£63**
- Bob = 5 × £21 = **£105**
- Carol = 7 × £21 = **£147**

**Verification:** £147 - £63 = £84 ✓

**(b) Percentage Bob receives [2 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Calculates total and sets up percentage calculation |
| A1 | 29.2% (to 1 d.p.) |

**Working:**
Total = £63 + £105 + £147 = £315

Percentage Bob receives $= \frac{105}{315} \times 100 = \frac{1}{3} \times 100 = 33.3\%$

**Alternative using ratio:**
Total parts = 3 + 5 + 7 = 15
Bob's percentage $= \frac{5}{15} \times 100 = \frac{1}{3} \times 100 = 33.3\%$ (to 1 d.p.)

**Answer:** (a) Alice = £63, Bob = £105, Carol = £147, (b) 33.3%

### Question 15 (5 marks)

There are 135 girls in Year 7.

(a) How many boys are in Year 7? **[2 marks]**

After some students join the school, the ratio of boys to girls becomes 3 : 4.

(b) Find the number of extra students who joined Year 7 if the same number of boys and girls joined. **[3 marks]**

---

**Mark Scheme Solution:**

**(a) Number of boys [2 marks]**

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Uses ratio to find one part or total |
| A1 | 108 boys |

**Working:**
- Boys : Girls = 4 : 5
- Girls = 5 parts = 135

| Method marks | Working |
|--------------|---------|
| M1 | Sets up equation with new ratio |
| M1 | Solves correctly |
| A1 | 9 students (total extra) |

**Working:**
Let $x$ boys and $x$ girls join (same number joins each).

New numbers:
- Boys = 108 + $x$
- Girls = 135 + $x$

New ratio: $(108 + x) : (135 + x) = 3 : 4$

$$\frac{108 + x}{135 + x} = \frac{3}{4}$$

Cross multiply:
$$4(108 + x) = 3(135 + x)$$
$$432 + 4x = 405 + 3x$$
$$x = 405 - 432 + 3x$$
$$-27 = -x$$
$$x = 27$$

Wait, let me recalculate:
$$432 + 4x = 405 + 3x$$
$$4x - 3x = 405 - 432$$
$$x = -27$$

This gives a negative result, which means students left rather than joined.

Let me reconsider: The question says "some students join". Let me check my working.

Actually, let me verify with the original numbers:
Original ratio 4:5 means for every 9 students, 4 are boys and 5 are girls.
135 girls means $135 \div 5 = 27$ sets, so 108 boys.

New ratio 3:4

If $x$ students of each gender join:
- Boys = $108 + x$
- Girls = $135 + x$
- New ratio = $3:4$

$$\frac{108 + x}{135 + x} = \frac{3}{4}$$
$$4(108 + x) = 3(135 + x)$$
$$432 + 4x = 405 + 3x$$
$$x = 405 - 432 = -27$$

The negative answer indicates students LEFT, not joined.

The question states students "joined", but mathematically we get a negative result. This suggests an error in the problem setup, or perhaps the question meant the ratio went from 3:4 to 4:5.

Let me reconsider with the correct interpretation:

If students JOIN and the ratio changes from 4:5 to 3:4:
Since $\frac{3}{4} < \frac{4}{5}$, the proportion of boys has decreased. This is only possible if MORE girls than boys joined.

The question says "the same number of boys and girls joined" but mathematically this leads to a contradiction. If equal numbers join, the ratio would stay the same.

**Correct interpretation:** The question may have an error, OR perhaps students LEFT the school.

If 27 students of each gender LEFT:
- Boys = 108 - 27 = 81
- Girls = 135 - 27 = 108

## Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

### Pattern 1: Multi-step Problems
Many questions combine topics:
1. Find prime factors
2. Use to find HCF/LCM
3. Apply to word problem

**Strategy:** Work systematically, showing each step clearly.

### Pattern 2: Working Backwards
Given:
- Final shares → find total
- HCF/LCM → find the numbers
- One part → find the ratio

**Strategy:** Use algebra ($x$ for one part) when appropriate.

### Pattern 3: Verification Questions
"Show that" or "Verify" questions require:
- Clear working
- Stating the expected result
- Confirming it matches

**Strategy:** Never skip verification - it confirms your method and can gain method marks even if calculation is wrong.

---

## Command Word Guidance

| Command Word | Action Required |
|--------------|-----------------|

## How to Gain Full Marks

### For Prime Factorisation (2-3 marks)
1. **M1:** Show division process clearly
2. **A1:** Write answer in index form: $2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5$
3. **A1 (if asked):** Use for further calculation

### For HCF/LCM (3-5 marks)
1. **M1:** Find prime factorisation of all numbers
2. **M1:** Identify common primes correctly
