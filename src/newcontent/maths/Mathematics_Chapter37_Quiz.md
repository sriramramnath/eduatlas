## Question 1

A fair six-sided die is rolled. What is the probability of rolling a number greater than 4?

A) $\frac{1}{6}$

B) $\frac{1}{3}$

C) $\frac{1}{2}$

D) $\frac{2}{3}$

**Answer: B)** $\frac{1}{3}$

**Explanation:**
- Numbers greater than 4: {5, 6}
- Number of favorable outcomes = 2
- Total outcomes = 6
- $P(\text{greater than 4}) = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$

## Question 2

A bag contains 5 red balls, 3 blue balls, and 2 green balls. A ball is selected at random. What is the probability it is NOT red?

A) $\frac{1}{2}$

B) $\frac{1}{5}$

C) $\frac{3}{10}$

D) $\frac{7}{10}$

**Answer: A)** $\frac{1}{2}$

**Explanation:**
- Total balls = 5 + 3 + 2 = 10
- Red balls = 5
- $P(\text{red}) = \frac{5}{10} = \frac{1}{2}$
- $P(\text{not red}) = 1 - P(\text{red}) = 1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$

**Alternative method:**
- Not red = blue + green = 3 + 2 = 5
- $P(\text{not red}) = \frac{5}{10} = \frac{1}{2}$

## SECTION B: TRUE/FALSE QUESTIONS

**Instructions:** State whether each statement is TRUE or FALSE. If FALSE, provide the correct statement.

---

## Statement 1

"The probability of an impossible event is equal to 1."

**FALSE**

**Correction:** The probability of an impossible event is equal to **0**. The probability of a **certain** event is equal to 1.

---

## Statement 2

"If the probability of winning a game is 0.6, then the probability of losing the game is 0.4."

**TRUE**

**Explanation:** Winning and losing are complementary events.
$P(\text{losing}) = 1 - P(\text{winning}) = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4$

---

## Statement 3

"The experimental probability of an event can be greater than 1."

**FALSE**

**Correction:** ALL probabilities, including experimental probability, must be between 0 and 1 inclusive. No probability can be greater than 1.

---

## Statement 4

"When rolling a fair die, the events 'rolling a 3' and 'rolling a 5' are mutually exclusive."

**TRUE**

**Explanation:** You cannot roll both a 3 and a 5 on a single roll of a die. Therefore, these events are mutually exclusive.

---

## Statement 5

"The theoretical probability of an event changes each time an experiment is performed."

**FALSE**

**Correction:** Theoretical probability is based on mathematical reasoning and does **not** change. It is the **experimental** probability that may vary with each experiment.

---

## Statement 6

"If two events A and B are mutually exclusive with $P(A) = 0.5$ and $P(B) = 0.6$, then $P(A \text{ or } B) = 1.1$."

**FALSE**

**Correction:** This situation is impossible. For mutually exclusive events, if $P(A) + P(B) > 1$, the events cannot exist as described since $P(A \text{ or } B)$ cannot exceed 1. This indicates an error in the given probabilities.

---

## Statement 7

"When tossing two coins, the probability of getting exactly one head is $\frac{1}{2}$."

**TRUE**

**Explanation:**
- Sample space: {HH, HT, TH, TT}
- Exactly one head: {HT, TH} → 2 outcomes
- $P(\text{exactly one head}) = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$

---

## Statement 8

"The experimental probability approaches the theoretical probability as the number of trials increases."

**TRUE**

**Explanation:** This is known as the **Law of Large Numbers**. With more trials, experimental probability converges to theoretical probability.

---

## Statement 9

"A probability of 0.05 means the event is very likely to occur."

**FALSE**

**Correction:** A probability of 0.05 means the event is **very unlikely** to occur. It is close to 0 (impossible) on the probability scale.

---

## Statement 10

"If a die is rolled 60 times, you would expect to roll a 3 approximately 10 times."

**TRUE**

**Explanation:**
- $P(3) = \frac{1}{6}$
- Expected frequency = $P \times \text{trials} = \frac{1}{6} \times 60 = 10$

---

## Statement 11

"For a fair coin, the theoretical probability of heads equals the theoretical probability of tails."

**TRUE**

**Explanation:** For a fair coin, $P(\text{heads}) = \frac{1}{2}$ and $P(\text{tails}) = \frac{1}{2}$, so they are equal.

---

## Statement 12

"When drawing a card from a standard deck, the events 'drawing a heart' and 'drawing a King' are mutually exclusive."

**FALSE**

**Correction:** These events are **NOT** mutually exclusive because the King of Hearts belongs to both categories - it is both a heart AND a King.

## Question 5 [3 marks]

A box contains only red, blue, and green pens. There are 12 red pens and 8 blue pens. The probability of randomly selecting a green pen is $\frac{1}{5}$.

Find the total number of pens in the box.

**Answer:** 25 pens

**Working:**
- Let total number of pens = $n$
- Number of red + blue = 12 + 8 = 20
- Number of green = $n - 20$
- $P(\text{green}) = \frac{n - 20}{n} = \frac{1}{5}$

**Method 1:**
- $\frac{n - 20}{n} = \frac{1}{5}$
- Cross multiply: $5(n - 20) = n$
- $5n - 100 = n$
- $4n = 100$
- $n = 25$

**Method 2:**
- If $P(\text{green}) = \frac{1}{5}$, then $P(\text{not green}) = \frac{4}{5}$
- Red + blue pens represent $\frac{4}{5}$ of total
- $20 = \frac{4}{5} \times n$
- $n = 20 \times \frac{5}{4} = 25$

**Mark allocation:**
- M1: Setting up correct equation or ratio [1 mark]
- M1: Correct algebraic manipulation [1 mark]
- A1: Correct answer [1 mark]

## Question 9 [5 marks]

In a game, a player rolls a fair six-sided die.

If the player rolls a 6, they win £5.
If the player rolls a 4 or 5, they win £2.
If the player rolls any other number, they win nothing.

(a) Find the probability of winning £5. [1 mark]

(b) Find the probability of winning £2. [1 mark]

(c) Find the probability of winning nothing. [2 marks]

(d) The game costs £1.50 to play. Explain whether this is a fair game. [1 mark]

**(a)** $P(\text{win } £5) = P(6) = \frac{1}{6}$

**Mark allocation:**
- A1: Correct answer [1 mark]

---

**(b)** $P(\text{win } £2) = P(4 \text{ or } 5) = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$

**Working:**
- Rolling 4 or 5: 2 outcomes
- $P = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$

**Mark allocation:**
- A1: Correct answer [1 mark]

---

**(c)** $P(\text{win nothing}) = P(1 \text{ or } 2 \text{ or } 3) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$

**Working:**
- Method 1: Winning nothing = 1, 2, or 3 → 3 outcomes
- $P = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$

- Method 2: $P(\text{nothing}) = 1 - P(£5) - P(£2) = 1 - \frac{1}{6} - \frac{2}{6} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$

**Mark allocation:**
- M1: Correct method [1 mark]
- A1: Correct answer [1 mark]

---

**(d)** The game is **NOT** fair (player loses money on average)

**Explanation:**
- Expected winnings = $(£5 \times \frac{1}{6}) + (£2 \times \frac{2}{6}) + (£0 \times \frac{3}{6})$
- Expected winnings = $£\frac{5}{6} + £\frac{4}{6} + £0 = £\frac{9}{6} = £1.50$
- Since expected winnings = cost (£1.50), the game IS fair.

**OR alternative conclusion:**
- If calculation is £1.50 expected win, game is fair (break even)
- Accept either answer with correct reasoning

**Mark allocation:**
- A1: Correct conclusion with valid explanation [1 mark]

## Question 10 [6 marks]

A bag contains $x$ red counters and $y$ blue counters. No other counters are in the bag.

A counter is taken from the bag at random, its colour is noted, and then it is returned to the bag.

This is repeated 100 times. A red counter is selected 40 times.

(a) Find the experimental probability of selecting a red counter. [1 mark]

(b) Write down an expression, in terms of $x$ and $y$, for the theoretical probability of selecting a red counter. [1 mark]

(c) Using your answers to (a) and (b), write down an equation involving $x$ and $y$. [1 mark]

(d) Given that there are 15 red counters in the bag, find the value of $y$. [3 marks]

**(a)** $P(\text{red}) = \frac{40}{100} = 0.4$

**Mark allocation:**
- A1: Correct answer [1 mark]

---

**(b)** $P(\text{red}) = \frac{x}{x + y}$

**Mark allocation:**
- A1: Correct expression [1 mark]

---

**(c)** $\frac{x}{x + y} = 0.4$ or equivalent

**Mark allocation:**
- A1: Correct equation [1 mark]

---

**(d)** $y = 22.5$ or $y = 23$ (if counters must be whole)

**Working:**
- $x = 15$
- $\frac{15}{15 + y} = 0.4$
- $15 = 0.4(15 + y)$
- $15 = 6 + 0.4y$
- $9 = 0.4y$
- $y = \frac{9}{0.4} = 22.5$

**Note:** Since we can't have 22.5 counters, either:
- Accept $y = 22.5$ (mathematical answer)
- Accept $y = 22$ or $y = 23$ (practical answer)

**Mark allocation:**
- M1: Substituting $x = 15$ [1 mark]
- M1: Correct algebraic manipulation [1 mark]
- A1: Correct answer (accept valid interpretation) [1 mark]

# EXAM FOCUS & STRATEGY

## Frequently Tested Question Types

### 1. Basic Probability Calculation
**Pattern:** Given outcomes → Find probability
**Strategy:** Count favorable ÷ Count total

### 2. Complementary Probability
**Pattern:** Find $P(\text{not } A)$
**Strategy:** Use $1 - P(A)$ directly

### 3. Two-Way Tables
**Pattern:** Read data from table
**Strategy:** Identify correct row/column, check totals

### 4. Expected Frequency
**Pattern:** "How many would you expect..."
**Strategy:** Expected = $P \times n$

### 5. Comparing Probabilities
**Pattern:** "Is the game fair?"
**Strategy:** Calculate expected value, compare to cost

---

## Command Word Guidance

| Word | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Calculate** | Show working, give numerical answer |
| **Find** | Determine the value |
| **Write down** | No working needed |
| **Explain** | Give mathematical reasons |
| **Show that** | Prove using algebra/calculations |

---

## Tips for Full Marks

1. ✅ **Show all working** - Even simple calculations
2. ✅ **Simplify fractions** - Unless told otherwise
3. ✅ **Check your answer** - Must satisfy $0 \leq P \leq 1$
4. ✅ **Use correct notation** - $P(A) = ...$
5. ✅ **Read carefully** - Probability or frequency?
6. ✅ **Check denominators** - Especially in conditional probability
