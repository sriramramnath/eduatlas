# Topic 1: Comparative Costs

## 1.1 Introduction to Comparative Costs

Comparative costs involve determining which option provides the **best value for money** when products are sold in different quantities, sizes, or units.

> **Key Concept:** Best value is NOT always about the lowest price - it's about the **lowest cost per unit**.

---

## 1.2 Unit Pricing Method

### Definition
**Unit pricing** involves calculating the cost per single unit (e.g., per gram, per litre, per item) to enable fair comparison.

### Formula
$$\text{Unit Price} = \frac{\text{Total Cost}}{\text{Quantity}}$$

### Step-by-Step Method

**Step 1:** Identify the quantity and total cost for each option

**Step 2:** Calculate the unit price for each option using the formula

**Step 3:** Compare the unit prices

**Step 4:** Select the option with the lowest unit price

---

### Worked Example 1

> **Question:** A supermarket sells washing powder in two sizes:
> - Size A: 2.4 kg for £3.60
> - Size B: 4 kg for £5.80
> 
> Which offers better value for money?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate the unit price for Size A
$$\text{Unit Price}_A = \frac{£3.60}{2.4 \text{ kg}} = £1.50 \text{ per kg}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate the unit price for Size B
$$\text{Unit Price}_B = \frac{£5.80}{4 \text{ kg}} = £1.45 \text{ per kg}$$

**Step 3:** Compare
- Size A: £1.50 per kg
- Size B: £1.45 per kg

**Step 4:** Conclusion
**Size B offers better value** as it costs less per kilogram.

---

### Worked Example 2 (Multi-pack Comparison)

> **Question:** A shop sells pens in three different packs:
> - Pack A: 6 pens for £2.40
> - Pack B: 10 pens for £3.50
> - Pack C: 20 pens for £6.80
> 
> Which pack offers the best value?

**Solution:**

**Pack A:**
$$\text{Cost per pen} = \frac{£2.40}{6} = £0.40 \text{ per pen}$$

**Pack B:**
$$\text{Cost per pen} = \frac{£3.50}{10} = £0.35 \text{ per pen}$$

**Pack C:**
$$\text{Cost per pen} = \frac{£6.80}{20} = £0.34 \text{ per pen}$$

**Conclusion:** Pack C offers the best value at 34p per pen.

---

## 1.3 Comparing Different Units

Sometimes products are measured in different units. You must **convert to the same unit** before comparing.

### Worked Example 3

> **Question:** Compare:
> - Product X: 500 g for £2.00
> - Product Y: 1.2 kg for £4.50

**Solution:**

Convert to the same unit (grams):
- Product X: 500 g
- Product Y: 1.2 kg = 1200 g

**Unit prices:**
$$\text{Product X: } \frac{£2.00}{500 \text{ g}} = £0.004 \text{ per g} = 0.4\text{p per g}$$

$$\text{Product Y: } \frac{£4.50}{1200 \text{ g}} = £0.00375 \text{ per g} = 0.375\text{p per g}$$

**Product Y offers better value.**

---

## 1.4 Best Buy Problems with Offers

### Types of Offers
1. **Percentage discounts** (e.g., 20% off)
2. **Buy X get Y free** (e.g., buy 2 get 1 free)
3. **Multi-buy discounts** (e.g., 3 for 2)
4. **Fractional discounts** (e.g., $\frac{1}{3}$ off)

### Worked Example 4

> **Question:** Two shops sell identical boxes of biscuits:
> - Shop A: £2.40 per box, or **buy 2 get 1 free**
> - Shop B: £2.50 per box, or **20% off all prices**
> 
> Which shop offers the better deal when buying 6 boxes?

**Solution:**

**Shop A (Buy 2 get 1 free):**
- For every 3 boxes, you pay for 2
- You pay for: $6 \times \frac{2}{3} = 4$ boxes
- Total cost: $4 \times £2.40 = £9.60$

**Shop B (20% off):**
- Price per box: $£2.50 \times 0.80 = £2.00$
- Total cost: $6 \times £2.00 = £12.00$

**Shop A is cheaper** at £9.60 compared to £12.00.

---

> ** Common Mistake Alert:** When comparing offers, always calculate the **total cost for the same quantity** you need to buy. Don't just compare the offers in isolation.

---

## 1.5 Quantity Adjustments

Sometimes you need to buy a specific amount, but products don't come in that exact quantity.

### Worked Example 5

> **Question:** A painter needs exactly 15 litres of paint.
> - 5-litre tin costs £18
> - 2-litre tin costs £8
> - 1-litre tin costs £5
> 
> Find the cheapest combination to get exactly 15 litres.

**Solution:**

| Combination | Cost Calculation | Total Cost |
|-------------|------------------|------------|
| 3 × 5L | $3 \times £18$ | £54 |
| 2 × 5L + 2 × 2L + 1 × 1L | $2 \times £18 + 2 \times £8 + £5$ | £47 |
| 1 × 5L + 5 × 2L | $£18 + 5 \times £8$ | £58 |
| 7 × 2L + 1 × 1L | $7 \times £8 + £5$ | £61 |

**Cheapest option:** 2 × 5L + 2 × 2L + 1 × 1L = **£47**

# Topic 2: Taxation (Salaries and Income Tax)

## 2.1 Understanding Income

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Gross Pay** | Total earnings before any deductions |
| **Net Pay** | Take-home pay after all deductions |
| **Deductions** | Money taken from gross pay (tax, NI, pensions) |

### Formula
$$\text{Net Pay} = \text{Gross Pay} - \text{Total Deductions}$$

---

## 2.2 Types of Income

### 2.2.1 Salary
- Fixed annual amount paid in equal monthly/weekly instalments
- Does not vary with hours worked

### Monthly Salary Calculation
$$\text{Monthly Salary} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{12}$$

### Weekly Salary Calculation
$$\text{Weekly Salary} = \frac{\text{Annual Salary}}{52}$$

---

### Worked Example 6

> **Question:** Ahmed has an annual salary of £31,200.
> (a) Calculate his monthly gross pay.
> (b) If his total monthly deductions are £620, find his net monthly pay.

**Solution:**

**(a) Monthly gross pay:**
$$\text{Monthly Pay} = \frac{£31,200}{12} = £2,600$$

**(b) Net monthly pay:**
$$\text{Net Pay} = £2,600 - £620 = £1,980$$

---

### 2.2.2 Hourly Wages

### Formula
$$\text{Wages} = \text{Hours Worked} \times \text{Hourly Rate}$$

### Overtime Pay
Overtime is often paid at a higher rate:
- **Time-and-a-half:** $1.5 \times$ normal rate
- **Double time:** $2 \times$ normal rate

$$\text{Overtime Pay} = \text{Overtime Hours} \times \text{Overtime Rate}$$

---

### Worked Example 7

> **Question:** Maria earns £12.50 per hour for a 35-hour week. Overtime is paid at time-and-a-half. Last week she worked 42 hours. Calculate her gross pay.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate basic pay
$$\text{Basic Pay} = 35 \times £12.50 = £437.50$$

**Step 2:** Calculate overtime hours
$$\text{Overtime Hours} = 42 - 35 = 7 \text{ hours}$$

**Step 3:** Calculate overtime rate
$$\text{Overtime Rate} = £12.50 \times 1.5 = £18.75 \text{ per hour}$$

**Step 4:** Calculate overtime pay
$$\text{Overtime Pay} = 7 \times £18.75 = £131.25$$

**Step 5:** Calculate total gross pay
$$\text{Gross Pay} = £437.50 + £131.25 = £568.75$$

---

### 2.2.3 Commission

Commission is earnings based on sales performance.

### Formula
$$\text{Commission} = \text{Percentage Rate} \times \text{Sales Value}$$

### Total Pay with Commission
$$\text{Total Pay} = \text{Basic Salary} + \text{Commission}$$

---

### Worked Example 8

> **Question:** A salesperson earns a basic salary of £22,000 per year plus 3% commission on all sales. Last year her total sales were £180,000. Calculate her total annual earnings.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate commission
$$\text{Commission} = \frac{3}{100} \times £180,000 = £5,400$$

**Step 2:** Calculate total earnings
$$\text{Total Earnings} = £22,000 + £5,400 = £27,400$$

---

## 2.3 Income Tax Calculation

### 2.3.1 Key Tax Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Personal Allowance** | Amount you can earn tax-free each year |
| **Taxable Income** | Income above the personal allowance |
| **Tax Bands/Rates** | Different percentages of tax for different income levels |
| **Tax Code** | Code that indicates your tax-free allowance |

---

### 2.3.2 Income Tax Bands (UK Example)

For exam purposes, tax bands will be given in the question. A typical structure:

| Tax Band | Taxable Income | Tax Rate |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| Personal Allowance | Up to £12,570 | 0% |
| Basic Rate | £12,571 to £50,270 | 20% |
| Higher Rate | £50,271 to £125,140 | 40% |
| Additional Rate | Over £125,140 | 45% |

> ** Note:** Tax bands and personal allowance may vary by year. Always use the values given in the exam question.

---

### 2.3.3 Step-by-Step Tax Calculation Method

**Step 1:** Identify the gross annual income

**Step 2:** Subtract the personal allowance to find taxable income

**Step 3:** Apply each tax band to the appropriate portion of income

**Step 4:** Sum all tax amounts

**Step 5:** Calculate net pay if required

---

### Worked Example 9

> **Question:** Calculate the annual income tax for someone earning £35,000 per year.
> 
> Personal allowance: £12,570
> Basic rate (20%): £12,571 to £50,270

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Gross annual income = £35,000

**Step 2:** Calculate taxable income
$$\text{Taxable Income} = £35,000 - £12,570 = £22,430$$

**Step 3:** Apply tax band
- All £22,430 falls within the basic rate band
$$\text{Tax} = 20\% \times £22,430 = £4,486$$

**Verification:**
- Taxable income: £22,430
- Tax: $0.20 \times 22,430 = 4,486$ ✓

---

### Worked Example 10 (Multiple Tax Bands)

> **Question:** Calculate the annual income tax for someone earning £68,000.
> 
> Personal allowance: £12,570
> Basic rate (20%): £12,571 to £50,270
> Higher rate (40%): £50,271 to £125,140

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate taxable income
$$\text{Taxable Income} = £68,000 - £12,570 = £55,430$$

**Step 2:** Split income into tax bands

| Band | Amount | Calculation |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Basic Rate | £50,270 - £12,570 = £37,700 | Full band used |
| Higher Rate | £55,430 - £37,700 = £17,730 | Remaining income |

**Step 3:** Calculate tax for each band
$$\text{Basic Rate Tax} = 20\% \times £37,700 = £7,540$$
$$\text{Higher Rate Tax} = 40\% \times £17,730 = £7,092$$

**Step 4:** Total tax
$$\text{Total Tax} = £7,540 + £7,092 = £14,632$$

---

## 2.4 National Insurance (NI)

National Insurance is another deduction from earnings. For exams, NI rates will be provided.

### Typical NI Structure
- No NI on earnings below a threshold
- A main rate (e.g., 12%) on earnings between thresholds
- An additional rate (e.g., 2%) on higher earnings

### Worked Example 11

> **Question:** Calculate National Insurance for weekly earnings of £850.
> 
> NI thresholds: No NI on first £242 per week
> Main rate: 12% on £242 to £967 per week
> Additional rate: 2% on earnings above £967 per week

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify which bands apply
- Earnings: £850
- First £242: No NI
- £242 to £850: Main rate (since £850 < £967)

**Step 2:** Calculate taxable earnings for NI
$$\text{NI-able Earnings} = £850 - £242 = £608$$

**Step 3:** Calculate NI
$$\text{NI} = 12\% \times £608 = £72.96$$

---

## 2.5 Complete Pay Calculation

### Worked Example 12

> **Question:** James earns £52,000 per year.
> 
> Personal allowance: £12,570
> Basic rate tax (20%): on next £37,700
> Higher rate tax (40%): on remaining income
> National Insurance: 12% on earnings above £12,570
> 
> Calculate his annual net pay.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate taxable income
$$\text{Taxable Income} = £52,000 - £12,570 = £39,430$$

**Step 2:** Calculate income tax
$$\text{Basic Rate Tax} = 20\% \times £37,700 = £7,540$$
$$\text{Higher Rate Tax} = 40\% \times (£39,430 - £37,700) = 40\% \times £1,730 = £692$$
$$\text{Total Income Tax} = £7,540 + £692 = £8,232$$

**Step 3:** Calculate NI
$$\text{NI} = 12\% \times £39,430 = £4,731.60$$

**Step 4:** Calculate net pay
$$\text{Net Pay} = £52,000 - £8,232 - £4,731.60 = £39,036.40$$

---

> ** Common Mistake Alert:** Always check if NI is calculated from gross pay or from taxable income. Read the question carefully!

# Topic 3: Foreign Currency Exchange

## 3.1 Exchange Rates

### Definition
An **exchange rate** is the value of one currency expressed in terms of another currency.

### Types of Exchange Rate Notation

| Notation | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| £1 = $1.25 | One pound buys 1.25 dollars |
| €1 = £0.85 | One euro buys 0.85 pounds |
| $1 = €0.92 | One dollar buys 0.92 euros |

---

## 3.2 Converting Currencies

### Method 1: Using Multiplication

When converting **from the base currency to the foreign currency:**

$$\text{Foreign Currency} = \text{Home Currency} \times \text{Exchange Rate}$$

### Method 2: Using Division

When converting **from foreign currency to the base currency:**

$$\text{Home Currency} = \frac{\text{Foreign Currency}}{\text{Exchange Rate}}$$

---

### Worked Example 13

> **Question:** The exchange rate is £1 = $1.28
> (a) Convert £450 to dollars.
> (b) Convert $800 to pounds.

**Solution:**

**(a) Converting pounds to dollars:**
$$\$ = £450 \times 1.28 = \$576$$

**(b) Converting dollars to pounds:**
$$£ = \frac{\$800}{1.28} = £625$$

---

### Worked Example 14 (Using Proportion)

> **Question:** The exchange rate is £1 = €1.17
> How many pounds can be exchanged for €234?

**Solution:**

**Method 1: Division**
$$£ = \frac{€234}{1.17} = £200$$

**Method 2: Using proportion**
If £1 = €1.17
Then $£x = €234$
$$x = \frac{234}{1.17} = 200$$

**Answer: £200**

---

## 3.3 Currency Conversion Tables

### Worked Example 15

> **Question:** Use the exchange rates below to convert $500 to euros.
> 
> | Currency | Rate |
> |----------|------|
> | $1 | = £0.79 |
> | €1 | = £0.86 |

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Convert dollars to pounds
$$£ = \$500 \times 0.79 = £395$$

**Step 2:** Convert pounds to euros
$$€ = \frac{£395}{0.86} = €459.30 \text{ (to 2 d.p.)}$$

---

## 3.4 Buying and Selling Currency

### Bank Rates vs Market Rates

When exchanging money at banks or bureaux de change:
- **Selling rate:** The rate at which the bank sells foreign currency to you
- **Buying rate:** The rate at which the bank buys foreign currency from you

> ** Key Insight:** The bank always gives you the less favourable rate. This is how they make profit on currency exchange.

---

### Worked Example 16

> **Question:** A bank offers these rates for converting pounds to euros:
> - Bank buys euros: £1 = €1.20
> - Bank sells euros: £1 = €1.15
> 
> (a) You want to buy €500 for your holiday. How much will it cost in pounds?
> (b) You return with €60 left. How many pounds will you get back?

**Solution:**

**(a) Buying euros:**
- The bank sells you euros at £1 = €1.15
$$£ = \frac{€500}{1.15} = £434.78$$

**(b) Selling euros back:**
- The bank buys euros at £1 = €1.20
$$£ = \frac{€60}{1.20} = £50$$

---

> **Exam Insight:** Notice the spread: buying euros costs more pounds (€1.15 per £1), but selling them back gives fewer pounds (€1.20 per £1). This difference is the bank's profit margin.

---

## 3.5 Commission and Fees

### Types of Fees
1. **Fixed fee:** A set charge per transaction
2. **Percentage commission:** A percentage of the amount exchanged
3. **Minimum fee:** The least amount charged regardless of percentage

### Total Cost Formula
$$\text{Total Cost} = \text{Currency Amount} + \text{Commission/Fees}$$

---

### Worked Example 17

> **Question:** A currency exchange charges 2.5% commission with a minimum fee of £5.
> Exchange rate: £1 = $1.30
> 
> (a) How much will it cost to buy $500?
> (b) How many dollars will £200 buy?

**Solution:**

**(a) Cost to buy $500:**
$$£ \text{ needed} = \frac{\$500}{1.30} = £384.62$$

Commission: $2.5\% \times £384.62 = £9.62$
Since £9.62 > £5, commission = £9.62

$$\text{Total Cost} = £384.62 + £9.62 = £394.24$$

**(b) Dollars from £200:**
Commission: $2.5\% \times £200 = £5$
This equals the minimum fee, so commission = £5

$$\text{Amount exchanged} = £200 - £5 = £195$$
$$\$ = £195 \times 1.30 = \$253.50$$

---

## 3.6 Comparing Exchange Deals

### Worked Example 18

> **Question:** Two companies offer different deals for converting £1000 to euros:
> 
> **Company A:** £1 = €1.12, no commission
> 
> **Company B:** £1 = €1.18, 3% commission
> 
> Which gives more euros?

**Solution:**

**Company A:**
$$€ = £1000 \times 1.12 = €1120$$

**Company B:**
$$\text{Commission} = 3\% \times £1000 = £30$$
$$\text{Amount exchanged} = £1000 - £30 = £970$$
$$€ = £970 \times 1.18 = €1144.60$$

**Company B gives more euros** (€1144.60 vs €1120)

---

## 3.7 Exchange Rate Changes

### Impact of Rate Changes

When exchange rates change:
- A **stronger pound** means you get more foreign currency
- A **weaker pound** means you get less foreign currency

### Worked Example 19

> **Question:** Last year, £1 = $1.45. This year, £1 = $1.22.
> 
> (a) Has the pound strengthened or weakened against the dollar?
> (b) A UK tourist wants to buy $1000. How much more/less does it cost this year compared to last year?

**Solution:**

**(a)** The pound has **weakened** - you now get fewer dollars per pound.

**(b) Last year:**
$$£ = \frac{\$1000}{1.45} = £689.66$$

**This year:**
$$£ = \frac{\$1000}{1.22} = £819.67$$

**Difference:**
$$£819.67 - £689.66 = £130.01 \text{ more expensive}$$

# Exam Focus & Strategy Section

## Frequently Tested Question Types

### Type 1: Best Value Comparison
**Pattern:** Compare 2-3 products with different quantities/prices

**Strategy:**
1. Always calculate unit prices
2. Convert to same units first if necessary
3. Show working clearly
4. State which is better value with reason

---

### Type 2: Income Tax Calculations
**Pattern:** Calculate tax for someone earning in one or more tax bands

**Strategy:**
1. Draw a table to organize tax bands
2. Work through each band systematically
3. Check which bands are fully/partially used
4. Total all tax amounts at the end

---

### Type 3: Currency Conversion Multi-step
**Pattern:** Convert through an intermediate currency or compare deals

**Strategy:**
1. Identify the chain of conversions needed
2. Work step by step
3. Check units at each stage
4. Round appropriately (usually 2 d.p.)

---

### Type 4: Real-world Financial Decisions
**Pattern:** Calculate costs for different options and make a recommendation

**Strategy:**
1. Calculate total cost for each option
2. Consider all fees and charges
3. Present a clear comparison
4. State the recommendation with numerical support

---

| Command Word | What to Do |
|--------------|------------|
| **Calculate** | Show working, give final answer |

## How to Gain Full Marks

### M Marks (Method Marks)
- Awarded for correct method
- Must show working clearly
- Even if answer is wrong, you can get method marks

### A Marks (Accuracy Marks)
- Awarded for correct answer
- Depends on method mark being earned
- Must be accurate (correct rounding)

### Common Marking Points
1. **Unit conversion** shown (1 mark)
2. **Correct unit price calculation** (1-2 marks)
3. **Comparison statement** with values (1 mark)
4. **Conclusion** clearly stated (1 mark)

---

## Key Exam Tips

> **✅ DO:**
> - Show all working
> - Use correct units throughout
> - Round only at the final answer
> - Check answers are reasonable
> - State conclusions clearly

> **❌ DON'T:**
> - Skip working steps
> - Mix up buy/sell rates
> - Forget to subtract personal allowance
> - Apply wrong tax rates to wrong bands
> - Round prematurely
