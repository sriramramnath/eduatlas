# 1. Standard Form

## 1.1 Understanding Standard Form

**Standard form** (also called scientific notation) is a way of writing very large or very small numbers in a compact format.

$$\text{Standard Form: } a \times 10^n$$

where:
- $a$ is a number between **1 and 10** (i.e., $1 \leq a < 10$)
- $n$ is an **integer** (positive, negative, or zero)

### Why Use Standard Form?

| Number | Ordinary Form | Standard Form |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Distance to Sun | 150,000,000,000 m | $1.5 \times 10^{11}$ m |
| Size of virus | 0.00000002 m | $2 \times 10^{-8}$ m |
| Speed of light | 300,000,000 m/s | $3 \times 10^8$ m/s |

>  **Exam Insight:** Standard form is essential in science and engineering. The IGCSE frequently tests conversions and calculations involving standard form.

---

## 1.2 Converting to Standard Form

### Converting Large Numbers (Positive Powers)

**Rule:** Count how many places the decimal point moves LEFT to get a number between 1 and 10. This count is the power of 10.

**Worked Example 1:**
Write 45,000 in standard form.

**Solution:**
$$45,000 = 4.5 \times 10^4$$

**Step-by-step:**
1. Identify the decimal point position: $45000.$ (after the last digit)
2. Move decimal left until we get a number between 1 and 10: $4.5$
3. Count the moves: 4 places left → power is $+4$
4. Write: $4.5 \times 10^4$

**Worked Example 2:**
Write 6,380,000 in standard form.

**Solution:**
$$6,380,000 = 6.38 \times 10^6$$

The decimal moves 6 places left.

### Converting Small Numbers (Negative Powers)

**Rule:** Count how many places the decimal point moves RIGHT to get a number between 1 and 10. This count is the **negative** power of 10.

**Worked Example 3:**
Write 0.00047 in standard form.

**Solution:**
$$0.00047 = 4.7 \times 10^{-4}$$

**Step-by-step:**
1. Start with 0.00047
2. Move decimal right until we get a number between 1 and 10: $4.7$
3. Count the moves: 4 places right → power is $-4$
4. Write: $4.7 \times 10^{-4}$

**Worked Example 4:**
Write 0.00000823 in standard form.

**Solution:**
$$0.00000823 = 8.23 \times 10^{-6}$$

The decimal moves 6 places right.

>  **Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the power is NEGATIVE for small numbers. Remember: small numbers = negative powers.

---

## 1.3 Converting FROM Standard Form

### Positive Powers

**Worked Example 5:**
Calculate $3.7 \times 10^5$

**Solution:**
$$3.7 \times 10^5 = 3.7 \times 100,000 = 370,000$$

**Method:** Move the decimal point 5 places RIGHT.

$$3.7 \rightarrow 370000.$$

### Negative Powers

**Worked Example 6:**
Calculate $5.2 \times 10^{-3}$

**Solution:**
$$5.2 \times 10^{-3} = 5.2 \div 1000 = 0.0052$$

**Method:** Move the decimal point 3 places LEFT.

$$5.2 \rightarrow .0052$$

## 1.4 Calculations with Standard Form

### Multiplying Numbers in Standard Form

**Rule:** Multiply the $a$ values, add the powers of 10.

$$(a \times 10^m) \times (b \times 10^n) = (a \times b) \times 10^{m+n}$$

**Worked Example 7:**
Calculate $(2 \times 10^4) \times (3 \times 10^5)$

**Solution:**
$$(2 \times 10^4) \times (3 \times 10^5) = (2 \times 3) \times 10^{4+5} = 6 \times 10^9$$

**Worked Example 8:**
Calculate $(4 \times 10^3) \times (5 \times 10^2)$. Give your answer in standard form.

**Solution:**
$$(4 \times 10^3) \times (5 \times 10^2) = 20 \times 10^5$$

But $20$ is not between 1 and 10! We need to adjust:

$$20 \times 10^5 = 2 \times 10^1 \times 10^5 = 2 \times 10^6$$

>  **Exam Tip:** Always check if your answer is in correct standard form. The first number must be between 1 and 10.

### Dividing Numbers in Standard Form

**Rule:** Divide the $a$ values, subtract the powers of 10.

$$\frac{a \times 10^m}{b \times 10^n} = \frac{a}{b} \times 10^{m-n}$$

**Worked Example 9:**
Calculate $(8 \times 10^7) \div (2 \times 10^3)$

**Solution:**
$$\frac{8 \times 10^7}{2 \times 10^3} = \frac{8}{2} \times 10^{7-3} = 4 \times 10^4$$

**Worked Example 10:**
Calculate $(3 \times 10^4) \div (6 \times 10^2)$. Give your answer in standard form.

**Solution:**
$$\frac{3 \times 10^4}{6 \times 10^2} = \frac{3}{6} \times 10^{4-2} = 0.5 \times 10^2$$

But $0.5$ is not between 1 and 10! We need to adjust:

$$0.5 \times 10^2 = 5 \times 10^{-1} \times 10^2 = 5 \times 10^1$$

### Adding and Subtracting in Standard Form

**Key Principle:** To add or subtract, the powers of 10 must be the SAME.

**Worked Example 11:**
Calculate $(3.2 \times 10^4) + (5.7 \times 10^4)$

**Solution:**
Since powers are the same, simply add:
$$(3.2 + 5.7) \times 10^4 = 8.9 \times 10^4$$

**Worked Example 12:**
Calculate $(5 \times 10^6) + (3 \times 10^5)$

**Solution:**
Different powers - convert one to match the other.

**Method 1 - Convert second number:**
$$3 \times 10^5 = 0.3 \times 10^6$$
$$5 \times 10^6 + 0.3 \times 10^6 = 5.3 \times 10^6$$

**Method 2 - Convert to ordinary numbers:**
$$5 \times 10^6 = 5,000,000$$
$$3 \times 10^5 = 300,000$$
$$5,000,000 + 300,000 = 5,300,000 = 5.3 \times 10^6$$

# 2. Percentages - Basic Calculations

## 2.1 Understanding Percentages

**Percent** means "per hundred" or "out of 100".

The symbol % represents a fraction with denominator 100.

$$x\% = \frac{x}{100}$$

### Converting Between Forms

| Fraction | Decimal | Percentage |
|----------|---------|------------|
| $\frac{1}{2}$ | 0.5 | 50% |
| $\frac{1}{4}$ | 0.25 | 25% |
| $\frac{1}{5}$ | 0.2 | 20% |
| $\frac{3}{4}$ | 0.75 | 75% |
| $\frac{1}{3}$ | 0.333... | 33$\frac{1}{3}$% |

### Percentage → Decimal
**Rule:** Divide by 100

$$x\% = \frac{x}{100} = x \div 100$$

**Worked Example 13:**
Convert 35% to a decimal.

**Solution:**
$$35\% = 35 \div 100 = 0.35$$

### Decimal → Percentage
**Rule:** Multiply by 100

$$x = x \times 100\%$$

**Worked Example 14:**
Convert 0.075 to a percentage.

**Solution:**
$$0.075 \times 100 = 7.5\%$$

### Percentage → Fraction
**Rule:** Write over 100 and simplify

**Worked Example 15:**
Express 36% as a fraction in its simplest form.

**Solution:**
$$36\% = \frac{36}{100} = \frac{9}{25}$$

>  **Exam Tip:** Memorise common conversions:
> - $10\% = \frac{1}{10} = 0.1$
> - $25\% = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25$
> - $50\% = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$
> - $75\% = \frac{3}{4} = 0.75$

---

## 2.2 Finding a Percentage of a Quantity

### Method 1: Using Decimals

**Formula:**
$$x\% \text{ of } y = \frac{x}{100} \times y$$

**Worked Example 16:**
Find 15% of 240.

**Solution:**
$$15\% \text{ of } 240 = 0.15 \times 240 = 36$$

### Method 2: Using Fractions

**Worked Example 17:**
Find 25% of 180.

**Solution:**
$$25\% \text{ of } 180 = \frac{1}{4} \times 180 = 45$$

### Method 3: Building Up (Non-Calculator)

This method is excellent for non-calculator papers.

**Key Building Blocks:**
- 10% = divide by 10
- 5% = half of 10%
- 1% = divide by 100
- 50% = divide by 2

**Worked Example 18:**
Find 17% of £350 (non-calculator method).

**Solution:**
$$\begin{align}
10\% \text{ of } £350 &= £35 \\
5\% \text{ of } £350 &= £17.50 \\
1\% \text{ of } £350 &= £3.50 \\
\end{align}$$

Therefore:
$$17\% = 10\% + 5\% + 1\% + 1\%$$
$$= £35 + £17.50 + £3.50 + £3.50 = £59.50$$

**Worked Example 19:**
Find 23% of 560 kg.

**Solution:**
$$\begin{align}
10\% \text{ of } 560 &= 56 \\
20\% \text{ of } 560 &= 112 \\
1\% \text{ of } 560 &= 5.6 \\
3\% \text{ of } 560 &= 5.6 \times 3 = 16.8 \\
\end{align}$$

$$23\% = 20\% + 3\% = 112 + 16.8 = 128.8 \text{ kg}$$

---

## 2.3 Expressing One Quantity as a Percentage of Another

**Formula:**
$$\text{Percentage} = \frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} \times 100\%$$

**Worked Example 20:**
Express 18 as a percentage of 72.

**Solution:**
$$\frac{18}{72} \times 100\% = \frac{1}{4} \times 100\% = 25\%$$

**Worked Example 21:**
In a class of 35 students, 14 wear glasses. What percentage wear glasses?

**Solution:**
$$\frac{14}{35} \times 100\% = \frac{2}{5} \times 100\% = 40\%$$

>  **Common Mistake:** Mixing up which number is the "part" and which is the "whole". Always divide the smaller number by the larger number when finding "what percentage of A is B?"

# 3. Percentage Increase and Decrease

## 3.1 Percentage Increase

**Concept:** When a quantity increases by a percentage, we find the increase and add it to the original.

### Method 1: Two-Step Method

**Step 1:** Calculate the increase (percentage of original)
**Step 2:** Add to original

**Worked Example 22:**
A jacket costs £80. Its price increases by 15%. Find the new price.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Increase} = 15\% \text{ of } £80 = 0.15 \times 80 = £12$$
$$\text{New price} = £80 + £12 = £92$$

### Method 2: Multiplier Method (One Step)

**Multiplier for increase:** $1 + \frac{\text{percentage}}{100}$

For an increase of $x\%$, the multiplier is $1 + \frac{x}{100}$

**Worked Example 23:**
A jacket costs £80. Its price increases by 15%. Find the new price using multipliers.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Multiplier} = 1 + 0.15 = 1.15$$
$$\text{New price} = £80 \times 1.15 = £92$$

>  **Why this works:** $100\% + 15\% = 115\%$. The new price is 115% of the original.

**Worked Example 24:**
The population of a town is 45,000. It increases by 8%. Find the new population.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Multiplier} = 1.08$$
$$\text{New population} = 45,000 \times 1.08 = 48,600$$

---

## 3.2 Percentage Decrease

**Concept:** When a quantity decreases by a percentage, we find the decrease and subtract it from the original.

### Method 1: Two-Step Method

**Step 1:** Calculate the decrease (percentage of original)
**Step 2:** Subtract from original

**Worked Example 25:**
A car was worth £12,000. Its value decreases by 20%. Find the new value.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Decrease} = 20\% \text{ of } £12,000 = 0.2 \times 12,000 = £2,400$$
$$\text{New value} = £12,000 - £2,400 = £9,600$$

### Method 2: Multiplier Method (One Step)

**Multiplier for decrease:** $1 - \frac{\text{percentage}}{100}$

For a decrease of $x\%$, the multiplier is $1 - \frac{x}{100}$

**Worked Example 26:**
A car was worth £12,000. Its value decreases by 20%. Find the new value using multipliers.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Multiplier} = 1 - 0.20 = 0.80$$
$$\text{New value} = £12,000 \times 0.80 = £9,600$$

>  **Why this works:** $100\% - 20\% = 80\%$. The new value is 80% of the original.

**Worked Example 27:**
A shop has a sale with 35% off all items. A TV originally costs £480. What is the sale price?

**Solution:**
$$\text{Multiplier} = 1 - 0.35 = 0.65$$
$$\text{Sale price} = £480 \times 0.65 = £312$$

---

## 3.3 Finding the Percentage Change

**Formula:**
$$\text{Percentage Change} = \frac{\text{change}}{\text{original}} \times 100\%$$

For increase: $\frac{\text{new} - \text{original}}{\text{original}} \times 100\%$

For decrease: $\frac{\text{original} - \text{new}}{\text{original}} \times 100\%$

**Worked Example 28:**
A house price increases from £180,000 to £207,000. Calculate the percentage increase.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Increase} = £207,000 - £180,000 = £27,000$$
$$\text{Percentage increase} = \frac{27,000}{180,000} \times 100\% = 15\%$$

**Worked Example 29:**
A shop reduces a jacket from £65 to £52. Calculate the percentage decrease.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Decrease} = £65 - £52 = £13$$
$$\text{Percentage decrease} = \frac{13}{65} \times 100\% = 20\%$$

>  **Common Mistake:** Dividing by the wrong number. Always divide by the ORIGINAL value, not the new value.

---

## 3.4 Finding the Original Value (Reverse Percentages)

This is when you know the value AFTER a percentage change and need to find the ORIGINAL value.

### Key Concept: Working Backwards

If the new value is AFTER an increase, it represents MORE than 100% of the original.
If the new value is AFTER a decrease, it represents LESS than 100% of the original.

**Worked Example 30:**
After a 20% increase, a population is 36,000. What was the original population?

**Solution:**
$$\text{New value} = 120\% \text{ of original}$$
$$36,000 = 120\% \times \text{original}$$
$$\text{Original} = \frac{36,000}{1.20} = 30,000$$

**Alternative method:**
$$120\% \rightarrow 36,000$$
$$1\% \rightarrow 36,000 \div 120 = 300$$
$$100\% \rightarrow 300 \times 100 = 30,000$$

**Worked Example 31:**
In a sale, an item costs £68 after a 15% discount. What was the original price?

**Solution:**
$$\text{New value} = 85\% \text{ of original}$$
$$£68 = 85\% \times \text{original}$$
$$\text{Original} = \frac{68}{0.85} = £80$$

>  **Common Mistake:** Many students calculate 15% of £68 and add it. This is WRONG! The percentage was applied to the ORIGINAL, not the new value.

**Worked Example 32:**
A car's value after one year is £17,850, which is a 5% depreciation from new. What was its original price?

**Solution:**
$$\text{New value} = 95\% \text{ of original}$$
$$£17,850 = 0.95 \times \text{original}$$
$$\text{Original} = \frac{17,850}{0.95} = £18,789.47 \text{ (to 2 d.p.)}$$

# 4. Multipliers

## 4.1 Understanding Multipliers

A **multiplier** is a decimal that represents a percentage change in a single number.

| Percentage Change | Multiplier | Explanation |
|-------------------|------------|-------------|
| Increase by 10% | 1.10 | $100\% + 10\% = 110\% = 1.10$ |
| Increase by 25% | 1.25 | $100\% + 25\% = 125\% = 1.25$ |
| Increase by 8% | 1.08 | $100\% + 8\% = 108\% = 1.08$ |
| Decrease by 10% | 0.90 | $100\% - 10\% = 90\% = 0.90$ |
| Decrease by 30% | 0.70 | $100\% - 30\% = 70\% = 0.70$ |
| Decrease by 15% | 0.85 | $100\% - 15\% = 85\% = 0.85$ |

### Quick Reference Table

| Type | Formula | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Increase by $x\%$ | Multiplier = $1 + \frac{x}{100}$ | Increase by 12%: $1.12$ |
| Decrease by $x\%$ | Multiplier = $1 - \frac{x}{100}$ | Decrease by 12%: $0.88$ |

---

## 4.2 Compound Percentage Changes

When a percentage change is applied repeatedly, we use **compound multipliers**.

### Formula for Compound Change

$$\text{Final Value} = \text{Original} \times \text{Multiplier}^n$$

where $n$ is the number of times the change is applied.

**Worked Example 33:**
A population of 50,000 increases by 4% each year for 3 years. What is the population after 3 years?

**Solution:**
$$\text{Multiplier} = 1.04$$
$$\text{Population after 3 years} = 50,000 \times 1.04^3$$
$$= 50,000 \times 1.124864 = 56,243.2 \approx 56,243$$

**Worked Example 34:**
A car worth £20,000 depreciates by 15% each year. What is its value after 4 years?

**Solution:**
$$\text{Multiplier} = 1 - 0.15 = 0.85$$
$$\text{Value after 4 years} = 20,000 \times 0.85^4$$
$$= 20,000 \times 0.52200625 = £10,440.13 \text{ (to 2 d.p.)}$$

---

## 4.3 Finding the Compound Interest Rate

Sometimes we need to find the annual percentage rate given the total change.

**Worked Example 35:**
A house valued at £200,000 increases to £231,525 over 3 years. Assuming a constant annual percentage increase, find the annual percentage increase.

**Solution:**
$$\text{Final} = \text{Original} \times (1 + r)^3$$
where $r$ is the annual rate (as a decimal).

$$231,525 = 200,000 \times (1 + r)^3$$
$$(1 + r)^3 = \frac{231,525}{200,000} = 1.157625$$
$$1 + r = \sqrt[3]{1.157625} = 1.05$$
$$r = 0.05 = 5\%$$

The annual increase is **5%**.

---

## 4.4 Compound Interest

**Compound Interest Formula:**

$$A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^n$$

where:
- $A$ = final amount
- $P$ = principal (original amount)
- $r$ = interest rate per period (as a percentage)
- $n$ = number of periods

**Worked Example 36:**
£5,000 is invested at 4% compound interest per annum for 6 years. Find the final amount.

**Solution:**
$$A = 5000 \times \left(1 + \frac{4}{100}\right)^6$$
$$A = 5000 \times 1.04^6$$
$$A = 5000 \times 1.265319... = £6,326.60 \text{ (to 2 d.p.)}$$

**Worked Example 37:**
How much should be invested now at 6% compound interest to have £10,000 in 5 years?

**Solution:**
$$A = P \times 1.06^5$$
$$10,000 = P \times 1.338225...$$
$$P = \frac{10,000}{1.338225...} = £7,472.58 \text{ (to 2 d.p.)}$$

## 🔥 Frequently Tested Question Types

### Standard Form
1. **Converting** between ordinary numbers and standard form
2. **Calculations** involving multiplication and division

### Percentages
1. **Finding percentages** of quantities (calculator and non-calculator)
2. **Percentage increase/decrease** problems
3. **Reverse percentages** (finding original value)
4. **Compound interest** and depreciation
5. **Profit and loss** calculations
6. **Multi-step problems** combining several percentage concepts

---

## 🧩 Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

### Pattern 1: The "Of" Means Multiply
When you see "X% of Y", always convert X% to a decimal or fraction and multiply.

### Pattern 2: Reverse Percentage Trap
If you know the final value after a percentage change:
- Identify whether it's an increase or decrease
- Determine what percentage the final value represents
- Divide to find the original

### Pattern 3: Compound Changes
For repeated percentage changes:
- Find the multiplier
- Raise to the power of the number of periods
- Multiply by the original amount

### Pattern 4: Standard Form Calculations
- For multiply/divide: work with $a$ values and powers separately
- For add/subtract: make powers the same first
- Always check final answer is in correct form

1. ✅ Check that $1 \leq a < 10$ in your answer
2. ✅ Show clear working in calculations
3. ✅ Remember negative powers for small numbers
4. ✅ Adjust the answer if $a$ is outside the range

### For Percentage Questions:
1. ✅ Identify whether it's increase or decrease
2. ✅ Use multipliers for efficiency
3. ✅ Show the multiplier method clearly
4. ✅ In reverse percentages, always DIVIDE by the multiplier
5. ✅ Check your answer makes sense (original should be larger/smaller as expected)

### General Tips:
1. ✅ Show all working - marks are for method
2. ✅ Check answers by estimation
3. ✅ Include units (£, kg, etc.) in your answer
4. ✅ Round only at the final step
5. ✅ Read the question twice before starting

---

##  Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---------|------------------|
| Standard form with $a \geq 10$ | Adjust: $25 \times 10^4 = 2.5 \times 10^5$ |
| Forgetting negative power for small numbers | 0.005 = $5 \times 10^{-3}$ (not $10^3$) |
| Reverse percentage: adding to new value | Always divide new value by multiplier |
| Confusing increase and decrease | Increase: multiply by $> 1$, Decrease: multiply by $< 1$ |
| Dividing by wrong value in % change | Always divide by the ORIGINAL value |
| Using simple interest formula for compound | Compound: $P(1+r)^n$, Simple: $P(1+rn)$ |
