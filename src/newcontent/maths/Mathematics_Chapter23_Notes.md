# 1. Distance-Time Graphs

## 1.1 Introduction to Distance-Time Graphs

A **distance-time graph** shows how the distance travelled by an object changes over time.

**Key Concept:**
- **Horizontal axis (x-axis):** Time (usually in seconds, minutes, or hours)
- **Vertical axis (y-axis):** Distance from a fixed point (usually in metres, kilometres, or miles)

---

## 1.2 Interpreting Distance-Time Graphs

### The Gradient Represents Speed

> **đź“Ś Key Formula:**
> $$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance Travelled}}{\text{Time Taken}} = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}$$

The **gradient (slope)** of a distance-time graph gives the **speed** of the object.

$$\text{Gradient} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\text{change in distance}}{\text{change in time}} = \text{speed}$$

---

### Different Sections of Distance-Time Graphs

| Gradient | Interpretation |
|----------|----------------|
| **Positive gradient** | Object moving away from starting point (positive speed) |
| **Zero gradient (horizontal line)** | Object is **stationary** (not moving) |
| **Steeper positive gradient** | Object moving faster |
| **Negative gradient** | Object returning towards starting point |
| **Steeper negative gradient** | Object returning faster |

---

## 1.3 Worked Examples

### Example 1: Finding Speed from a Distance-Time Graph

**Question:** A distance-time graph shows a straight line from the origin to the point (20 s, 100 m). Calculate the speed.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify the coordinates
- Starting point: $(0, 0)$
- Ending point: $(20, 100)$

**Step 2:** Calculate the gradient
$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{change in distance}}{\text{change in time}} = \frac{100 - 0}{20 - 0} = \frac{100}{20} = 5 \text{ m/s}$$

**Answer:** The speed is **5 m/s**

---

### Example 2: Multi-Stage Journey

**Question:** The distance-time graph below shows a journey:

| Section | Time (s) | Distance (m) |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| A | 0 to 10 | 0 to 50 |
| B | 10 to 30 | 50 to 50 (horizontal) |
| C | 30 to 50 | 50 to 150 |

Find:
(a) The speed in section A
(b) What happens in section B
(c) The speed in section C

**Solution:**

**(a) Speed in section A:**
$$\text{Speed}_A = \frac{50 - 0}{10 - 0} = \frac{50}{10} = 5 \text{ m/s}$$

**(b) Section B:**
- The line is horizontal (zero gradient)
- This means the object is **stationary** (stopped) for 20 seconds

**(c) Speed in section C:**
$$\text{Speed}_C = \frac{150 - 50}{50 - 30} = \frac{100}{20} = 5 \text{ m/s}$$

---

### Example 3: Finding Average Speed

**Question:** A person walks 3 km in 30 minutes, rests for 10 minutes, then walks a further 2 km in 20 minutes. Calculate the average speed for the whole journey.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate total distance
$$\text{Total distance} = 3 + 2 = 5 \text{ km}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate total time
$$\text{Total time} = 30 + 10 + 20 = 60 \text{ minutes} = 1 \text{ hour}$$

**Step 3:** Calculate average speed
$$\text{Average speed} = \frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Total time}} = \frac{5}{1} = 5 \text{ km/h}$$

> ** Common Mistake:** Do NOT average the individual speeds. Always use total distance Ă· total time.

---

## 1.4 Curved Distance-Time Graphs

When the distance-time graph is **curved**, the object is **accelerating or decelerating** (changing speed).

- **Curving upwards (getting steeper):** Speed is **increasing** (acceleration)
- **Curving downwards (getting less steep):** Speed is **decreasing** (deceleration)

### Finding Instantaneous Speed

To find the speed at a particular instant on a curved distance-time graph:

1. Draw a **tangent** to the curve at that point
2. Calculate the **gradient of the tangent**
3. This gradient equals the instantaneous speed

# 2. Speed Interpretation

## 2.1 Understanding Speed

**Speed** is a measure of how fast an object is moving.

> **đź“Ś Definition:**
> $$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}$$

**Units of Speed:**
- metres per second (m/s)
- kilometres per hour (km/h)
- miles per hour (mph)

---

## 2.2 Average Speed vs Instantaneous Speed

| Type | Definition | How to Find |
|------|------------|-------------|
| **Average Speed** | Total distance Ă· Total time | Gradient of line joining start and end points |
| **Instantaneous Speed** | Speed at a specific moment | Gradient of tangent at that point |

---

## 2.3 Converting Between Units

### km/h to m/s

To convert from km/h to m/s, **multiply by** $\frac{1000}{3600} = \frac{5}{18}$

$$\text{Speed in m/s} = \text{Speed in km/h} \times \frac{5}{18}$$

**Why?**
- 1 km = 1000 m
- 1 hour = 3600 seconds
- Therefore: $1 \text{ km/h} = \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{3600 \text{ s}} = \frac{5}{18} \text{ m/s}$

### m/s to km/h

To convert from m/s to km/h, **multiply by** $\frac{18}{5} = 3.6$

$$\text{Speed in km/h} = \text{Speed in m/s} \times 3.6$$

---

### Example 4: Unit Conversion

**Question:** Convert:
(a) 72 km/h to m/s
(b) 25 m/s to km/h

**Solution:**

**(a) 72 km/h to m/s:**
$$72 \text{ km/h} = 72 \times \frac{5}{18} = 72 \times 0.278 = 20 \text{ m/s}$$

**(b) 25 m/s to km/h:**
$$25 \text{ m/s} = 25 \times 3.6 = 90 \text{ km/h}$$

---

## 2.4 Distance = Speed Ă— Time

From the speed formula, we can derive:

> **đź“Ś Key Formulas:**
> $$\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$$
> $$\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$$

---

### Example 5: Calculating Distance

**Question:** A car travels at an average speed of 54 km/h for 2 hours 30 minutes. How far does it travel?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Convert time to hours
$$\text{Time} = 2 \text{ h } 30 \text{ min} = 2.5 \text{ h}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate distance
$$\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time} = 54 \times 2.5 = 135 \text{ km}$$

# 3. Speed-Time Graphs

## 3.1 Introduction to Speed-Time Graphs

A **speed-time graph** shows how the speed of an object changes over time.

**Key Concept:**
- **Horizontal axis (x-axis):** Time
- **Vertical axis (y-axis):** Speed

---

## 3.2 Interpreting Speed-Time Graphs

### The Gradient Represents Acceleration

> **đź“Ś Key Formula:**
> $$\text{Acceleration} = \frac{\text{Change in Speed}}{\text{Time Taken}} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$$

The **gradient (slope)** of a speed-time graph gives the **acceleration**.

$$\text{Gradient} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\text{change in speed}}{\text{change in time}} = \text{acceleration}$$

---

### Different Sections of Speed-Time Graphs

| Shape | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| **Horizontal line (zero gradient)** | Constant speed (no acceleration) |
| **Positive gradient (sloping up)** | Speeding up (positive acceleration) |
| **Negative gradient (sloping down)** | Slowing down (negative acceleration / deceleration) |
| **Steeper gradient** | Greater acceleration/deceleration |

---

## 3.3 Distance from Speed-Time Graphs

> **đź“Ś Key Concept:**
> **The area under a speed-time graph represents the distance travelled.**

$$\text{Distance} = \text{Area under speed-time graph}$$

### Why is this true?

$$\text{Area} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time} = \text{Distance}$$

---

## 3.4 Calculating Areas Under Speed-Time Graphs

| Shape | Area Formula |
|-------|--------------|
| Rectangle | $\text{length} \times \text{width}$ |
| Triangle | $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$ |
| Trapezium | $\frac{1}{2}(a + b) \times h$ |

---

## 3.5 Worked Examples

### Example 6: Interpreting a Speed-Time Graph

**Question:** A speed-time graph shows:
- Section A: Straight line from (0, 0) to (10, 20)
- Section B: Horizontal line from (10, 20) to (30, 20)
- Section C: Straight line from (30, 20) to (40, 0)

Find:
(a) The acceleration in section A
(b) The speed in section B
(c) The deceleration in section C
(d) The total distance travelled

**Solution:**

**(a) Acceleration in section A:**
$$\text{Acceleration} = \frac{\text{change in speed}}{\text{time}} = \frac{20 - 0}{10 - 0} = \frac{20}{10} = 2 \text{ m/s}^2$$

**(b) Speed in section B:**
- The line is horizontal at speed = 20 m/s
- The object travels at **constant speed of 20 m/s** for 20 seconds

**(c) Deceleration in section C:**
$$\text{Deceleration} = \frac{0 - 20}{40 - 30} = \frac{-20}{10} = -2 \text{ m/s}^2$$

The deceleration is **2 m/sÂ˛** (we give deceleration as a positive value).

**(d) Total distance travelled:**

**Step 1:** Calculate area of each section

**Section A (Triangle):**
$$\text{Area}_A = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 20 = 100 \text{ m}$$

**Section B (Rectangle):**
$$\text{Area}_B = 20 \times 20 = 400 \text{ m}$$

**Section C (Triangle):**
$$\text{Area}_C = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 20 = 100 \text{ m}$$

**Step 2:** Total distance
$$\text{Total distance} = 100 + 400 + 100 = 600 \text{ m}$$

---

### Example 7: Trapezium Area

**Question:** A car accelerates uniformly from 10 m/s to 25 m/s in 8 seconds. Calculate the distance travelled during this time.

**Solution:**

**Method 1: Using the trapezium area formula**
$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(a + b) \times h = \frac{1}{2}(10 + 25) \times 8 = \frac{1}{2} \times 35 \times 8 = 140 \text{ m}$$

**Method 2: Using average speed**
$$\text{Average speed} = \frac{10 + 25}{2} = 17.5 \text{ m/s}$$
$$\text{Distance} = 17.5 \times 8 = 140 \text{ m}$$

**Answer:** 140 m

---

## 3.6 Curved Speed-Time Graphs

When the speed-time graph is curved:

- The **instantaneous acceleration** at any point = gradient of tangent at that point
- The **distance travelled** = area under the curve (estimated by counting squares or using approximation methods)

# 4. Acceleration

## 4.1 Definition of Acceleration

**Acceleration** is the rate of change of speed (or velocity).

> **đź“Ś Key Formula:**
> $$\text{Acceleration} = \frac{\text{Final Speed} - \text{Initial Speed}}{\text{Time Taken}}$$
> $$a = \frac{v - u}{t}$$

Where:
- $a$ = acceleration
- $v$ = final speed
- $u$ = initial speed
- $t$ = time

**Units:** m/sÂ˛ (metres per second squared)

---

## 4.2 Positive and Negative Acceleration

| Type | Meaning | Speed Change |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **Positive acceleration** | Object speeding up | $v > u$ |
| **Zero acceleration** | Constant speed | $v = u$ |
| **Negative acceleration** | Object slowing down (deceleration) | $v < u$ |

> ** Exam Note:** In exams, "deceleration" is usually given as a positive value (e.g., "deceleration of 3 m/sÂ˛" means acceleration of -3 m/sÂ˛).

---

## 4.3 Rearranging the Acceleration Formula

From $a = \frac{v - u}{t}$, we can derive:

> **đź“Ś Derived Formulas:**
> $$v = u + at$$
> $$u = v - at$$
> $$t = \frac{v - u}{a}$$

---

### Example 8: Calculating Acceleration

**Question:** A car accelerates from 15 m/s to 35 m/s in 5 seconds. Calculate its acceleration.

**Solution:**
$$a = \frac{v - u}{t} = \frac{35 - 15}{5} = \frac{20}{5} = 4 \text{ m/s}^2$$

---

### Example 9: Calculating Final Speed

**Question:** A cyclist travelling at 8 m/s accelerates at 2 m/sÂ˛ for 6 seconds. What is their final speed?

**Solution:**
$$v = u + at = 8 + (2 \times 6) = 8 + 12 = 20 \text{ m/s}$$

---

### Example 10: Calculating Time

**Question:** A train decelerates from 30 m/s to 10 m/s at a rate of 4 m/sÂ˛. How long does this take?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify the deceleration
$$a = -4 \text{ m/s}^2 \text{ (negative because it's deceleration)}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate time
$$t = \frac{v - u}{a} = \frac{10 - 30}{-4} = \frac{-20}{-4} = 5 \text{ seconds}$$

---

## 4.4 Uniform Acceleration Equations (Extension)

For **uniform (constant) acceleration**, these equations apply:

> **đź“Ś SUVAT Equations (for reference):**
> 
> $$v = u + at$$
> $$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$
> $$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$
> $$s = \frac{(u + v)t}{2}$$

Where:
- $s$ = displacement (distance)
- $u$ = initial velocity
- $v$ = final velocity
- $a$ = acceleration
- $t$ = time

> **đź“‹ Note:** These equations are useful to know but check your syllabus for whether they're required.

# 6. Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

##  Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|------------|
| **Calculating average speed by averaging individual speeds** | Use: $\frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}$ |
| **Forgetting to convert units** | Always check units match before calculating |
| **Confusing distance-time and speed-time graphs** | Remember: gradient of distance-time = speed; gradient of speed-time = acceleration |
| **Giving deceleration as a negative value** | State as positive with "deceleration" label |
| **Not including units in the answer** | Always write the unit (m/s, m/sÂ˛, m, s, etc.) |
| **Reading values incorrectly from axes** | Check scale carefully, especially if it doesn't start at zero |
| **Forgetting that area under speed-time graph = distance** | Always calculate area when asked for distance from speed-time graph |

# 8. Practice Problems

## Problem 1: Distance-Time Graph

A train travels between two stations. The distance-time graph for the journey is shown:

| Section | Time (minutes) | Distance (km) |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| A | 0 to 5 | 0 to 10 |
| B | 5 to 15 | 10 to 10 |
| C | 15 to 25 | 10 to 30 |

(a) Calculate the speed in section A.
(b) What happens during section B?
(c) Calculate the speed in section C.
(d) Calculate the average speed for the whole journey.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

**(a) Speed in section A:**
$$\text{Speed} = \frac{10}{5} = 2 \text{ km/min} = 120 \text{ km/h}$$

**(b) Section B:**
The train is stationary (stopped at a station) for 10 minutes.

**(c) Speed in section C:**
$$\text{Speed} = \frac{30 - 10}{25 - 15} = \frac{20}{10} = 2 \text{ km/min} = 120 \text{ km/h}$$

**(d) Average speed:**
$$\text{Average speed} = \frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Total time}} = \frac{30}{25} = 1.2 \text{ km/min} = 72 \text{ km/h}$$

</details>

## Problem 2: Speed-Time Graph

A car's speed-time graph shows:
- Accelerates from rest to 24 m/s in 8 seconds
- Travels at constant speed for 12 seconds
- Decelerates to rest in 6 seconds

(a) Calculate the acceleration.
(b) Calculate the total distance travelled.
(c) Calculate the average speed for the whole journey.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

**(a) Acceleration:**
$$a = \frac{24 - 0}{8} = 3 \text{ m/s}^2$$

**(b) Total distance:**
- Section 1 (triangle): $\frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times 24 = 96 \text{ m}$
- Section 2 (rectangle): $12 \times 24 = 288 \text{ m}$
- Section 3 (triangle): $\frac{1}{2} \times 6 \times 24 = 72 \text{ m}$
- **Total distance:** $96 + 288 + 72 = 456 \text{ m}$

**(c) Average speed:**
- Total time: $8 + 12 + 6 = 26 \text{ s}$
- Average speed: $\frac{456}{26} = 17.5 \text{ m/s}$ (to 1 d.p.)

</details>

## Problem 3: Mixed Problem

A cyclist travels at a constant speed of 18 km/h for 20 minutes, then stops for 5 minutes, then travels a further 6 km in 15 minutes.

(a) Convert 18 km/h to m/s.
(b) Calculate the total distance travelled.
(c) Calculate the average speed for the whole journey.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

**(a) Convert 18 km/h to m/s:**
$$18 \times \frac{5}{18} = 5 \text{ m/s}$$

**(b) Total distance:**
- First part: $18 \times \frac{20}{60} = 6 \text{ km}$
- Second part: $0 \text{ km}$ (stopped)
- Third part: $6 \text{ km}$
- **Total:** $12 \text{ km}$

**(c) Average speed:**
- Total time: $20 + 5 + 15 = 40 \text{ minutes} = \frac{2}{3} \text{ hour}$
- Average speed: $\frac{12}{\frac{2}{3}} = 12 \times \frac{3}{2} = 18 \text{ km/h}$

</details>
