# 1. Quadratic Graphs

## 1.1 Introduction to Quadratic Graphs

A **quadratic graph** is the graph of a quadratic function of the form:

> **📌 Standard Form:**
> $$y = ax^2 + bx + c$$

Where:
- $a$, $b$, and $c$ are constants
- $a \neq 0$ (if $a = 0$, it becomes a linear equation)
- $x$ is the variable

---

## 1.2 The Shape of a Quadratic Graph

The graph of a quadratic function is called a **parabola**.

### Key Features of a Parabola:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Shape** | A smooth, symmetrical curve (U-shape or ∩-shape) |
| **Vertex** | The turning point (maximum or minimum point) |
| **Axis of symmetry** | A vertical line passing through the vertex |
| **y-intercept** | Where the curve crosses the y-axis (at point $(0, c)$) |
| **x-intercepts (roots)** | Where the curve crosses the x-axis (solutions to $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$) |

---

## 1.3 Drawing Quadratic Graphs

### Method: Using a Table of Values

**Step-by-step process:**

1. **Choose suitable x-values** (typically from -3 to 3, or as required)
2. **Substitute each x-value** into the equation
3. **Calculate the corresponding y-values**
4. **Plot the points** on a coordinate grid
5. **Join the points** with a smooth curve

---

### Example 1: Drawing $y = x^2$

**Question:** Draw the graph of $y = x^2$ for $-3 \leq x \leq 3$

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Create a table of values

| $x$ | -3 | -2 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| $y = x^2$ | 9 | 4 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 4 | 9 |

**Step 2:** Calculate each y-value
- $x = -3$: $y = (-3)^2 = 9$
- $x = -2$: $y = (-2)^2 = 4$
- $x = -1$: $y = (-1)^2 = 1$
- $x = 0$: $y = (0)^2 = 0$
- $x = 1$: $y = (1)^2 = 1$
- $x = 2$: $y = (2)^2 = 4$
- $x = 3$: $y = (3)^2 = 9$

**Step 3:** Plot and join with a smooth curve

**Key Observations:**
- The graph is **U-shaped** (opens upwards)
- The vertex is at $(0, 0)$
- The axis of symmetry is $x = 0$ (the y-axis)
- The y-intercept is at $(0, 0)$

---

### Example 2: Drawing $y = x^2 - 4x + 3$

**Question:** Draw the graph of $y = x^2 - 4x + 3$ for $-1 \leq x \leq 5$

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Create a table of values

| $x$ | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| $x^2$ | 1 | 0 | 1 | 4 | 9 | 16 | 25 |
| $-4x$ | 4 | 0 | -4 | -8 | -12 | -16 | -20 |
| $+3$ | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 |
| **$y$** | **8** | **3** | **0** | **-1** | **0** | **3** | **8** |

**Step 2:** Calculate each y-value systematically
- $x = -1$: $y = (-1)^2 - 4(-1) + 3 = 1 + 4 + 3 = 8$
- $x = 0$: $y = 0^2 - 4(0) + 3 = 0 + 0 + 3 = 3$
- $x = 1$: $y = 1^2 - 4(1) + 3 = 1 - 4 + 3 = 0$
- $x = 2$: $y = 2^2 - 4(2) + 3 = 4 - 8 + 3 = -1$
- $x = 3$: $y = 3^2 - 4(3) + 3 = 9 - 12 + 3 = 0$
- $x = 4$: $y = 4^2 - 4(4) + 3 = 16 - 16 + 3 = 3$
- $x = 5$: $y = 5^2 - 4(5) + 3 = 25 - 20 + 3 = 8$

**Step 3:** Plot and join with a smooth curve

**Key Observations:**
- The graph is U-shaped
- The vertex is at $(2, -1)$
- The axis of symmetry is $x = 2$
- The y-intercept is at $(0, 3)$
- The x-intercepts (roots) are at $x = 1$ and $x = 3$

---

## 1.4 Common Mistakes When Drawing Quadratic Graphs

> ** Common Mistakes:**
> 1. **Using straight lines** between points – always use a smooth curve
> 2. **Calculating $-x^2$ incorrectly** – remember $(-x)^2 \neq -x^2$
>    - $(-3)^2 = 9$ ✓
>    - $-(3^2) = -9$ ✗
> 3. **Not using enough points** – use at least 5-7 points for accuracy
> 4. **Incorrect substitution** – especially with negative numbers
> 5. **Not extending the curve** beyond the plotted points when needed

# 2. Shape of Parabolas

## 2.1 The Effect of the Coefficient $a$

The value of $a$ (the coefficient of $x^2$) determines:

1. **The direction the parabola opens**
2. **How "wide" or "narrow" the parabola is**

### Direction of Opening

| Value of $a$ | Direction | Shape |
|--------------|-----------|-------|
| $a > 0$ (positive) | Opens **upwards** | U-shaped (minimum point) |
| $a < 0$ (negative) | Opens **downwards** | ∩-shaped (maximum point) |

> **📌 Key Insight:**
> - If $a > 0$: The vertex is a **minimum point** (lowest point on the graph)
> - If $a < 0$: The vertex is a **maximum point** (highest point on the graph)

---

### Width of the Parabola

| Value of $a$ | Effect on Width |
|--------------|-----------------|
| $\|a\| > 1$ (large magnitude) | Parabola is **narrower** |
| $\|a\| = 1$ | Standard parabola |
| $0 < \|a\| < 1$ (small magnitude) | Parabola is **wider** |

---

### Example 3: Comparing Parabolas

**Question:** Describe how the graphs of the following equations differ:
- (a) $y = x^2$
- (b) $y = 2x^2$
- (c) $y = \frac{1}{2}x^2$
- (d) $y = -x^2$

**Solution:**

**(a) $y = x^2$:**
- Standard parabola
- Opens upwards (U-shaped)
- Vertex at $(0, 0)$

**(b) $y = 2x^2$:**
- Opens upwards (U-shaped)
- **Narrower** than $y = x^2$
- Vertex at $(0, 0)$
- For the same x-value, y-values are **twice as large**

**(c) $y = \frac{1}{2}x^2$:**
- Opens upwards (U-shaped)
- **Wider** than $y = x^2$
- Vertex at $(0, 0)$
- For the same x-value, y-values are **half as large**

**(d) $y = -x^2$:**
- Opens **downwards** (∩-shaped)
- Same width as $y = x^2$
- Vertex at $(0, 0)$ but it's a **maximum point**
- y-values are the **negative** of $y = x^2$

---

## 2.2 The Effect of Adding a Constant ($c$)

Adding a constant $c$ to the quadratic function **shifts the parabola vertically**.

> **📌 Key Formula:**
> $$y = ax^2 + c$$

| Value of $c$ | Effect |
|--------------|--------|
| $c > 0$ | Shifts the parabola **upwards** by $c$ units |
| $c < 0$ | Shifts the parabola **downwards** by $|c|$ units |
| $c = 0$ | No vertical shift |

### Example 4: Vertical Shifts

**Question:** Compare the graphs of:
- (a) $y = x^2$
- (b) $y = x^2 + 3$
- (c) $y = x^2 - 2$

**Solution:**

| Graph | Vertex | y-intercept |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| $y = x^2$ | $(0, 0)$ | $(0, 0)$ |
| $y = x^2 + 3$ | $(0, 3)$ | $(0, 3)$ |
| $y = x^2 - 2$ | $(0, -2)$ | $(0, -2)$ |

All three parabolas have the **same shape** but are shifted vertically.

---

## 2.3 The Effect of the Linear Term ($bx$)

The term $bx$ in $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ affects the **horizontal position** of the vertex.

### Finding the Vertex

> **📌 Key Formulas:**
> 
> **x-coordinate of vertex:**
> $$x = -\frac{b}{2a}$$
> 
> **y-coordinate of vertex:**
> Substitute $x = -\frac{b}{2a}$ back into the equation.

---

### Example 5: Finding the Vertex

**Question:** Find the vertex of the parabola $y = x^2 - 6x + 5$

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Find the x-coordinate of the vertex
$$x = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{-6}{2(1)} = \frac{6}{2} = 3$$

**Step 2:** Find the y-coordinate by substituting $x = 3$
$$y = (3)^2 - 6(3) + 5$$
$$y = 9 - 18 + 5$$
$$y = -4$$

**Answer:** The vertex is at $(3, -4)$

**Step 3:** Determine if it's a maximum or minimum
- Since $a = 1 > 0$, the parabola opens **upwards**
- Therefore, $(3, -4)$ is a **minimum point**

---

## 2.4 The Axis of Symmetry

The **axis of symmetry** is a vertical line that divides the parabola into two mirror images.

> **📌 Key Formula:**
> $$\text{Axis of symmetry: } x = -\frac{b}{2a}$$

**Note:** The axis of symmetry always passes through the vertex.

---

### Example 6: Finding the Axis of Symmetry

**Question:** Find the equation of the axis of symmetry for $y = 2x^2 + 8x + 5$

**Solution:**

Identify $a = 2$ and $b = 8$

$$x = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{8}{2(2)} = -\frac{8}{4} = -2$$

**Answer:** The axis of symmetry is $x = -2$

---

## 2.5 Finding y-intercept and x-intercepts

### y-intercept

> **📌 Key Point:**
> The y-intercept occurs when $x = 0$, so the y-intercept is always at the point $(0, c)$.

---

### x-intercepts (Roots)

The x-intercepts are the solutions to the equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.

> **📌 Key Formula (Quadratic Formula):**
> $$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

---

### Types of Solutions (Discriminant)

> **📌 The Discriminant:**
> $$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$$

| Discriminant | Number of x-intercepts | Description |
|--------------|------------------------|-------------|
| $b^2 - 4ac > 0$ | **Two distinct** x-intercepts | Parabola crosses x-axis twice |
| $b^2 - 4ac = 0$ | **One** x-intercept | Parabola touches x-axis (vertex on x-axis) |
| $b^2 - 4ac < 0$ | **No** x-intercepts | Parabola doesn't cross x-axis |

---

### Example 7: Finding Intercepts

**Question:** For the parabola $y = x^2 - 5x + 6$, find:
(a) The y-intercept
(b) The x-intercepts
(c) The vertex

**Solution:**

**(a) y-intercept:**
- When $x = 0$: $y = 0^2 - 5(0) + 6 = 6$
- y-intercept is at $(0, 6)$

**(b) x-intercepts:**
- Set $y = 0$: $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$
- Factorise: $(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$
- $x = 2$ or $x = 3$
- x-intercepts are at $(2, 0)$ and $(3, 0)$

**(c) Vertex:**
$$x = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{-5}{2(1)} = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5$$
$$y = (2.5)^2 - 5(2.5) + 6 = 6.25 - 12.5 + 6 = -0.25$$
- Vertex is at $(2.5, -0.25)$

# 3. Solving Quadratic Equations Graphically

## 3.1 Using Graphs to Solve Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ can be solved by:

1. **Drawing the graph** of $y = ax^2 + bx + c$
2. **Finding where the curve crosses the x-axis**

> **📌 Key Concept:**
> The solutions (roots) of $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ are the **x-coordinates** where the parabola crosses the x-axis (where $y = 0$).

---

## 3.2 Step-by-Step Method

### Method for Solving Graphically:

1. **Draw an accurate graph** of $y = ax^2 + bx + c$
2. **Identify where the curve crosses the x-axis**
3. **Read off the x-values** at these points
4. **Verify** by substituting back into the equation

---

### Example 8: Solving $x^2 - 4 = 0$ Graphically

**Question:** Solve $x^2 - 4 = 0$ by drawing the graph of $y = x^2 - 4$

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Draw the graph of $y = x^2 - 4$

| $x$ | -3 | -2 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| $y$ | 5 | 0 | -3 | -4 | -3 | 0 | 5 |

**Step 2:** Identify x-intercepts

From the table (or graph):
- At $x = -2$, $y = 0$
- At $x = 2$, $y = 0$

**Answer:** $x = -2$ or $x = 2$

**Verification:**
- $(-2)^2 - 4 = 4 - 4 = 0$ ✓
- $(2)^2 - 4 = 4 - 4 = 0$ ✓

---

## 3.3 Solving Equations of the Form $ax^2 + bx + c = k$

To solve $ax^2 + bx + c = k$:

1. Draw $y = ax^2 + bx + c$
2. Draw the line $y = k$
3. Find where the curve and line intersect
4. Read off the x-coordinates

---

### Example 9: Solving $x^2 - 2x - 3 = 1$

**Question:** Solve $x^2 - 2x - 3 = 1$ by graphical method.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Draw $y = x^2 - 2x - 3$

| $x$ | -2 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| $y$ | 5 | 0 | -3 | -4 | -3 | 0 | 5 |

**Step 2:** Draw the line $y = 1$ on the same axes

**Step 3:** Find intersection points

The parabola and the line $y = 1$ intersect at:
- $x \approx -1.4$ and $x \approx 3.4$

**Answer:** $x \approx -1.4$ or $x \approx 3.4$

---

## 3.4 Solving Using Linear-Quadratic Graphs

Some problems require drawing two graphs and finding their intersection.

---

### Example 10: Solving $x^2 = 2x + 3$

**Question:** Solve $x^2 = 2x + 3$ graphically.

**Solution:**

**Method:** Draw $y = x^2$ and $y = 2x + 3$ on the same axes, find intersections.

**Step 1:** Table for $y = x^2$

| $x$ | -2 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| $y = x^2$ | 4 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 4 | 9 |

**Step 2:** Table for $y = 2x + 3$

| $x$ | -2 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| $y = 2x + 3$ | -1 | 1 | 3 | 5 | 7 | 9 |

**Step 3:** Find intersection points

Looking at where the values are equal:
- At $x = -1$: $y = 1$ for both
- At $x = 3$: $y = 9$ for both

**Answer:** $x = -1$ or $x = 3$

**Verification:**
- $(-1)^2 = 1$ and $2(-1) + 3 = 1$ ✓
- $(3)^2 = 9$ and $2(3) + 3 = 9$ ✓

---

## 3.5 Finding Maximum and Minimum Values Graphically

The **maximum or minimum value** of a quadratic function is the y-coordinate of the vertex.

---

### Example 11: Finding Minimum Value

**Question:** Find the minimum value of $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$ and the value of $x$ at which it occurs.

**Solution:**

**Method 1: Using the formula**

$$x = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{-4}{2(1)} = 2$$

Substitute $x = 2$:
$$y = (2)^2 - 4(2) + 5 = 4 - 8 + 5 = 1$$

**Answer:** Minimum value is $y = 1$, occurring at $x = 2$

**Method 2: Completing the square**

$$y = x^2 - 4x + 5$$
$$y = (x - 2)^2 - 4 + 5$$
$$y = (x - 2)^2 + 1$$

Since $(x - 2)^2 \geq 0$ for all $x$, the minimum occurs when $(x - 2)^2 = 0$, i.e., $x = 2$.

At $x = 2$: $y = 0 + 1 = 1$

---

## 3.6 Estimating Solutions from Graphs

When exact solutions cannot be read from the graph, we **estimate** to a suitable degree of accuracy.

> **📌 Exam Tip:**
> - Read the question carefully for required accuracy (usually 1 or 2 decimal places)
> - Draw graphs accurately using a sharp pencil
> - Use a ruler to help read values from the axes
> - Show your reading clearly on the graph

---

### Example 12: Estimating Solutions

**Question:** The graph of $y = x^2 + 3x - 1$ is drawn. Use the graph to estimate the solutions to $x^2 + 3x - 1 = 0$.

**Solution:**

From the graph, the curve crosses the x-axis at approximately:
- $x \approx -3.3$ and $x \approx 0.3$

**Answer:** $x \approx -3.3$ or $x \approx 0.3$ (to 1 decimal place)

**Note:** The exact solutions are $x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{13}}{2} \approx -3.30$ and $0.30$

# 5. Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

##  Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|------------|
| **Calculating $(-x)^2$ incorrectly** | $(-x)^2 = x^2$, NOT $-x^2$ |
| **Joining points with straight lines** | Use a smooth curve |
| **Not plotting enough points** | Use at least 5-7 points |
| **Incorrect reading of graph** | Check scale carefully |
| **Forgetting to label axes** | Always label x and y axes |
| **Wrong direction of parabola** | Check sign of $a$ |
| **Reading y-intercept incorrectly** | It's where $x = 0$, not the vertex |
| **Confusing x-intercepts with vertex** | x-intercepts are where $y = 0$; vertex is the turning point |

   - Read x-intercepts from graph

## 📌 Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

### Pattern 1: "Draw the graph of $y = ax^2 + bx + c$"
1. Create table of values (usually given or self-selected range)
2. Calculate y-values carefully
3. Plot all points accurately
4. Join with smooth curve
5. Label the graph

### Pattern 2: "Use your graph to solve $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$"
1. Locate where curve crosses x-axis
2. Read x-values at these points
3. State as solutions

### Pattern 3: "Find the minimum/maximum value"
1. Identify vertex on graph, OR
2. Use formula $x = -\frac{b}{2a}$, then substitute

### Pattern 4: "Solve $ax^2 + bx + c = k$ graphically"
1. Draw line $y = k$ on same axes
2. Find intersection points
3. Read x-values

---

1. **Complete all calculations correctly** – marks for table values

1. **Show reading clearly** – draw lines from curve to axis

### For Finding Features:

1. **Show method** – even if reading from graph
2. **State coordinates** – as ordered pairs $(x, y)$
3. **Use correct terminology** – vertex, axis of symmetry, etc.

## Problem 3: Finding Vertex and Intercepts

For the parabola $y = -x^2 + 6x - 5$:
(a) Find the y-intercept
(b) Find the x-intercepts
(c) Find the vertex
(d) State whether the vertex is a maximum or minimum

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

**(a) y-intercept:**
$y = -0^2 + 6(0) - 5 = -5$
Point: $(0, -5)$

**(b) x-intercepts:**
$-x^2 + 6x - 5 = 0$
$-(x^2 - 6x + 5) = 0$
$(x - 1)(x - 5) = 0$
$x = 1$ or $x = 5$
Points: $(1, 0)$ and $(5, 0)$

**(c) Vertex:**
$$x = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{6}{2(-1)} = \frac{6}{2} = 3$$
$$y = -(3)^2 + 6(3) - 5 = -9 + 18 - 5 = 4$$
Vertex: $(3, 4)$

**(d) Maximum or minimum:**
Since $a = -1 < 0$, parabola opens downwards.
Vertex $(3, 4)$ is a **maximum point**.

</details>

** Key Takeaways:**
- The graph of $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ is always a parabola
- The sign of $a$ determines direction: $a > 0$ opens up, $a < 0$ opens down
- The vertex x-coordinate is $x = -\frac{b}{2a}$
- x-intercepts are found where $y = 0$
- y-intercept is at $(0, c)$
- Use smooth curves when drawing quadratic graphs
- Graphical solutions are read from where curves meet axes or lines
