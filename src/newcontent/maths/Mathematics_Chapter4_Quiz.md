### Question 1
£2000 is invested at 5% compound interest per annum for 3 years. What is the final amount?

**A)** £2300.00  
**B)** £2315.25  
**C)** £2150.00  
**D)** £2205.00

**Answer: B**

**Working:**
$$A = 2000 \times (1.05)^3$$
$$= 2000 \times 1.157625$$
$$= £2315.25$$

**Note:** Simple interest would give $2000 + (2000 \times 0.05 \times 3) = £2300$, but compound interest gives more.

### Question 2
A car depreciates at 20% per year. Its value after 2 years is £6400. What was its original value?

**A)** £8000  
**B)** £9600  
**C)** £10000  
**D)** £7680

**Answer: C**

**Working:**
$$Original = \frac{6400}{(0.80)^2}$$
$$= \frac{6400}{0.64}$$
$$= £10000$$

**Verification:** $10000 \times 0.80 \times 0.80 = £6400$ ✓

### Question 4

After 4 years = 6802 (not enough)  
After 5 years = 7347 (exceeds 7000) ✓

**Answer: B (4 years)**

- After 4 years: 6802.44 < 7000
- After 5 years: 7346.64 > 7000

So after **5 years** it first exceeds 7000. **Correct answer: C**

### Question 5

**A)** 0%  
**B)** +4%  
**C)** −4%

### Question 7
£500 is invested at 4% compound interest compounded quarterly for 1 year. What is the final amount?

**A)** £520.00  
**B)** £520.30  
**C)** £520.60  
**D)** £521.00

**Answer: B**

**Working:**
Quarterly rate = $\frac{4}{4} = 1\%$ per quarter  
Number of periods = 4

$$A = 500 \times \left(1 + \frac{4}{400}\right)^4$$
$$= 500 \times (1.01)^4$$
$$= 500 \times 1.040604$$
$$= £520.30 \text{ (to 2 d.p.)}$$

### Question 8
A house price increased from £200000 to £226800 over 3 years. What is the annual percentage increase?

**A)** 4%  
**B)** 5%  
**C)** 6%  
**D)** 7%

**Answer: A**

**Working:**
$$226800 = 200000 \times \left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^3$$
$$\frac{226800}{200000} = \left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^3$$
$$1.134 = \left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^3$$
$$\sqrt[3]{1.134} = 1 + \frac{r}{100}$$
$$1.04 = 1 + \frac{r}{100}$$
$$r = 4\%$$

### Question 10
A sum of money triples in 12 years at compound interest. What is the annual rate?

**A)** 8.33%  
**B)** 9.59%  
**C)** 10.00%  
**D)** 11.50%

**Answer: B**

**Working:**

$$3 = \left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^{12}$$
$$\sqrt[12]{3} = 1 + \frac{r}{100}$$
$$1.0959 \approx 1 + \frac{r}{100}$$
$$r \approx 9.59\%$$

## SECTION B: TRUE OR FALSE

**Instructions:** State whether each statement is TRUE or FALSE. If FALSE, provide the correction.

---

### Question 1
**Statement:** If a price is increased by 20% and then decreased by 20%, the final price equals the original price.

**FALSE**

**Correction:** The final price is 96% of the original (a 4% decrease overall).

**Reasoning:**
$$1.20 \times 0.80 = 0.96$$

This represents a 4% decrease, not no change.

---

### Question 2
**Statement:** For reverse percentage problems, you divide the new value by the multiplier $(1 + \frac{r}{100})$ or $(1 - \frac{r}{100})$.

**TRUE**

**Reasoning:** This is the correct method for finding the original value before a percentage change was applied.

---

### Question 3
**Statement:** Compound interest always gives the same result as simple interest for periods less than one year.

**FALSE**

**Correction:** Compound interest can differ from simple interest even for periods less than one year if interest is compounded more frequently than annually.

**Example:** £1000 at 12% for 6 months:
- Simple: $1000 \times 0.12 \times 0.5 = £60$ interest
- Compound (semi-annually): $1000 \times (1.06)^1 - 1000 = £60$ interest
- But monthly: $1000 \times (1.01)^6 - 1000 = £61.52$ interest

---

### Question 4
**Statement:** The multiplier for a 15% decrease is 0.85.

**TRUE**

**Reasoning:** $100\% - 15\% = 85\% = 0.85$

---

### Question 5
**Statement:** When finding the original price after a 25% discount, you multiply by 0.75.

**FALSE**

**Correction:** You **divide** by 0.75, not multiply.

**Example:** After 25% discount, price is £60.
$$Original = \frac{60}{0.75} = £80$$

---

### Question 6
**Statement:** If an investment grows by 100%, it doubles in value.

**TRUE**

**Reasoning:** $100\%$ increase means the value becomes $200\%$ of original, which is double.

$$Original \times 2.00 = 2 \times Original$$

---

### Question 7
**Statement:** Compound interest formula can be used for depreciation calculations by using a multiplier greater than 1.

**FALSE**

**Correction:** For depreciation, the multiplier must be **less than 1** (using the form $1 - \frac{r}{100}$).

---

### Question 8
**Statement:** The formula $A = P(1.05)^n$ calculates the value after n years with 5% compound interest.

**TRUE**

**Reasoning:** The multiplier 1.05 represents a 5% increase, and raising it to the power n accounts for n periods of compound growth.

---

### Question 9
**Statement:** If a quantity decreases by 50% and then decreases by another 50%, the total decrease is 75%.

**TRUE**

**Reasoning:**
$$0.50 \times 0.50 = 0.25$$

Final value is 25% of original, meaning a 75% decrease overall.

---

### Question 10
**Statement:** For a reverse percentage problem involving a 30% profit, you divide by 1.30 to find the cost price.

**TRUE**

**Reasoning:** After a 30% profit, selling price = cost price × 1.30, so cost price = selling price ÷ 1.30.

---

### Question 11
**Statement:** Quarterly compounding yields the same result as annual compounding if the annual rate is used.

**FALSE**

**Correction:** Quarterly compounding yields MORE than annual compounding because interest is added more frequently and earns interest on interest.

**Example:** £1000 at 8% for 1 year:
- Annual: $1000 \times 1.08 = £1080$
- Quarterly: $1000 \times (1.02)^4 = £1082.43$

---

### Question 12
**Statement:** A car depreciating at 10% per year will be worth nothing after exactly 10 years.

**FALSE**

**Correction:** A car depreciating at 10% per year will still have value after 10 years (about 34.9% of original value).

$$Value = P \times (0.90)^{10} = P \times 0.3487$$

The value approaches zero asymptotically but never reaches exactly zero.

## 🎯 Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

### Pattern 1: Standard Compound Interest
**Recognise:** "Invested for n years at r% compound interest"
**Action:** Use $A = P(1 + \frac{r}{100})^n$

### Pattern 2: Reverse Percentage
**Recognise:** "After a discount/increase of r%, the price is..."
**Action:** Divide by $(1 + \frac{r}{100})$ or $(1 - \frac{r}{100})$

### Pattern 3: Comparison
**Recognise:** "Which is better: simple or compound?"
**Action:** Calculate both, compare results

### Pattern 4: Finding Rate
**Recognise:** "Find the annual rate if... grows to... in n years"
**Action:** Use $(1 + \frac{r}{100})^n = \frac{Final}{Initial}$, then take nth root

### Pattern 5: Finding Time
**Recognise:** "How long until... exceeds..."
**Action:** Trial and improvement or logarithms

- ✅ Correct substitution into formula

### Accuracy Marks (A marks)
- ✅ Round appropriately (usually 2 d.p. for money)
- ✅ State the final answer clearly
- ✅ Include units (£, %, years)

### Communication Marks (C marks)
- ✅ State conclusions clearly
- ✅ Answer the question asked
- ✅ Compare using correct mathematical language

##  Common Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | How to Avoid |
|---------|--------------|
| Using simple interest for compound | Look for word "compound" in question |
| Subtracting instead of dividing for reverse % | Always divide by the multiplier |
| Forgetting that +r% then -r% ≠ 0% | Calculate the overall multiplier |
| Wrong number of decimal places | Check question for rounding instructions |
| Calculator errors with powers | Use brackets: $(1.05)^3$ not $1.05^3$ |
