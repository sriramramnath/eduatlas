### Question 1

The graph of $y = x^2 - 5x + 6$ crosses the x-axis at two points. What are the solutions to $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$?

A) $x = 2$ and $x = 3$  
B) $x = -2$ and $x = -3$  
C) $x = 1$ and $x = 6$  
D) $x = -1$ and $x = -6$

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Factorise $x^2 - 5x + 6 = (x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$
- $x - 2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$
- $x - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 3$

These are the x-coordinates where the graph crosses the x-axis.

### Question 2

To solve $x^2 - 3x + 2 = 5$ graphically using the graph of $y = x^2 - 3x + 2$, what line should be drawn?

A) $y = 5$  
B) $x = 5$  
C) $y = -5$  
D) $y = x + 5$

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** The equation $x^2 - 3x + 2 = 5$ means we want the x-values where the function equals 5. Since the graph shows $y = x^2 - 3x + 2$, we draw the horizontal line $y = 5$ and find where it intersects the parabola.

### Question 3

The graph of $y = x^2 + 2x + 5$ does not touch or cross the x-axis. How many real solutions does $x^2 + 2x + 5 = 0$ have?

A) 0  
B) 1  
C) 2  
D) Cannot be determined

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** If a parabola does not cross the x-axis, the equation has no real solutions. We can verify using the discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac = 4 - 20 = -16 < 0$, confirming no real solutions.

A) Linear equations  
B) Quadratic equations

**Answer: C**

### Question 5

The solutions of $\begin{cases} y = x^2 \\ y = 2x \end{cases}$ are:

A) $(0, 0)$ only  
B) $(2, 4)$ only  
C) $(0, 0)$ and $(2, 4)$  
D) $(0, 0)$ and $(-2, 4)$

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Set $x^2 = 2x$, giving $x^2 - 2x = 0$, so $x(x - 2) = 0$.
- $x = 0$: $y = 0^2 = 0$ â†’ $(0, 0)$
- $x = 2$: $y = 2^2 = 4$ â†’ $(2, 4)$

### Question 7

## SECTION B: TRUE/FALSE QUESTIONS (10 marks)

**State whether each statement is TRUE or FALSE. If FALSE, provide the correction.**

---

### Question 11

**Statement:** The solutions to $f(x) = 0$ are the y-intercepts of the graph $y = f(x)$.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** The solutions to $f(x) = 0$ are the **x-intercepts** of the graph $y = f(x)$. At x-intercepts, $y = 0$, so $f(x) = 0$. The y-intercept is found when $x = 0$.

---

### Question 12

**Statement:** To solve $x^2 + 3x - 4 = 7$ graphically, draw the line $y = 7$ on the graph of $y = x^2 + 3x - 4$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** The equation $x^2 + 3x - 4 = 7$ asks for x-values where the function equals 7. Drawing $y = 7$ (horizontal line) and finding intersections gives the graphical solution.

---

### Question 13

**Statement:** A quadratic equation always has exactly two solutions.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** A quadratic equation can have **0, 1, or 2 real solutions**, depending on the discriminant. If $b^2 - 4ac < 0$, there are no real solutions; if $b^2 - 4ac = 0$, there is one repeated solution; if $b^2 - 4ac > 0$, there are two distinct solutions.

---

### Question 14

**Statement:** When solving simultaneous equations graphically, you only need to find the x-coordinates of intersection points.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** You must give **both x and y coordinates** of the intersection points. Solutions to simultaneous equations are ordered pairs $(x, y)$ that satisfy both equations.

---

### Question 15

**Statement:** If a line does not intersect a parabola, the simultaneous equations have no real solutions.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** The graphical interpretation of simultaneous equations is finding intersection points. If there are no intersections, the equations have no real solutions that satisfy both simultaneously.

---

### Question 16

**Statement:** The equation $x^2 = 2x + 3$ can be solved by finding where $y = x^2$ intersects $y = 2x + 3$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** This is the "two graph method." Drawing both $y = x^2$ (parabola) and $y = 2x + 3$ (straight line), the x-coordinates of intersection points give the solutions.

---

### Question 17

**Statement:** A cubic equation always has three real solutions.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** A cubic equation can have **1, 2, or 3 real solutions**. It must have at least one real solution, but the other roots may be complex. A repeated root counts as one distinct solution.

---

### Question 18

**Statement:** When solving $\frac{4}{x} = x$, the solution can be found by finding where $y = \frac{4}{x}$ meets $y = x$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** The equation $\frac{4}{x} = x$ rearranges to $x^2 = 4$, giving $x = \pm 2$. Graphically, this is where the reciprocal curve intersects the line $y = x$. The intersection points are $(2, 2)$ and $(-2, -2)$.

---

### Question 19

**Statement:** Graphical solutions always give exact answers.

**Answer: FALSE**

**Correction:** Graphical solutions typically give **approximate answers** to a certain accuracy (usually 1 decimal place or nearest 0.5 small square). For exact solutions, algebraic methods are needed.

---

### Question 20

**Statement:** For the simultaneous equations $\begin{cases} x^2 + y^2 = r^2 \\ y = mx + c \end{cases}$, substituting $y = mx + c$ into the circle equation gives a quadratic in $x$.

**Answer: TRUE**

**Explanation:** Substituting $y = mx + c$ into $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$:
$$x^2 + (mx + c)^2 = r^2$$
$$x^2 + m^2x^2 + 2mcx + c^2 = r^2$$
$$(1 + m^2)x^2 + 2mcx + (c^2 - r^2) = 0$$

This is a quadratic equation in $x$, which can have 0, 1, or 2 solutions depending on the discriminant.

### Question 22 (4 marks)

The graph of $y = x^2 - 2x - 3$ has been drawn for $-2 \leq x \leq 4$.

(a) Write down the x-intercepts of the graph. (2 marks)

(b) Use the graph to solve $x^2 - 2x - 3 = 2$. (2 marks)

**Solution:**

(a) **Finding x-intercepts:**
Set $y = 0$: $x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0$
Factorise: $(x - 3)(x + 1) = 0$
- $x - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 3$
- $x + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -1$

**x-intercepts: $x = -1$ and $x = 3$**

(b) **Solving $x^2 - 2x - 3 = 2$:**
- Draw horizontal line $y = 2$ on the graph
- Find intersection points with the parabola
- Read off x-coordinates

From the graph: **$x \approx -1.4$ and $x \approx 3.4$**

**Verification:**
- $x = -1.4$: $(-1.4)^2 - 2(-1.4) - 3 = 1.96 + 2.8 - 3 = 1.76 \approx 2$ âś“
- $x = 3.4$: $(3.4)^2 - 2(3.4) - 3 = 11.56 - 6.8 - 3 = 1.76 \approx 2$ âś“

**Mark Scheme:**
(a)
- M1: Correct method (factorising or using graph)
- A1: Both x-intercepts correct

(b)
- M1: Method to find intersection (draw line $y = 2$)

### Question 26 (5 marks)

A circle has equation $x^2 + y^2 = 25$.

(a) State the centre and radius of the circle. (2 marks)

(b) The line $y = x + 1$ intersects the circle. Find the coordinates of the points of intersection graphically or algebraically. (3 marks)

**Solution:**

(a) **Circle analysis:**
- Standard form: $(x - 0)^2 + (y - 0)^2 = 5^2$
- **Centre: $(0, 0)$**
- **Radius: $5$**

(b) **Finding intersection points:**

**Algebraic Method:**

Substitute $y = x + 1$ into $x^2 + y^2 = 25$:
$$x^2 + (x + 1)^2 = 25$$
$$x^2 + x^2 + 2x + 1 = 25$$
$$2x^2 + 2x - 24 = 0$$
$$x^2 + x - 12 = 0$$
$$(x + 4)(x - 3) = 0$$

$$x = -4 \quad \text{or} \quad x = 3$$

**Find y-values:**
- When $x = -4$: $y = -4 + 1 = -3$
- When $x = 3$: $y = 3 + 1 = 4$

**Intersection points: $(-4, -3)$ and $(3, 4)$**

**Verification:**
- $(-4)^2 + (-3)^2 = 16 + 9 = 25$ âś“
- $3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$ âś“

**Mark Scheme:**
(a)
- B1: Centre $(0, 0)$
- B1: Radius 5

(b)
- M1: Correct substitution method
- M1: Correct quadratic equation formed and solved
- A1: Both coordinate pairs correct

### Question 27 (4 marks)

(a) Explain how to use this graph to solve $x^2 + 2x = x + 3$. (2 marks)

(b) Solve the equation algebraically and verify your answer. (2 marks)

**Solution:**

(a) **Graphical method:**

**Option 1 (Rearrangement):**
1. Rearrange: $x^2 + 2x - x = 3$, so $x^2 + x = 3$
2. Draw line $y = 3$ on the graph of $y = x^2 + x$ (need to modify graph)

1. Rearrange to $x^2 + 2x = x + 3$
2. On the graph of $y = x^2 + 2x$, draw the line $y = x + 3$
3. Find intersection points
4. Read x-coordinates

(b) **Algebraic solution:**

$$x^2 + 2x = x + 3$$
$$x^2 + x - 3 = 0$$

Using quadratic formula:
$$x = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 12}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{13}}{2}$$

$$x = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{13}}{2} \approx 1.30$$
$$x = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{13}}{2} \approx -2.30$$

**Verification:**
- $x = 1.30$: $x^2 + 2x \approx 1.69 + 2.60 = 4.29$ and $x + 3 = 4.30$ âś“
- $x = -2.30$: $x^2 + 2x \approx 5.29 - 4.60 = 0.69$ and $x + 3 = 0.70$ âś“

**Mark Scheme:**

### Question 30 (4 marks)

The graph of $y = x^2 - 6x + 5$ is drawn.

(a) Explain why the equation $x^2 - 6x + 5 = -5$ has no real solutions. (2 marks)

(b) What is the minimum value of $y$ on the graph? (2 marks)

**Solution:**

(a) **Analysis:**

**Method 1 - Using discriminant:**
Rearrange: $x^2 - 6x + 10 = 0$
Discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac = 36 - 40 = -4 < 0$

Since the discriminant is negative, there are no real solutions.

**Method 2 - Graphical:**
The parabola opens upward (coefficient of $x^2$ is positive).
The minimum point lies above $y = -5$, so the graph never reaches $y = -5$.

(b) **Finding minimum value:**

Complete the square:
$$y = x^2 - 6x + 5$$
$$y = (x - 3)^2 - 9 + 5$$
$$y = (x - 3)^2 - 4$$

The minimum value occurs when $(x - 3)^2 = 0$, i.e., at $x = 3$.

**Minimum value: $y = -4$**

This is above $y = -5$, confirming part (a).

**Mark Scheme:**
(a)
- M1: Correct explanation method (discriminant or vertex analysis)
- A1: Clear conclusion that graph doesn't reach $y = -5$

(b)
- M1: Method to find minimum (completing square or vertex formula)
- A1: Minimum value $y = -4$ correctly stated

## Frequently Tested Question Types

### Type 1: Solving Quadratics Graphically
**Frequency:**  (Very Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- "Use your graph to solve..."
- "Find the x-coordinates where..."
- "Write down the solutions to..."

**Strategy:**
1. Identify whether you need a horizontal line or two graphs
2. Draw construction lines clearly
3. Read values accurately (usually 1 d.p.)
4. State both coordinates for simultaneous equations

---

### Type 2: Non-Linear Simultaneous Equations
**Frequency:**  (Very Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- "Solve the simultaneous equations..."
- "Find the points of intersection..."
- "How many solutions are there?"

**Strategy:**
1. For graphical: Draw both graphs accurately
2. For algebraic: Set equations equal, solve quadratic
3. Always give both x and y coordinates
4. Verify by substitution

---

### Type 3: Number of Solutions
**Frequency:**  (Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- "Explain why there are no solutions..."
- "How many points of intersection?"
- "Use the discriminant to determine..."

**Strategy:**
1. Use discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac$
2. Relate to graph behaviour (tangent, secant, no intersection)
3. Provide clear justification

---

### Type 4: Rearranging for Graphical Solution
**Frequency:**  (Common)

**What examiners ask:**
- "Given the graph of $y = x^2 + 3x$, solve $x^2 + 3x = 5$"
- "Hence solve..."

**Strategy:**
1. Rearrange to match graph form
2. Draw appropriate horizontal line
3. Find intersections

## Typical Problem-Solving Patterns

### Pattern 1: Linear + Quadratic Simultaneous
1. Set $y$ expressions equal
2. Rearrange to $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$
3. Factorise or use formula
4. Find y-values using linear equation
5. Give as $(x, y)$ pairs

### Pattern 2: Solving $f(x) = k$
1. Draw graph $y = f(x)$
2. Draw horizontal line $y = k$
3. Read x-coordinates at intersections

### Pattern 3: Two Graph Method
1. Draw both graphs accurately
2. Mark intersections clearly
3. Read both coordinates
4. Verify algebraically if required

### Pattern 4: Proving No Solutions
1. Form the equation
2. Calculate discriminant
3. Show $b^2 - 4ac < 0$
4. Conclude no real solutions

3. **Check for all solutions** - there may be more than one
4. **Verify answers** by substitution
5. **Use the discriminant** to predict number of solutions
