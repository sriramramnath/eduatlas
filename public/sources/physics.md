## Chapter 1: Movement and Position

### Main Points

1. **Speed and Velocity**
   - **Speed**: Distance travelled per unit time
   - **Velocity**: Speed in a given direction (vector quantity)
   - **Equation**: speed = distance ÷ time, or v = s/t
   - **Units**: m/s (metres per second) or km/h (kilometres per hour)
   - **Average speed**: Total distance ÷ total time
   - **Instantaneous speed**: Speed at a particular moment

2. **Distance-Time Graphs**
   - **Gradient** = speed
   - **Steep gradient** = high speed
   - **Flat/horizontal line** = stationary (speed = 0)
   - **Straight line** = constant speed
   - **Curved line** = changing speed (acceleration/deceleration)
   - **Negative gradient** = moving in opposite direction

3. **Acceleration**
   - **Definition**: Rate of change of velocity
   - **Equation**: acceleration = change in velocity ÷ time, or a = (v - u)/t
   - **Units**: m/s² (metres per second squared)
   - **Deceleration**: Negative acceleration (slowing down)
   - **Constant acceleration**: Velocity changes by same amount each second

4. **Velocity-Time Graphs**
   - **Gradient** = acceleration
   - **Area under graph** = distance travelled
   - **Flat/horizontal line** = constant velocity (acceleration = 0)
   - **Steep positive gradient** = large acceleration
   - **Negative gradient** = deceleration
   - **Curved line** = changing acceleration

5. **Equations of Motion**
   - v = u + at (final velocity = initial velocity + acceleration × time)
   - s = ut + ½at² (distance = initial velocity × time + ½ × acceleration × time²)
   - v² = u² + 2as (final velocity² = initial velocity² + 2 × acceleration × distance)
   - Where: v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, a = acceleration, t = time, s = distance

### Things to Remember

- **Speed is scalar** (magnitude only), **velocity is vector** (magnitude and direction)
- **Distance-time graph gradient** gives speed
- **Velocity-time graph gradient** gives acceleration
- **Area under velocity-time graph** gives distance
- **Always include units** in calculations
- **Convert units** before calculations (e.g., km/h to m/s: divide by 3.6)
- **Deceleration** is negative acceleration

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: A car travels 120 km in 2 hours. Calculate its average speed. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Speed = distance ÷ time (1 mark)
- Speed = 120 km ÷ 2 h = 60 km/h (1 mark)
- OR: Speed = 120,000 m ÷ 7,200 s = 16.7 m/s (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Describe the motion shown by a distance-time graph with a straight line sloping upwards. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Constant speed (1 mark)
- Moving away from starting point (1 mark)
- Speed = gradient of line (1 mark)

**Question 3**: A car accelerates from rest to 30 m/s in 10 seconds. Calculate its acceleration. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Acceleration = (v - u) ÷ t (1 mark)
- Acceleration = (30 - 0) ÷ 10 (1 mark)
- Acceleration = 3 m/s² (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Explain what the area under a velocity-time graph represents. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Distance travelled (1 mark)
- Total displacement (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing speed and velocity
- Forgetting to convert units (km/h to m/s)
- Using wrong equation for calculations
- Not reading graphs correctly (gradient vs area)
- Confusing distance-time and velocity-time graphs
- Forgetting that deceleration is negative acceleration

## Chapter 2: Forces and Shape

### Main Points

1. **Forces**
   - **Definition**: Push or pull on an object
   - **Unit**: Newton (N)
   - **Forces are vectors**: Have magnitude and direction
   - **Effects of forces**: Can change shape, speed, direction of motion

2. **Types of Forces**
   - **Weight**: Force due to gravity (acts downwards)
   - **Normal/Contact force**: Force perpendicular to surface
   - **Friction**: Force opposing motion
   - **Air resistance/Drag**: Friction from air
   - **Tension**: Force in strings/cables
   - **Upthrust**: Force pushing up in fluids
   - **Magnetic force**: Force between magnets
   - **Electrostatic force**: Force between charges

3. **Resultant Force**
   - **Definition**: Single force that has same effect as all forces combined
   - **Same direction**: Add forces together
   - **Opposite direction**: Subtract forces
   - **Perpendicular forces**: Use Pythagoras' theorem
   - **Zero resultant force**: Balanced forces (object stationary or constant velocity)

4. **Forces and Shape**
   - **Elastic deformation**: Object returns to original shape when force removed
   - **Plastic deformation**: Object stays deformed when force removed
   - **Elastic limit**: Maximum force before permanent deformation
   - **Hooke's Law**: Extension proportional to force (up to elastic limit)
   - **Equation**: F = kx, where F = force, k = spring constant, x = extension

5. **Force-Extension Graphs**
   - **Straight line** (up to elastic limit): Extension proportional to force
   - **Gradient** = spring constant (k)
   - **Curved section**: Beyond elastic limit (plastic deformation)
   - **Area under graph** = energy stored in spring

### Things to Remember

- **Forces are vectors** - need direction
- **Resultant force** determines motion
- **Balanced forces** = no acceleration (Newton's First Law)
- **Hooke's Law** only applies up to elastic limit
- **Spring constant** measures stiffness (larger k = stiffer spring)
- **Elastic limit** is maximum force before permanent deformation

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: State two effects that a force can have on an object. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Change shape (1 mark)
- Change speed (1 mark)
- Change direction (1 mark)
- Start or stop motion (1 mark)

**Question 2**: A spring extends by 5 cm when a force of 20 N is applied. Calculate the spring constant. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- F = kx (1 mark)
- k = F ÷ x = 20 N ÷ 0.05 m (1 mark)
- k = 400 N/m (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain the difference between elastic and plastic deformation. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Elastic deformation: object returns to original shape when force removed (1 mark)
- Plastic deformation: object stays deformed when force removed (1 mark)
- Elastic limit is the boundary between them (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing weight and mass
- Not including direction when describing forces
- Forgetting to convert units (cm to m)
- Stating Hooke's Law applies beyond elastic limit
- Confusing extension and length

## Chapter 3: Forces and Movement

### Main Points

1. **Balanced vs Unbalanced forces**

   - **Balanced forces**: Resultant force = 0, no acceleration
   - **Unbalanced forces**: Resultant force ≠ 0, causes acceleration

2. **Relationship between force, mass and acceleration**
   - **Statement**: Force = mass × acceleration, or F = ma
   - **Units**: F in Newtons (N), m in kilograms (kg), a in m/s²
   - **Larger force** = greater acceleration (if mass constant)
   - **Larger mass** = smaller acceleration (if force constant)
   - **1 N** = force needed to accelerate 1 kg at 1 m/s²

3. **Weight**
   - **Definition**: Force due to gravity
   - **Equation**: W = mg, where W = weight, m = mass, g = gravitational field strength
   - **g on Earth**: 10 N/kg (or 9.8 N/kg)
   - **Weight is a force** (vector, acts downwards)
   - **Mass is scalar** (amount of matter, constant)

4. **Friction**
   - **Definition**: Force opposing motion
   - **Types**: Static friction, sliding friction, air resistance
   - **Effects**: Slows objects down, causes heating, wears surfaces
   - **Factors affecting**: Surface roughness, normal force, materials
   - **Useful**: Walking, braking, gripping
   - **Problematic**: Wastes energy, causes wear

5. **Terminal Velocity**
   - **Definition**: Maximum velocity reached by falling object
   - **Process**: 
     - Object accelerates due to weight
     - Air resistance increases with speed
     - When air resistance = weight, forces balanced
     - No acceleration, constant velocity (terminal velocity)
   - **Factors**: Mass, surface area, shape, air density


### Things to Remember

- **Forces**: Balanced forces = no acceleration
- **F = ma**: Force causes acceleration
- **Weight = mg**: Weight depends on gravitational field strength
- **Mass ≠ Weight**: Mass is constant, weight varies with location
- **Terminal velocity**: When air resistance = weight
- **Action and reaction** act on different objects

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: A car of mass 1000 kg accelerates at 2 m/s². Calculate the resultant force acting on it. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- F = ma (1 mark)
- F = 1000 kg × 2 m/s² (1 mark)
- F = 2000 N (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why a skydiver reaches terminal velocity. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Initially, weight is greater than air resistance (1 mark)
- Skydiver accelerates downwards (1 mark)
- Air resistance increases with speed (1 mark)
- When air resistance equals weight, forces are balanced (1 mark)
- No acceleration, constant velocity (terminal velocity) (1 mark)


**Question 3**: Calculate the weight of a 5 kg object on Earth. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- W = mg (1 mark)
- W = 5 kg × 10 N/kg = 50 N (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing mass and weight
- Stating F = ma applies when forces balanced (it doesn't)
- Forgetting that action and reaction act on different objects
- Not understanding terminal velocity concept
- Using wrong value for g (should be 10 N/kg for IGCSE)

## Chapter 4: Mains Electricity

### Main Points

1. **Mains Electricity**
   - **Voltage**: 230 V (UK/Europe) or 120 V (USA)
   - **Frequency**: 50 Hz (UK/Europe) or 60 Hz (USA)
   - **Alternating Current (AC)**: Current changes direction
   - **Direct Current (DC)**: Current flows in one direction (batteries)

2. **Plug Wiring**
   - **Live wire (brown)**: Carries current, 230 V
   - **Neutral wire (blue)**: Completes circuit, 0 V
   - **Earth wire (green/yellow)**: Safety, 0 V
   - **Fuse**: Safety device, melts if current too high
   - **Cable grip**: Holds cable securely

3. **Fuses and Circuit Breakers**
   - **Fuse**: Wire that melts when current exceeds rating
   - **Rating**: Maximum current fuse can carry (e.g., 3 A, 5 A, 13 A)
   - **Circuit breaker**: Electromagnetic switch that trips when current too high
   - **Advantages of circuit breaker**: Can be reset, faster response
   - **Function**: Protect against overcurrent, prevent fires

4. **Electrical Safety**
   - **Earthing**: Earth wire connects metal case to ground
   - **If live wire touches case**: Large current flows to earth, fuse blows
   - **Double insulation**: Two layers of insulation, no earth wire needed
   - **RCCB (Residual Current Circuit Breaker)**: Detects difference in live and neutral currents, trips if difference detected (prevents electric shock)

5. **Power**
   - **Definition**: Energy transferred per second
   - **Equation**: Power = Voltage × Current, or P = VI
   - **Units**: Watts (W) or kilowatts (kW)
   - **1 kW** = 1000 W
   - **Energy transferred**: E = Pt, or E = VIt

6. **Energy and Cost**
   - **Energy units**: Joules (J) or kilowatt-hours (kWh)
   - **1 kWh** = energy transferred by 1 kW device in 1 hour
   - **Cost**: Energy (kWh) × Cost per kWh
   - **Efficiency**: Useful energy output ÷ Total energy input × 100%

### Things to Remember

- **Mains is AC**: Alternating current, 230 V, 50 Hz
- **Live wire is dangerous**: 230 V, can cause electric shock
- **Earth wire is safety**: Carries current to ground if fault occurs
- **Fuse rating** must be just above normal current
- **P = VI**: Power = voltage × current
- **1 kWh** = unit of energy used by electricity companies

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: State the voltage and frequency of UK mains electricity. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Voltage: 230 V (1 mark)
- Frequency: 50 Hz (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain the function of the earth wire in a plug. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Connected to metal case of appliance (1 mark)
- If live wire touches case, current flows to earth (1 mark)
- Large current causes fuse to blow (1 mark)
- Prevents electric shock (1 mark)

**Question 3**: A device operates at 230 V and draws a current of 5 A. Calculate its power. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- P = VI (1 mark)
- P = 230 V × 5 A = 1150 W (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Calculate the cost of using a 2 kW heater for 3 hours if electricity costs 15p per kWh. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Energy = Power × Time = 2 kW × 3 h = 6 kWh (1 mark)
- Cost = 6 kWh × 15p = 90p (1 mark)
- OR: Cost = £0.90 (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing AC and DC
- Stating wrong voltage/frequency for mains
- Not understanding earth wire function
- Confusing power and energy
- Not converting units correctly (kW to W, hours to seconds)
- Forgetting to include units in answers

## Chapter 5: Current and Voltage in Circuits

### Main Points

1. **Electric Current**
   - **Definition**: Flow of electric charge
   - **Equation**: Current = Charge ÷ Time, or I = Q/t
   - **Units**: Amperes (A) or milliamperes (mA)
   - **1 A** = 1000 mA
   - **Current is same** at all points in series circuit
   - **Current splits** in parallel circuit

2. **Voltage (Potential Difference)**
   - **Definition**: Energy transferred per unit charge
   - **Equation**: Voltage = Energy ÷ Charge, or V = E/Q
   - **Units**: Volts (V)
   - **Voltage across component**: Energy transferred to/from component
   - **Voltage splits** in series circuit
   - **Voltage same** across parallel branches

3. **Series Circuits**
   - **Current**: Same at all points (I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃)
   - **Voltage**: Sum of voltages across components (V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃)
   - **Resistance**: Sum of resistances (R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃)
   - **If one component breaks**: Circuit broken, no current flows

4. **Parallel Circuits**
   - **Current**: Sum of currents in branches (I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃)
   - **Voltage**: Same across all branches (V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃)
   - **Resistance**: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
   - **If one component breaks**: Other branches still work

5. **Energy Transfer**
   - **Energy transferred**: E = VIt, or E = VQ
   - **Power**: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
   - **Heating effect**: Energy converted to heat (P = I²R)

### Things to Remember

- **Current = Q/t**: Charge flow per second
- **Voltage = E/Q**: Energy per charge
- **Series**: Current same, voltage splits
- **Parallel**: Voltage same, current splits
- **Power = VI = I²R = V²/R**

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: A current of 2 A flows for 5 seconds. Calculate the charge that flows. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Q = It (1 mark)
- Q = 2 A × 5 s = 10 C (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why the current is the same at all points in a series circuit. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Charge cannot be created or destroyed (1 mark)
- Same charge flows through all components (1 mark)
- Current = charge flow per second, so same everywhere (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Two resistors of 6 Ω and 3 Ω are connected in parallel. Calculate the total resistance. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ (1 mark)
- 1/R = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/6 + 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2 (1 mark)
- R = 2 Ω (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing current and voltage
- Not understanding series vs parallel
- Using wrong formula for parallel resistance
- Forgetting that current splits in parallel
- Confusing charge and current

## Chapter 6: Electrical Resistance

### Main Points

1. **Resistance**
   - **Definition**: Opposition to current flow
   - **Equation**: Resistance = Voltage ÷ Current, or R = V/I (Ohm's Law)
   - **Units**: Ohms (Ω)
   - **Ohm's Law**: For conductor at constant temperature, V ∝ I
   - **Resistance increases** with temperature (for metals)

2. **Factors Affecting Resistance**
   - **Length**: Longer wire = higher resistance (R ∝ L)
   - **Cross-sectional area**: Thicker wire = lower resistance (R ∝ 1/A)
   - **Material**: Different materials have different resistivities
   - **Temperature**: Higher temperature = higher resistance (for metals)

3. **Resistivity**

   - **Low resistivity**: Good conductors (copper, silver)
   - **High resistivity**: Poor conductors (rubber, glass)

4. **I-V Characteristics**
   - **Resistor (Ohmic conductor)**: Straight line through origin (V ∝ I)
   - **Filament lamp**: Curved line (resistance increases with temperature)
   - **Diode**: Current only flows one direction, high resistance in reverse
   - **Thermistor**: Resistance decreases as temperature increases
   - **LDR (Light Dependent Resistor)**: Resistance decreases as light intensity increases

5. **Variable Resistors**
   - **Sliding contact resistor**: Variable resistor, changes resistance by changing length
   - **Potentiometer**: Variable resistor with three terminals
   - **Uses**: Volume controls, dimmer switches, speed controls

6. **Resistors in Series and Parallel**
   - **Series**: R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ (total resistance increases)
   - **Parallel**: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ (total resistance decreases)
   - **Parallel resistance**: Always less than smallest individual resistance

### Things to Remember

- **Ohm's Law**: V = IR (at constant temperature)
- **Resistance increases** with temperature (metals)
- **Longer/thinner wire** = higher resistance
- **I-V graph gradient** = resistance (for ohmic conductor)
- **Thermistor**: Resistance decreases with temperature
- **LDR**: Resistance decreases with light intensity

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: State Ohm's Law. (1 mark)

**Answer**: For a conductor at constant temperature, voltage is proportional to current, or V = IR (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why the resistance of a filament lamp increases as the current increases. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Current causes heating (1 mark)
- Temperature increases (1 mark)
- Resistance increases with temperature (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Describe the I-V characteristic of a thermistor. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Curved line (not straight) (1 mark)
- Resistance decreases as temperature increases (1 mark)
- Current increases more than proportionally with voltage (1 mark)


### Common Mistakes

- Not understanding Ohm's Law conditions (constant temperature)
- Confusing resistance and resistivity
- Not understanding I-V characteristics
- Forgetting that resistance increases with temperature for metals
- Not understanding how length and area affect resistance

## Chapter 7: Properties of Waves

### Main Points

1. **Wave Properties**
   - **Wave**: Transfer of energy without transferring matter
   - **Transverse waves**: Vibrations perpendicular to direction of travel (e.g., light, water waves)
   - **Longitudinal waves**: Vibrations parallel to direction of travel (e.g., sound, ultrasound)
   - **Amplitude**: Maximum displacement from equilibrium (height of wave)
   - **Wavelength (λ)**: Distance between two consecutive similar points (e.g., crest to crest)
   - **Frequency (f)**: Number of waves per second (Hz)
   - **Period (T)**: Time for one complete wave (T = 1/f)

2. **Wave Equation**
   - **Wave speed**: v = fλ, where v = speed, f = frequency, λ = wavelength
   - **Units**: v in m/s, f in Hz, λ in m
   - **All waves obey**: v = fλ

3. **Wave Behavior**
   - **Reflection**: Wave bounces off surface (angle of incidence = angle of reflection)
   - **Refraction**: Wave changes speed and direction when entering different medium
   - **Diffraction**: Wave spreads out when passing through gap or around obstacle
   - **Interference**: Two waves combine (constructive = larger amplitude, destructive = smaller amplitude)

4. **Wave Speed**
   - **Depends on medium**: Different speeds in different materials
   - **Sound**: Faster in solids than liquids, faster in liquids than gases
   - **Light**: Slower in materials than vacuum (refraction occurs)
   - **Frequency doesn't change**: When wave enters different medium (wavelength changes)

### Things to Remember

- **v = fλ**: Wave speed equation
- **Transverse**: Vibrations perpendicular (light, water)
- **Longitudinal**: Vibrations parallel (sound)
- **Frequency doesn't change**: When wave enters different medium
- **Reflection**: Angle of incidence = angle of reflection
- **Diffraction**: More noticeable when gap ≈ wavelength

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: A wave has a frequency of 50 Hz and a wavelength of 6 m. Calculate its speed. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- v = fλ (1 mark)
- v = 50 Hz × 6 m = 300 m/s (1 mark)

**Question 2**: State two differences between transverse and longitudinal waves. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Transverse: vibrations perpendicular to direction (1 mark)
- Longitudinal: vibrations parallel to direction (1 mark)
- OR: Transverse example: light (1 mark), Longitudinal example: sound (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain why frequency doesn't change when a wave enters a different medium. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Frequency depends on source (1 mark)
- Not affected by medium (1 mark)
- Wavelength changes instead (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing transverse and longitudinal
- Using wrong wave equation
- Not understanding wave behavior (reflection, refraction)
- Confusing frequency and wavelength
- Stating frequency changes in different medium

## Chapter 8: The Electromagnetic Spectrum

### Main Points

1. **Electromagnetic Waves**
   - **Type**: Transverse waves
   - **All travel at**: Speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s) in vacuum
   - **Can travel through vacuum**: No medium needed
   - **Consist of**: Oscillating electric and magnetic fields

2. **Electromagnetic Spectrum Order**
   - **Low to high frequency**: Radio waves → Microwaves → Infrared → Visible light → Ultraviolet → X-rays → Gamma rays
   - **Low to high wavelength**: Gamma rays → X-rays → Ultraviolet → Visible light → Infrared → Microwaves → Radio waves
   - **All obey**: v = fλ (in vacuum, v = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)

3. **Radio Waves**
   - **Frequency**: ~10⁴ to 10⁹ Hz
   - **Wavelength**: ~10⁴ to 10⁻¹ m
   - **Uses**: Radio and TV broadcasting, communications, radar
   - **Properties**: Long wavelength, low frequency, low energy

4. **Microwaves**
   - **Frequency**: ~10⁹ to 10¹² Hz
   - **Wavelength**: ~10⁻¹ to 10⁻³ m
   - **Uses**: Cooking (heat water molecules), mobile phones, satellite communications
   - **Properties**: Medium wavelength, medium frequency

5. **Infrared**
   - **Frequency**: ~10¹² to 10¹⁵ Hz
   - **Wavelength**: ~10⁻³ to 10⁻⁶ m
   - **Uses**: Heating, remote controls, thermal imaging, night vision
   - **Properties**: Felt as heat

6. **Visible Light**
   - **Frequency**: ~10¹⁵ Hz
   - **Wavelength**: ~400-700 nm (nanometres)
   - **Colours**: Red (longest wavelength) to Violet (shortest wavelength)
   - **Uses**: Vision, photography, optical fibres

7. **Ultraviolet (UV)**
   - **Frequency**: ~10¹⁵ to 10¹⁷ Hz
   - **Wavelength**: ~10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁸ m
   - **Uses**: Sterilisation, detecting forgeries, tanning
   - **Dangers**: Can cause skin cancer, eye damage

8. **X-rays**
   - **Frequency**: ~10¹⁷ to 10²⁰ Hz
   - **Wavelength**: ~10⁻⁸ to 10⁻¹¹ m
   - **Uses**: Medical imaging (bones), security scanning, cancer treatment
   - **Dangers**: Can cause cancer, cell damage

9. **Gamma Rays**
   - **Frequency**: >10²⁰ Hz
   - **Wavelength**: <10⁻¹¹ m
   - **Uses**: Cancer treatment, sterilisation, medical tracers
   - **Dangers**: Very dangerous, can cause severe cell damage

10. **Dangers of EM Radiation**
    - **Ionising radiation**: UV, X-rays, Gamma rays (can damage cells)
    - **Non-ionising**: Radio, Microwaves, Infrared, Visible (generally safer)
    - **Protection**: Distance, shielding, time limits

### Things to Remember

- **All EM waves**: Travel at speed of light in vacuum
- **Order**: Radio → Microwaves → Infrared → Visible → UV → X-rays → Gamma
- **Higher frequency** = shorter wavelength = higher energy
- **Ionising**: UV, X-rays, Gamma (dangerous)
- **Non-ionising**: Radio, Microwaves, Infrared, Visible (safer)
- **All are transverse waves**

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: List the electromagnetic spectrum in order of increasing frequency. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Radio waves (1 mark)
- Microwaves (1 mark)
- Infrared (1 mark)
- Visible light (1 mark)
- Ultraviolet (1 mark)
- X-rays (1 mark)
- Gamma rays (1 mark)

**Question 2**: State the speed of all electromagnetic waves in vacuum. (1 mark)

**Answer**: 3 × 10⁸ m/s or speed of light (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain why gamma rays are more dangerous than radio waves. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Gamma rays have higher frequency/energy (1 mark)
- Can ionise atoms, damage cells (1 mark)
- Radio waves have lower energy, non-ionising (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Give two uses of microwaves. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Cooking food (1 mark)
- Mobile phone communications (1 mark)
- Satellite communications (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Not knowing EM spectrum order
- Confusing frequency and wavelength order
- Stating different speeds for different EM waves (all same in vacuum)
- Not understanding dangers of ionising radiation
- Confusing uses of different EM waves

## Chapter 9: Light and Sound Waves

### Main Points

1. **Reflection**
   - **Law of reflection**: Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection
   - **Normal**: Line perpendicular to surface
   - **Plane mirror**: Forms virtual image (same size, same distance behind mirror)
   - **Image characteristics**: Virtual, upright, laterally inverted, same size

2. **Refraction**
   - **Definition**: Change in direction when light enters different medium
   - **Caused by**: Change in speed
   - **Towards normal**: When entering denser medium (slower speed)
   - **Away from normal**: When entering less dense medium (faster speed)
   - **Refractive index**: n = sin i / sin r (Snell's law)

3. **Total Internal Reflection**
   - **Conditions**: Light in denser medium, angle greater than critical angle
   - **Critical angle**: Angle where refraction angle = 90°
   - **Uses**: Optical fibres (communications, endoscopes), prisms in periscopes
   - **Advantage**: No energy loss (unlike reflection from mirror)

4. **Lenses**
   - **Converging lens (convex)**: Thicker in middle, converges light rays
   - **Diverging lens (concave)**: Thinner in middle, diverges light rays
   - **Principal focus**: Point where parallel rays converge/diverge
   - **Focal length**: Distance from lens to principal focus

5. **Image Formation (Converging Lens)**
   - **Object beyond 2F**: Real, inverted, smaller image (between F and 2F)
   - **Object at 2F**: Real, inverted, same size image (at 2F)
   - **Object between F and 2F**: Real, inverted, larger image (beyond 2F)
   - **Object at F**: No image (rays parallel)
   - **Object inside F**: Virtual, upright, larger image (same side as object)

6. **Dispersion**
   - **Definition**: Separation of white light into spectrum
   - **Caused by**: Different wavelengths refracted by different amounts
   - **Red light**: Least refracted (longest wavelength)
   - **Violet light**: Most refracted (shortest wavelength)
   - **Rainbow**: Caused by dispersion in water droplets

7. **Colour**
   - **Primary colours**: Red, Green, Blue (additive mixing)
   - **Secondary colours**: Cyan, Magenta, Yellow (subtractive mixing)
   - **White light**: Contains all colours
   - **Coloured objects**: Absorb some colours, reflect others
   - **Black object**: Absorbs all colours
   - **White object**: Reflects all colours

### Things to Remember

- **Reflection**: Angle of incidence = angle of reflection
- **Refraction**: Towards normal in denser medium
- **Total internal reflection**: Only in denser medium, angle > critical angle
- **Converging lens**: Thicker in middle, converges rays

## Chapter 10: Energy Transfers

### Main Points

1. **Energy Stores**
   - **Kinetic**: Energy of moving objects (E = ½mv²)
   - **Gravitational potential**: Energy due to height (E = mgh)
   - **Elastic potential**: Energy stored in stretched/compressed objects
   - **Chemical**: Energy stored in chemicals (food, fuel, batteries)
   - **Nuclear**: Energy stored in nuclei
   - **Thermal**: Energy due to temperature
   - **Magnetic**: Energy stored in magnetic fields
   - **Electrostatic**: Energy stored in electric fields

2. **Energy Transfers**
   - **Mechanically**: By forces (work done)
   - **Electrically**: By electric current
   - **By heating**: Due to temperature difference
   - **By radiation**: By waves (light, infrared)

3. **Conservation of Energy**
   - **Principle**: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred
   - **Total energy**: Always constant (in closed system)
   - **Energy transfers**: From one store to another
   - **Wasted energy**: Usually transferred to thermal store (heating)

4. **Energy Transfer Examples**
   - **Falling object**: Gravitational potential → Kinetic
   - **Battery**: Chemical → Electrical
   - **Light bulb**: Electrical → Light + Thermal (wasted)
   - **Car engine**: Chemical → Kinetic + Thermal (wasted)
   - **Solar panel**: Light → Electrical

5. **Efficiency**
   - **Definition**: Percentage of input energy transferred usefully
   - **Equation**: Efficiency = (Useful energy output ÷ Total energy input) × 100%
   - **Always less than 100%**: Some energy always wasted (usually as heat)
   - **Ways to improve**: Reduce friction, reduce air resistance, better insulation

### Things to Remember

- **Energy conserved**: Cannot be created or destroyed
- **Energy transfers**: From one store to another
- **Efficiency < 100%**: Always some wasted energy
- **Wasted energy**: Usually becomes heat
- **GPE = mgh**: Gravitational potential energy
- **KE = ½mv²**: Kinetic energy

## Chapter 11: Thermal Energy

### Main Points

1. **Temperature and Heat**
   - **Temperature**: Measure of average kinetic energy of particles
   - **Heat**: Energy transferred due to temperature difference
   - **Units**: Temperature in °C or K, Heat in Joules (J)
   - **Heat flows**: From hot to cold (until temperatures equal)

2. **Specific Latent Heat**
   - **Definition**: Energy needed to change state of 1 kg without temperature change
   - **Specific latent heat of fusion**: Melting/freezing (solid ↔ liquid)
   - **Specific latent heat of vaporisation**: Boiling/condensing (liquid ↔ gas)
   - **Equation**: E = mL, where E = energy, m = mass, L = specific latent heat
   - **Units**: J/kg
   - **During state change**: Temperature constant, energy used to break/form bonds

3. **Conduction**
   - **Definition**: Transfer of heat through solids by particle vibration
   - **Process**: Hot particles vibrate more, collide with neighbours, transfer energy
   - **Good conductors**: Metals (free electrons carry energy)
   - **Poor conductors (insulators)**: Non-metals, air, water
   - **Applications**: Cooking pans (metal), handles (insulator)

4. **Convection**
   - **Definition**: Transfer of heat by movement of fluids (liquids/gases)
   - **Process**: Hot fluid expands, becomes less dense, rises; cold fluid sinks
   - **Convection current**: Circular movement of fluid
   - **Only in fluids**: Not in solids
   - **Applications**: Heating rooms, sea breezes

5. **Radiation**
   - **Definition**: Transfer of heat by infrared waves
   - **No medium needed**: Can travel through vacuum
   - **All objects emit**: Hotter objects emit more radiation
   - **Dark, matt surfaces**: Good absorbers and emitters
   - **Light, shiny surfaces**: Poor absorbers and emitters (good reflectors)
   - **Applications**: Solar panels (dark), radiators (dark), thermos flasks (shiny)

### Things to Remember

- **Temperature ≠ Heat**: Temperature is measure, heat is energy
- **Conduction**: Through solids, by particle vibration
- **Convection**: In fluids, by movement
- **Radiation**: By infrared waves, no medium needed
- **Dark surfaces**: Good absorbers/emitters of radiation

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain the difference between temperature and heat. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Temperature is measure of average kinetic energy (1 mark)
- Heat is energy transferred due to temperature difference (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why convection cannot occur in solids. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Convection requires movement of particles (1 mark)
- Particles in solids are fixed in position (1 mark)
- Cannot move to form convection currents (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Describe how heat is transferred by radiation. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- By infrared waves (1 mark)
- No medium needed, can travel through vacuum (1 mark)
- All objects emit radiation (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing temperature and heat
- Stating convection occurs in solids
- Not understanding latent heat (no temperature change during state change)

## Chapter 12: Work and Power

### Main Points

1. **Work Done**
   - **Definition**: Energy transferred by force
   - **Equation**: Work = Force × Distance, or W = Fd
   - **Units**: Joules (J)
   - **1 J** = 1 N m
   - **Work done** = energy transferred
   - **Work done against gravity**: W = mgh (lifting object)

2. **Power**
   - **Definition**: Rate of energy transfer (or work done per second)
   - **Equation**: Power = Energy ÷ Time, or P = E/t
   - **Also**: Power = Work ÷ Time, or P = W/t
   - **Units**: Watts (W) or kilowatts (kW)
   - **1 W** = 1 J/s
   - **1 kW** = 1000 W

3. **Power and Force**
   - **Power = Force × Velocity**: P = Fv
   - **When force and velocity in same direction**
   - **Useful for**: Moving objects at constant speed

4. **Efficiency and Power**
   - **Useful power output**: Power used for intended purpose
   - **Total power input**: Power supplied to device
   - **Efficiency**: (Useful power output ÷ Total power input) × 100%
   - **Wasted power**: Usually converted to heat

5. **Examples**
   - **Lifting object**: Work done = mgh, Power = mgh/t
   - **Moving object**: Work done = Fd, Power = Fd/t or Fv

### Things to Remember

- **Work done = Fd**: Energy transferred by force
- **Power = E/t**: Rate of energy transfer
- **Power = W/t**: Work done per second
- **Power = Fv**: Force × velocity (when moving)
- **1 W = 1 J/s**: Definition of watt
- **Work done = energy transferred**

**Answer**:
- Work done = mgh = 10 kg × 10 N/kg × 2 m = 200 J (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Explain the relationship between work done and energy transferred. (1 mark)

**Answer**: Work done equals energy transferred (1 mark)

- Not using correct formula for power

## Chapter 13: Density and Pressure

### Main Points

1. **Density**
   - **Definition**: Mass per unit volume
   - **Equation**: Density = Mass ÷ Volume, or ρ = m/V
   - **Units**: kg/m³ or g/cm³
   - **1 g/cm³** = 1000 kg/m³
   - **Solids**: Usually highest density
   - **Liquids**: Medium density
   - **Gases**: Lowest density
   - **Floating**: Less dense objects float on more dense fluids

2. **Pressure**
   - **Definition**: Force per unit area
   - **Equation**: Pressure = Force ÷ Area, or P = F/A
   - **Units**: Pascals (Pa) or N/m²
   - **1 Pa** = 1 N/m²
   - **Larger force** = higher pressure (if area same)
   - **Smaller area** = higher pressure (if force same)

3. **Pressure in Liquids**
   - **Increases with depth**: Deeper = higher pressure
   - **Equation**: P = ρgh, where P = pressure, ρ = density, g = gravitational field strength, h = depth
   - **Same depth**: Same pressure (in all directions)
   - **Applications**: Dams (thicker at bottom), diving (pressure increases with depth)

4. **Pressure in Gases**
   - **Caused by**: Particles colliding with walls of container
   - **Factors affecting**: Temperature (higher temp = higher pressure), Volume (smaller volume = higher pressure), Number of particles
   - **Atmospheric pressure**: ~100,000 Pa (100 kPa) at sea level
   - **Decreases with altitude**: Less air above = lower pressure

5. **Boyle's Law**
   - **Statement**: For fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure × volume = constant
   - **Equation**: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
   - **Graph**: Pressure vs volume is curve (inverse relationship)
   - **As volume decreases**: Pressure increases (more collisions per unit area)

6. **Pressure and Temperature**
   - **At constant volume**: Pressure ∝ Temperature (in Kelvin)
   - **As temperature increases**: Particles move faster, more collisions, higher pressure
   - **Absolute zero**: 0 K (-273°C), particles have minimum energy

### Things to Remember

- **Density = m/V**: Mass per unit volume
- **Pressure = F/A**: Force per unit area
- **Liquid pressure = ρgh**: Increases with depth
- **Boyle's Law**: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant temperature)
- **Pressure increases** with depth in liquids
- **Pressure increases** with temperature in gases (constant volume)

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Calculate the density of an object with mass 500 g and volume 250 cm³. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Density = m/V (1 mark)
- Density = 500 g ÷ 250 cm³ = 2 g/cm³ (1 mark)

**Question 2**: A force of 100 N acts on an area of 0.5 m². Calculate the pressure. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- P = F/A (1 mark)
- P = 100 N ÷ 0.5 m² = 200 Pa (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain why pressure increases with depth in a liquid. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Weight of liquid above increases with depth (1 mark)
- More force on area below (1 mark)
- Pressure = force ÷ area, so pressure increases (1 mark)

**Question 4**: A gas has a pressure of 100 kPa and volume of 2 m³. If the volume is reduced to 1 m³ at constant temperature, calculate the new pressure. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (1 mark)
- 100 × 2 = P₂ × 1 (1 mark)
- P₂ = 200 kPa (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing density and pressure
- Not understanding liquid pressure formula
- Using wrong gas law
- Confusing pressure and force
- Not understanding Boyle's Law
- Forgetting units

## Chapter 14: Solids, Liquids and Gases

### Main Points

1. **States of Matter**
   - **Solid**: Fixed shape and volume, particles close together, vibrate in fixed positions
   - **Liquid**: Fixed volume, takes shape of container, particles close together, can move
   - **Gas**: No fixed shape or volume, particles far apart, move randomly

2. **Particle Model**
   - **All matter**: Made of tiny particles (atoms/molecules)
   - **Particles in constant motion**: More energy = faster motion
   - **Forces between particles**: Strong in solids, weaker in liquids, very weak in gases
   - **Temperature**: Related to average kinetic energy of particles

3. **Changes of State**
   - **Melting**: Solid → Liquid (energy needed to break bonds)
   - **Freezing**: Liquid → Solid (energy released as bonds form)
   - **Boiling/Evaporation**: Liquid → Gas (energy needed)
   - **Condensation**: Gas → Liquid (energy released)
   - **Sublimation**: Solid → Gas directly (e.g., dry ice)
   - **During state change**: Temperature constant (energy used to change state, not temperature)

4. **Particle Arrangement**
   - **Solid**: Regular arrangement, strong forces, particles vibrate
   - **Liquid**: Random arrangement, weaker forces, particles can move
   - **Gas**: Very far apart, very weak forces, particles move freely

5. **Properties Related to Particle Model**
   - **Solids**: Fixed shape (strong forces), fixed volume (particles close)
   - **Liquids**: No fixed shape (weaker forces), fixed volume (particles still close)
   - **Gases**: No fixed shape or volume (very weak forces, particles far apart)

### Things to Remember

- **Solid**: Fixed shape, particles vibrate
- **Liquid**: Fixed volume, particles can move
- **Gas**: No fixed shape/volume, particles move randomly
- **During state change**: Temperature constant
- **Energy needed**: To break bonds (melting, boiling)
- **Energy released**: When bonds form (freezing, condensing)

## Chapter 15: Magnetism and Electromagnetism

### Main Points

1. **Magnets**
   - **Permanent magnets**: Always magnetic (e.g., bar magnet)
   - **Magnetic poles**: North and South (always in pairs)
   - **Like poles**: Repel (N-N, S-S)
   - **Unlike poles**: Attract (N-S)
   - **Magnetic field**: Region where magnetic force acts
   - **Field lines**: Show direction of force (N to S)

2. **Magnetic Materials**
   - **Ferromagnetic**: Strongly attracted to magnets (iron, nickel, cobalt, steel)
   - **Non-magnetic**: Not attracted (most materials)
   - **Induced magnetism**: Temporary magnetism in magnetic material near magnet

3. **Electromagnets**
   - **Definition**: Magnet created by electric current
   - **Made from**: Coil of wire (solenoid) with iron core
   - **Advantages**: Can be switched on/off, strength can be varied, polarity can be reversed
   - **Factors affecting strength**: Current, number of turns, core material
   - **Uses**: Electric bells, relays, scrap yard cranes, MRI scanners

4. **Magnetic Fields**
   - **Around bar magnet**: Field lines from N to S
   - **Around current-carrying wire**: Circular field lines
   - **Around solenoid**: Similar to bar magnet (N and S poles)
   - **Field strength**: Closer lines = stronger field

5. **Electromagnetic Induction (Basic)**
   - **Definition**: Generating electricity from changing magnetic field
   - **Process**: Moving magnet near coil (or moving coil in magnetic field) induces voltage
   - **Factors affecting**: Speed of movement, strength of magnet, number of turns
   - **Direction**: Depends on direction of movement

6. **Transformers**
   - **Definition**: Device that changes voltage using electromagnetic induction
   - **Structure**: Primary coil, secondary coil, iron core
   - **Step-up transformer**: More turns on secondary, increases voltage
   - **Step-down transformer**: Fewer turns on secondary, decreases voltage
   - **Equation**: Vp/Vs = Np/Ns (voltage ratio = turns ratio)
   - **Power**: P = VI, if ideal transformer, VpIp = VsIs

### Things to Remember

- **Like poles repel**, **unlike poles attract**
- **Electromagnets**: Can be switched on/off
- **Magnetic field**: Region where force acts
- **Field lines**: Show direction of force
- **Transformer**: Vp/Vs = Np/Ns
- **Electromagnetic induction**: Moving magnet/coil generates electricity

## Chapter 16: Electric Motors and Electromagnetic Induction

### Main Points

1. **Motor Effect**
   - **Definition**: Force on current-carrying conductor in magnetic field
   - **Fleming's Left Hand Rule**: Thumb = Motion, First finger = Field, Second finger = Current
   - **Force direction**: Perpendicular to both current and field
   - **Force increases**: With current, magnetic field strength, length of conductor
   - **Force = 0**: When current parallel to field

2. **Electric Motor**
   - **Definition**: Device that converts electrical energy to kinetic energy
   - **Uses motor effect**: Force on coil in magnetic field causes rotation
   - **Components**: Coil, magnets, commutator, brushes
   - **Commutator**: Reverses current every half turn, keeps rotation in same direction
   - **Brushes**: Connect power supply to commutator
   - **Uses**: Fans, drills, washing machines, electric vehicles

3. **Electromagnetic Induction**
   - **Definition**: Generating electricity from changing magnetic field
   - **Process**: Moving magnet near coil (or moving coil in magnetic field) induces voltage
   - **Factors affecting**: Speed of movement, strength of magnet, number of turns
   - **Direction**: Depends on direction of movement (Fleming's Right Hand Rule)
   - **Lenz's Law**: Induced current opposes change causing it

4. **Generator (AC)**
   - **Definition**: Device that converts kinetic energy to electrical energy
   - **Uses electromagnetic induction**: Rotating coil in magnetic field
   - **Produces**: Alternating current (AC)
   - **Components**: Coil, magnets, slip rings, brushes
   - **Slip rings**: Allow current to flow without tangling wires
   - **Uses**: Power stations, wind turbines, hydroelectric dams

5. **Fleming's Rules**
   - **Left Hand Rule (Motor)**: Thumb = Motion, First finger = Field, Second finger = Current
   - **Right Hand Rule (Generator)**: Thumb = Motion, First finger = Field, Second finger = Induced Current
   - **Remember**: Left for Motor, Right for Generator

6. **Transformers and Induction**
   - **Works by**: Electromagnetic induction
   - **AC only**: Needs changing current to work
   - **Step-up**: Increases voltage (more turns on secondary)
   - **Step-down**: Decreases voltage (fewer turns on secondary)
   - **Power transmission**: High voltage reduces energy loss

### Things to Remember

- **Motor effect**: Force on current in magnetic field
- **Fleming's Left Hand Rule**: For motor effect
- **Electric motor**: Converts electrical to kinetic energy
- **Electromagnetic induction**: Moving magnet/coil generates electricity
- **Fleming's Right Hand Rule**: For generators
- **Generator**: Converts kinetic to electrical energy
- **AC generator**: Produces alternating current

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Describe how an electric motor works. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Current flows through coil in magnetic field (1 mark)
- Motor effect produces force on coil (1 mark)
- Force causes coil to rotate (1 mark)
- Commutator reverses current every half turn (1 mark)
- Keeps rotation in same direction (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain how a generator produces electricity. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Coil rotates in magnetic field (1 mark)
- Magnetic field through coil changes (1 mark)
- Electromagnetic induction produces voltage (1 mark)
- Current flows if circuit complete (1 mark)
- AC current produced (1 mark)

**Question 3**: State Fleming's Left Hand Rule and explain when it is used. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Thumb = Motion, First finger = Field, Second finger = Current (1 mark)
- Used for motor effect / electric motors (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Explain why a generator produces AC current. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Coil rotates, changing direction relative to field (1 mark)
- Induced current changes direction (1 mark)
- Alternating current produced (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing motor and generator
- Using wrong Fleming's rule
- Not understanding motor effect
- Not understanding electromagnetic induction
- Confusing commutator and slip rings
- Not understanding AC vs DC in generators

## General Exam Tips

### Command Words
- **State/Name**: Give a single word, phrase or number
- **Describe**: Give details about what happens
- **Explain**: Give reasons for why something happens
- **Calculate**: Work out a numerical answer (show working)
- **Compare**: Identify similarities and differences
- **Suggest**: Use your knowledge to propose an answer
- **Sketch**: Draw approximate shape (not to scale)
- **Plot**: Mark points accurately on graph
- **Draw**: Accurate diagram with labels

### Common Mistakes Across All Topics
- Not reading questions carefully
- Not using correct physics terminology
- Confusing similar concepts (e.g., speed/velocity, mass/weight)
- Not showing working in calculations
- Not including units in answers
- Not using correct number of significant figures
- Forgetting to convert units before calculations
- Not labeling diagrams clearly
- Not understanding what graphs represent

- Always include **units** in measurements and calculations

- Use **appropriate apparatus** and techniques

- Draw **clear, labeled diagrams**

- Draw **line of best fit** (not dot-to-dot)
