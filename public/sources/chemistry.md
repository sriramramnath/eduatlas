## Chapter 1: States of Matter

### Main Points

1. **Three States of Matter**
   - **Solid**: Fixed shape and volume, particles close together, vibrate in fixed positions
   - **Liquid**: Fixed volume, takes shape of container, particles close together, can move
   - **Gas**: No fixed shape or volume, particles far apart, move randomly

2. **Particle Model**
   - **All matter**: Made of tiny particles (atoms, molecules, ions)
   - **Particles in constant motion**: More energy = faster motion
   - **Forces between particles**: Strong in solids, weaker in liquids, very weak in gases
   - **Temperature**: Related to average kinetic energy of particles

3. **Changes of State**
   - **Melting**: Solid → Liquid (energy needed to break bonds)
   - **Freezing**: Liquid → Solid (energy released as bonds form)
   - **Boiling/Evaporation**: Liquid → Gas (energy needed)
   - **Condensation**: Gas → Liquid (energy released)
   - **Sublimation**: Solid → Gas directly (e.g., iodine, dry ice)
   - **During state change**: Temperature constant (energy used to change state, not temperature)

4. **Diffusion**
   - **Definition**: Spreading out of particles from high concentration to low concentration
   - **Occurs in**: All states, fastest in gases, slowest in solids
   - **Examples**: Smell spreading in air, food coloring in water, copper sulfate in water
   - **Rate increases**: With temperature (particles move faster)

5. **Brownian Motion**
   - **Definition**: Random movement of small particles suspended in fluid
   - **Caused by**: Collisions with fast-moving particles
   - **Evidence**: Supports particle model of matter

### Things to Remember

- **Solid**: Fixed shape, particles vibrate
- **Liquid**: Fixed volume, particles can move
- **Gas**: No fixed shape/volume, particles move randomly
- **During state change**: Temperature constant
- **Diffusion**: Particles spread from high to low concentration
- **Faster diffusion**: Higher temperature, gases diffuse faster than liquids

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Describe the arrangement and movement of particles in a solid. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Particles close together (1 mark)
- Arranged in regular pattern (1 mark)
- Vibrate in fixed positions (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why the temperature stays constant during melting. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Energy is used to break bonds (1 mark)
- Not used to increase kinetic energy/temperature (1 mark)
- Temperature constant until all solid melted (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain what is meant by diffusion. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Spreading out of particles (1 mark)
- From high concentration to low concentration (1 mark)
- Due to random movement of particles (1 mark)

**Question 4**: State why diffusion occurs faster in gases than in liquids. (1 mark)

**Answer**: Particles in gases move faster / have more energy / are further apart (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing states of matter
- Not understanding particle model
- Stating temperature changes during state change
- Not understanding diffusion
- Confusing melting and freezing
- Not understanding why temperature constant during state change

## Chapter 2: Elements, Compounds and Mixtures

### Main Points

1. **Elements**
   - **Definition**: Substance made of only one type of atom
   - **Cannot be broken down**: Into simpler substances by chemical means
   - **Examples**: Oxygen (O₂), Hydrogen (H₂), Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu)
   - **Represented by**: Chemical symbols (e.g., O, H, Fe, Cu)

2. **Compounds**
   - **Definition**: Substance made of two or more different elements chemically combined
   - **Fixed composition**: Always same ratio of elements
   - **Properties**: Different from constituent elements
   - **Cannot be separated**: By physical means (need chemical reaction)
   - **Examples**: Water (H₂O), Carbon dioxide (CO₂), Sodium chloride (NaCl)

3. **Mixtures**
   - **Definition**: Two or more substances not chemically combined
   - **Variable composition**: Can have different ratios
   - **Properties**: Similar to constituent substances
   - **Can be separated**: By physical means (filtration, distillation, chromatography, etc.)
   - **Examples**: Air, sea water, crude oil, alloys

4. **Separation Techniques**
   - **Filtration**: Separates insoluble solid from liquid (e.g., sand from water)
   - **Evaporation**: Separates soluble solid from solution (e.g., salt from salt water)
   - **Distillation**: Separates liquids with different boiling points (e.g., water and ethanol)
   - **Fractional distillation**: Separates mixture of liquids (e.g., crude oil)
   - **Chromatography**: Separates mixtures of colored substances (e.g., ink, dyes)
   - **Crystallization**: Separates soluble solid from solution by forming crystals

5. **Differences**
   - **Elements vs Compounds**: Elements = one type of atom, Compounds = two or more elements chemically combined
   - **Compounds vs Mixtures**: Compounds = fixed composition, chemically combined; Mixtures = variable composition, physically mixed

### Things to Remember

- **Element**: One type of atom only
- **Compound**: Two or more elements chemically combined
- **Mixture**: Two or more substances physically mixed
- **Compounds**: Fixed composition, properties different from elements
- **Mixtures**: Variable composition, can be separated physically
- **Separation**: Use physical methods for mixtures

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain the difference between an element and a compound. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Element contains only one type of atom (1 mark)
- Compound contains two or more different elements chemically combined (1 mark)
- Compound has fixed composition (1 mark)
- Compound has properties different from constituent elements (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Describe how you would separate a mixture of sand and water. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Use filtration (1 mark)
- Sand remains on filter paper, water passes through (1 mark)

**Question 3**: State two differences between a compound and a mixture. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Compound has fixed composition, mixture has variable composition (1 mark)
- Compound cannot be separated by physical means, mixture can (1 mark)
- Compound properties different from elements, mixture properties similar to constituents (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Name a method to separate a mixture of ethanol and water. (1 mark)

**Answer**: Fractional distillation (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing elements and compounds
- Confusing compounds and mixtures
- Not understanding that compounds have fixed composition
- Stating mixtures can be separated chemically (they use physical methods)
- Not understanding that compound properties differ from elements

## Chapter 3: Atomic Structure

### Main Points

1. **Structure of Atom**
   - **Nucleus**: Contains protons and neutrons (positively charged, very small, dense)
   - **Electrons**: Orbit nucleus in shells (negatively charged, very light)
   - **Proton**: Charge +1, mass 1, in nucleus
   - **Neutron**: Charge 0, mass 1, in nucleus
   - **Electron**: Charge -1, mass ~0 (1/1840), in shells
   - **Atom**: Neutral (same number of protons and electrons)

2. **Atomic Number and Mass Number**
   - **Atomic number (Z)**: Number of protons (also number of electrons in neutral atom)
   - **Mass number (A)**: Number of protons + neutrons
   - **Number of neutrons**: Mass number - Atomic number
   - **Determines element**: Atomic number determines which element

3. **Electron Shells**
   - **First shell**: Maximum 2 electrons
   - **Second shell**: Maximum 8 electrons
   - **Third shell**: Maximum 8 electrons (for first 20 elements)
   - **Electrons fill**: Inner shells first
   - **Outer shell**: Determines chemical properties

4. **Isotopes**
   - **Definition**: Atoms of same element with different number of neutrons
   - **Same atomic number**: Same number of protons
   - **Different mass number**: Different number of neutrons
   - **Same chemical properties**: Same number of electrons
   - **Different physical properties**: Different mass, may be radioactive
   - **Examples**: Carbon-12 and Carbon-14, Hydrogen-1, Hydrogen-2, Hydrogen-3

5. **Relative Atomic Mass (Ar)**
   - **Definition**: Average mass of atoms compared to carbon-12
   - **Takes into account**: Abundance of different isotopes
   - **Calculation**: (Mass₁ × Abundance₁ + Mass₂ × Abundance₂) ÷ 100
   - **Units**: No units (relative)

6. **Ions**
   - **Definition**: Charged particles (atoms that have gained or lost electrons)
   - **Cations**: Positive ions (lost electrons, e.g., Na⁺, Ca²⁺)
   - **Anions**: Negative ions (gained electrons, e.g., Cl⁻, O²⁻)
   - **Formed**: To achieve stable electron arrangement (full outer shell)

### Things to Remember

- **Atomic number** = number of protons = number of electrons (in neutral atom)
- **Mass number** = protons + neutrons
- **Isotopes**: Same element, different neutrons
- **Electron shells**: 2, 8, 8 (for first 20 elements)
- **Ions**: Charged particles (gained or lost electrons)
- **Relative atomic mass**: Average mass considering isotopes

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: An atom has atomic number 11 and mass number 23. State the number of protons, neutrons and electrons. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Protons: 11 (1 mark)
- Neutrons: 23 - 11 = 12 (1 mark)
- Electrons: 11 (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain what is meant by isotopes. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Atoms of same element (1 mark)
- With different number of neutrons (1 mark)
- Same atomic number, different mass number (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Draw the electron arrangement for an atom of oxygen (atomic number 8). (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- First shell: 2 electrons (1 mark)
- Second shell: 6 electrons (1 mark)
- OR: 2,6 arrangement (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Explain why isotopes have the same chemical properties. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Same number of electrons (1 mark)
- Same electron arrangement (1 mark)
- Chemical properties depend on electron arrangement (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing atomic number and mass number
- Not understanding isotopes
- Drawing wrong electron arrangements
- Confusing protons and electrons
- Not understanding ions
- Confusing relative atomic mass with mass number

## Chapter 4: The Periodic Table

### Main Points

1. **Periodic Table Structure**
   - **Periods**: Horizontal rows (elements with same number of electron shells)
   - **Groups**: Vertical columns (elements with same number of outer electrons)
   - **Metals**: Left side and center
   - **Non-metals**: Right side
   - **Metalloids**: Between metals and non-metals (e.g., silicon, germanium)

2. **Group 1 (Alkali Metals)**
   - **Elements**: Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Rubidium (Rb), Cesium (Cs), Francium (Fr)
   - **Properties**: Soft, low density, reactive, one outer electron
   - **Trends**: More reactive down group, lower melting point down group
   - **Reactions**: React with water to form alkalis, react with oxygen to form oxides

3. **Group 7 (Halogens)**
   - **Elements**: Fluorine (F), Chlorine (Cl), Bromine (Br), Iodine (I), Astatine (At)
   - **Properties**: Colored, diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂), reactive, seven outer electrons
   - **Trends**: Less reactive down group, higher melting/boiling point down group, darker color down group
   - **Reactions**: Form salts with metals, displace less reactive halogens

4. **Group 0 (Noble Gases)**
   - **Elements**: Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), Radon (Rn)
   - **Properties**: Inert (unreactive), full outer shell, monatomic, low density
   - **Uses**: Helium (balloons), Neon (signs), Argon (light bulbs)

5. **Transition Metals**
   - **Location**: Middle of periodic table (Groups 3-12)
   - **Properties**: Hard, high density, high melting points, form colored compounds, act as catalysts
   - **Examples**: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Zinc (Zn), Nickel (Ni)

6. **Periodic Trends**
   - **Across period**: Metallic character decreases, atomic radius decreases
   - **Down group**: Reactivity changes (Group 1 increases, Group 7 decreases), atomic radius increases
   - **Metallic character**: Decreases left to right, increases top to bottom

### Things to Remember

- **Periods**: Same number of shells
- **Groups**: Same number of outer electrons
- **Group 1**: More reactive down group
- **Group 7**: Less reactive down group
- **Group 0**: Inert, full outer shell
- **Transition metals**: Form colored compounds, act as catalysts

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain why elements in the same group have similar chemical properties. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Same number of outer electrons (1 mark)
- Same electron arrangement (1 mark)
- Chemical properties depend on outer electrons (1 mark)

**Question 2**: State the trend in reactivity down Group 1. (1 mark)

**Answer**: Reactivity increases down the group (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain why noble gases are unreactive. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Full outer shell (1 mark)
- Stable electron arrangement (1 mark)
- Do not need to gain or lose electrons (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Name two properties of transition metals. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Form colored compounds (1 mark)
- Act as catalysts (1 mark)
- High melting points (1 mark)
- High density (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing periods and groups
- Not understanding group trends
- Confusing Group 1 and Group 7 reactivity trends
- Not understanding why noble gases are unreactive
- Confusing metals and non-metals

## Chapter 5: Chemical Formulae, Equations and Calculations

### Main Points

1. **Chemical Formulae**
   - **Shows**: Elements present and ratio of atoms
   - **Examples**: H₂O (2 hydrogen, 1 oxygen), CO₂ (1 carbon, 2 oxygen), NaCl (1 sodium, 1 chlorine)
   - **Ionic compounds**: Formula shows ratio of ions (e.g., NaCl, CaCl₂, Al₂O₃)
   - **Molecular compounds**: Formula shows actual number of atoms (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, NH₃)

2. **Writing Formulae**
   - **Ionic compounds**: Balance charges (e.g., Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl, Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → CaCl₂)
   - **Use valency**: Number of bonds atom can form
   - **Cross-over method**: For ionic compounds (e.g., Ca²⁺O²⁻ → Ca₂O₂ → CaO)

3. **Balancing Equations**
   - **Law of Conservation of Mass**: Atoms cannot be created or destroyed
   - **Balanced equation**: Same number of each type of atom on both sides
   - **Method**: Add coefficients (numbers in front) to balance atoms
   - **Example**: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (4H, 2O on both sides)

4. **Relative Formula Mass (Mr)**
   - **Definition**: Sum of relative atomic masses in formula
   - **Calculation**: Add up Ar values for all atoms
   - **Example**: H₂O = (2 × 1) + 16 = 18
   - **Units**: No units (relative)

5. **Moles**
   - **Definition**: Amount of substance containing 6.02 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's constant)
   - **Molar mass**: Mass of one mole (same as Mr in grams)
   - **Number of moles**: Mass ÷ Molar mass, or n = m/Mr
   - **Units**: mol

6. **Calculations**
   - **Mass from moles**: Mass = Moles × Mr
   - **Moles from mass**: Moles = Mass ÷ Mr
   - **Percentage by mass**: (Mass of element ÷ Mass of compound) × 100
   - **Empirical formula**: Simplest whole number ratio of atoms

7. **Empirical Formula**
   - **Definition**: Simplest whole number ratio of atoms
   - **Method**: 
     - Find mass/percentage of each element
     - Divide by Ar to get moles
     - Divide by smallest to get ratio
   - **Example**: CH₂O (glucose is C₆H₁₂O₆, empirical is CH₂O)

### Things to Remember

- **Balanced equations**: Same atoms on both sides
- **Mr**: Sum of Ar values
- **Mole**: 6.02 × 10²³ particles
- **n = m/Mr**: Moles = mass ÷ molar mass
- **Empirical formula**: Simplest ratio
- **Percentage by mass**: (Element mass ÷ Compound mass) × 100

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Balance the equation: Mg + O₂ → MgO (2 marks)

**Answer**: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (1 mark for correct coefficients, 1 mark for balanced equation)

**Question 2**: Calculate the relative formula mass of CaCO₃. (Ar: Ca = 40, C = 12, O = 16) (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Mr = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) (1 mark)
- Mr = 100 (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Calculate the number of moles in 18 g of water (H₂O). (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Mr of H₂O = (2 × 1) + 16 = 18 (1 mark)
- Moles = 18 ÷ 18 = 1 mol (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Calculate the percentage by mass of oxygen in H₂O. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Mr of H₂O = 18 (1 mark)
- % O = (16 ÷ 18) × 100 = 88.9% (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Not balancing equations correctly
- Confusing Mr and Ar
- Not understanding moles
- Making calculation errors
- Not finding empirical formula correctly
- Forgetting units in calculations

## Chapter 6: Ionic Bonding

### Main Points

1. **Ionic Bonding**
   - **Definition**: Transfer of electrons from metal to non-metal
   - **Forms**: Ions (charged particles)
   - **Occurs between**: Metals and non-metals
   - **Result**: Oppositely charged ions attract (electrostatic attraction)

2. **Formation of Ions**
   - **Metals**: Lose electrons to form positive ions (cations)
   - **Non-metals**: Gain electrons to form negative ions (anions)
   - **Reason**: To achieve stable electron arrangement (full outer shell)
   - **Examples**: 
     - Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (loses 1 electron)
     - Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (gains 1 electron)
     - Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (loses 2 electrons)

3. **Ionic Compounds**
   - **Structure**: Giant ionic lattice (regular arrangement of ions)
   - **Properties**:
     - High melting/boiling points (strong bonds)
     - Soluble in water (usually)
     - Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move)
     - Brittle (shatter when hit)
   - **Examples**: NaCl, MgO, CaCl₂, Al₂O₃

4. **Dot and Cross Diagrams**
   - **Show**: Transfer of electrons
   - **Dots**: Electrons from one atom
   - **Crosses**: Electrons from other atom
   - **After bonding**: Ions shown with charges

5. **Formulae of Ionic Compounds**
   - **Balance charges**: Total positive charge = total negative charge
   - **Examples**: 
     - Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl
     - Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → CaCl₂
     - Al³⁺ + 3Cl⁻ → AlCl₃
     - Ca²⁺ + O²⁻ → CaO

### Things to Remember

- **Ionic bonding**: Transfer of electrons
- **Metals lose**, **non-metals gain** electrons
- **Ionic compounds**: High melting points, conduct when molten/dissolved
- **Giant ionic lattice**: Regular arrangement
- **Formulae**: Balance charges
- **Ions**: Achieve stable electron arrangement

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain how ionic bonding occurs between sodium and chlorine. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Sodium atom loses one electron (1 mark)
- Forms Na⁺ ion (1 mark)
- Chlorine atom gains one electron (1 mark)
- Forms Cl⁻ ion (1 mark)
- Oppositely charged ions attract (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why ionic compounds have high melting points. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Strong electrostatic forces between ions (1 mark)
- Large amount of energy needed to break bonds (1 mark)
- Giant ionic lattice structure (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain why sodium chloride conducts electricity when molten but not when solid. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Ions are free to move when molten (1 mark)
- Ions are fixed in position when solid (1 mark)
- Electricity needs moving charged particles (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Write the formula for calcium oxide. (1 mark)

**Answer**: CaO (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Not understanding electron transfer
- Confusing ionic and covalent bonding
- Not understanding why ionic compounds have high melting points
- Not understanding conductivity
- Writing wrong formulae
- Not drawing dot and cross diagrams correctly

## Chapter 7: Covalent Bonding

### Main Points

1. **Covalent Bonding**
   - **Definition**: Sharing of electrons between non-metal atoms
   - **Forms**: Molecules
   - **Occurs between**: Non-metals
   - **Reason**: To achieve stable electron arrangement (full outer shell)

2. **Formation of Covalent Bonds**
   - **Shared pair**: Two electrons shared between two atoms
   - **Single bond**: One shared pair (e.g., H-H, H-Cl)
   - **Double bond**: Two shared pairs (e.g., O=O, C=O)
   - **Triple bond**: Three shared pairs (e.g., N≡N)

3. **Covalent Molecules**
   - **Simple molecules**: Small molecules (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, NH₃, CH₄)
   - **Giant covalent structures**: Large networks (e.g., diamond, graphite, silicon dioxide)
   - **Properties of simple molecules**:
     - Low melting/boiling points (weak intermolecular forces)
     - Do not conduct electricity (no ions or free electrons)
     - Usually insoluble in water (except some like HCl, NH₃)

4. **Giant Covalent Structures**
   - **Diamond**: Each carbon bonded to 4 others, very hard, high melting point, does not conduct
   - **Graphite**: Each carbon bonded to 3 others, layers slide, conducts electricity, soft
   - **Silicon dioxide (SiO₂)**: Similar to diamond, high melting point, hard

5. **Dot and Cross Diagrams**
   - **Show**: Shared electrons
   - **Dots**: Electrons from one atom
   - **Crosses**: Electrons from other atom
   - **Shared pairs**: Shown between atoms

6. **Displayed Formulae**
   - **Show**: All atoms and bonds
   - **Lines**: Represent bonds (single, double, triple)
   - **Example**: H-O-H (water), O=C=O (carbon dioxide)

### Things to Remember

- **Covalent bonding**: Sharing electrons
- **Between non-metals**: Only
- **Simple molecules**: Low melting points
- **Giant structures**: High melting points
- **Graphite conducts**: Has delocalized electrons
- **Diamond does not conduct**: All electrons used in bonding

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain how a covalent bond forms between two hydrogen atoms. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Each hydrogen atom shares one electron (1 mark)
- Shared pair of electrons forms covalent bond (1 mark)
- Both atoms achieve stable electron arrangement (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain why simple covalent molecules have low melting points. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Weak intermolecular forces between molecules (1 mark)
- Little energy needed to break these forces (1 mark)
- Covalent bonds within molecules are strong (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain why graphite conducts electricity but diamond does not. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Graphite has delocalized electrons between layers (1 mark)
- These electrons can move and carry charge (1 mark)
- Diamond has all electrons used in covalent bonds (1 mark)
- No free electrons in diamond (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Draw a dot and cross diagram for methane (CH₄). (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Carbon atom in center (1 mark)
- Four hydrogen atoms around it (1 mark)
- Each H shares one electron with C (1 mark)
- C shares one electron with each H (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing ionic and covalent bonding
- Not understanding why simple molecules have low melting points
- Confusing intermolecular and intramolecular forces
- Not understanding graphite structure
- Drawing wrong dot and cross diagrams
- Confusing simple and giant covalent structures

## Chapter 8: The Alkali Metals

### Main Points

1. **Group 1 Elements**
   - **Elements**: Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Rubidium (Rb), Cesium (Cs), Francium (Fr)
   - **Properties**: 
     - Soft (can be cut with knife)
     - Low density (float on water, except lithium)
     - Shiny when cut (tarnish quickly)
     - Good conductors of heat and electricity
     - One outer electron

2. **Reactivity**
   - **Trend**: More reactive down the group
   - **Reason**: Outer electron further from nucleus, easier to lose
   - **Reactions**: Lose one electron to form +1 ions
   - **Most reactive**: Francium (bottom of group)

3. **Reaction with Water**
   - **Products**: Metal hydroxide + Hydrogen gas
   - **Equation**: Metal + Water → Metal hydroxide + Hydrogen
   - **Example**: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
   - **Observations**: 
     - Fizzes/moves on surface
     - Produces hydrogen (burns with pop)
     - Forms alkaline solution
   - **Reactivity**: Lithium (slow), Sodium (vigorous), Potassium (very vigorous, flame)

4. **Reaction with Oxygen**
   - **Products**: Metal oxide
   - **Equation**: 4Metal + O₂ → 2Metal₂O
   - **Example**: 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O
   - **Observations**: Tarnish quickly, form oxide layer

5. **Reaction with Chlorine**
   - **Products**: Metal chloride
   - **Equation**: 2Metal + Cl₂ → 2MetalCl
   - **Example**: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
   - **Observations**: Vigorous reaction, white solid formed

6. **Uses**
   - **Sodium**: Street lamps, making compounds
   - **Lithium**: Batteries
   - **Potassium**: Fertilizers

### Things to Remember

- **Group 1**: Alkali metals, one outer electron
- **More reactive down group**: Easier to lose electron
- **React with water**: Form alkalis and hydrogen
- **Stored in oil**: Prevent reaction with air/water
- **Form +1 ions**: Lose one electron
- **Soft and low density**: Physical properties

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: State the trend in reactivity down Group 1. (1 mark)

**Answer**: Reactivity increases down the group (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Write a balanced equation for the reaction between sodium and water. (2 marks)

**Answer**: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ (1 mark for correct reactants/products, 1 mark for balanced)

**Question 3**: Explain why potassium is more reactive than sodium. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Outer electron further from nucleus in potassium (1 mark)
- Less attraction to nucleus (1 mark)
- Easier to lose electron (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Describe what you would observe when sodium reacts with water. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Fizzes/moves on surface (1 mark)
- Produces hydrogen gas (1 mark)
- Forms alkaline solution (1 mark)
- May produce flame (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing Group 1 with Group 7 reactivity trends
- Not understanding why reactivity increases down group
- Writing unbalanced equations
- Not describing observations correctly
- Confusing alkali metals with other groups
- Not understanding why stored in oil

## Chapter 9: The Halogens

### Main Points

1. **Group 7 Elements**
   - **Elements**: Fluorine (F), Chlorine (Cl), Bromine (Br), Iodine (I), Astatine (At)
   - **Properties**:
     - Colored (F₂ = pale yellow, Cl₂ = green, Br₂ = red-brown, I₂ = dark grey/black)
     - Diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂)
     - Poisonous
     - Seven outer electrons
     - Non-metals

2. **Reactivity**
   - **Trend**: Less reactive down the group
   - **Reason**: Outer shell further from nucleus, harder to gain electron
   - **Reactions**: Gain one electron to form -1 ions
   - **Most reactive**: Fluorine (top of group)

3. **Displacement Reactions**
   - **Definition**: More reactive halogen displaces less reactive halogen from its compound
   - **Rule**: Halogen higher in group displaces halogen lower in group
   - **Example**: Cl₂ + 2NaBr → 2NaCl + Br₂ (chlorine displaces bromine)
   - **Example**: Br₂ + 2NaI → 2NaBr + I₂ (bromine displaces iodine)
   - **No reaction**: Less reactive halogen cannot displace more reactive one

4. **Reaction with Metals**
   - **Products**: Metal halides (salts)
   - **Example**: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
   - **Example**: Fe + Cl₂ → FeCl₃
   - **Observations**: Vigorous reaction, colored salt formed

5. **Reaction with Hydrogen**
   - **Products**: Hydrogen halides
   - **Example**: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl
   - **Properties**: Form acidic solutions in water

6. **Physical Properties**
   - **State at room temperature**: 
     - Fluorine: Gas
     - Chlorine: Gas
     - Bromine: Liquid
     - Iodine: Solid (sublimes)
   - **Color**: Gets darker down group
   - **Melting/Boiling point**: Increases down group

7. **Uses**
   - **Chlorine**: Water treatment, making bleach, making PVC

#### **Activity 1: Using Copper to Measure the Percentage of Oxygen in Air**

**Method**
- Set up the apparatus with a silica tube packed with copper filings connected to two gas syringes.
- Push the plunger of one gas syringe fully in and pull the other out to 100 cm³ so the apparatus contains exactly 100 cm³ of air.
- Heat the silica tube strongly using a roaring Bunsen burner.
- Push the plunger of the left-hand gas syringe so the air passes over the hot copper, pushing out the plunger on the right-hand gas syringe.
- Push the plungers in and out repeatedly so the air keeps passing over the heated copper.
- Move the Bunsen burner along the tube to ensure fresh copper is always heated.
- Continue until the volume of gas no longer changes.
- Allow the apparatus to cool to room temperature.
- Record the final volume of gas in the syringe.
- Calculate the volume of oxygen used by subtracting the final volume from 100 cm³.
- Calculate the percentage of oxygen using  
  (volume of oxygen used ÷ original volume of air) × 100.

 **Precautions**
- Heat the copper strongly so it reacts fully with oxygen.
- Ensure the apparatus is airtight to prevent gas loss.
- Allow the apparatus to cool before taking the final reading.
- Use excess copper so all the oxygen can react.
- Read the gas syringe at eye level to avoid parallax error.

 **Why the Percentage of Oxygen May Be Incorrect**
- **Lower than 21%**
  - The copper was not heated strongly enough.
  - Not enough copper was used, so some oxygen remained.
  - The apparatus was not left long enough for the reaction to finish.
  - Air leaked from the apparatus.
- **Higher than 21%**
  - The final volume was measured before cooling.
  - Gas syringe readings were inaccurate.

## Chapter 11: Reactivity Series

### Main Points

1. **Reactivity Series**
   - **Order** (most reactive to least reactive): Potassium, Sodium, Lithium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminum, Zinc, Iron, Lead, Hydrogen, Copper, Silver, Gold
   - **Mnemonic**: Please Stop Calling Me A Zebra In Lead Hardly Causing Serious Grief
   - **Metals above hydrogen**: React with acids to produce hydrogen
   - **Metals below hydrogen**: Do not react with acids

2. **Reactions with Water**
   - **Very reactive** (K, Na, Li, Ca): React with cold water, produce hydrogen and metal hydroxide
   - **Moderately reactive** (Mg, Al, Zn, Fe): React with steam, produce hydrogen and metal oxide
   - **Unreactive** (Cu, Ag, Au): Do not react with water

3. **Reactions with Acids**
   - **Metals above hydrogen**: React with acids, produce salt + hydrogen
   - **Equation**: Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
   - **Example**: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
   - **Metals below hydrogen**: Do not react

4. **Reactions with Oxygen**
   - **Most metals**: React with oxygen to form metal oxides
   - **Reactivity**: More reactive metals react more vigorously
   - **Example**: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

5. **Displacement Reactions**
   - **Definition**: More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from its compound
   - **Example**: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu (zinc displaces copper)
   - **Example**: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (iron displaces copper)
   - **No reaction**: Less reactive metal cannot displace more reactive one

6. **Extraction of Metals**
   - **Unreactive metals** (Au, Ag): Found as native metals (uncombined)
   - **Moderately reactive** (Zn, Fe, Pb): Extracted by reduction with carbon
   - **Very reactive** (K, Na, Ca, Al): Extracted by electrolysis

7. **Uses Based on Reactivity**
   - **Unreactive** (Au, Ag): Jewelry (do not tarnish)
   - **Reactive** (Fe): Rusts, needs protection (painting, galvanizing)

### Things to Remember

- **Reactivity series**: Order of reactivity
- **Metals above H**: React with acids
- **Displacement**: More reactive displaces less reactive
- **Extraction**: Method depends on reactivity
- **Reaction with water**: Depends on reactivity
- **Hydrogen**: Reference point in series

## Chapter 12: Acids and Alkalis

### Main Points

1. **Acids**
   - **Definition**: Substances that produce H⁺ ions in water
   - **Properties**: 
     - Sour taste (do not taste!)
     - Turn blue litmus red
     - pH less than 7
     - React with metals above hydrogen to produce hydrogen
     - React with bases to form salts
   - **Examples**: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), Nitric acid (HNO₃)

2. **Alkalis**
   - **Definition**: Bases that dissolve in water, produce OH⁻ ions
   - **Properties**:
     - Bitter taste, soapy feel (do not taste or touch!)
     - Turn red litmus blue
     - pH greater than 7
     - React with acids to form salts
   - **Examples**: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), Potassium hydroxide (KOH), Calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂)

3. **Bases**
   - **Definition**: Substances that neutralize acids
   - **Include**: Metal oxides, metal hydroxides, alkalis
   - **Alkalis**: Bases that dissolve in water
   - **Examples**: Copper oxide (base, not alkali), Sodium hydroxide (alkali)

4. **pH Scale**
   - **Range**: 0 to 14
   - **Acids**: pH 0-6 (lower = stronger acid)
   - **Neutral**: pH 7 (water)
   - **Alkalis**: pH 8-14 (higher = stronger alkali)
   - **Indicators**: Universal indicator, litmus, phenolphthalein, methyl orange

5. **Neutralization**
   - **Definition**: Reaction between acid and base/alkali
   - **Products**: Salt + Water
   - **Equation**: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
   - **Example**: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
   - **Ionic equation**: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O

6. **Acid + Metal**
   - **Products**: Salt + Hydrogen
   - **Equation**: Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
   - **Only metals above hydrogen**: React
   - **Example**: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂

7. **Acid + Metal Carbonate**
   - **Products**: Salt + Water + Carbon Dioxide
   - **Equation**: Metal carbonate + Acid → Salt + Water + CO₂
   - **Example**: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

8. **Acid and Base Theory**
   
   - **Acids**: Proton donors (donate H⁺ ions)
   - **Bases**: Proton acceptors (accept H⁺ ions)
   - **Conjugate pairs**: Acid-base pairs that differ by one H⁺
   - **Example**: HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
     - HCl is acid (donates H⁺)
     - H₂O is base (accepts H⁺)
     - H₃O⁺ is conjugate acid
     - Cl⁻ is conjugate base
   
   **Lewis Theory** (Advanced):
   - **Acids**: Electron pair acceptors
   - **Bases**: Electron pair donors
   - **Broader definition**: Includes more substances
   
   **Strength of Acids and Bases**:
   - **Strong acids**: Completely ionize in water (e.g., HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃)
   - **Weak acids**: Partially ionize in water (e.g., CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃)
   - **Strong bases**: Completely dissociate in water (e.g., NaOH, KOH)
   - **Weak bases**: Partially dissociate in water (e.g., NH₃, Ca(OH)₂)
   - **pH**: Strong acids have lower pH, strong bases have higher pH
   

### Things to Remember

- **Acids**: Produce H⁺ ions, pH < 7
- **Alkalis**: Produce OH⁻ ions, pH > 7
- **Neutralization**: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
- **pH scale**: 0-14, 7 is neutral
- **Acid + Metal**: Salt + Hydrogen (metals above H only)
- **Acid + Carbonate**: Salt + Water + CO₂
- **Acids**: Produce H⁺ ions (proton donors)
- **Bases**: Produce OH⁻ ions or accept H⁺ (proton acceptors)
- **Strong acids/bases**: Completely ionize/dissociate
- **Weak acids/bases**: Partially ionize/dissociate

## Chapter 13: Acids, Bases and Salt Preparations

### Main Points

1. **Salt Preparation Methods**

   **Acid + Metal**:
   - **Products**: Salt + Hydrogen
   - **Suitable for**: Salts of metals above hydrogen
   - **Example**: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂
   - **Not suitable**: Very reactive metals (too dangerous), unreactive metals (no reaction)

   **Acid + Base/Alkali**:
   - **Products**: Salt + Water
   - **Suitable for**: All salts
   - **Example**: CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O
   - **Example**: NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O
   - **Titration**: Used for soluble salts from alkalis

   **Acid + Metal Carbonate**:
   - **Products**: Salt + Water + Carbon Dioxide
   - **Suitable for**: Salts of metals above hydrogen
   - **Example**: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
   - **Advantage**: Easy to see when reaction complete (stops fizzing)

2. **Precipitation**
   - **Definition**: Formation of insoluble solid when two solutions mix
   - **Method**: Mix solutions of soluble salts
   - **Example**: AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)
   - **Filter**: To separate precipitate
   - **Wash and dry**: To purify

3. **Crystallization**
   - **Method**: 
     - Evaporate solution until saturated
     - Cool to form crystals
     - Filter and dry
   - **Used for**: Soluble salts
   - **Example**: Making copper sulfate crystals

4. **Titration**
   - **Purpose**: Find exact volume of acid/alkali needed for neutralization
   - **Method**:
     - Add indicator to alkali
     - Add acid from burette until color changes
     - Record volume
     - Repeat for accuracy
   - **Used for**: Making soluble salts from alkalis

5. **Common Salts**
   - **Sodium chloride (NaCl)**: Acid + Alkali, or Acid + Carbonate
   - **Copper sulfate (CuSO₄)**: Acid + Base (CuO), or Acid + Carbonate
   - **Calcium chloride (CaCl₂)**: Acid + Carbonate, or Acid + Base
   - **Zinc sulfate (ZnSO₄)**: Acid + Metal, or Acid + Base

6. **Insoluble Salts**
   - **Prepared by**: Precipitation
   - **Examples**: Silver chloride (AgCl), Barium sulfate (BaSO₄), Lead iodide (PbI₂)

### Things to Remember

- **Acid + Metal**: Salt + H₂ (metals above H)
- **Acid + Base**: Salt + H₂O (all salts)
- **Acid + Carbonate**: Salt + H₂O + CO₂
- **Precipitation**: For insoluble salts
- **Crystallization**: For soluble salts
- **Titration**: For accurate neutralization

## General Exam Tips

### Command Words
- **State/Name**: Give a single word, phrase or number
- **Describe**: Give details about what happens
- **Explain**: Give reasons for why something happens
- **Calculate**: Work out a numerical answer (show working)
- **Compare**: Identify similarities and differences
- **Suggest**: Use your knowledge to propose an answer
- **Write**: Write an equation or formula
- **Balance**: Balance a chemical equation

### Common Mistakes Across All Topics
- Not reading questions carefully
- Not using correct chemical terminology
- Confusing similar concepts (e.g., elements/compounds/mixtures, acids/bases/alkalis)
- Not balancing equations correctly
- Not showing working in calculations
- Not including units in answers
- Forgetting state symbols in equations
- Not understanding chemical tests
- Confusing ionic and covalent bonding

### Practical Skills
- Always include **state symbols** in equations: (s), (l), (g), (aq)
- **Balance equations**: Same atoms on both sides
- **Describe tests clearly**: Include all steps
- Use **correct chemical names** and formulae
- **Show working** in calculations
- **Include units** in answers
- **Repeat** experiments for reliability
- **Control variables** to ensure fair test

### Formula Sheet Reminders
- **n = m/Mr** (moles = mass ÷ molar mass)
- **Percentage by mass**: (Element mass ÷ Compound mass) × 100
- **Concentration**: Mass ÷ Volume, or Moles ÷ Volume
- **Gas volume**: Moles × 24 dm³ (at room temperature and pressure)
