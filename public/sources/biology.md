## Chapter 1: Life Processes

### Main Points

1. **Characteristics of Living Organisms (MRS GREN)**
   - **M**ovement - by muscles in animals, slow growth movements in plants
   - **R**espiration - release energy from food
   - **S**ensitivity - respond to stimuli/changes in surroundings
   - **H**omeostasis - maintenence of internal body conditions within a narrow limit
   - **G**rowth - increase in size and complexity
   - **R**eproduction - produce offspring
   - **E**xcretion - get rid of waste products
   - **N**utrition - plants make their own food, animals eat other organisms

2. **Cell Structure**
   - **Animal cells** contain: nucleus, cytoplasm, cell membrane, mitochondria, ribosomes, temporary vacuoles
   - **Plant cells** contain all animal cell structures PLUS: cell wall (cellulose), permanent vacuole, chloroplasts
   - **Organelles and functions:**
     - Nucleus: contains DNA, controls cell activities
     - Cytoplasm: site of chemical reactions
     - Cell membrane: controls what enters/leaves cell (selectively permeable)
     - Mitochondria: site of aerobic respiration
     - Chloroplasts: site of photosynthesis (plants only)
     - Ribosomes: site of protein synthesis
     - Vacuole: stores cell sap (plants)

3. **Enzymes**
   - Biological catalysts that speed up reactions without being used up
   - Work by lowering activation energy
   - **Lock and key model**: substrate fits into active site
   - **Factors affecting enzyme activity:**
     - **Temperature**: increases rate up to optimum (37°C for humans), then denatures above 40°C
     - **pH**: most work best at pH 7 (neutral), except pepsin (pH 2)
   - Denaturation: permanent change in shape of active site, enzyme stops working

4. **Respiration**
   - **Aerobic respiration**: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy)
   - Occurs in mitochondria
   - Produces ATP (adenosine triphosphate) - energy currency of cells
   - **Anaerobic respiration**: glucose → lactate (+ some energy)
   - Occurs when oxygen supply is insufficient (e.g., during intense exercise)
   - Produces oxygen debt - extra oxygen needed to break down lactate

5. **Movement of Materials**
   - **Diffusion**: net movement of particles from high to low concentration (down concentration gradient)
   - **Osmosis**: net movement of water from dilute to concentrated solution across partially permeable membrane
   - **Active transport**: movement against concentration gradient, requires energy (ATP)

6. **Organ Systems**
   - Digestive system
   - Gas exchange system (lungs)
   - Circulatory system (heart and blood vessels)
   - Excretory system (kidneys)
   - Nervous system (brain, spinal cord, nerves)
   - Endocrine system (hormone-producing glands)
   - Reproductive system

### Things to Remember

- **Respiration ≠ Breathing**: Respiration is the chemical reaction releasing energy; breathing is the mechanical process moving air
- **Metabolism**: Sum of all chemical reactions in a cell
- **Enzymes are proteins** - denatured by heat and extreme pH
- **ATP** is the energy currency - used for active transport, muscle contraction, cell division
- **Diffusion is passive** (no energy needed), **active transport requires energy**
- **Osmosis** only involves water molecules
- **Stem cells** can differentiate into specialized cells

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Compare and contrast plant and animal cells. (4 marks)

**Answer**: 
- Both have: nucleus (1 mark), cytoplasm (1 mark), cell membrane (1 mark), mitochondria (1 mark), ribosomes (1 mark)
- Plant cells have: cell wall made of cellulose (1 mark), permanent vacuole (1 mark), chloroplasts (1 mark)
- Animal cells have: temporary vacuoles (1 mark), no cell wall (1 mark), no chloroplasts (1 mark)

**Question 1b**: Name three structures found in plant cells but not in animal cells. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Cell wall (1 mark)
- Chloroplasts (1 mark)
- Permanent vacuole (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Explain how temperature affects enzyme activity. (6 marks)

**Answer**: 
- As temperature increases, enzyme activity increases (1 mark) - molecules move faster, more collisions (1 mark)
- Up to optimum temperature, rate increases (1 mark)
- At optimum temperature, maximum rate of reaction occurs (1 mark) - most enzyme-substrate collisions (1 mark)
- Above optimum, enzyme denatures (1 mark) - active site changes shape permanently (1 mark)
- Denatured enzyme can no longer catalyze reactions (1 mark) - substrate no longer fits (1 mark)
- Graph shows bell-shaped curve (1 mark)

**Question 2b**: An enzyme has an optimum temperature of 37°C. Explain why the rate decreases above 60°C. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- High temperature denatures the enzyme (1 mark)
- Heat breaks bonds in protein (1 mark)
- Active site changes shape permanently (1 mark)
- Substrate can no longer fit (1 mark)
- Enzyme cannot catalyze reaction (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Describe the process of aerobic respiration. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Glucose and oxygen react together (1 mark)
- Occurs in mitochondria (1 mark)
- Produces carbon dioxide and water (1 mark)
- Releases energy (1 mark) stored in ATP molecules (1 mark)
- Equation: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy) (1 mark)
- Used for cellular processes like active transport (1 mark) and muscle contraction (1 mark)

**Question 3b**: State the word equation for aerobic respiration. (1 mark)

**Answer**: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water (+ Energy) (1 mark)

**Question 3c**: Name two uses of the energy released in respiration. (2 marks)

**Answer**:
- Active transport (1 mark)
- Muscle contraction (1 mark)
- Cell division (1 mark)
- Building large molecules (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing respiration with breathing
- Saying enzymes are "used up" in reactions (they are not consumed)
- Stating that all enzymes work at 37°C (only mammalian enzymes)
- Confusing diffusion and active transport (diffusion is passive, active transport requires energy)
- Forgetting that osmosis only involves water
- Stating plant cells don't have mitochondria (they do!)
- Confusing egestion (removal of faeces) with excretion (removal of metabolic waste)

## Chapter 2: The Variety of Living Organisms

### Main Points

1. **Classification of Organisms**
   - **Five Kingdoms**: Animals, Plants, Fungi, Protoctists, Bacteria
   - **Eukaryotic organisms**: Have nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (animals, plants, fungi, protoctists)
   - **Prokaryotic organisms**: No nucleus, simpler structure (bacteria)

2. **Animals**
   - Multicellular
   - No cell walls
   - No chloroplasts (cannot photosynthesize)
   - Store carbohydrate as glycogen
   - Feed on other organisms
   - Have nervous system for coordination

3. **Plants**
   - Multicellular
   - Have cell walls (cellulose)
   - Have chloroplasts (photosynthesize)
   - Store carbohydrate as starch or sucrose
   - Examples: maize, peas, flowering plants

4. **Fungi**
   - Can be multicellular (mushrooms, moulds) or unicellular (yeast)
   - Have cell walls (chitin, not cellulose)
   - No chloroplasts
   - Feed by saprotrophic nutrition (absorb nutrients from dead/decaying matter)
   - Examples: mushrooms, yeast, moulds

5. **Protoctists**
   - Mostly unicellular
   - Some have chloroplasts (algae), some don't (protozoa)
   - Examples: Amoeba, Chlorella, Plasmodium (causes malaria)

6. **Bacteria**
   - Prokaryotic (no nucleus)
   - Single-celled, very small (1-5µm)
   - Three shapes: spheres, rods, spirals
   - Have cell wall (peptidoglycan, not cellulose)
   - Some have flagella for movement
   - Examples: Lactobacillus bulgaricus, Pneumococcus
   - Some are pathogens (disease-causing)

7. **Viruses**
   - **NOT considered living organisms**
   - Much smaller than bacteria (0.01-0.1µm)
   - Structure: protein coat containing genetic material (DNA or RNA)
   - Cannot carry out life processes independently
   - Reproduce by taking over host cell's genetic machinery
   - Examples: influenza, HIV, tobacco mosaic virus

### Things to Remember

- **Viruses are NOT alive** - they don't feed, respire, excrete, move, grow, or respond (except reproduce parasitically)
- **Bacteria are prokaryotic** - no nucleus, simpler than eukaryotic cells
- **Fungi cell walls are made of chitin**, not cellulose
- **Protoctists** are a "dustbin kingdom" - organisms that don't fit elsewhere
- **Eukaryotic** = "true nucleus", **Prokaryotic** = "before nucleus"
- **Pathogen**: organism that causes disease

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain why viruses are not considered to be living organisms. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Viruses cannot carry out life processes independently
- They do not feed, respire, excrete, move, grow, or respond to stimuli
- They can only reproduce by taking over a host cell's machinery
- They are on the border between living and non-living

**Question 2**: Describe the differences between eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Eukaryotic cells have a nucleus surrounded by a membrane; prokaryotic cells have no true nucleus
- Eukaryotic cells have membrane-bound organelles (mitochondria, chloroplasts); prokaryotic cells do not
- Eukaryotic cells are larger (10-50µm); prokaryotic cells are smaller (1-5µm)
- Eukaryotic cells have linear DNA; prokaryotic cells have circular DNA

**Question 3**: Name the five kingdoms and give one example of each. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Animals: e.g., humans, insects, fish
- Plants: e.g., maize, peas, flowering plants
- Fungi: e.g., mushrooms, yeast, moulds
- Protoctists: e.g., Amoeba, Chlorella, Plasmodium
- Bacteria: e.g., Lactobacillus, Pneumococcus

### Common Mistakes

- Stating viruses are living organisms
- Confusing bacteria and viruses (bacteria are cells, viruses are not)
- Saying fungi have cellulose cell walls (they have chitin)
- Forgetting that plants store starch, animals store glycogen
- Stating all protoctists are unicellular (some seaweeds are multicellular)
- Confusing prokaryotic and eukaryotic structures

## Chapter 3: Breathing and Gas Exchange

### Main Points

1. **Structure of Gas Exchange System**
   - **Thorax**: chest cavity containing lungs
   - **Trachea**: windpipe, splits into two bronchi
   - **Bronchi**: lead to each lung, divide into bronchioles
   - **Bronchioles**: smaller tubes ending at alveoli
   - **Alveoli**: tiny air sacs where gas exchange occurs
   - **Diaphragm**: muscular sheet separating thorax from abdomen
   - **Intercostal muscles**: between ribs (external and internal)
   - **Pleural membranes**: surround lungs, contain pleural fluid for lubrication
   - **Cartilage rings**: keep airways open (C-shaped in trachea)

2. **Ventilation (Breathing)**
   - **Inhalation (breathing in)**:
     - External intercostal muscles contract
     - Ribs move up and out
     - Diaphragm contracts and flattens
     - Volume of thorax increases
     - Pressure in thorax decreases
     - Air moves into lungs
   
   - **Exhalation (breathing out)**:
     - External intercostal muscles relax
     - Internal intercostal muscles contract
     - Ribs move down and in
     - Diaphragm relaxes and becomes dome-shaped
     - Volume of thorax decreases
     - Pressure in thorax increases
     - Air moves out of lungs

3. **Gas Exchange in Alveoli**
   - **Adaptations for efficient gas exchange**:
     - Large surface area (millions of alveoli)
     - Thin walls (one cell thick)
     - Moist surface (for gas dissolution)
     - Rich blood supply (capillaries)
     - Short diffusion distance
   
   - **Gases exchanged**:
     - Oxygen diffuses from alveoli into blood
     - Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli

4. **Composition of Air**
   - **Atmospheric air**: 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.04% carbon dioxide
   - **Exhaled air**: 79% nitrogen, 16% oxygen, 4% carbon dioxide
   - Exhaled air is warmer and saturated with water vapour

5. **Effects of Smoking**
   - **Tar**: damages cilia, causes smoker's cough, increases risk of lung cancer
   - **Nicotine**: addictive, increases heart rate and blood pressure
   - **Carbon monoxide**: combines with haemoglobin, reduces oxygen-carrying capacity
   - **Diseases caused**:
     - **Bronchitis**: inflammation of airways, excess mucus
     - **Emphysema**: breakdown of alveoli walls, reduced surface area
     - **Lung cancer**: uncontrolled cell division
     - **Coronary heart disease**: blocked arteries

### Things to Remember

- **Breathing ≠ Respiration**: Breathing is mechanical movement of air; respiration is chemical reaction
- **Lungs don't actively pump air** - pressure changes cause air movement
- **Alveoli have huge surface area** - about 70m² in humans
- **Smoking kills cilia** - prevents cleaning of airways
- **Carbon monoxide** binds more tightly to haemoglobin than oxygen
- **Emphysema is irreversible** - no cure

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Describe how air moves into the lungs during inhalation. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- External intercostal muscles contract
- Ribs move up and out
- Diaphragm contracts and flattens
- Volume of thorax increases
- Pressure in thorax decreases (below atmospheric pressure)
- Air moves into lungs down pressure gradient

**Question 2**: Explain how alveoli are adapted for gas exchange. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Large surface area due to millions of alveoli
- Thin walls (one cell thick) for short diffusion distance
- Moist surface allows gases to dissolve
- Rich blood supply maintains concentration gradient
- Close contact between alveoli and capillaries

**Question 3**: Explain how smoking causes emphysema. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Chemicals in smoke damage the walls of alveoli
- Alveoli break down and fuse together
- Forms enlarged, irregular air spaces
- Greatly reduces surface area for gas exchange
- Gas exchange becomes very inefficient

### Common Mistakes

- Saying lungs "suck in" air (pressure changes cause air movement)
- Confusing breathing with respiration
- Stating alveoli actively pump gases (diffusion is passive)
- Forgetting that exhalation involves muscle contraction (internal intercostals)
- Stating smoking only affects lungs (also affects heart and circulation)
- Confusing bronchitis and emphysema
- Not mentioning that carbon monoxide reduces oxygen transport

## Chapter 4: Food and Digestion

### Main Points

1. **Biological Molecules**
   - **Carbohydrates**:
     - Elements: Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen
     - Made from simple sugars (glucose)
     - Starch and glycogen are storage forms
     - Function: energy source, structural support
   
   - **Proteins**:
     - Elements: Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen (sometimes Sulfur)
     - Made from amino acids
     - Function: growth, repair, enzymes, hormones
   
   - **Lipids (Fats and Oils)**:
     - Elements: Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen
     - Made from glycerol and three fatty acids
     - Function: energy storage, insulation, protection, cell membranes

2. **Food Tests**
   - **Starch**: Iodine solution → blue-black colour
   - **Glucose**: Benedict's solution + heat → brick red precipitate
   - **Protein**: Biuret test (potassium hydroxide + copper sulfate) → purple/violet
   - **Lipids**: Ethanol + water → milky white emulsion

3. **Balanced Diet**
   - **Components**: Carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, vitamins, minerals, water, dietary fibre
   - **Energy requirements vary** with:
     - Activity level
     - Age
     - Pregnancy
     - Gender
   
   - **Vitamins**:
     - **Vitamin A**: Sources - liver, carrots; Function - vision, healthy skin
     - **Vitamin C**: Sources - citrus fruits; Function - healthy skin, prevents scurvy
     - **Vitamin D**: Sources - sunlight, fish; Function - calcium absorption, prevents rickets
   
   - **Minerals**:
     - **Calcium**: Sources - milk, cheese; Function - strong bones and teeth
     - **Iron**: Sources - red meat, spinach; Function - haemoglobin production, prevents anaemia
   
   - **Dietary fibre**: Prevents constipation, maintains healthy gut

4. **Digestive System Structure**
   - **Mouth**: Mechanical digestion (teeth), chemical digestion (amylase in saliva)
   - **Oesophagus**: Peristalsis moves food to stomach
   - **Stomach**: Stores food, secretes hydrochloric acid (kills bacteria, pH 2), pepsin digests proteins
   - **Small intestine**:
     - **Duodenum**: Receives bile and pancreatic juices
     - **Ileum**: Site of absorption (villi)
   - **Large intestine (Colon)**: Absorbs water, forms faeces
   - **Rectum**: Stores faeces
   - **Anus**: Egestion of faeces
   - **Pancreas**: Produces digestive enzymes
   - **Liver**: Produces bile
   - **Gall bladder**: Stores bile

5. **Digestion Process**
   - **Peristalsis**: Wave-like muscle contractions move food along gut
   - **Mechanical digestion**: Physical breakdown (chewing, churning)
   - **Chemical digestion**: Enzymes break down large molecules
   
   - **Enzymes**:
     - **Amylase**: Starch → maltose (mouth, pancreas)
     - **Maltase**: Maltose → glucose (small intestine wall)
     - **Pepsin**: Proteins → peptides (stomach, pH 2)
     - **Trypsin**: Proteins → peptides (pancreas)
     - **Peptidases**: Peptides → amino acids (small intestine wall)
     - **Lipase**: Lipids → fatty acids + glycerol (pancreas)
   
   - **Bile**: Produced by liver, stored in gall bladder
     - Neutralizes stomach acid
     - Emulsifies lipids (breaks into small droplets)

6. **Absorption in Small Intestine**
   - **Villi adaptations**:
     - Large surface area (finger-like projections)
     - Thin walls (one cell thick epithelium)
     - Rich blood supply (capillaries)
     - Lacteal (lymph vessel) for lipid absorption
     - Microvilli on surface cells (increase surface area further)
     - Muscle fibres (keep villi moving)
   
   - **Products of digestion absorbed**:
     - Glucose and amino acids → blood capillaries
     - Fatty acids and glycerol → lacteal (lymph system)
     - Vitamins, minerals, water → blood

### Things to Remember

- **Egestion ≠ Excretion**: Egestion is removal of undigested food (faeces); excretion is removal of metabolic waste
- **Digestion converts large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules**
- **Bile does NOT contain enzymes** - it emulsifies and neutralizes
- **Villi are in constant motion** - maintains concentration gradient
- **Most absorption occurs in ileum** (small intestine)
- **Hydrochloric acid kills bacteria** - important protective function
- **Pepsin works at pH 2** - adapted to stomach conditions

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Describe the test for starch in a food sample. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Place food sample on spotting tile
- Add drops of iodine solution
- Positive result: blue-black colour
- Negative result: remains brown/yellow

**Question 2**: Explain how the small intestine is adapted for absorption. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Villi provide large surface area
- Thin walls (one cell thick) for short diffusion distance
- Rich blood supply maintains concentration gradient
- Microvilli on surface cells further increase surface area
- Muscle fibres keep villi moving, maintaining gradient
- Lacteal absorbs lipids

**Question 3**: Describe the role of bile in digestion. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Produced by liver, stored in gall bladder
- Neutralizes stomach acid (makes contents alkaline)
- Emulsifies lipids (breaks into small droplets)
- Increases surface area for lipase to act

### Common Mistakes

- Confusing egestion with excretion
- Stating bile contains enzymes (it doesn't)
- Forgetting that bile neutralizes acid
- Not mentioning microvilli when describing villi adaptations
- Stating all digestion happens in stomach (most in small intestine)
- Confusing pepsin and trypsin (pepsin in stomach, trypsin in small intestine)
- Not mentioning that lipids go to lacteal, not directly to blood
- Forgetting to mention hot ethanol when testing for starch in leaves

## Chapter 5: Blood and Circulation

### Main Points

1. **Need for Transport System**
   - **Unicellular organisms**: Can rely on diffusion (large surface area to volume ratio)
   - **Multicellular organisms**: Need transport system (small surface area to volume ratio, cells deep inside body)

2. **Circulatory Systems**
   - **Single circulation** (fish): Heart → gills → body → heart (blood passes through heart once)
   - **Double circulation** (mammals): 
     - **Pulmonary circulation**: Heart → lungs → heart
     - **Systemic circulation**: Heart → body → heart
     - More efficient (higher pressure maintained)

3. **Heart Structure**
   - **Four chambers**: Right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle
   - **Valves**: 
     - Bicuspid (mitral) valve: between left atrium and ventricle
     - Tricuspid valve: between right atrium and ventricle
     - Semi-lunar valves: at base of aorta and pulmonary artery
   - **Blood vessels**:
     - **Vena cava**: Returns deoxygenated blood to right atrium
     - **Pulmonary artery**: Carries deoxygenated blood to lungs
     - **Pulmonary vein**: Returns oxygenated blood to left atrium
     - **Aorta**: Carries oxygenated blood to body

4. **Cardiac Cycle**
   - **Atrial systole**: Atria contract, blood forced into ventricles
   - **Ventricular systole**: Ventricles contract, blood forced into arteries
   - **Diastole**: Heart relaxes, chambers fill with blood
   - Valves prevent backflow

5. **Control of Heart Rate**
   - **Medulla** (brain) controls heart rate
   - **Accelerator nerve**: Increases heart rate (during exercise)
   - **Decelerator nerve**: Decreases heart rate (at rest)
   - **Adrenaline**: Hormone that increases heart rate (fight or flight)

6. **Blood Vessels**
   - **Arteries**:
     - Carry blood away from heart
     - Thick, muscular walls
     - Elastic tissue (stretch and recoil)
     - High pressure
     - No valves (except semi-lunar)
   
   - **Veins**:
     - Carry blood towards heart
     - Thin walls
     - Less elastic tissue
     - Low pressure
     - Valves prevent backflow
   
   - **Capillaries**:
     - Connect arteries and veins
     - Very thin walls (one cell thick)
     - Large surface area
     - Site of exchange with tissues

7. **Blood Composition**
   - **Plasma** (55%):
     - Liquid part (mainly water)
     - Transports: carbon dioxide, digested food, urea, hormones, heat energy
   
   - **Red blood cells (Erythrocytes)**:
     - Biconcave disc shape (increases surface area)
     - No nucleus (more space for haemoglobin)
     - Contains haemoglobin
     - Function: Transport oxygen
     - Made in bone marrow, destroyed in spleen
   
   - **White blood cells**:
     - **Phagocytes**: Engulf and digest pathogens
     - **Lymphocytes**: Produce antibodies specific to pathogens
   
   - **Platelets**:
     - Cell fragments
     - Function: Blood clotting (prevent blood loss, prevent entry of microorganisms)

8. **Immune System**
   - **Primary response**: First infection, slow antibody production
   - **Secondary response**: Re-infection, faster and greater antibody production (memory cells)
   - **Vaccination**: Artificial immunity using weakened/dead pathogens or antigens

9. **Coronary Heart Disease**
   - **Risk factors**: Smoking, high-fat diet, lack of exercise, stress, genetics
   - **Causes**: Fatty deposits block coronary arteries
   - **Effects**: Reduced blood flow to heart muscle, heart attack

### Things to Remember

- **Double circulation is more efficient** - maintains higher pressure
- **Valves prevent backflow** - ensure one-way flow
- **Red blood cells have no nucleus** - more space for haemoglobin
- **Haemoglobin loads oxygen in lungs, unloads in tissues**
- **Platelets are fragments**, not whole cells
- **Vaccination produces memory cells** - faster response to future infections
- **Carbon monoxide from smoking** reduces oxygen-carrying capacity

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain how red blood cells are adapted to transport oxygen. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Biconcave disc shape increases surface area for gas exchange
- No nucleus provides more space for haemoglobin
- Contains haemoglobin which binds to oxygen in lungs
- Small size allows them to fit through narrow capillaries

**Question 2**: Describe the differences between arteries and veins. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Arteries carry blood away from heart; veins carry blood towards heart
- Arteries have thick, muscular walls; veins have thin walls
- Arteries have elastic tissue; veins have less elastic tissue
- Arteries carry high-pressure blood; veins carry low-pressure blood
- Veins have valves; arteries don't (except semi-lunar valves)

**Question 3**: Explain how vaccination provides immunity. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Vaccine contains weakened/dead pathogen or antigens
- Lymphocytes recognize antigens and produce antibodies
- Memory cells are produced and remain in blood
- On re-infection, memory cells produce antibodies faster and in greater quantity
- Prevents pathogen from causing disease

### Common Mistakes

- Stating red blood cells have a nucleus (they don't)
- Confusing arteries and veins (direction of blood flow)
- Forgetting that veins have valves
- Stating all white blood cells are the same (phagocytes and lymphocytes are different)
- Confusing primary and secondary immune responses
- Not mentioning memory cells in vaccination
- Stating platelets are cells (they are fragments)
- Confusing pulmonary and systemic circulation

## Chapter 6: Coordination

### Main Points

1. **Stimulus and Response**
   - **Stimulus**: Change in environment (internal or external)
   - **Receptor**: Detects stimulus (sense organ)
   - **Effector**: Produces response (muscle or gland)
   - **Coordinated response**: Stimulus → Receptor → CNS → Effector → Response

2. **Nervous System**
   - **Central Nervous System (CNS)**: Brain and spinal cord
   - **Peripheral nerves**: Connect CNS to sense organs and effectors
   - **Function**: Rapid communication using electrical impulses

3. **Neurones (Nerve Cells)**
   - **Sensory neurone**: Carries impulses from receptor to CNS
   - **Motor neurone**: Carries impulses from CNS to effector
   - **Relay neurone**: Connects sensory and motor neurones (in CNS)
   - **Structure**: Cell body, axon (long fibre), dendrites (branches)

4. **Reflex Actions**
   - **Reflex arc**: Automatic, rapid response to danger
   - **Example**: Withdrawing hand from hot object
   - **Pathway**: Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory neurone → Relay neurone (in spinal cord) → Motor neurone → Effector → Response
   - **Advantages**: Protects body from harm, doesn't require conscious thought

5. **Synapses**
   - **Gap** between neurones
   - **Neurotransmitters**: Chemicals that cross synapse
   - **Function**: Allows impulses to pass from one neurone to next
   - **One-way transmission**: Prevents impulses going backwards

6. **The Eye (Receptor)**
   - **Structure**:
     - **Cornea**: Transparent front, refracts light
     - **Iris**: Colored part, controls pupil size
     - **Pupil**: Hole in iris, allows light in
     - **Lens**: Focuses light onto retina
     - **Retina**: Contains light-sensitive cells (rods and cones)
     - **Optic nerve**: Carries impulses to brain
     - **Ciliary muscles**: Control lens shape
     - **Suspensory ligaments**: Hold lens in place
   
   - **Focusing**:
     - **Near objects**: Ciliary muscles contract, suspensory ligaments slacken, lens becomes fatter (more curved)
     - **Distant objects**: Ciliary muscles relax, suspensory ligaments tighten, lens becomes thinner (less curved)
   
   - **Response to light intensity**:
     - **Bright light**: Circular muscles contract, radial muscles relax, pupil constricts (smaller)
     - **Dim light**: Circular muscles relax, radial muscles contract, pupil dilates (larger)

### Things to Remember

- **Reflex actions are automatic** - don't involve brain (spinal cord only)
- **Synapses use chemicals** (neurotransmitters), not electrical impulses
- **Impulses travel in one direction** - due to synapses
- **Eye focuses by changing lens shape**, not position
- **Pupil size controls light entering**, not focusing
- **Rods detect light intensity**, **cones detect color**

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Describe a reflex action, using the example of withdrawing a hand from a hot object. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Heat detected by receptors in skin
- Impulse travels along sensory neurone to spinal cord
- Impulse passes through relay neurone (synapse)
- Impulse travels along motor neurone to arm muscles
- Muscles contract, hand moves away
- Automatic response, doesn't involve brain

**Question 2**: Explain how the eye focuses on near and distant objects. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- **Near objects**: Ciliary muscles contract, suspensory ligaments slacken, lens becomes fatter/more curved, light rays converge more
- **Distant objects**: Ciliary muscles relax, suspensory ligaments tighten, lens becomes thinner/less curved, light rays converge less

**Question 3**: Explain the role of synapses in the nervous system. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Gap between neurones
- Neurotransmitters allow impulses to cross synapse
- Ensures one-way transmission of impulses
- Allows integration of signals from multiple neurones

### Common Mistakes

- Stating reflex actions involve the brain (they use spinal cord)
- Confusing sensory and motor neurones
- Stating impulses cross synapse electrically (they use chemicals)
- Confusing pupil size with focusing (pupil controls light, lens controls focus)
- Not mentioning ciliary muscles and suspensory ligaments when describing focusing
- Stating all neurones are the same (sensory, motor, and relay are different)

## Chapter 7: Chemical Coordination in Animals (Humans)

### Main Points

1. **Hormones and the Endocrine System**
   - **Hormones**: Chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands
   - **Characteristics of hormones**:
     - Produced in small amounts
     - Secreted directly into bloodstream
     - Transported in blood plasma
     - Target specific organs (have receptors)
     - Slower than nervous system but longer-lasting effects
     - Not broken down quickly (persist in blood)
   
   - **Comparison with nervous system**:
     - Nervous: Electrical impulses, fast (milliseconds), short-lived, specific pathway
     - Hormonal: Chemical messengers, slow (seconds to hours), long-lasting, transported in blood

2. **Key Endocrine Glands and Their Hormones**

   **Table 7.1: Major Endocrine Glands and Hormones**
   
   | Gland | Hormone(s) | Function(s) |
   |-------|------------|-------------|
   | Pituitary | FSH, LH, ADH, Growth hormone | Master gland, controls other glands; FSH/LH control reproductive system; ADH controls water balance; Growth hormone controls growth |
   | Thyroid | Thyroxine | Controls metabolic rate, growth, development |
   | Adrenal | Adrenaline | Fight or flight response; increases heart rate, breathing rate, blood glucose |
   | Pancreas | Insulin, Glucagon | Insulin lowers blood glucose; Glucagon raises blood glucose |
   | Ovaries | Oestrogen, Progesterone | Control menstrual cycle, female secondary sexual characteristics |
   | Testes | Testosterone | Controls male secondary sexual characteristics |

3. **Control of Blood Glucose Concentration**
   - **Normal range**: 80-100 mg/100cm³ blood (approximately 4-6 mmol/dm³)
   - **High blood glucose** (after eating):
     - Pancreas detects high glucose
     - Beta cells in pancreas secrete insulin
     - Insulin causes:
       - Liver and muscle cells to take up glucose
       - Liver converts glucose to glycogen (glycogenesis)
       - Cells increase glucose uptake
     - Blood glucose decreases to normal
   
   - **Low blood glucose** (between meals, during exercise):
     - Pancreas detects low glucose
     - Alpha cells in pancreas secrete glucagon
     - Glucagon causes:
       - Liver converts glycogen to glucose (glycogenolysis)
       - Liver may produce glucose from amino acids (gluconeogenesis)
     - Blood glucose increases to normal
   
   - **Negative feedback**: System reverses the change (maintains homeostasis)

4. **Diabetes**
   - **Type 1 Diabetes**:
     - Body cannot produce insulin
     - Beta cells in pancreas destroyed (autoimmune)
     - Treatment: Insulin injections, controlled diet
     - Usually develops in childhood/young adulthood
   
   - **Type 2 Diabetes**:
     - Body produces insulin but cells don't respond (insulin resistance)
     - Often linked to obesity, lack of exercise
     - Treatment: Diet control, exercise, sometimes medication
     - Usually develops in older adults

5. **Adrenaline (Fight or Flight Response)**
   - **Produced by**: Adrenal glands (above kidneys)
   - **Stimulus**: Fear, stress, excitement, danger
   - **Effects**:
     - Increases heart rate and force of contraction
     - Increases breathing rate
     - Dilates pupils
     - Increases blood glucose (liver breaks down glycogen)
     - Diverts blood to muscles (vasoconstriction in skin/gut, vasodilation in muscles)
     - Increases mental alertness
   - **Purpose**: Prepares body for action (fight or flight)

6. **ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone) and Water Balance**
   - **Produced by**: Pituitary gland
   - **Stimulus**: High concentration of blood (low water content)
   - **Effect**: Increases permeability of collecting duct in kidney
   - **Result**: More water reabsorbed into blood, less urine produced, more concentrated urine
   - **Negative feedback**: When blood concentration returns to normal, ADH secretion decreases

7. **Thyroxine**
   - **Produced by**: Thyroid gland (in neck)
   - **Function**: Controls metabolic rate
   - **Effects**: 
     - Increases rate of chemical reactions in cells
     - Controls growth and development
     - Regulates body temperature
   - **Deficiency**: Causes goitre (swollen thyroid), slow metabolism, weight gain
   - **Excess**: Causes hyperthyroidism, fast metabolism, weight loss


### Things to Remember

- **Hormones are chemical messengers** - different from nervous system (electrical)
- **Hormones are slower** but longer-lasting than nervous responses
- **Negative feedback** maintains homeostasis (reverses changes)
- **Insulin lowers**, **glucagon raises** blood glucose
- **Adrenaline** prepares body for action (fight or flight)
- **ADH** controls water balance by affecting kidney
- **Diabetes** is failure to control blood glucose
- **Hormones target specific cells** with receptors

**Answer**:
- Nervous system uses electrical impulses (1 mark); hormonal system uses chemical messengers (1 mark)
- Nervous system is fast/milliseconds (1 mark); hormonal system is slow/seconds to hours (1 mark)
- Nervous system effects are short-lived (1 mark); hormonal system effects are long-lasting (1 mark)
- Nervous system uses neurones/specific pathways (1 mark); hormonal system uses blood transport (1 mark)
- Nervous system targets specific muscles/glands (1 mark); hormonal system targets organs with receptors (1 mark)

**Answer**:
- Blood glucose monitored by pancreas (1 mark)
- **High glucose**: Pancreas secretes insulin (1 mark)

- Insulin increases glucose uptake by cells (1 mark)
- Blood glucose decreases (1 mark)
- **Low glucose**: Pancreas secretes glucagon (1 mark)

- This is negative feedback (maintains constant level) (1 mark)

**Answer**:
- Increases heart rate (1 mark)

- Increases mental alertness (1 mark)
- Dilates pupils (1 mark)
- Prepares body for fight or flight response (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Explain what happens when a person has Type 1 diabetes. (4 marks)

- Confusing nervous and hormonal systems
- Stating hormones are faster than nervous system (they're slower)

- Forgetting negative feedback mechanism
- Confusing Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes

## Chapter 10: Transport in Plants

### Main Points

1. **Need for Transport**
   - Plants need water and mineral ions from roots
   - Leaves need water for photosynthesis
   - Products of photosynthesis need to be transported

2. **Plant Transport Tissues**
   - **Xylem**: 
     - Transports water and mineral ions (upwards)
     - Dead cells, no cytoplasm
     - Lignified walls (strength)
     - Continuous tubes from roots to leaves
   
   - **Phloem**:
     - Transports sugars (sucrose) and amino acids
     - Bidirectional (up and down)
     - Living cells (sieve tubes and companion cells)
     - Translocation

3. **Water Uptake by Roots**
   - **Root hairs**: Increase surface area for absorption
   - **Osmosis**: Water moves from soil (dilute) to root cells (concentrated)
   - **Active transport**: Mineral ions absorbed against concentration gradient

4. **Transpiration**
   - **Definition**: Loss of water vapour from leaves
   - **Process**: Water evaporates from mesophyll cells → diffuses through stomata
   - **Factors affecting rate**:
     - Temperature (increases)
     - Humidity (decreases)
     - Wind speed (increases)
     - Light intensity (increases - stomata open)
   
   - **Transpiration stream**: Water pulled up xylem from roots to leaves
   - **Cohesion-tension theory**: Water molecules stick together (cohesion), pulled up by evaporation (tension)

5. **Stomata**
   - **Structure**: Pores in leaf epidermis, surrounded by guard cells
   - **Function**: Control gas exchange and water loss
   - **Opening**: Guard cells take in water, become turgid, pore opens
   - **Closing**: Guard cells lose water, become flaccid, pore closes

### Things to Remember

- **Xylem transports upwards only** (water and minerals)
- **Phloem transports both directions** (sugars and amino acids)
- **Transpiration is a consequence**, not a purpose (unavoidable water loss)
- **Root hairs increase surface area** for absorption
- **Stomata control water loss** - close in dry conditions
- **Cohesion** keeps water column continuous

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain how water moves from roots to leaves. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Water absorbed by root hairs via osmosis
- Enters xylem vessels
- Water evaporates from mesophyll cells in leaves
- Creates tension (pulling force)
- Water molecules cohere (stick together)
- Continuous column pulled up xylem
- Transpiration stream

**Question 2**: Describe how stomata control water loss. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Guard cells control pore size
- When turgid (full of water), stomata open
- When flaccid (lose water), stomata close
- In dry conditions, guard cells lose water, stomata close
- Reduces water loss by transpiration

**Question 3**: Compare xylem and phloem. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- **Xylem**: Dead cells, transports water/minerals upwards only, lignified walls
- **Phloem**: Living cells, transports sugars/amino acids both directions, no lignification
- **Xylem**: No cytoplasm, continuous tubes
- **Phloem**: Sieve tubes with companion cells

### Common Mistakes

- Stating phloem transports only downwards (it's bidirectional)
- Confusing xylem and phloem functions
- Stating transpiration is a purpose (it's a consequence)
- Not mentioning cohesion when explaining water movement
- Forgetting root hairs increase surface area
- Stating xylem cells are alive (they're dead)

## Chapter 11: Plants and Food

### Main Points

1. **Photosynthesis - The Process**
   - **Word equation**: Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen (in presence of light and chlorophyll)
   - **Symbol equation**: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
   - **Definition**: Process by which plants make glucose (and other carbohydrates) using light energy
   - **Location**: Chloroplasts (contain chlorophyll)
   - **Raw materials**: Carbon dioxide (from air), Water (from soil)
   - **Products**: Glucose (used immediately or stored as starch), Oxygen (waste product)
   - **Energy conversion**: Light energy → Chemical energy (stored in glucose)

2. **Structure of Leaf (Adaptations for Photosynthesis)**

   **Table 11.1: Leaf Structure and Function**
   
   | Structure | Function |
   |-----------|----------|
   | Upper epidermis | Transparent, allows light through; waxy cuticle reduces water loss |
   | Palisade mesophyll | Tightly packed cells with many chloroplasts; main site of photosynthesis; near upper surface for maximum light |
   | Spongy mesophyll | Loosely packed cells with air spaces; allows gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ out) |
   | Lower epidermis | Contains stomata for gas exchange |
   | Stomata | Pores controlled by guard cells; allow CO₂ in, O₂ out, water vapour out |
   | Veins (xylem/phloem) | Xylem brings water; Phloem removes glucose |
   | Chloroplasts | Contain chlorophyll; absorb light energy |

3. **Testing for Starch in Leaves**
   - **Method**:
     1. Kill leaf in boiling water (stops all reactions) - 30 seconds
     2. Turn off Bunsen burner (safety - ethanol is flammable)
     3. Place leaf in boiling ethanol (removes chlorophyll/green color)
     4. Remove when pale/colorless
     5. Wash in cold water (softens leaf)
     6. Spread on tile, add iodine solution
     7. Blue-black color = starch present
   
   - **Important**: Plant must be in light for several hours before test
   - **De-starching**: Place plant in dark for 2-3 days to remove stored starch

4. **Requirements for Photosynthesis**
   - **Light**: Provides energy (usually sunlight)
   - **Chlorophyll**: Green pigment that absorbs light energy
   - **Carbon dioxide**: From air (enters through stomata)
   - **Water**: From soil (absorbed by roots, transported in xylem)
   - **Suitable temperature**: Enzymes need optimum temperature

5. **Investigating Requirements for Photosynthesis**
   
   **Table 11.2: Experiments to Test Requirements**
   
   | Requirement | Method | Expected Result |
   |-------------|--------|-----------------|
   | Light | Cover part of leaf with black paper/aluminum foil for 48 hours, test for starch | Covered part: no starch (yellow-brown with iodine); Exposed part: starch (blue-black) |
   | Carbon dioxide | Place plant in bell jar with soda lime (absorbs CO₂) or place leaf in sealed bag with CO₂ absorber | No starch produced |
   | Chlorophyll | Use variegated leaf (part green, part white), test for starch | Green parts: starch present; White parts: no starch |
   | Water | Difficult to test directly (plant would die) | - |

6. **Limiting Factors of Photosynthesis**
   - **Definition**: Factor that limits the rate when in short supply
   - **Main limiting factors**:
     - **Light intensity**: Low light = slow rate; increases up to saturation point
     - **Carbon dioxide concentration**: Low CO₂ = slow rate; increases up to saturation point
     - **Temperature**: Low temp = slow (enzymes work slowly); increases up to optimum; above optimum enzymes denature
   
   - **Graph interpretation**:
     - Steep slope = factor is limiting
     - Plateau/flat = another factor is limiting
     - At plateau, factor is no longer limiting

7. **Uses of Glucose in Plants**
   - **Immediate use**: 
     - Respiration (to release energy)
     - Converted to other substances
   
   - **Storage**:
     - Converted to starch (insoluble, compact, doesn't affect osmosis)
     - Stored in roots (carrots, potatoes), stems (sugar cane), seeds (wheat, rice)
   
   - **Other conversions**:
     - Cellulose (for cell walls)
     - Proteins (with nitrates from soil)
     - Lipids (for energy storage)
     - Sucrose (for transport in phloem)

8. **Mineral Ions Needed by Plants**
   
   **Table 11.3: Essential Mineral Ions**
   
   | Mineral Ion | Source | Function | Deficiency Symptoms |
   |-------------|--------|----------|---------------------|
   | Nitrates | Soil (absorbed by roots) | Make proteins, DNA, chlorophyll | Stunted growth, yellow leaves (chlorosis) |
   | Phosphates | Soil | Make DNA, cell membranes, ATP | Poor root growth, purple leaves |
   | Potassium | Soil | Enzyme activation, water balance | Yellow leaves with dead spots, weak stems |
   | Magnesium | Soil | Makes chlorophyll | Yellow leaves (chlorosis) between veins |

9. **Gas Exchange in Leaves**
   - **During day (light)**:
     - Photosynthesis occurs (produces O₂, uses CO₂)
     - Respiration also occurs (uses O₂, produces CO₂)
     - Net: O₂ out, CO₂ in (photosynthesis rate > respiration rate)
   
   - **During night (dark)**:
     - Only respiration occurs (no photosynthesis)
     - Net: O₂ in, CO₂ out

10. **Adaptations of Leaves for Photosynthesis**
    - Large surface area (captures more light)
    - Thin (short diffusion distance for gases)
    - Many chloroplasts (especially in palisade layer)
    - Stomata for gas exchange
    - Air spaces in spongy mesophyll (allows gas diffusion)
    - Network of veins (brings water, removes products)

### Things to Remember

- **Photosynthesis converts light energy to chemical energy**
- **Starch test requires removal of chlorophyll first** (boiling ethanol)
- **Light, CO₂, and temperature can all limit photosynthesis**
- **Plants respire all the time** (day and night), but only photosynthesize in light
- **Glucose is converted to starch for storage** (starch is insoluble)
- **Mineral ions are absorbed by active transport** (against concentration gradient)
- **Stomata allow gas exchange** but also cause water loss
- **Chlorophyll is essential** - white parts of variegated leaves don't photosynthesize

## Chapter 12: Coordination in Plants

### Main Points

1. **Tropisms - Plant Growth Responses**
   - **Definition**: Directional growth response to a stimulus
   - **Types**:
     - **Phototropism**: Response to light
     - **Gravitropism (Geotropism)**: Response to gravity
     - **Hydrotropism**: Response to water
   
   - **Positive tropism**: Growth towards stimulus
   - **Negative tropism**: Growth away from stimulus

2. **Phototropism**
   - **Shoots**: Positive phototropism (grow towards light)
   - **Roots**: Usually negative phototropism (grow away from light, but weak response)
   - **Mechanism**:
     - Light detected by tip of shoot (photoreceptors)
     - Auxin (plant hormone) produced in tip
     - Auxin moves down shoot
     - Light causes auxin to move to shaded side
     - Higher auxin concentration on shaded side
     - Auxin causes cell elongation (cells grow longer)
     - Shaded side grows faster than lit side
     - Shoot bends towards light
   
   - **Advantage**: Maximizes light capture for photosynthesis

3. **Gravitropism (Geotropism)**
   - **Roots**: Positive gravitropism (grow downwards, towards gravity)
   - **Shoots**: Negative gravitropism (grow upwards, away from gravity)
   - **Mechanism**:
     - Gravity detected by root tip/shoot tip
     - Auxin accumulates on lower side (due to gravity)
     - **In roots**: High auxin concentration inhibits growth, lower side grows slower, root bends downwards
     - **In shoots**: High auxin concentration promotes growth, lower side grows faster, shoot bends upwards
   
   - **Advantage**: 
     - Roots anchor plant and reach water/minerals
     - Shoots reach light for photosynthesis

4. **Auxins - Plant Hormones**
   - **Production**: Produced in tips of shoots and roots
   - **Transport**: Moves down from tip (not up)
   - **Functions**:
     - Control cell elongation (growth)
     - Cause phototropism
     - Cause gravitropism
     - Can inhibit root growth (at high concentrations)
     - Promote root growth (at low concentrations)
   
   - **Effect on cells**: 
     - Makes cell walls more flexible
     - Allows cells to take in water
     - Cells elongate (grow longer)
   
   - **Distribution**: Uneven distribution causes bending

5. **Experiments on Tropisms**

   **Table 12.1: Classic Experiments**
   
   | Experiment | Method | Result | Conclusion |
   |------------|--------|--------|------------|
   | Phototropism - tip removed | Remove tip of shoot, expose to light from one side | No bending occurs | Tip detects light and produces auxin |
   | Phototropism - tip covered | Cover tip with opaque cap, expose to light from one side | No bending | Tip must receive light to respond |
   | Phototropism - tip separated | Place tip on agar block, place block on one side of cut shoot | Shoot bends away from block | Auxin moves from tip, causes growth |
   | Gravitropism - root | Place root horizontally | Root bends downwards | Root responds to gravity |
   | Gravitropism - rotating | Place on rotating platform (removes gravity effect) | No gravitropic response | Gravity needed for response |

6. **Other Plant Responses**
   - **Hydrotropism**: Roots grow towards water
     - Stronger than gravitropism in some plants
     - Important for water uptake


7. **Commercial Uses of Plant Hormones**
   - **Auxins**:
     - Rooting powders (promote root growth in cuttings)
     - Weedkillers (selective - kill broad-leaved weeds, not grasses)
     - Fruit setting (prevent fruit drop)
   
   - **Gibberellins**:
     - Promote seed germination
     - Increase fruit size
     - Delay fruit ripening
   
   - **Ethene (Ethylene)**:
     - Promotes fruit ripening
     - Used in fruit storage/transport

8. **Comparison: Plant vs Animal Coordination**

   **Table 12.2: Coordination Systems**
   
   | Feature | Plants | Animals |
   |---------|-------|---------|
   | Type | Hormones only | Nervous + Hormonal |
   | Speed | Slow (hours/days) | Fast (milliseconds) or slow |
   | Response | Growth (irreversible) | Movement (reversible) |
   | Communication | Hormones in phloem/xylem | Electrical impulses + hormones in blood |
   | Examples | Tropisms | Reflexes, hormonal responses |

### Things to Remember

- **Tropisms are growth responses** - involve cell elongation, not movement
- **Auxins are produced in tips** - move down, not up
- **Auxin causes cell elongation** - makes cells grow longer
- **Uneven auxin distribution causes bending**
- **Shoots are positively phototropic** (towards light), **negatively gravitropic** (away from gravity)
- **Roots are positively gravitropic** (towards gravity), usually negatively phototropic
- **Tip must be present** for tropic responses (tip detects stimulus and produces auxin)
- **High auxin promotes shoot growth** but **inhibits root growth**

### Past Paper Questions

**Question 1**: Explain how a plant shoot responds to light from one side (phototropism). (6 marks)

**Answer**:
- Light detected by tip of shoot (1 mark)
- Auxin produced in tip (1 mark)
- Auxin moves to shaded side (1 mark) - light causes uneven distribution (1 mark)
- Higher auxin concentration on shaded side (1 mark)
- Auxin causes cell elongation on shaded side (1 mark)
- Shaded side grows faster than lit side (1 mark)
- Shoot bends towards light (1 mark)

**Question 2**: Describe an experiment to show that the tip of a shoot is needed for phototropism. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Remove tip from one shoot (1 mark)
- Leave tip on another shoot (control) (1 mark)
- Expose both to light from one side (1 mark)
- Shoot with tip bends towards light (1 mark)
- Shoot without tip does not bend (1 mark)
- Conclusion: tip needed for phototropism (1 mark)

**Question 3**: Explain the difference between phototropism and gravitropism. (4 marks)

**Answer**:
- Phototropism is response to light (1 mark); gravitropism is response to gravity (1 mark)
- Shoots show positive phototropism (towards light) (1 mark)
- Shoots show negative gravitropism (away from gravity/upwards) (1 mark)
- Roots show positive gravitropism (towards gravity/downwards) (1 mark)
- Both involve auxin distribution (1 mark)

**Question 4**: Explain why roots grow downwards and shoots grow upwards. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Gravity detected by tips (1 mark)
- Auxin accumulates on lower side due to gravity (1 mark)
- **Roots**: High auxin inhibits growth, lower side grows slower, root bends down (1 mark)
- **Shoots**: High auxin promotes growth, lower side grows faster, shoot bends up (1 mark)
- Advantage: roots reach water/minerals, shoots reach light (1 mark)

**Question 5**: The graph shows the effect of auxin concentration on growth of shoots and roots. Describe and explain the results. (5 marks)

**Answer**:
- Low auxin concentration: roots grow, shoots grow slowly (1 mark)
- Medium auxin concentration: roots grow less, shoots grow more (1 mark)
- High auxin concentration: roots inhibited, shoots grow most (1 mark)
- Shows auxin promotes shoot growth but inhibits root growth at high concentrations (1 mark)
- Different tissues respond differently to same hormone (1 mark)

**Question 6**: Explain one commercial use of plant hormones. (3 marks)

**Answer**:
- Rooting powders contain auxins (1 mark)
- Applied to cuttings (1 mark)
- Promotes root growth (1 mark)
- Allows propagation of plants from cuttings (1 mark)
- OR: Selective weedkillers contain auxins (1 mark) - kill broad-leaved weeds but not grasses (1 mark)

### Common Mistakes

- Stating auxins are produced in roots (produced in tips)
- Confusing phototropism and gravitropism
- Stating tropisms involve movement (they involve growth/cell elongation)
- Not mentioning that tip must be present
- Stating auxin always promotes growth (inhibits root growth at high concentrations)
- Confusing positive and negative tropisms
- Not explaining uneven auxin distribution
- Stating plants have nervous system (they only have hormonal coordination)
